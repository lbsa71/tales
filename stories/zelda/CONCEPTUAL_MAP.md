# Conceptual Map / Story Spine

## A Rabbit's Life: Narrative Arc and Themes

**Format**: Short Story (7,754 words across 10 chapters)
**Current Version**: v2.0 (condensed from 12,441-word novella)

---

## Act 1: Origins (Chapters 1-3)

### Story Events
- Birth in litter, first sensations, separation and pair-bond forms
- Move to outdoor area by hutch, first earth contact
- Establishing dominance through eating order
- Brief escape through gap in wire, night in the woods
- Return and reinforced wire ("No hole. Wall. More wall.")

### Themes
- **Embodiment**: The immediacy of physical existence
- **Instinct without outlet**: Natural drives with no natural fulfillment
- **Burrow-longing**: The pull toward earth that cannot be satisfied
- **Freedom glimpsed and denied**: Brief taste of beyond, then walls

### Tone
Wonder, innocence, gentle unease, brief exhilaration, stark finality

### Key Sensory Elements
- Darkness and warmth of birth
- Mother's milk scent, straw, wood shavings
- First light, first cold air
- Wood of hutch walls, no earth beneath
- First contact with earth in yard
- Wire reinforcement, escape route sealed

---

## Act 2: Living (Chapters 4-6)

### Story Events
- Nesting behavior and its emptiness
- Middle years, seasonal cycles
- Human puberty observed through scent/sound changes
- Routines established
- Predator (kitten) drops into yard, smaller sister drives it back repeatedly
- Adult cat watches from beyond fence, respects learned boundary

### Themes
- **Repetition**: Days cycling without progress
- **Captivity normalized**: The invisible walls of habit
- **Time passing**: Bodies changing, rhythms continuing
- **Protection through dominance**: The smaller sister as defender

### Tone
Meditative, rhythmic, subtle melancholy

### Key Sensory Elements
- Hay scent, belly fur pulled for nesting
- Gate sounds marking human arrival
- Seasonal temperature shifts
- Human scent changes (puberty)
- Predator scent, confrontation

---

## Act 3: Loss (Chapters 7-9)

### Story Events
- Illness begins in larger sister (nester): scent changes, swollen belly (uterine cancer)
- Vet visits: antiseptic, metal, shaved fur
- Death and removal of larger sister
- Body's return, surviving smaller sister noses cold form
- Predator enters hutch (door left open); surviving smaller sister cannot defend alone

### Themes
- **Dissolution**: Body failing, instincts dimming
- **Incomprehension**: Medical intervention without understanding
- **Bodily truth**: Death as physical reality, not concept
- **Fierceness lost**: The defender discovers aggression required someone to protect

### Tone
Stark, tender, elegiac

### Key Sensory Elements
- Iron and sweetness in scent (illness in larger sister)
- Metal and antiseptic (veterinary)
- Cold body, no response
- Human weeping sounds
- Predator inside hutch: the body that once defended now only freezes

---

## Act 4: Solitude (Chapter 10)

### Story Events
- Survivor (smaller sister) alone, scent memories fading
- Final winter, body failing
- Death and integration with all-being

### Themes
- **Completion**: One life-facet finishing
- **Return to earth**: What was denied in life, given in death
- **Facet closing**: Individual merging back into universal

### Tone
Serene, cosmic, quietly devastating

### Key Sensory Elements
- Wind through hawthorn (sounds like sister)
- Fading scent in hollow
- Cold, slowing heartbeat
- Earth receiving body

---

## Narrative Frame

### Opening (Chapter 1)
- **Perspective**: Sensory flashback from survivor
- **Tense**: Past, but immediate and vivid
- **Establishes**: Birth, warmth, two bodies, burrow-longing
- **Reader entry**: "One facet" of universal experience

### Closing (Chapter 10)
- **Tense shift**: Gradual move from past to present
- **Dissolution**: Senses dissolving into world
- **Integration**: Individual consciousness merges with all-soul
- **Reader exit**: Understanding they witnessed one complete life

---

## Core Questions for Each Chapter

1. What sensory experience dominates this scene?
2. What instinct is present but unfulfilled?
3. How does captivity shape this moment without being named?
4. What does the reader feel that the rabbit cannot conceptualize?
5. How does this scene build toward the ethical reckoning?
