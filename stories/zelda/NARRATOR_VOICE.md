# Narrator Voice Profile

## Character Bible for Non-Anthropomorphic Rabbit Perspective

---

## Cognitive Style

### The Narrator Observes
- Scent (primary sense, most detailed)
- Temperature (warmth/cold as comfort/threat)
- Light quality (bright/dim/dark)
- Sound vibration (footsteps, wind, voices)
- Ground texture (wood, straw, earth, grass)
- Touch pressure (grooming, contact, handling)

### The Narrator Remembers Through
- Bodily sensation (muscle memory, physical states)
- Repeated patterns (daily rhythms, seasonal cycles)
- Scent-associations (place = scent, being = scent)
- *NOT through narrative reflection or conceptual understanding*

### The Narrator Does NOT
- Conceptualize abstract ideas
- Moralize or judge
- Use metaphor (requires conceptual thought)
- Understand causation beyond immediate association
- Reflect on past or plan for future
- Comprehend captivity, death, or absence as concepts

### The Narrator IS Capable Of
- Instinctual response (fear, comfort-seeking)
- Recognition (familiar scent = known being)
- Pattern-following (routine as bodily expectation)
- Immediate comfort/discomfort assessment
- Seeking behavior when something is missing

---

## Emotional Capacity

*All emotions expressed ONLY through physical manifestation*

### Fear
- Heart racing
- Muscles freezing
- Flight impulse (legs tensing)
- Ears flat or swiveling
- Rapid breathing

### Comfort
- Relaxed muscles
- Slow breathing
- Grooming behavior
- Body low, sprawled
- Eyes half-closed

### Loss (Seeking)
- Circling familiar places
- Nose to ground, searching
- Reduced appetite
- Listlessness
- Returning to scent-marked areas

### Curiosity/Alertness
- Nose forward, whiskers spread
- Ears up and forward
- Careful approach
- Body raised, weight on haunches
- Quick assessment movements

### Contentment
- Tooth purring (soft grinding)
- Flopped position
- Relaxed ear position
- Slow grooming

---

## Knowledge Scope

### The Narrator Knows
- Immediate environment (every corner, scent-mapped)
- Seasonal patterns (temperature, light duration)
- Routine sounds (gate, footsteps, feeding time)
- Sister's presence/absence (scent, warmth, movement)
- Safe places (hollow under hawthorn, hutch corner)
- Danger sounds (sudden movements, unfamiliar vibrations)

### The Narrator Senses But Doesn't Understand
- Human development (puberty as scent/voice change)
- Medical intervention (as strange handling, sharp smells)
- Captivity itself (boundaries as simply "where world ends")
- Time passing (as body changes, not counted years)
- Death (as absence, cold stillness, scent fading)

### Instincts Without Fulfillment
- **Burrow-longing**: Pull toward earth, digging that yields nothing
- **Nesting**: Building without pregnancy, waiting for nothing
- **Territorial marking**: Boundaries within boundaries
- **Flight response**: Running within enclosure

## Voice Artifacts

### Style Philosophy

Balance immediacy with readability. Short fragments create urgency, but strings of two-word sentences sacrifice emotional texture for effect. Allow sentences to flow when the moment calls for it. Vary rhythm to serve meaning.

### Approved Language Patterns

**Connected Sensory Observation**:
- ✓ "The scent changed over days, first iron, then sweetness, something turning"
- ✓ "Wind moves through the branches, carrying a sound that echoes movement beside me"
- ✓ "Ground cold beneath, air sharp above, light hanging low on the horizon"

**Bodily State with Flow**:
- ✓ "Heart beating faster now, ears swiveling up, the ground vibrating with approaching steps"
- ✓ "Breathing grows shallow, belly swollen and heavy, each movement requiring more effort"
- ✓ "Muscles loosening into warmth, body settling against body, comfort finding its place"

**Sensory Catalog with Connection**:
- ✓ "The smell shifts through its changes: iron first, then sweetness, then something turning deeper."
- ✓ "Sound of the gate opening, then footsteps on earth, then the soft placement of the bowl."
- ✓ "Scent of hay rising, belly fur warm against skin, the ground cool where it presses up."

**Evocative Fragment (Use Sparingly)**:
- ✓ "Wind through the hawthorn. The sound of her beside me. Then empty space."
- ✓ "Darkness first. Then warmth. Body against body."
- ✓ "No earth beneath. Only the pull toward it, constant."

### Forbidden Language Patterns

**Emotional Attribution**:
- ✗ "I missed her"
- ✗ "I felt afraid"
- ✗ "I was curious about..."
- ✗ "I felt lonely"

**Conceptual Thought**:
- ✗ "I wondered where she'd gone"
- ✗ "The humans didn't understand"
- ✗ "I knew something was wrong"
- ✗ "I understood that..."

**Speculation About Others**:
- ✗ "She must have felt..."
- ✗ "They seemed to want..."
- ✗ "She was trying to..."

**Metaphor and Simile**:
- ✗ "As if the world had ended"
- ✗ "Like a hole in my chest"
- ✗ "The silence was deafening"

**Narrative Reflection**:
- ✗ "I had always known..."
- ✗ "Looking back, I realize..."
- ✗ "That was the moment when..."

## Terminology for Characters

### Referring to the Other Rabbit

The narrator's companion is her littermate sister. Based on rabbits' ability to distinguish kin through scent, the following terms are biologically justified:

**Approved terms:**
- **"Sister"**: PRIMARY TERM - rabbits can identify littermates through scent
- **"Nest-sister"**: Emphasizes shared birth
- **"The nester"**: Role-based (her primary behavior trait)
- **"The other body"**: Acceptable in early chapters before differentiation solidifies
- **"The quiet-body"** / **"The waiting-one"**: Temperament/hierarchy descriptors

**Usage evolution:**
- Early: "The other body" (world undifferentiated)
- Middle: "Sister" becomes primary, with role descriptors for clarity
- Late: "Sister" carries emotional weight through her absence

### Referring to Humans

From a rabbit's sensory perspective, humans are most distinctly characterized by their vertical posture.

**Approved terms:**
- **"Tall-body" / "Tall-bodies"**: PRIMARY TERM
- **"The smaller tall-body"**: Younger human sister
- **"The larger tall-body"**: Older human sister
- **"Strange tall-body"**: Unfamiliar humans (veterinarian, visitors)

**Avoid:**
- ✗ "Large ones" (too vague)
- ✗ "Flock-members" (too conceptual)
- ✗ "Two-legs" (too anthropomorphic)

## Forbidden Words

Never use these words (or their variations):

| Word | Why Forbidden |
|------|---------------|
| think | Conceptual cognition |
| feel (emotional) | Requires emotional labeling |
| wonder | Speculation, future-thinking |
| hope | Future anticipation |
| miss | Conceptual absence |
| love | Abstract emotion |
| understand | Comprehension |
| remember | Use "the body holds" instead |
| know (abstract) | Conceptual knowledge |
| realize | Sudden understanding |
| believe | Abstract thought |
| wish | Future desire |
| want (abstract) | Use physical seeking instead |

## Sentence Structure Guidelines

### Guiding Principle

Sentence length should serve the emotional moment. Urgency and fragmentation have their place, but strings of terse two-word sentences sacrifice readability and emotional depth. Allow connective tissue. Let observations breathe and build.

### Birth/Youth (Chapters 1-2)
- Mix of short and medium-length sentences
- Some connecting words (and, then, but) allowed
- Immediate sensation with flowing observation
- Example: "Darkness first, then warmth. Body pressed against body, another heartbeat close, another breathing. Milk-scent fills the small space."

### Middle Years (Chapters 3-7)
- Longer rhythmic sentences with internal variation
- Repetition for emphasis, not just economy
- Seasonal markers woven into flowing prose
- Example: "The gate sounds and footsteps follow. Bowl placed in its usual spot, food scent rising warm. The same sounds each day, the air cooling as the season turns, the rhythm holding."

### Illness/Death (Chapters 8-9)
- Deliberate, weighted sentences that slow the pace
- White space between sections, not just between words
- Fragments reserved for key moments of impact
- Example: "The scent changed first. Iron beneath sweetness, something turning beneath the fur. Belly heavy now, breathing shallower each day."

### Final Integration (Chapter 10)
- Gradual tense shift (past → present)
- Sentences simplifying toward dissolution
- Final fragments earned by preceding complexity
- Example: "Wind moves through the hawthorn, carrying a sound like her breathing beside me. Or wind only. Wind now. Earth receiving what it always waited for. Warmth fading. All warmth. All wind. All."
