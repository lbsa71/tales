# Style Guide

## Language and Style References for "A Rabbit's Life"

---

## Core Style Principles

1. **Clinical precision**: Biologically accurate, observationally exact
2. **Poetic rhythm**: Lyrical without becoming decorative
3. **Devastating restraint**: Emotional power through what is NOT said
4. **Non-anthropomorphic rigor**: No human concepts attributed to rabbits

---

## Approved Sentence Structures

### Principle: Varied Complexity

The prose should balance immediacy with flow. While sensory fragments create urgency and animal awareness, overly terse constructions can impede readability. Allow sentences to breathe and connect, creating rhythm through variety rather than uniform brevity.

**Balance guideline**: Mix short sensory bursts with longer, flowing observations. Aim for sentences that carry emotional weight through their structure, not just through terseness.

### Sensory Fragments (Use Sparingly)
```
Darkness first. Then warmth.
No earth beneath. Only wood, only straw.
```

### Connected Sensory Observation (Primary Mode)
```
Body pressed against body, warmth shared between two heartbeats.
The scent of milk and straw fills the small space, familiar and close.
The pull toward earth persists, toward a darkness that should exist below.
```

### Sensory Catalog with Flow
```
The smell changes over days: first iron, then sweetness, then something turning beneath the fur.
Sound of the gate, followed by footsteps, followed by the soft click of bowl against earth.
Air moves through fur, ground presses up from beneath, warmth settles in beside.
```

### Bodily State with Texture
```
Heart beats faster, muscles tightening as ears swivel forward.
Breathing grows shallow, belly heavy and swollen, each movement slower than the last.
Body loosens into the straw, eyes half-closing, the soft grinding of tooth-purring beginning.
```

### Flowing Observation (Rhythmic)
```
Wind moves through the hawthorn branches, carrying a sound that could be her breathing beside me.
Morning light touches the grass, dew catching it in small bright points, and the gate sound comes.
Cold has settled into the ground where warmth once was, the hollow holding only emptiness now.
```

### Extended Sensory Passage
```
The gate sounds and footsteps follow, heavy on the packed earth of the path.
The larger tall-body approaches, the one whose scent has changed these past 
seasons, sharper now, carrying that musky edge of maturity. Hands lower the bowl 
to its usual spot. Food scent rises, filling the space. The body moves toward it, 
pulled by the smell, by the rhythm of days repeating.
```

## Forbidden Constructions

### Emotional Attribution
- ❌ "I think/feel/wonder/hope/miss/love..."
- ❌ "I was sad/happy/afraid/curious..."
- ❌ "I felt a sense of..."

### Metaphor and Simile
- ❌ "As if..." / "Like a..."
- ❌ "It was as though..."
- ❌ "The world seemed to..."

### Speculation
- ❌ "She must have..."
- ❌ "They probably..."
- ❌ "I suppose..."

### Understanding
- ❌ "I understand/realize/know that..."
- ❌ "It became clear that..."
- ❌ "I came to see that..."

### Narrative Reflection
- ❌ "I had always known..."
- ❌ "Looking back..."
- ❌ "That was when I first..."
- ❌ "I would later learn..."

### Past Perfect for Memory
- ❌ "I had remembered..."
- ❌ "I had felt this before..."

## Rhythm Guidance by Section

### General Principle

Sentence complexity should serve emotional truth. Short sentences create urgency; longer sentences create flow and contemplation. Vary between them to create rhythm. Avoid strings of two-word sentences that sacrifice readability for effect.

### Birth/Youth (Chapters 1-2)
**Style**: Present, immediate, but with connective tissue

```
Darkness first, then warmth. Body pressed against body, 
another heartbeat close, another rhythm of breathing. Two of us 
in the small space. The pull downward persists, toward earth 
that isn't there. Wood beneath instead, and straw. The scent 
of mother fading with each passing of light.
```

**Characteristics**:
- Mix of short and medium sentences
- Some connecting words allowed (and, then, but)
- Present sensation with slight connective flow
- Commas creating rhythm within sentences

### Middle Years (Chapters 3-6)
**Style**: Rhythmic, meditative, showing routine through variation

```
The gate sounds and footsteps follow. Bowl placed in its spot, 
food scent rising. The same sequence repeats each day as light 
changes angle through the seasons. Summer grass gives way to 
autumn damp, then to winter frost, then to spring mud warming 
again. The gate sounds. Footsteps. The rhythm holds everything 
in place, day after day, the body knowing the pattern without 
counting.
```

**Characteristics**:
- Flowing sentences with internal rhythm
- Repetition of key phrases for emphasis
- Seasonal markers woven into longer passages
- Time compressed through pattern and variation

### Illness/Death (Chapters 7-9)
**Style**: Slowing, more deliberate, weight on each observation

```
The scent changed first.

Iron mingled with sweetness, something turning beneath 
the fur, beneath the skin. Belly grown heavy now, 
breathing shallower than before.

The other body moves less with each passing day. She lies 
in the hollow, does not rise for the gate sound, does not 
push forward to eat first.

The roles between them shift. The body holds these changes 
without understanding them.
```

**Characteristics**:
- White space between sections
- Deliberate, weighted sentences
- Pauses created through line breaks, not just fragments
- Physical details of decline given full sentences

### Final Integration (Chapter 10)
**Style**: Tense shift, gradual dissolution

```
Wind moves through the hawthorn. It carries a sound that 
could be her breathing beside me.

Or only wind. Only wind now. The scent fading from the hollow, 
from the earth, from the straw she once carried in her mouth.

Cold comes slowly. The body slows with it. Heart beating 
slower. Breath growing shallow. The pull toward earth, 
the earth that was always there, beneath, waiting 
through all the seasons of longing. Earth receiving now 
what it was always meant to hold.

Warmth fading. All warmth.
All wind.
All.
```

**Characteristics**:
- Shift from past to present tense
- Sentences gradually simplifying toward fragments
- Line breaks increasing toward end
- Final dissolution into white space

## Punctuation Guidelines

### Periods
- Use for full stops in sensory observation
- Create rhythm through short sentences

### Commas
- Use within sensory lists
- Create flowing observation sequences

### Dashes and Hyphens
- **FORBIDDEN**: Em-dashes (—), en-dashes (–), and hyphens used for subordinate clauses
- The "statement, subordinate clause" structure created by dashes produces a pithy, self-consciously clever rhythm that becomes clichéd and does not feel rabbity
- Instead of dashes, use periods to create separate sentences, or use commas for natural flow
- ❌ "Body against body—another heartbeat close"
- ✓ "Body against body. Another heartbeat close."
- ✓ "Body against body, another heartbeat close"

### Ellipsis
- **Avoid** in most cases
- Reserve for final dissolution only

### Line Breaks
- Increase in illness/death sections
- Show slowing of experience
- Final chapter: frequent breaks

## Word Choice Guidelines

### Prefer
| Category | Approved Words |
|----------|----------------|
| Movement | hop, bound, freeze, circle, press, dig, nose |
| Sensation | scent, warmth, cold, pressure, vibration |
| Body | muscles, heart, breath, belly, ears, fur |
| Environment | earth, straw, wood, wind, light, dark |
| States | tight, loose, heavy, slow, still, racing |

### Avoid
| Category | Forbidden Words |
|----------|-----------------|
| Emotion | sad, happy, lonely, afraid, love |
| Cognition | think, wonder, realize, understand |
| Abstraction | meaning, purpose, reason, hope |
| Time | remember, anticipate, expect |

## Sample Transformations

### Wrong → Right

**Wrong**: "I felt lonely without her."
**Right**: "The hollow held no warmth anymore. Her scent faded from the straw with each passing day."

**Wrong**: "I wondered where she had gone."
**Right**: "Nose pressed to earth, circling the hollow where she had been. The scent grew weaker with each pass."

**Wrong**: "The humans seemed sad."
**Right**: "Salt-water scent rose from the tall-bodies. Strange sounds came from their throats, broken and wet, their bodies shaking as they stood."

**Wrong**: "I remembered our youth."
**Right**: "The body holds what the mind cannot: warmth beside warmth, two heartbeats close together, the sweet weight of milk-scent filling a small dark space."

**Wrong**: "I was afraid of the vet."
**Right**: "Metal scent filled the space, antiseptic sharp beneath it. Heart raced, muscles pulled tight, the urge to flee pressing against walls that offered no escape."

## Character Terminology

### The Two Rabbits

**Primary distinction**: Use "sister" for the companion rabbit, establishing the littermate bond that rabbits can sense through shared scent.

**Companion rabbit (larger, docile, nests):**
- **"Sister"**: PRIMARY - use most frequently
- **"Nest-sister"**: When emphasizing shared origin
- **"The nester"**: During nesting behavior scenes
- **"The waiting-one"**: In feeding hierarchy contexts
- **"The other body"**: Acceptable in Chapter 1, phase out after

**Narrator (smaller, dominant, escapes):**
- **"The body"**: Primary self-reference
- Implied through action and hierarchy
- Avoid naming self; experience is universal

**Clarity through context:**
- Vary terms to avoid repetition
- Make referent clear through action/location
- Use "sister" when bond or relationship is emphasized

### Humans

**Primary term**: "Tall-body" / "Tall-bodies" (emphasizes vertical posture, key sensory distinction)

**Specific references:**
- "The smaller tall-body" (younger sister)
- "The larger tall-body" (older sister)
- "Strange tall-body" (unfamiliar humans)
- "The one with metal-scent" (veterinarian)

**Avoid:**
- ✗ "Large ones" (too vague)
- ✗ "Flock-members" (over-conceptual)
- ✗ "Two-legs" (too anthropomorphic)

## Checklist for Every Sentence

1. ☐ Is this sensory, not conceptual?
2. ☐ Is this physical, not emotional?
3. ☐ Is this immediate, not reflective?
4. ☐ Does it avoid forbidden words?
5. ☐ Does it match the chapter's rhythm?
6. ☐ Could a rabbit experience this?
