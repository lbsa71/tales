# Felis Catus

## Research Dossier: Cat Behavior and Physiology Relevant to Rabbit-Cat Interactions

---

## Overview

*Felis catus* (domestic cat) is a small carnivorous mammal and natural predator of small prey including rabbits. This document compiles biological and behavioral research to ensure accuracy in depicting cat-rabbit interactions from the rabbit's sensory perspective in "A Rabbit's Life."

---

## Sensory Systems

### Primary: Vision

Cats are visual hunters with exceptional low-light vision.

**Visual characteristics:**
- **Night vision**: 6-8 times better than humans due to tapetum lucidum (reflective layer behind retina)
- **Motion detection**: Highly sensitive to movement; small, rapid movements trigger prey drive
- **Color vision**: Limited; dichromatic (blue and yellow visible, not red)
- **Binocular vision**: ~140 degrees of overlapping visual field for depth perception
- **Peripheral vision**: ~200 degrees total field of view

**Relevance to rabbit narrator:**
- Cat's visual focus is palpable: "Eyes fix on the bodies"
- Freezing behavior in rabbits exploits cat's motion-based hunting
- Cat stare is intense, direct, predatory

### Secondary: Olfaction (Smell)

Cats have approximately 200 million scent receptors (humans: 5 million, rabbits: 100 million).

**Olfactory characteristics:**
- **Vomeronasal organ**: Flehmen response (mouth open, lip curled) to process pheromones
- **Scent marking**: Facial glands, urine, scratching deposits chemical signals
- **Prey detection**: Can smell prey animals and their fear pheromones
- **Territorial marking**: Strong ammonia scent in urine

**Scent profile as perceived by rabbit:**
- **Musty**: Natural fur oils
- **Sharp/ammonia**: Urine markers
- **Carnivore markers**: Meat, blood traces on breath and in body odor
- **Predator pheromones**: Chemical signals that trigger prey instinct
- Distinctly different from herbivore (rabbit) or omnivore (human) scent

### Tertiary: Hearing

Cats have excellent hearing, capable of detecting ultrasonic frequencies used by rodent prey.

**Auditory characteristics:**
- **Frequency range**: 48 Hz to 85,000 Hz (humans: 20-20,000 Hz, rabbits: 360-42,000 Hz)
- **Directional ears**: Can rotate independently 180 degrees
- **Ultrasound detection**: Hear high-frequency distress calls of small mammals
- **Hunting advantage**: Detect prey movement by sound alone

**Relevance to rabbit narrator:**
- Cat footfalls are deliberate, controlled (predator stealth)
- Contrast with human footfalls (heavier, less controlled)

## Predatory Behavior

### Hunting Sequence

Cats are solitary ambush predators with distinct behavioral phases.

**Typical hunting sequence:**
1. **Detection**: Visual or auditory cue triggers interest
2. **Stalk**: Low posture, slow, deliberate movement, body pressed low
3. **Pause**: Frequent freezing to assess prey and avoid detection
4. **Pounce preparation**: Hindquarters elevated, body tense, wiggling (preparing leg muscles)
5. **Rush/pounce**: Rapid sprint, usually 3-5 meters maximum
6. **Capture**: Bite to neck or spine (killing bite)
7. **Play/eating**: May engage in object play with captured prey before consuming

**From rabbit perspective:**
- "Movement slow, low to the ground" (stalk phase)
- "Eyes fix on the bodies" (visual lock)
- Approach pattern predictable but terrifying

### Prey Drive Variables

Not all cats display equal prey drive toward rabbits.

**Factors affecting prey drive:**
- **Age**: Kittens and young cats highest prey drive
- **Hunger**: Well-fed cats may hunt for play rather than food
- **Experience**: Cats raised with rabbits may have reduced or absent prey drive
- **Individual variation**: Some cats are highly motivated hunters, others disinterested
- **Size differential**: Cats may be intimidated by adult rabbits close to their own size

**Play vs. predation:**
- **Play postures**: Elevated hindquarters, sideways hopping, brief pursuits
- **Predatory postures**: Low, flat, sustained stalking, direct approaches
- **Ambiguity**: Rabbit cannot distinguish intent; both trigger fear response

### Intimidation and Learned Avoidance

Cats can be intimidated by prey animals that fight back.

**Conditions for cat intimidation:**
- **Unexpected aggression**: Prey that lunges instead of fleeing disrupts hunting sequence
- **Noise**: Loud sounds (rabbit thumping) startles cats
- **Size perception**: Rabbit rising on haunches appears larger
- **Pain/surprise**: Rabbit kicks or bites can discourage further approaches
- **Early experience**: Young cats intimidated once may retain wariness into adulthood

**Learned avoidance:**
- A cat frightened by a rabbit during formative period (kitten to young adult) may develop lasting wariness
- Pattern: Approach → Rabbit aggression → Retreat → Future approaches more cautious
- Cat maintains interest but respects boundary
- **Story relevance**: The predator in Chapter 6 learns to keep distance after initial confrontation

**Long-term dynamics:**
- Cat may continue to visit but maintains safe distance
- Curiosity persists but approach is inhibited
- Relationship becomes "managed threat" rather than active danger

## Physical Characteristics

### Size and Build

Domestic cats vary in size but are generally similar to or slightly larger than adult rabbits.

**Average domestic cat:**
- **Weight**: 4-5 kg (8-11 lbs)
- **Length**: 45-50 cm body + 25-30 cm tail
- **Height**: 23-25 cm at shoulder

**Average domestic rabbit (medium breed):**
- **Weight**: 2-5 kg (4.5-11 lbs)
- **Length**: 40-50 cm
- **Height**: 20-25 cm

**Relevance to narrative:**
- Size parity means rabbits and cats are evenly matched physically
- Dominant rabbit can intimidate cat through aggression
- Without confidence, rabbit is vulnerable

### Movement Patterns

Cats move with distinct patterns recognizable to prey animals.

**Locomotion characteristics:**
- **Gait**: Digitigrade (walk on toes), silent due to retractable claws
- **Stalking**: Belly low, slow, deliberate, body serpentine
- **Pouncing**: Explosive acceleration from stillness
- **Tail movements**: Twitching tail tip indicates hunting focus

**From rabbit perspective:**
- Cat movement is **controlled** and **deliberate** (vs. human movement which is casual)
- "Movement slow, low to the ground"
- Tail movements may be visible and threatening

## Territorial Behavior

### Scent Marking

Cats are territorial and mark their range extensively.

**Marking methods:**
- **Urine spraying**: High-value territory markers, strong ammonia scent
- **Scratching**: Visual and scent marks (paw pad glands)
- **Facial rubbing**: Cheek glands deposit pheromones on objects
- **Fecal marking**: Sometimes left uncovered in territorial disputes

**Cat territory in domestic settings:**
- **Home range**: Varies widely (indoor cats: single home; outdoor cats: up to several acres)
- **Core territory**: Areas where cat spends most time
- **Hunting grounds**: Areas patrolled regularly for prey
- **Respect for barriers**: Most cats respect fences as territorial boundaries unless motivated

**Story relevance:**
- Cat's scent appears repeatedly: "musty and sharp, carrying traces of ammonia"
- Rabbit yard falls within cat's hunting grounds
- Wire fence initially respected, but cat tests boundaries

## Age and Development

### Kitten to Adult Progression

Cat behavior and confidence changes significantly with age.

**Kitten (8 weeks - 6 months):**
- **Prey drive**: Developing but playful, ineffective
- **Confidence**: Low; easily startled
- **Learning period**: Critical for socialization and fear responses
- **Intimidation susceptibility**: Highest; negative experiences shape future behavior

**Juvenile (6 months - 2 years):**
- **Prey drive**: Peak hunting behavior
- **Confidence**: Increasing but still vulnerable to intimidation
- **Risk-taking**: High exploration and testing of boundaries
- **Physical maturity**: Approaching adult size

**Adult (2-7 years):**
- **Prey drive**: Stable; may decrease slightly with age
- **Confidence**: Established; behaviors mostly fixed
- **Territorial**: Strong territory establishment and defense
- **Hunting efficiency**: Peak skills

**Senior (7+ years):**
- **Prey drive**: Gradual decline
- **Confidence**: Remains stable if healthy
- **Activity**: Reduced; less interested in hunting
- **Caution**: May become more conservative in risk-taking

**Story application:**
- Cat in Chapter 6 is likely juvenile or young adult (active hunting, testing boundaries)
- Intimidation during this period creates lasting wariness
- Cat in Chapter 9 is now adult/senior, emboldened by rabbit's changed behavior

## Cat-Rabbit Dynamics

### Scenario 1: Confident Rabbit, Intimidated Cat

**Establishment phase (Chapter 6):**
- Cat approaches with predatory interest
- Dominant rabbit responds aggressively (thumping, lunging, charging)
- Cat retreats, startled by unexpected response
- Pattern reinforces over repeated encounters

**Maintenance phase:**
- Cat returns periodically (curiosity, territorial patrol)
- Cat maintains safe distance
- Rabbit's presence alone deters approach
- Equilibrium: "managed but not forgotten"

**Behavioral stability:**
- Once established, pattern persists for years
- Cat learns: rabbit yard = aggressive prey = avoid
- Rabbit learns: aggression = successful defense

### Scenario 2: Loss of Protector

**Changed dynamic (Chapter 9):**
- Companion rabbit (the one defended) has died
- Surviving rabbit (the defender) faces cat alone
- Critical shift: Aggression requires motivation beyond self-preservation
- Without companion to protect, dominant rabbit becomes submissive

**Psychological basis:**
- Rabbits are social animals; defense is often performed for group
- Solitary rabbit reverts to prey instinct: freeze and flee
- The "fierceness" was relational, not intrinsic

**Cat's response:**
- Cat detects behavioral change: "The predator senses the change"
- Approaches grow bolder: "Approaches without the caution it once showed"
- Learned avoidance fades when aggression ceases
- Cat interprets frozen rabbit as vulnerable prey

### Scenario 3: Gate Breach

**Opportunity:**
- Unlatched gate = access to previously defended space
- Cat enters yard: "Inside now. Inside the space the body once defended"
- Rabbit trapped in corner, unable to flee or fight

**Outcome:**
- Human intervention prevents predation
- Rabbit experiences fundamental vulnerability
- Realization (sensory, not conceptual): alone = prey

## Sensory Markers for Writing

### Cat Scent Profile (from rabbit's nose)

**Primary scent notes:**
- **Musty**: Natural fur oils with sharp, distinct quality
- **Ammonia**: Territorial urine markers
- **Carnivore markers**: Meat proteins in saliva and breath
- **Predator pheromones**: Chemical signals triggering prey fear response

**Scent intensity indicators:**
- **Faint**: Cat at distance, scent carried on wind
- **Present**: Cat nearby but not immediate threat
- **Strong**: Cat at wire fence, investigating
- **Overwhelming**: Cat inside yard, close proximity

### Visual Markers (from rabbit's eyes)

**Body posture:**
- **Stalking**: "Low to the ground", "belly pressed low"
- **Hunting focus**: "Eyes fix on the bodies"
- **Approach**: "Deliberate movement", "nose forward"
- **Retreat**: "Draws back", "turns", "disappears"

**Movement quality:**
- **Slow**: Controlled, deliberate (stalk)
- **Paused**: Frozen assessment
- **Rapid**: Pounce or retreat

### Auditory Markers (from rabbit's ears)

**Cat sounds:**
- **Soft footfalls**: Nearly silent compared to humans
- **Rustling**: Movement through grass or brush
- **Absence of sound**: Predator stealth

**Contrast with human sounds:**
- Humans: Heavy, rhythmic, predictable
- Cats: Light, irregular, silent

## Narrative Applications

### The Cat as Symbol

**In Chapter 6:**
- External threat introduces fear into captive routine
- Dominant rabbit's successful defense demonstrates agency
- Pattern: threat → response → mastery
- **Thematic function**: Even in captivity, a rabbit can assert dominance over some threats

**In Chapter 9:**
- Same threat, changed response
- Loss of companion removes motivation for defense
- Vulnerability revealed: fierceness was relational
- **Thematic function**: Solitude unmasks fundamental prey identity

### Biological Accuracy Requirements

**Cat behavior must reflect:**
- Natural hunting instincts (stalk, pause, pounce sequence)
- Learned avoidance after intimidation
- Emboldening when threat (rabbit aggression) disappears
- Territorial curiosity that persists despite wariness

**Rabbit perception must reflect:**
- Scent-first detection (primary sense)
- Instinctive freeze response to predator pheromones
- Shift from dominance to submission based on social context
- No understanding of "cat" as concept, only sensory markers of "predator"

**Human role must reflect:**
- Humans as protectors but not present for every threat
- Gate maintenance as literal life-or-death boundary
- Human intervention in Chapter 9 prevents tragedy but doesn't restore rabbit's agency

## Terminology for Narrative

### Referring to the Cat

From rabbit's sensory perspective, the cat is not "cat" but a category:

**Approved terms:**
- **"Predator"**: PRIMARY TERM - category recognition based on scent/movement
- **"The predator"**: Specific individual (same cat returning)
- **Descriptive phrases**: "Thick fur", "patches of orange and white", "large"

**Avoid:**
- "Cat" (human word, rabbit has no name for species)
- "It" (acceptable but limit use; "the predator" maintains tension)
- Over-description (rabbit notes key features, not detailed inventory)

### Sensory References

**Scent:**
- "Musty and sharp, carrying traces of ammonia"
- "Predator scent"
- "Something that hunts"

**Visual:**
- "Large, fur thick, patches of orange and white"
- "Movement slow, low to the ground"
- "Eyes fix on the bodies"

**Movement:**
- "Approaches"
- "Pauses at the wire"
- "Retreats"
