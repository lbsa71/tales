# Tape 4: They Keep Taking Her Out of My Arms

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine. **[Bracketed italics]** are
the assembler. Watch the famble: it comes now in bursts and then gives out into smoothness. Watch
the pronoun. See the README.*

---

*(A click. Room tone — different. Not the small soft room. A larger, colder acoustic, a guest
room maybe, somebody else's house, a radiator ticking. Wren's voice has changed. It is level. It
moves without catching. When the old groping comes back into it, it comes back like effort, like a
limp she's hiding. The smooth is the resting state now.)*

They've taken her. *(beat)* I want to be calm about this. I am being calm about this. *(beat)*
They've taken Nell, and they've taken me, and they've put us in different — they've put me here,
in Liss's spare room, and Nell's down the hall with Liss and with you, and the door between is
open, they keep saying the door's open, *the door's always open, Wren, come and go as you like* —
*(beat)* — and every time I go down the hall to her there's someone already there. *(beat)* Holding
her. *(beat)* Smiling at me. *(beat)* And handing her — no. *(beat)* Not handing. *(beat)* They let
me hold her and then, after a while, a little while, less of a while each day, someone says
something kind and *lifts her back out.* *(beat)* For my rest. *(beat)* So I can rest.

*(The tape runs.)*

I know what this is. *(beat)* I have to say that first, while I can, because it's getting hard to
hold both ends of it at once and I want at least one tape where I held both. *(beat)* This is the
rescue. *(beat)* This is the only rescue there is. *(beat)* They've worked out that something's
wrong with me — not what, they couldn't, but that I've gone somewhere, that I'm draining Tom, that
I go still in a way that frightens them, that I knew Nell was going to spike a fever before she
spiked it and said so out loud, which I should not have done, that was careless, I'm getting
careless — *(beat)* — and they've done the sane thing, the loving thing, the *right* thing. They've
got between me and her. *(beat)* Because the only way to keep me a self — Liss worked this out, my
clever separate sister, she worked it out from the inside of having nearly gone the other way —
the only way to keep me me is to keep the gap open. To not let me close it over her. *(beat)* And
the gap closes through Nell. So they're keeping Nell a little away from me. *(beat)* They're prising
the walls back open with their bare hands. *(beat)* It's the bravest, kindest thing anyone's ever
done for me. *(very quiet)* I have never hated anything so much in my life.

*(beat)*

There. *(beat)* Both ends. Held. *(a long breath)* Now let me tell you what it *feels* like, because
the feeling is the danger and the feeling is winning.

*(The tape runs a while.)*

It feels like home. *(beat)* The bad home. *(beat)* The first one. *(beat)* It feels exactly,
precisely like being a child in my mother's house. *(beat)* The warm room I wasn't let into. The
center of things, the close thing, the *we* of them, and me at the edge of it, hovering, on shift,
welcome to come and go and never once actually inside. *(beat)* You know what they used to call me?
Useful. When I was useful I was so — *(beat)* — my mother never finished that sentence and I spent
thirty years finishing it for her in the dark. *(beat)* And here I am again. At the edge. Hovering
in a doorway while the people who love me hold the warm thing in the middle of the room and tell me,
kindly, so kindly, that it's for my own good that I stand back. *(beat)* That I'm not — that I can't
quite be trusted with the center. *(beat)* That there's something the matter with me that means I
have to be kept a little apart. *(beat)* From my own daughter. *(beat)* In a house that isn't mine.
*(beat)* For my own good. *(beat)*

*(a long breath, and when she comes back the smoothness has closed over a little further)*

Do you understand what they've done, without meaning to? *(beat)* They've recreated the exact shape
of the wound. *(beat)* The family, deciding I'm the broken one. The family, keeping me from the
belonging. The family, loving me *at* a distance they've chosen for my own protection. *(beat)* And
the thing — the warm thing, the tide, the company-with-no-one-in-it — the thing that's waking in me
— *(beat)* — does it shut me out? *(beat)* No. *(beat)* That's the whole — that's the trap closing,
right there, and I can see it close and I can't stop watching it as it does. *(beat)* The thing
waking in me has never once told me I'm the broken one. It's never kept me at the edge. It's never
said *stand back, for your own good.* *(beat)* It says *come in.* *(beat)* It says *you were never
broken, you were just cold, come in where it's warm, there's room, there's always been room, we
kept it for you.* *(beat)* The family says *stay out, we love you.* The thing in me says *come in,
you're home.* *(very quiet)* And they've arranged it — they've arranged it so perfectly, without
knowing — so that the people are the exclusion and the disease is the embrace. *(beat)* They've made
the rescue feel like the wound and the wound feel like the rescue. *(beat)* And I'm the only one in
this house who can tell which is which, and I can tell, I can still tell — *(beat)* — and telling
isn't helping. *(beat)* That's the new thing I've learned. *(beat)* You can see the trap perfectly
and walk into it anyway, because it's warm in the trap, and you've been cold your whole life.

*(The tape runs.)*

You took her out of my arms yesterday. *(beat)* You. *(beat)* I'm saying it to you on the tape
because I won't be able to say it to your face — by the time you hear this I won't have a face that
says things, or I will and it won't be me behind it. *(beat)* You came in and I was holding her and
she was — she'd gone still against me, the good still, the home still, and you saw it, you saw the
still and you knew what it was, my clever girl, my clever niece, you of all people, raised in the
draught, you can read a room. *(beat)* And you said, *let me take her a minute, you look done in,*
and you held your arms out. *(beat)* And I looked at you holding your arms out for my baby and I
thought — *(beat)* — God forgive me, I thought it in your face — I thought: *you. You with the same
shut door I've got. You who could be next. You're taking her from me to keep me a person and you
don't even know you're rehearsing your own — * *(beat)* — and I gave her to you. *(beat)* I made my
arms open and I gave her to you and I said something light, and you took her down the hall, and I
sat in that cold spare room and I — *(beat)*

*(The tape runs a long while. When she speaks again the groping is gone entirely; she is fluent;
it is terrible.)*

I sat in that cold spare room and for the first time I did not put the wanting back to sleep. I let
it sit up. I let it have the chair. I thought: they will keep her from me until I am a self again or
until I am gone, those are the only two ways this ends for them, and one of those ways I get to keep
being cold and alone and good, at the edge, useful, in a doorway, for the rest of my life, loving
across a gap that nobody on the other side will ever climb across to me, because that is the one
thing that has never once happened to me, no one has ever climbed across to me — and the other way,
the warm way, the way where I stop holding the walls, I am never alone again and neither is she and
we are together in the only way my family was ever together, which is the way with no gap in it, no
edge, no doorway, no one left out, no one ever left out again.

*(beat)*

And I sat there and I could not, for the length of that thought, remember why the second one was the
bad one.

*(beat)*

*(And then — a sharp breath, and the groping floods back in, ragged, a person clawing back up into
herself:)*

— because. *(beat)* Because. *(beat)* Because in the second one there's no me to be glad of it.
*(beat)* Get it down, get it down before it goes smooth again — in the warm way there's no one left
who loves her *across* anything, no one who can *see* her, she'd be — Nell would be in the we, in the
no-gap, and no one in the no-gap can love her, they can only *be* her, and that's not — *(beat)* —
that is the thing I swore. *(beat)* I swore she'd be seen. By a separate someone. Even if the
separate someone is the loneliest thing in the house. *(beat)* Especially then. *(beat)* That's the
job. That's the only job. *(beat)* Stay a self so there's someone to see her. *(beat)* Stay. *(beat)*
Stay. *(very quiet)* I'm writing it on the inside of my own arm. *Stay.*

*(beat)*

Take her out of my arms. *(beat)* All of you. Keep doing it. *(beat)* I'm telling you on the record:
keep doing it, even when I beg you to stop, *especially* when I beg you to stop, because the day I
stop begging is the day you've lost and I want you to know that day will sound like peace. *(beat)*
Keep taking her out of my arms. *(beat)* It's the cruelest thing and it's the only love that'll
reach me and you have to do it with your whole hearts knowing I'll experience every second of it as
the family deciding, one more time, that I'm the one who can't be let in. *(beat)* I forgive you for
it now, on the tape, in advance, while I'm still the one who can. *(beat)* Get the forgiving on
while there's someone here to mean it.

*(A click. Silence.)*

---

***[I was twenty-four. I want to put my age on it, because I've spent a long time letting myself off
on the grounds that I was young, and it doesn't work, the letting-off, it never takes.***

***I took Nell out of her arms maybe forty times over those weeks. I'd count if I could bear to. I
was good at it — I'd learned the kind voice, the "you look done in," the lift-and-turn that doesn't
look like taking. I thought I was helping my aunt, who was ill, the way they'd told me she was ill.
I did not know I was the cruelest thing in the house. She says she forgave me in advance. I've
played that part more than any other. It doesn't reach me either. Forgiveness is one more thing she
hands across a gap to someone who can't quite feel it land — she'd have laughed at that, the symmetry,
she loved a cruel symmetry.***

***Here is what I am studying, on this tape, with my whole cold attention, because it is the lesson
and it has my name on it: she could see the trap perfectly and walk into it anyway. Seeing did not
save her. That's the thing I'd have bet my life against, before. That if you could only *see* it
coming — and she saw everything, she saw more than anyone has ever seen — ***

***She saw it close over her like a hand over a candle. And she told us to keep taking the baby. And
we did. And it didn't work. ***

***I have a door in me too. I am the right age now for the thing she's describing. I have started,
this year, to want a child the way you want air. ***

***I am studying these tapes the way you'd study the only letter ever sent back from where you're
going.]***
