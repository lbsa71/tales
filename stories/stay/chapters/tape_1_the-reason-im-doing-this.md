# Tape 1: The Reason I'm Doing This

*The book is a recording. Plain text is Wren, talking into the dictaphone — spoken, not
written; read it as a voice. The cues in italics — (beat), (a long breath) — are the sound of
the machine: the pauses, the room, the running tape. They are also the clock; watch what
happens to them. **[Bracketed italics]** are the one who assembled the tapes — Liss's daughter,
the never-woken child, the person Wren is talking to — reconstructing, too late, with love. See
the README.*

---

***[There are seven of these. I don't know that's all of them. I know it's all the ones I
have. I have spent a long time deciding what order to put them in, and I have decided to stop
deciding, and just go by the only thing I can trust, which is what she says. She says, on this
one, that she's starting. So this one is first.***

***You should know before you begin: I am the person she is talking to. She says "you," and
she means me. I have thought about taking that out — about turning her toward the wall instead,
so you could listen without being implicated. I can't. It's the truest thing on the tapes, that
she's talking to someone. So you'll be me, for a while. I'm sorry. It's the only way I know to
let her be heard.]***

---

*(A click. Room tone — a small room, soft, the hum of a fridge somewhere through a wall. A
chair. Then her voice, close to the machine, dry, certain, the voice of someone who has decided
to do a hard thing and is doing it.)*

Right. *(beat)* So. This is on. The little light's on. *(a small dry sound)* I've been carrying
this thing around for a week not turning it on, which is — that's me all over, isn't it. Buy
the thing, hold the thing, build a whole speech about the thing, and then —

*(beat)*

Liss bought it for me. I want that on the record, since this is a record. My sister bought me a
dictaphone, the kind a doctor would've used forty years ago, found it in a drawer of somebody's
cleared-out house, and she put it in my hand and she said — she didn't say *talk into it.* She's
careful, my sister, she's learned to be careful with me the way you're careful with a person
who flinches. She said: *leave yourself something.* *(beat)* That's what she said. *Leave
yourself something, in case.*

*(a long breath)*

In case. *(beat)* You'd know all about *in case,* wouldn't you. You grew up in *in case.* The
two of us raising you with one eye always on the door — I'm sorry about that, by the way, I've
been meaning to say it where it'd stay said. We did our best and our best had a draught coming
off it you could feel from the next room. *(beat)* But that's not this. This is a different *in
case.* This one's mine.

*(beat)*

Let me do the hard part first, while I've still got — while it's still just me in here. *(beat)*
I'm going to have a baby. *(a breath)* There. Said into a machine, out loud, in a quiet room.
Three more weeks, give or take; she's low, the midwife says, she's ready, she's a good size, she
turned when she was meant to. A girl. *(beat)* I'm forty-six years old and I'm having a daughter,
and I married a man who loves me, and I have a small house with a fridge that hums, and by every
account, including my own, this is the best thing that has ever happened to me. *(beat)* I want
you to hear me say that and mean it. Write it down. *She was happy.* Whatever comes after on
these tapes — and something's coming, that's the whole reason for the tapes — you hold on to the
fact that the woman at the start of it was *happy,* and not stupid, and not asking for it.
*(very quiet)* I'd had so little for so long. And then I had the lot.

*(The tape runs. Nothing. Then:)*

And I'm terrified. *(beat)* Not the way you're meant to be. Not — *what if I drop her, what if I
don't love her enough,* the ordinary terrors, I'd give a finger for the ordinary terrors. *(beat)*
I'm terrified because I am the one person in the whole history of my family who knows exactly what
might be about to happen, and I can't tell a single soul who'd believe me, except a machine, and
you, after.

*(beat)*

So let me tell it to you straight, the way I was never told anything straight, the way I've had
to dig every true thing out of myself with my fingernails. *(beat)* You know most of it. You sat
and listened to the other tapes — the long ones, the ones I made about Daniel and your mother and
the house. I talked into the dark for years so you'd have it. So I won't do all that again.
*(beat)* But here's the spine of it, the one sentence, the thing I need sitting under everything
else I say:

*(beat)*

A child of my family is born with the — there's a thing inside, a — *(she gropes; this is the
sound to remember; she is reaching for a word across a gap)* — call it a door. Born with a door
in them. And the door doesn't open by itself. It has to be opened from the other side, by
someone who's already got theirs open, when you're small, before you've got a — before there's a
*you* there to mind. *(beat)* That's how they make a new one. They open the door on a baby and
the baby goes through and stops being a separate anyone, ever. *(beat)* That's the whole engine.
That's what was done to your mother and her mother and back and back past the word for back.
*(beat)* And it's what *wasn't* done to me. They tried. It didn't take. The door's in me — I can
feel the draught of it some nights, I always could — but it never opened, and so I got to be a
person, by accident, by a failure, which is the only reason I could ever — *(beat)* — which is
the only reason I could love your mother across the gap that one night, and love you, badly,
across the gap your whole life. The door staying shut is the worst thing that ever happened to me
and it's the only good thing in me. I've made my peace with that. Took thirty years.

*(beat)*

And it's what wasn't done to *you.* *(beat)* Now listen, because this is the part that's supposed
to keep me safe, and I want you to hear me leaning on it, so that later, if it gives way, you'll
have heard exactly how much weight I put on it. *(beat)* You've got the door too. Course you have.
You're your mother's daughter; it's in you same as it's in all of us. But yours never opened
either, and we know *why* yours never opened — because by the time you were born there was no one
left with an open door to open it. The chord was dead. Your mother got out; I never got in; the
old ones are gone. There was nobody home to wake you. *(beat)* So you grew up dark and safe and
ordinary and faintly *off* in a way no one could name, same as me, and you're fine. You're a
grown woman and you're fine. *(beat)* And I have watched you be fine for — for your whole life,
and I have thought, every single day: *if she's fine, I'm fine.* Same door. Same nobody-to-open-
it. The thing that could open it is dead. *(beat)* It can't be opened. *(beat)* It can't.

*(beat. Then, lower:)*

You hear how good that is? *(a small dry sound)* It's a good argument. It's a *true* argument. I
could win it in front of anyone. *(beat)* I've been winning it in front of myself for nine months.

*(The tape runs.)*

Here's the crack in it. *(beat)* The one I can't quite — *(beat)* They open the door from the
other side. With somebody who's already through; an open door wakes a shut one. *(beat)* But it
works the other way too. I learned that the way I learn everything, too late, reading between the
lines of a thing nobody meant me to read. *(beat)* A shut door, grown up, no one ever opened it —
if *she* has a baby, and the baby's got the door, and the baby reaches for her the way they all
reach — *(beat)* — the baby can open the mother. *(beat)* From the inside. Backwards.

*(beat)*

And I am a woman with a shut door. *(beat)* And in three weeks I am going to be holding a baby
with the door in her. *(very quiet)* And she is going to reach for me. *(beat)* Because that's
what they do. That's the first thing they do. They reach.

*(a long breath)*

So you see my trouble. *(almost a laugh, and there is no laugh in it)* I've got the good argument
— the chord's dead, nobody can open me, look at you, you're fine — and I've got the crack, which
is that the thing that opens a castaway isn't the chord. It never was. *(beat)* It's her own
child. *(beat)* And nothing's dead about my child. She's kicking me right now, as it happens.
*(beat)* Hello. *(beat)* Yes. I know. I'm talking about you. Go back to sleep.

*(The tape runs a while with nothing on it.)*

This is why the machine. *(beat)* I've thought about why it has to be the machine and not — Liss
would sit with me. Tom would. You'd come, if I asked, you'd sit in that chair and let me say all
this to your face. *(beat)* And I can't. *(beat)* Because here's the thing I worked out, the one
useful thing I ever worked out, the night I went into the room I was never allowed in and found
your mother on the floor: love is being *seen* by a separate someone. From outside. Across a gap.
That's the whole of it. That's all love ever is — a separate person on the far side of you,
looking, and not looking away. *(beat)* And the thing that's maybe coming for me closes the gap.
It melts the separate someone into you until there's no outside left, no one across from you, no
gap to be seen across — and it does it while feeling, the entire time, *exactly like being
loved.* Better than being loved. Like never being alone again. *(beat)* So I can't trust a
person. *(beat)* Any person I love, if they sit with me through this, the closer they get the
more it'll feel like the thing pulling me under, and I won't be able to tell their love from the
tide, and neither will they. *(beat)* But this. *(she must mean the machine; you can hear her
touch it)* This little dead thing. It listens and it stays separate. It can't be merged. It can't
melt into me, because there's no one in it. *(beat)* It's the one guaranteed-separate other I've
got left. *(a long breath)* A woman who learned that love is being seen by a separate someone,
ends up confiding in the one thing in her life that's separate forever because it can't see her
at all. *(beat)* I know. *(beat)* I do hear it. I just don't have a better idea.

*(beat)*

That's what these are. *(beat)* I'm going to talk to this machine every few days, or every day,
or whenever I can hold a thought still long enough — and I'm going to do it *because* it's an
effort. Because reaching for the right word, getting it wrong, reaching again — *(beat)* — that's
the sound of me. That's the muscle. The being-a-separate-person muscle. The thing the tide
relaxes. *(beat)* So every time I sit here and grope for a word and curse and find it — that's me,
flexing it, on purpose, keeping it from going soft. *(beat)* You'll be able to hear, on these
tapes, whether the muscle's holding. *(very quiet)* That's the other reason for them. Not just to
leave you a message. To give me something to fail at out loud, so somebody can hear me failing,
so it isn't — so I don't go quietly. *(beat)* I have watched people in my family go quietly. I am
not going quietly. Whatever else.

*(The tape runs.)*

One more thing and then I'll let the poor machine rest. *(beat)* There's a sentence I said to you,
on the old tapes, about Daniel. *(beat)* You'd have been — I don't know, you were small, you
won't remember the saying of it, but it'll be there, in his story, near the end. I said: *I must
tell you that he gave himself up happily.* *(beat)* I said it because it was the one mercy I could
do him and the one cruelty I couldn't spare you. That he wasn't dragged. That nobody held him
down. That my family used the whole of him — every lonely hopeful thing in him — and the only
part of the bargain he was ever let in on was the part where he was *happy.* He climbed those
stairs like a man going to his own good life. *(beat)* And I've always thought that was the worst
thing in the whole story. Worse than if they'd forced him. *(beat)* That he *wanted* it. That he
gave himself up, and was glad.

*(beat)*

I don't know why I'm thinking about that sentence tonight. *(beat)* I've a lot on. I should sleep.
*(beat)* Hello, you. *(beat — she means the baby)* All right. All right. I'm going.

*(a long breath)*

*She was happy.* Remember I made you write it down. *(beat)* Whatever the rest of these say in
whatever voice they end up saying it in — the woman at the start of it had everything she ever
wanted, and knew it, and was glad, and was so, so frightened, and turned a little dead machine on
in a quiet room, all by herself, to try and stay.

*(beat)*

Off, now. *(A click. Silence.)*

---

***[She was right that I wouldn't remember her saying it. I've gone and found it since — the
sentence, on Daniel's tape, near the end, exactly where she says. "I must tell you that he gave
himself up happily." Her voice is completely steady on it.***

***I've listened to this first one more times than the rest put together, because of how she is
on it. You can hear everything she's about to lose. It's all still in there — the dryness, the
way she circles a word and pounces on it, the little jokes she doesn't finish. The muscle, she
called it. It's all there, in Tape One. She's all there.***

***I'm telling you that now, while it's true.]***
