# Tape 2: Five Days Old

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine — the pauses, the room.
**[Bracketed italics]** are the one who assembled the tapes. The famble is the clock: listen to
the groping. See the README.*

---

*(A click. New room tone — softer, closer, a window cracked somewhere, a thread of street under
it. And, very faint, the breathing of a sleeping newborn, near the machine. Wren's voice is
lower than last time, tired, but lit up underneath, the tiredness of someone who can't believe
their luck.)*

She's here. *(beat)* Obviously. You can probably — if this thing's any good you can probably hear
her. That little — that. *(beat)* Five days. Five days old today. *(very quiet)* God.

*(beat)*

Her name's Nell. *(beat)* I want to do this properly, for the record, because names matter in
this and you of all people know it — half my family never had one, never needed one, you don't
name a hand. *(beat)* So. On the record: her name is Nell. Tom wanted something with more to it,
something you could grow into, a name for a barrister or a — and I said no. I said small. I want
her name small and plain and *outside-sounding,* a name like a real person has, a name like —
*(beat)* — well. Like mine. *(a small dry sound)* The plainest name in the house, that was always
me. So she's Nell. She's a Nell. You look at her and there's no other thing she could be called.

*(beat)*

The birth was — I won't. You don't need the birth. It was long and it was ordinary and it was
the most violent ordinary thing I've ever done and at the end of it they put this hot furious
red person on my chest and I — *(she stops; reaches)* — there isn't a — *(beat)* — people use big
words for it and the big words are all wrong, the big words are all about being filled up, and it
wasn't that. *(beat)* It was the opposite. It was like a window I didn't know was painted shut
coming open. *(beat)* Air I hadn't had. *(beat)* That's the closest I can — air I hadn't had.
*(beat)* All right. Moving on. The muscle's working; I groped and I got it; air I hadn't had.
Good.

*(The tape runs. The baby stirs, settles.)*

Tom's gone soft as butter. *(warm)* I wish you could see him. This big careful man tiptoeing
round his own house like the floor's made of her. He does the nights — he insisted, he said *you
grew her, I'll walk her,* and he walks her up and down the landing at three in the morning singing
something tuneless and I lie there and listen to the two of them and I think — *(beat)* — I think,
*so this is the thing. This is the thing everyone has and I thought I'd missed.* *(beat)* I didn't
miss it. *(beat)* It came late and it came strange and it came to the wrong sort of woman and it
came anyway. *(very quiet)* Don't let me make him sound like furniture in this. Tom. He's the
whole — he's the reason any of it. I'll do a proper tape about Tom. He deserves a proper tape.

*(beat)*

Now. *(beat)* The other thing. *(beat)* The reason I'm not just lying in there being happy with
them.

*(a long breath)*

I'm going to tell you some things that happened this week, and I'm going to tell them to you flat,
the way I'd tell them to a doctor, and then I'm going to tell you the two ways of reading them,
and I want you to notice that I genuinely cannot tell which reading is true. *(beat)* That's not
me being modest. That's the actual horror of it, that I've spent thirty years learning to see
clearly and I am looking at this as clearly as I have ever looked at anything and *I cannot tell.*

*(beat)*

One. *(beat)* I know when she's going to cry before she cries. *(beat)* Not — every parent says
that, I know, *you just learn their little signs.* It's not that. There are no signs. She's
asleep, flat asleep, not a flicker, and I'll be in the kitchen two rooms off and I'll put the cup
down and go in, and I'll get to the cot, and I'll get my hands under her — and *then* she wakes,
and opens her mouth to cry, and there I already am. *(beat)* Before the sign. *(beat)* Ahead of
it.

*(beat)*

Two. *(beat)* I know which cry it is. Before. Hungry, wet, hot, frightened — I know which, walking
toward her, in the dark, before she's made the sound that tells you. *(beat)* I just — it's just
there. In me. Like knowing your own arm's asleep.

*(beat)*

Three — and this is the one I keep — *(beat)* — Tuesday night she was screaming, the real
screaming, the kind that empties you out, and nothing — fed, dry, walked, nothing — and I took
her off Tom and I held her and I went still. *(beat)* You know the still I mean. The still I used
to go in cafés, for the men. The going-quiet-inside. I went that still, with her, without deciding
to, and she — *(beat)* — she stopped. *(beat)* Mid-scream. Like a tap. *(beat)* And Tom looked at
me like I was a miracle, and I smiled at him, and inside I had gone absolutely cold, because I
knew that still. I know exactly what that still is and what it's for and what it does to whoever's
on the other end of it. *(very quiet)* I did it to her. *(beat)* And she went quiet the way they
all went quiet. The way a man goes quiet when you've gone still for him. *(beat)* Soothed. *(beat)*
Taken.

*(The tape runs.)*

Now. The two readings. *(beat)* Reading one: I am a brand-new mother, wrung out, frightened, sleep-
smashed, who has just had a baby and read four books about babies and is doing the thing every
new mother on earth does, which is *over-read.* I'm finding lions in the curtains. The knowing-
before-she-cries is ordinary attunement that all the books say is real and good and what's meant
to happen. The going-still is — I learned to soothe people young, of course I'm good at soothing a
baby, that's not sinister, that's the one wholesome use the whole rotten skill ever had. *(beat)*
Reading one says: you are a tired woman pathologising your own competence because you grew up in a
house of monsters and you can't believe a good thing isn't a trap. *(beat)* Reading one is kind to
me. Reading one is probably true.

*(beat)*

And reading one has the best evidence in the world behind it. *(beat)* You. *(beat)* Because if
this were the door opening — if Nell reaching for me were turning the key — then it'd have happened
to *you* and your mother, wouldn't it? You reached for Liss. Every baby reaches. And nothing woke
in her, because — *(beat)* — because there was nothing reaching back, no, that's not — *(she catches
herself, reorders it)* — because *she* was already out, already separate, the door already — no.
*(beat)* Wait. *(beat)* That's not the same case. *(beat)* You reaching for your mother is not the
same as a baby reaching for a castaway. Your mother was already a self. I'm — *(beat)* — I'm the
castaway. I'm the shut door. *(beat)*

*(a long breath)*

Funny. *(beat)* I had that the wrong way round for a second. I had myself reassured for a whole
second there. *(beat)* The comfort was: *it didn't wake Liss, so it won't wake me.* And it took me
saying it out loud to a machine to hear that those aren't the same thing at all. Liss was never a
castaway with a shut door. Liss was a self the chord couldn't hold. Different. *(beat)* The case
that's actually like mine isn't you and your mother. *(beat)* The case that's like mine is the
founding. The first one. The woman at the very start of all of us, a castaway with a shut door,
ordinary, living her life, who had a baby — *(beat)* — and the baby reached — *(beat)* — and woke
her. *(beat)* That's my case. *(beat)* I've only got one comparison in the whole world and it's the
worst one.

*(beat)*

See, this is what I was afraid of. Not the knowing-before-she-cries. *(beat)* This. *(beat)* My own
head, handing me comfort, and the comfort being wrong, and it being *me* — clear-eyed me, careful
me, the one who sees — that has to catch it. *(beat)* I nearly walked off with *it didn't wake
Liss.* I'd have slept on it. I'd have slept *well* on it. *(very quiet)* How many more of those are
coming. The good wrong arguments. And will I always catch them. And what happens on the night I
don't.

*(The tape runs a while. The baby breathes.)*

I want to tell you the part I'm ashamed of. *(beat)* Because if I only tell you the frightened part
I'll have lied to you the way everything in my family lied, by leaving out the wanting. *(beat)*
When she goes still against me — when I do the thing, the going-quiet, and she stops crying and
softens into me and her whole self just — *(beat)* — eases, toward me, into me, like she's coming
home to the only place — *(beat)* — it is the single best feeling I have ever had in my life.
*(beat)* And I have wanted to feel that my whole life. *(beat)* Not the soothing-her. The — being
the place something comes home to. Not being alone in here. *(beat)* I have stood outside that my
entire life with my ear against the door. *(beat)* And now it's in my arms and it weighs eight
pounds and it smells of bread and it's *mine* and when it presses into me the loneliness I have
carried since before I could talk just — stops. *(beat)* For a minute. It stops.

*(beat)*

So when I tell you I'm frightened of the thing that's coming — *(beat)* — I need you to hear that
in the same breath I'm telling you it's the thing I have wanted since I was a child. *(beat)* It
doesn't come at me like an enemy. *(very quiet)* It comes at me like the answer. *(beat)* That's
what I can't make anyone understand. There's no monster at the window. There's just my own deepest
wish, walking up the path, with its arms out, saying *you don't have to be alone anymore.* *(beat)*
And how do you fight that. *(beat)* How do you even want to.

*(beat)*

We had a good day today, the two of — *(she stops, dead)*

*(beat)*

*We.* *(beat)* Did you hear it. *(beat)* "We had a good day." *(beat)* Meaning her and me. *(beat)*
That's — every mother says *we.* "We slept through, we had our jab, we're teething." It's nothing.
It's the most ordinary word in the — *(beat)* — it's what you say. Her and me, we, a mother and a
baby, two people, *we.* *(beat)*

*(a long breath)*

I'm going to leave that in. *(beat)* I nearly went back over it and I'm going to leave it in,
because this is a record, and on the record, on the fifth day, with the muscle still working and my
sight still clear and my good wrong arguments still getting caught — *(beat)* — I said *we,* and I
meant nothing by it, and a small cold part of me, the part that's read between every line its whole
life, sat up. *(beat)* For no reason. *(beat)* Over the most ordinary word there is.

*(beat)*

That's all for tonight. She's stirring and I'd rather get to her before — *(beat)* — well. Before
she has to ask. *(beat)* While it's still me choosing to.

*(A click. Silence. Then, faintly, before the tape truly ends — the smallest sound of the baby
beginning to wake, and the chair, already moving, ahead of it.)*

---

***[I have listened to the "we" on this one a hundred times. You can't hear anything in it. That's
the thing I need you to understand before the later tapes — on the day it started, it sounded
exactly like nothing. It sounded like a tired woman loving her baby. It was a tired woman loving
her baby. That's not the opposite of what was happening to her. That's what it sounded like the
whole way down.***

***She caught it, on day five. She sat up over one ordinary word. I keep wanting to climb into the
tape and tell her she was right, trust it, run. ***

***She did trust it. It didn't matter that she trusted it. That's the part I'm studying. That's the
part I need.]***
