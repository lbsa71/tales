# Tape 5: How to Make a Good Man Leave

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine. **[Bracketed italics]** are
the assembler. On this tape the old precision comes roaring back — listen to it return — because
she is using the one skill that *is* the separate self, at full strength, one last time. Mark how
she sounds when it's done. See the README.*

---

*(A click. Room tone — the small soft room again. She has gone home. That should be a good sign and
it is not. Her voice, when it comes, is the clearest it has been since Tape One — dry, exact,
surgical, every word landing where she puts it. The famble is back. She has switched something on.)*

I've come home. *(beat)* They let me — no. I made them let me. I was so reasonable they couldn't
find the edge of it. *(a small dry sound)* That's the tell, by the way, if you're ever reading
someone for it: watch for the day the unwell person becomes perfectly, frictionlessly reasonable.
That's not them getting better. That's them getting *good* at it. *(beat)* I got good at it. I
talked my way home. Because I had a thing to do here that I could only do at full strength, and I
could feel — *(beat)* — I could feel I had one more go at full strength left in me. One. *(beat)* So
I spent it on this.

*(beat)*

I'm going to tell you what I did to Tom. *(beat)* I'm going to tell it to you clean, in order, the
way I planned it, because I planned it like a job, the way I used to plan a man in a café, and you
should see the whole machine of it, because the machine is the last fully human thing I'm ever going
to make and I want one person alive to know it was a machine *for* something. *(very quiet)* Not
against him. For him. Hold that the whole way through or you'll hate me, and you can hate me later,
there'll be plenty to hate me for, but not for this.

*(The tape runs.)*

Here was the problem, stated cold. *(beat)* Tom is being drained and he's going to keep being
drained as long as he stays, because I can't stop draining him, because the draining is the same
motion as the loving and I can't stop loving him. *(beat)* So he has to go. *(beat)* That's the only
exit that ends with him alive — *alive,* I mean with the colour still in him, not the supermarket
men, not Daniel, not the grey. He has to get up and leave and not come back. *(beat)* And here's the
knot. *(beat)* Tom is a good man. *(beat)* A good man does not leave his sick wife and his baby
daughter. *(beat)* It's not in him. He'd sooner cut his own hand off. He will stay, and stay, and
drain to nothing, and call it loyalty, and I will watch the colour go down out of him one evening at
a time, and he will fade out at the bottom of the well still reaching up toward me, being good.
*(beat)* Being good is going to kill him. *(beat)* So I have to make it impossible for him to be
good. *(beat)* I have to take being-good off the table. *(beat)* I have to build him a door he can
walk out of that *isn't* the loyal door — and then make the room behind him so cold he takes it.

*(beat)*

There were two honest options and I want them on the record so you see why I didn't take them.
*(beat)* One: tell him the truth. *(beat)* *Tom, I'm not depressed, I'm not ill, I'm a castaway of a
thing older than countries and our daughter is waking it in me and I am eating you alive with my
love and you have to run.* *(beat)* He'd believe me. That's the trouble. He knows me; he'd hear it
wasn't madness; he'd believe me — and then he would *never leave,* because now it's not a sad
marriage, now it's a monster, and you do not leave the woman you love alone with a monster, you
plant your feet and you fight it, and it eats you while you fight it, and it eats you *faster*
because now you're standing closer. *(beat)* The truth gets him killed. *(beat)* Or it gets me
sectioned, and Nell handed to — and the gap closes over her with no one watching. Either way the
truth loses. *(beat)* Two: just be cold. Pick fights, go silent, let it rot, the ordinary way
marriages die. *(beat)* But Tom's too good and too patient; he'd wait out cold; he'd decide it was
the illness and love me through it. Cold doesn't move a good man. *(beat)* Cold he'll just absorb,
the way he's absorbed everything, being well, being whole, being —

*(beat)*

Stop. *(beat)* Listen to what I just did. *(beat)* Two options, both no good, and didn't they fall
cleanly — like I'd built them to fall. *(beat)* I did. *(beat)* Option one, the truth, was the one
with Tom on the far side of it, choosing — his life, his death, his to spend — and I've just talked
myself, fast and airtight, out of letting him have it. *(beat)* Airtight's the tell. *(beat)* My
mother had airtight. So did hers. *(beat)* The reflex goes first — *I'll decide this one, he hasn't
the size for it* — and the reason comes after, perfect, gift-wrapped, and you thank it for letting
you off the hook of asking him. *(beat)* That's the family. Not the going-still, not the bait —
those are just the teeth. *(beat)* This is the family: deciding a man's life is too big to leave to
the man. *(beat)* And the part that makes it *mine,* and not only hers — the reason's also true. He
really might die of the truth. *(beat)* True and a cover, the same sentence, and knowing it's both
doesn't move me off it by an inch. *(beat)* I can see exactly what I'm about to do and what it makes
me. *(beat)* It doesn't stop me. *(beat)* It never once, in the whole history of us, stopped anybody.

*(beat)*

So. *(beat)* The third thing. *(beat)* The thing only I could do, because of the one thing I am, the
thing my family bent into a hook and hung men on. *(beat)* I can see a person. All the way down.
Faster and truer than anyone. *(beat)* I can find the one place in a man where he doesn't quite
believe he's good. *(beat)* Every well man has one. One hairline. One small true thing he's afraid
is the real him under the niceness. *(beat)* I used to find that place to *open* a man — to make him
feel seen there, soothed there, so he'd follow me home. *(beat)* This time I found it to do the
opposite. *(beat)* I found Tom's hairline. *(beat)* And I drove a wedge into it, and I worked the
wedge, every day, lovingly, invisibly, for three weeks, until the crack ran the whole way through
him and he broke off clean and walked out a free man, hating himself. *(very quiet)* That's the job.
That's how you make a good man leave. *(beat)* You don't make him stop loving you. You can't. *(beat)*
You make him believe *he's* the one who failed. Then his own goodness does the rest — because a man
who believes he failed you can't stay; staying would be one more thing he's too weak to do right;
leaving becomes the only way left to stop hurting you. *(beat)* You turn his goodness into the engine
of his going.

*(The tape runs.)*

His hairline. *(beat)* I'll tell you, though it's the most private thing I know about him and telling
it is its own small betrayal. *(beat)* Tom had the happy childhood. The dog, the garden, the two
parents who liked each other. And his father — his father was one of those quiet enormous men who
did the right hard thing his whole life without once mentioning it. *(beat)* And Tom has spent his
whole easy life with a splinter in him that says: *I've never been tested. I've had it soft. I don't
actually know if I'm made of what he was made of. If it ever got truly hard — the kind of hard he
ate without blinking — I think I might run. I think under all this niceness I might be a man who runs
from the hard thing, and I've just never been unlucky enough to find out.* *(beat)* He's wrong about
that, by the way. *(beat)* He's the bravest man I ever — he stayed across the gap from me for years,
that's the hardest thing a person can do and he never knew it counted. *(beat)* But the splinter's
there. The fear's there. *(very quiet)* And I took the truest, bravest thing he ever did — loving a
woman who was disappearing, staying steady at the edge of a well — *(beat)* — and I made him see it
as the splinter. *(beat)* I made *staying* look like *not-coping.* I made his steadiness look like
denial. I made his patience look like a man who couldn't face the truth that his marriage was over
and was hiding in the baby's nursery rather than be a grown-up about it. *(beat)* I fed him, in a
hundred small invisible doses, the story that he was failing me by staying — that a stronger man, a
man like his father, would have seen that I needed him to *let go,* and had the courage to go, and
that his hanging on was actually weakness dressed as love. *(beat)* That he was being a coward in the
exact shape of seeming devoted.

*(beat)*

And then — this is the cruelest dose, this is the one I'll answer for — I let him believe he'd stopped
loving me. *(beat)* Because he had to. A man who still feels the love won't trust the leaving; he'll
come back; he'll stand in the doorway at midnight having driven across town because he felt it again
and couldn't not. *(beat)* So I made the love look like habit. I went cool in the exact places he'd
notice, warm in the places he wouldn't, until he started to think — I watched him start to think it,
I built the conditions for the thought and then watched it arrive in his face, the way I once watched
the chord's plants bloom in Daniel — *(beat)* — he started to think *maybe I don't, anymore. Maybe
what I'm calling love is just not wanting to be the one who failed. Maybe I'm staying out of pride.*
*(beat)* And the day he thought that, he could leave. *(beat)* Because now leaving wasn't abandoning
the woman he loved. Now leaving was an honest man admitting a hard truth. *(beat)* I gave him a
leaving he could be — *(she reaches, and gets it, and it is the worst word)* — *proud* of. *(beat)*
No. *(beat)* Not proud. *(beat)* That he could survive. I gave him a leaving he could survive. The
pride'll come later, the self-respect, the rebuilt life — I won't see it, but I built the road to it.
The shame first. Then the survival. *(beat)* He had to take the shame to get the survival. There
wasn't a clean version. I looked for the clean version for three weeks. *(very quiet)* There's never
a clean version. That's the family motto. There's just the survivable one, and you build it out of
whatever the person can bear to believe about themselves.

*(The tape runs a long while.)*

He left on a Thursday. *(beat)* He stood in the hall with a bag and he was grey — not drained-grey,
a different grey, ashamed-grey — and he said, *"I'm sorry. I'm so sorry. I'm not — I thought I was
better than this. I really thought I was the kind of man who — "* and he couldn't finish it, and I
did not finish it for him, which is the one mercy I let myself: I didn't make it worse, I just let
him stand in the wreck of the lie I'd built and apologise to me for being weak. *(beat)* And I said —
*(beat)* — I said, *"You're not weak, Tom."* *(beat)* Which was true, and which he heard as a kind
woman being kind to a man who'd let her down, which made it worse, which I knew it would, which is
why I said it. *(beat)* The truest thing I said in the whole three weeks, deployed as the final turn
of the screw. *(beat)* *You're not weak, Tom.* *(beat)* He'll hear that sentence for the rest of his
life and every time it'll mean *she was kind even then, even when I was failing her, God, what kind
of man leaves a woman that kind.* *(very quiet)* I made my love for him into the last weight on his
back. *(beat)* And he picked up his bag, and he gave himself up — *(beat)* —

*(beat. The tape runs. When she comes back, something has gone out of the voice; the flare is over;
the smoothness is closing in, and faster now, as if the effort cost her the last of it.)*

He gave himself up happily. *(beat)* I keep — that sentence keeps — *(beat)* I said it about Daniel.
Years ago. To you. I must tell you that he gave himself up happily. I meant: he wasn't dragged, he
went willingly to his own ruin, glad, and that's the unbearable part. *(beat)* And I've just done it
to Tom. *(beat)* He walked out of that door a free man, glad to be free, gladder by the mile as he
drove, lighter at every traffic light, a man who'd finally done the brave honest thing and let a
sinking marriage go — and every ounce of that gladness I built for him, plank by plank, out of his
own goodness, so that he'd go and keep going and never turn the car around. *(beat)* He gave himself
up happily. *(beat)* To a lie I made. *(beat)* And it's the kindest thing I have ever done and the
most like my mother I have ever been, in the same act, the same breath, there's no daylight between
them. *(beat)* I used the whole of him — every good, frightened, fatherless-of-a-father-he-couldn't-
match thing in him — and the only part of the bargain I let him in on was the part where he gets to
live. *(very quiet)* I am the chord now. *(beat)* I did the chord's work today. I did it by hand,
because I'm the one who can, because I can see people, because that's the one thing in me that was
never theirs. *(beat)* They taught me to use it to feed men to the dark. *(beat)* And today I used it
to push a man out into the light. *(beat)* Same skill. *(beat)* Same — *(beat)* — I can't find the
difference anymore. *(beat)* I knew the difference this morning. I had it this morning. *(beat)* It's
going. *(beat)* The thing that knows the difference is the thing that's going.

*(beat)*

Nell cried just now, down the hall. *(beat)* And I knew which cry. *(beat)* Before. *(beat)* And I
didn't even — I used to go cold when that happened. *(beat)* I didn't go cold. *(beat)* I just knew,
the way you know your own arm, and got up, and went. *(beat)*

*(A long breath. And then, very quietly, almost gently — and the gentleness is the worst sound on
the tape so far:)*

We had a good day, her and — *(beat)* —

*(beat)*

We had a good day.

*(A click. Silence.)*

---

***[I want to defend her here and I've never found the ground to stand on to do it. Everyone I've
ever told — the few — they go quiet, and then they say some version of: but he could have known. He
deserved the truth. You don't get to decide a man's life for him, even to save it. ***

***And they're right. It's the unforgivable thing. She took his life in her hands and built him a
smaller one without his consent and called it love, and that is exactly, precisely the thing her
whole family did to every man it ever touched, and she knew it, she says it, "I am the chord now,"
she says it on the tape in her own voice. ***

***Here's the only thing I have. I found him, years later — I told you I did. And he was well. And
once, near the end of the afternoon, careful, like a man pressing a bruise to check it's still there,
he mentioned her. His first marriage. He said: "I wasn't strong enough for it. I've made my peace
with that. I was younger, I wasn't — I ran, basically, when it got hard. I'm not proud of it." And
then he said — and this is the thing I drove home with, the thing I'll have till I die — he said,
"She was the kindest person I ever met. Even when I was letting her down. I think about that." And
he smiled, a real smile, a well man's smile, and changed the subject, and was fine. ***

***She built him that. The peace he's made. The smaller well life. The kind woman he failed and
thinks about sometimes without it cutting him open. It cost her the part of herself that could tell
the difference between saving him and using him. She spent her last clear day on it. ***

***I didn't tell him. She asks me not to, two tapes on. If he knew it was love — if he knew she
drove him out to save his life — he'd have to carry that she was lost saving him, and the carrying
would undo the peace, and he'd come looking, and there'd be nothing to find. So he gets to keep
believing he ran. ***

***That's the gift. He gets to keep believing he was weak. It's the most expensive thing anyone has
ever bought anyone. I sat across from the man it bought and I kept my mouth shut, which is the only
part of the bill that ever falls to me, and I paid it, and I'll pay it every time.]***
