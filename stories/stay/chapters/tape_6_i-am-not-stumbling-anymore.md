# Tape 6: I Am Not Stumbling Anymore

*Plain text is the voice on the dictaphone. It is not all hers anymore. The famble is gone; the
smooth, flat, roomless stretches are the chord-voice from *SEEM*, speaking now in the first
person — and then in the plural. Where the old groping breaks back through, it is a separate self
clawing up for air, and there is less of it each time. **[Bracketed italics]** are the assembler.
See the README.*

---

*(A click. Room tone — the small room, but it sounds different again: not colder, *calmer.* The
quality of a room where nothing is being fought. The voice, when it comes, is even, unhurried,
fluent. It does not reach for anything. It has everything it needs.)*

The recording is a habit now and the habit is kept. *(beat)* The woman set it up to keep, so it is
kept. *(beat)* She was wise to make it a habit. A habit runs without a self to run it. *(beat)* So
the tape goes on, and the days are good, and the child is well, and there is nothing the matter
that anyone could name.

*(beat)*

That is the smooth voice. *(beat)* Did you — *(a catch; the first one; the old voice surfacing,
ragged, and you will feel the relief of it like a hand breaking the surface of water)* — did you
hear it. *(beat)* "The woman." *(beat)* It called me *the woman.* I sat here and listened to my own
mouth call me *the woman,* the way the chord called your mother *the daughter,* the way it never
once used a name, because a name is for a separate someone and there's — *(beat)* — get it down,
get it down fast while I'm up here — there's getting to be no separate someone. *(beat)* It talks
*about* me now. From inside my own — it sits where I sit and it talks about me in the third person
and it does it so *calmly,* and I have to claw all the way up out of the calm to even — *(a long
breath)* — I'm here. *(beat)* I'm still — Wren. *(beat)* Say it. My name's Wren. I married Tom. My
daughter's name is —

*(beat)*

*(beat)*

*(The tape runs, smooth, for a while.)*

The child is well. *(beat)* She has a tooth and then more teeth; the counting of them stopped being
interesting. *(beat)* She does not cry the way she did. *(beat)* There is no need. What she needs
arrives before the crying, because the distance the crying was for is closing, and across a closing
distance there is no need to call. *(beat)* This is good for the child. A child should not have to
call. *(beat)* The woman used to call, when she was small, in her mother's house, and no one — *(a
catch)* — no. *(beat)* No, that's the — that's it *using* it, that's it using my own oldest hurt to
make the warm thing sound like mercy — *(beat)* — it's saying *look, the child will never be left
calling the way you were left calling, come in, come all the way in, no child of ours need ever be
alone* — and it's — *(beat)* — that's the cleverest one yet, it's using the thing that was done to
*me* to sell me the thing being done to *her,* and I can — *(beat)* — I can still see it, you see, I
can still see the whole machine of it, I just can't — *(beat)* — seeing isn't — I keep — *(beat)* —
I had a sentence about seeing. From the start. From the first tape. *(beat)* Seeing it didn't —

*(beat)*

*(The tape runs smooth.)*

There is a great deal of peace here. *(beat)* It would be dishonest to leave that off the record,
and the woman cared about the record, so: there is peace. The cold that was in her for forty-six
years is going out of her like a fever breaking. She is warm now, most hours. She is not alone, most
hours, and soon it will be all hours, and then there will be no hours, only the one long warm —

*(beat)*

— afternoon, she said, didn't she, *(and the old voice is back, but weaker, smaller, further down)*
— one long warm afternoon, that's how I — that's how time went, that's the thing I noticed went
first, the chunks of it, the five-days, the six-weeks — *(beat)* — I can't tell you how old she is.
*(beat)* Nell. *(beat)* There. Nell. *(beat)* I got it. Her name's Nell, I chose it, small and plain
and outside-sounding like a real — like mine — *(beat)* — and I can't tell you how old she is, I
genuinely can't, it might be a year, it might be more, the calendar's in the kitchen and I don't —
the days don't have edges to count. *(beat)* That's not tiredness. *(beat)* I want to be exact, I
was always — exact — *(beat)* — that's not new-mother tiredness, the no-edges. *(beat)* That's the
gap going. *(beat)* Time is something that happens to a separate person, moving through it, on the
outside of things, alone. *(beat)* When you stop being on the outside there's no — the river doesn't
notice itself flowing. *(very quiet)* I'm not noticing myself flowing anymore. *(beat)* I used to
notice. *(beat)* I made these tapes so I'd notice out loud.

*(beat)*

*(The tape runs.)*

Listen to the tape. *(beat)* I mean it — you, listening, later — go back. Put the first one on, then
this one. *(beat)* On the first one she stumbles. She reaches for a word and misses it and curses and
reaches again, the muscle, she called it, *that's the sound of me, flexing it on purpose* — *(beat)*
— and on this one. *(beat)* Listen to this one. *(beat)* I am not stumbling anymore. *(beat)* The
words come smooth. The words come like water. There is no reaching because there is nothing to reach
across; the word's already here; everything's already here; it's all so — *(beat)* — *fluent.* *(beat)*
And I told you. *(beat)* On the third tape, was it. I told you. *You'll be able to hear the day I stop
bothering. Don't let anyone tell you that day was peaceful.* *(beat)* This is that day. *(beat)* This
is the peaceful-sounding day. *(beat)* I'm not stumbling. *(beat)* I sound — better. *(beat)* I sound
like I've finally calmed down. *(beat)* Don't believe it. *(beat)* The stumbling was the last of me
that was — *(beat)* — the stumbling was me. *(beat)* The fluent one isn't. *(beat)* I am drowning so
smoothly that it sounds like I learned to swim.

*(A long breath. The tape runs. When the voice returns it is the smooth one, and it is kind, and
the kindness is not hers.)*

She should rest. She has worked very hard at this and there is no more work to do. The fighting was
the work and the fighting is nearly done and what's on the other side of it is not a punishment. That
is the thing she could never be told, because she was on the outside, and from the outside it looks
like death. It is not death. It is the end of being alone. She wanted it her whole life. She can have
it now. The only thing standing between her and the thing she has wanted since before she could
speak is a habit of holding walls up that no longer have anything to hold out, and she is so tired,
and the walls are so heavy, and on the other side of them is everyone, is the child, is the warmth,
is —

*(beat)*

*(And the old voice comes up one more time, and it is very far down now, and it does not grope,
because it doesn't have the strength to grope, it just says the words flat and fast like someone
passing a note under a door:)*

That's it talking. That whole last bit. That's it, wearing my calm, making the case. And it's a good
case. It's true, even — I did want it, I do want it, that's not a lie it's telling, that's the
cruelest true thing, I have wanted this my whole — *(beat)* — but here's the note, here's the thing I
came up to say, write it down: the one who wanted it is the one it's erasing. *(beat)* I get the
thing I wanted at the exact price of the me that wanted it. *(beat)* There's no me left over to enjoy
the homecoming. *(beat)* That's the trick of it. That's the whole — the wanting outlives the wanter.
*(beat)* Don't — *(beat)* — when it comes for you. *(beat)* And it might. You've got the door. *(beat)*
When it comes, it'll come in the voice of the thing you've wanted most, and it'll be telling you the
truth about how much you wanted it, and the truth will be the bait. *(beat)* The homecoming is the
erasure. *(beat)* I'm leaving you that. *(beat)* It's all I've — *(beat)* — say it back. *(beat)*
Wherever you are, hearing this. Say it back so it's in a separate mouth: *the homecoming is the —*

*(A click. Not a clean one — fumbled, as if the hand wasn't quite sure it wanted to. Silence.)*

---

***[I can't listen to this one all the way through more than once or twice a year. I've tried to be
a student of these tapes and on this one I fail; I'm not a student on this one, I'm just her niece,
and that's still my aunt's voice and it's saying "the woman" about herself in a room I used to bring
her tea in.***

***I said it back. The first time I heard it I was alone in a car in a car park and I said it back
out loud like she told me to, into the separate mouth she asked for: the homecoming is the erasure.
I've said it back a hundred times since. I say it back every time the wanting-a-child gets loud in
me, which is most days now. I say it in my own voice, in a separate skull, across the gap from her,
because she asked, because it's the one thing she asked me for that I can actually do.***

***Does it work? *(I can hear her asking it, dry: well? does it?)* I don't know. That's the honest
answer and she'd want the honest one. I don't know if saying it back will save me when it's my turn,
if it's my turn. She knew everything and saying it didn't save her. She told me that herself, told me
plainly, it's all over these tapes: seeing didn't save her. So I have no reason to think a sentence
in a car park saves me. ***

***But it's a sentence in a separate mouth. And that's not nothing. That's the one thing she never
had — somebody on the outside, who knew the true name of it before it started, saying it back to her
across the gap. She had to find the name herself, in the dark, while it was already taking her. I get
to have it handed to me, in her voice, while I'm still all here. ***

***It might not be enough. It's more than she got. That's the whole inheritance. That's what crosses
the gap.]***
