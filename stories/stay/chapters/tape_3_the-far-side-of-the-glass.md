# Tape 3: The Far Side of the Glass

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine. **[Bracketed italics]** are
the assembler. The famble is the clock — by now, listen for where the groping thins, and where she
makes herself do it on purpose. See the README.*

---

*(A click. Room tone — the same small room, but something has changed in it; it sounds emptier,
or stiller, harder to say. No baby breathing near the machine this time. Wren's voice is calm.
Calmer than it should be. The tiredness is gone out of it and that is not a good sign, though you
won't know that yet.)*

I've lost track of how old she is. *(beat)* That's not — I could work it out. I could count it on
the calendar in the kitchen. *(beat)* I mean I've lost the *feel* of it. The five-days, the six-
weeks, the — time used to come at me in those little chunks, each one a thing to get through, and
now it's just. *(beat)* One long warm afternoon. *(beat)* She's a good size. She laughs. She's got
a tooth. Put that down as the date. *(a small dry sound)* A tooth.

*(beat)*

I'm going to do this one carefully because I can feel that careful is getting harder, and the harder
a thing gets the more it needs doing. *(beat)* So. Carefully.

*(The tape runs.)*

Tom.

*(beat)*

I said I'd do a proper tape about Tom and I keep — *(beat)* — I keep meaning to and not, and I've
worked out why, and the why is the tape. *(beat)* So here it is. The proper one. *(a long breath)*

When I met him I thought, *this is the wrong kind of man, he's far too — he's well.* He's a well
man. That's the thing about Tom. No hole in him. He had a happy childhood, can you imagine, an
actual happy one, a dog and a garden and two parents who liked each other, and he came out of it
the way bread comes out of a good oven, just — *(beat)* — *whole.* Steady. Kind without it costing
him anything, kind like it's just the weather he's made of. *(beat)* And I thought, men like that
don't — they don't end up with women like me, women with the draught coming off them. They end up
with their own kind. With warm rooms that people actually live in. *(beat)* And he just. Didn't go
away. *(beat)* I kept stepping back, the way I step back, the way I was built to step back, and he
kept being there when I'd finished stepping. *(very quiet)* He's the only person who ever stayed
on the far side of the gap and didn't either climb in or wander off. He just stood there, across
it, looking at me. *(beat)* Loving me across it. *(beat)* For years. He let me be a separate person
and loved the separate person. *(beat)* Do you know what that is, for one of us? *(beat)* That's the
whole prize. That's the thing I learned, the night with your mother, dressed up as a man with a
dog and a garden, standing in my kitchen, not going away.

*(beat)*

I'm telling you what I'm losing. *(beat)* That's what this tape is. I'm telling you what he is so
you'll understand what it means that I'm losing him.

*(The tape runs a while.)*

Here's how it goes, and I'll tell it flat. *(beat)* I reach for him and there's less to reach with.
*(beat)* That's the whole of it, said plain. *(beat)* I'll be sitting with him in the evening, Nell
down, the two of us on the sofa the way we've sat a thousand nights, and he'll say something — a
real thing, a Tom thing, something kind or funny or just *his* — and I'll go to meet it. The way
you do. The way you reach across to a person you love and take the thing they've handed you and
hand something back. *(beat)* And I reach, and — *(she gropes, and this is real groping now, not
performance; she is genuinely trying to find the edge of a thing that is going)* — there's a — it's
like reaching into a pocket you know had your keys in it. *(beat)* The reaching's all there. The
hand's all there. The pocket's — *(beat)* — there's less in the pocket than there was. *(beat)*
Every night a little less. *(beat)* And I cover it. I'm so good at covering. I hand him back
something that *looks* like what I used to hand him, and he takes it, and most nights he doesn't —

*(beat)*

He's started to notice. *(beat)* That's the part I can't — *(a long breath)* — last week he said
the truest thing anyone's said to me since the night your mother said *I can't feel you.* He said
it in the kitchen, not even looking at me, drying a cup. *(beat)* He said: *"I feel like I'm
disappearing."* *(beat)* And then he laughed, to take the weight off it, the way he does, and he
said, *"Is that mad? I feel like — you're right here, you're lovely, you're — and I feel like
you've gone somewhere I can't follow you, and the harder I — it's like calling down a well. I can
hear you're down there. You sound fine. But I can't reach you, and I'm starting to think I'm the
problem, I'm starting to think I've gone needy or — "* *(beat)* And he stopped, and dried the cup,
and put it away. *(beat)* And I said something. I don't remember what. Something that closed it.
Something kind. *(very quiet)* I'm so good at closing things kindly.

*(beat)*

He's exactly right. *(beat)* That's the thing I have to get down. He is *exactly, precisely right,*
and he thinks he's being a fool. *(beat)* He thinks it's him. He thinks he's gone clingy, or that
the baby's come between us the way it does between couples, the ordinary way, the way the books warn
you. *(beat)* He's reading it as a marriage going through the thing marriages go through. *(beat)*
And it is *literally him disappearing.* *(beat)* He can feel himself draining and he's calling it
neediness. *(beat)* He's feeling the exact true thing and apologising for feeling it. *(beat)*
Where have I — *(she stops)* — I know where I've seen that. *(very quiet)* I've seen that my whole
life. In the mirror. *(beat)* A person being drained, reading the drain as their own defect. *(beat)*
He's doing what I did. He's me. *(beat)* Oh, he's me, and I'm — *(beat)*

*(a long breath. The tape runs.)*

I have to say the next part and I've been not-saying it for three tapes' worth of not-saying it.
*(beat)* It's me. *(beat)* I'm the one draining him. *(beat)* Not on purpose. God, not on purpose,
I'd cut my hands off — but that's what's happening. The thing that's waking in me is hungry, the
way they're all hungry, and there's a well man sitting on my sofa every night, full to the top with
the thing it needs, the separateness, the *self,* the lovely whole untouched self of him — *(beat)*
— and I am taking it. *(beat)* With my love. *(beat)* That's the part. I'm not taking it instead of
loving him. *(beat)* I'm taking it *by* loving him. The reaching for him, the going-still with him,
the — the same gesture that's love is the gesture that drains. *(beat)* He feels me loving him and
fading him out in the one motion and he can't tell them apart because they're not two things.
*(beat)* Daniel never knew he was loved by a void. *(beat)* Tom knows he's loved. *(very quiet)*
That's worse. I've made it worse. He's being eaten by something that genuinely loves him and he can
feel both halves and he thinks the wrongness is *him.*

*(The tape runs a long while.)*

All right. *(beat)* The other thing. The wanting. *(beat)* Because I promised myself I'd never do a
tape that's only the horror, that leaves out the wanting, because that's the lie my whole family
lived inside.

*(beat)*

It's getting — *(beat)* — I keep wanting to say *better.* The thing that's happening to me keeps
getting *better.* *(beat)* That's the word that wants to come and I have to fight it and I'm going
to leave the fight in. *(beat)* The loneliness — you have to understand what my loneliness was. It
wasn't sad. Sad's a weather, it passes. Mine was the *room.* The being-in-a-room-with-walls, every
hour, awake and asleep, the walls of myself, no one else in here, no one ever in here, that's what
a self *is,* I used to think, a self is the loneliest possible — *(beat)* — and it is, that's not
wrong, that's true, a self is a room you die in alone. *(beat)* And the thing that's coming is taking
the walls down. *(beat)* Slowly. Gently. One brick at a time, and every brick it takes out, the
room gets less — *(beat)* — less *alone.* *(beat)* I can feel, some evenings now, that there's
something on the other side of the wall. Not Nell. *(beat)* Through Nell. Past her. A — a warmth
that isn't a person, a — *(she gropes, and gets it, and hates that she got it)* — a company. *(beat)*
A company with no one in it. *(beat)* And it is the most relief I have ever felt and I have wanted
it since before I could speak and it is killing the man on my sofa and it is reaching for my
daughter and I lie in the warm of it at night and I think, *I could just stop.* *(beat)* I could
just stop fighting and let the walls come down and never be alone again. *(beat)* And no one would
even — it'd look like getting *better.* It'd look like a tired mother finally relaxing into her
life. *(very quiet)* That's the offer. That's it, every night, in the only voice I ever wanted to
hear. *Stop holding the walls up. Come in out of it. You've been so cold for so long.*

*(beat)*

So here's my rep. *(beat)* You'll have heard me say *we* on the last tape and sit up over it. Here's
what I do now, every day, the exercise, the keeping-the-muscle. *(beat)* I say *I.* On purpose. Out
loud. *(beat)* I make myself grope for words even when the smooth one's right there, because the
groping is me and the smooth is — *(beat)* — the smooth is the tide. *(beat)* I pick the harder
word. I correct myself when I don't need to. I sit here and I make this difficult on purpose,
because difficult is the sound of a separate person reaching across a gap, and easy is the sound of
no gap left. *(beat)* You'll be able to hear, on the tapes, the day I stop bothering. *(beat)* Don't
let anyone tell you that day was peaceful. It'll *sound* peaceful. *(very quiet)* It'll sound like I
finally calmed down.

*(beat)*

I have to go to him. *(beat)* Tom. He's in there pretending to read and feeling like a well you can
hear someone living at the bottom of. *(beat)* I'm going to go and sit with him and reach for him
with whatever's left in the pocket and hand him something that looks right. *(beat)* It's a lie. I
know it's a lie. *(beat)* It's the kindest lie I've got and I'm going to keep telling it as long as
there's anything left to tell it with, because the truth would either lock me up or make him stay,
and a man who stays — *(beat)* — a man who stays gets drained to nothing. *(beat)* I've seen the
nothing. I married it once removed; my mother made the nothing for thirty years and sent it out into
supermarkets. *(very quiet)* I will not make Tom into the nothing. *(beat)* Whatever I have to do.
*(beat)* Hold that. I'll need you to hold that, later, when you hear what I do to him. *(beat)* Hold
that I decided it here, while I was still mostly me, with my eyes open, out of love. *(beat)*

*(a long breath)*

Off.

*(A click. Silence.)*

---

***[I went and found Tom. After. Years after — I'm not going to tell you how, or where, because she
asks me not to, later, on a tape I'll get to. But I went.***

***He's well. I want you to know that, here, before it gets unbearable, because it's the one mercy
in the whole thing and she bought it for him at a price you won't believe until you hear it. He's
well. He has a life. There's grey in him — she'd have said the colour's turned down, and it is, a
little, you can see it — but he laughs, and he meant the laugh, and he has people, and he's *well.*
***

***He believes something about himself that isn't true. I sat across from him and watched him
believe it. And I didn't tell him. Because she asks me not to. Because if he knew, he'd —***

***I'm getting ahead. I keep getting ahead. It's hard not to, when you already know.]***
