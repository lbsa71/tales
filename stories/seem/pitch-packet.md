# PITCH PACKET — *(working title TBD)*
### Companion to the Story Bible · a prestige literary-horror audio drama

*Three documents. The first sells it, the second proves it, the third protects it. The
rule across all three: **the machinery stays invisible.** No metabolism, no triad, no
suppression-training, no witch in the public-facing language. The strangeness is meant
to arrive in the listening, never in the logline.*

# 1 · SELL SHEET  *(the one-pager)*

### LOGLINE
A rejected daughter of a family that shares one mind is sent to lure the men they
need — until she falls for the one man she was supposed to deliver, and her first act
of mercy destroys the only people who were ever hers.

### THE PREMISE *(lore-invisible)*
A young man meets a magnetic young woman and her mother. He believes it's the best
night of his life. He is right about the feeling and wrong about everything else —
the warmth he's falling into is being made for him, by people who cannot feel it back.
Our narrator is the daughter the family keeps but never quite counts: the one sent out
to bring men home. She has done it many times, expertly, without ever letting herself
understand what she does. This time, she lingers too long, sees the man too clearly,
and does the smallest, kindest, most human thing — and it brings the whole house down.

### THE HOOK — *why it has to be sound*
The drama is built from **three kinds of listening at once:** what is said aloud
(warm, ordinary), what is thought beneath it (cold, appraising, not one voice but
several breathing as one), and a third voice — the man's — where we hear feelings
*installed* a moment before he claims them as his own. When a single **"I"** finally
tears loose from that many-voiced chord, *you hear a self being born.* It could not be
a film. It would have nothing to show you. **That is the point.**

### FORMAT
A season of **6–8 half-hour episodes.** Contained cast. Sound-led horror — dread
built by pattern and voice, not jump-scares. Retrospective narrator (the daughter,
reconstructing it all afterward). Strong "episode-end dread" structure.

### THE LANE *(positioning, not equivalence)*
Prestige scripted-fiction audio / premium literary horror. For listeners of elevated
horror audio and BBC-style radio drama; the readers' shelf is *Under the Skin*, *The
Vegetarian*, *Our Wives Under the Sea*, Shirley Jackson; the form's ancestors are
Beckett's radio plays and the European *Hörspiel* tradition.

### WHAT THE LISTENER CARRIES OUT *(the usable truth)*
That the thing she was taught was her brokenness — the part of her that needs people
too much, that never quite belongs — was never the flaw. **It was the capacity to
love.** The ones who never feel alone may be the ones who can only ever love
themselves. A horror story that ends by handing you something to keep.

### WHAT IT IS *(one line, for the room)*
Not a gender-war story and not a monster story. **A story about the difference between
merging with people and actually loving them** — turned up to eleven, and made into horror.

### STATUS / ASK
Bible complete; proof-of-concept scene below (§2). Seeking: [production partner /
commissioning editor / development]. Ideal next step: fund a fully-produced **pilot
scene** to demonstrate the three-stream sound design.

# 2 · PROOF-OF-CONCEPT SCRIPT  *(the opening lure)*

*This is the teaser/pilot fragment — roughly 5–6 minutes of audio. Its only jobs: prove
the three-stream device works in the ear, grip immediately, and seed the whole engine
without explaining a word of it. Names are placeholders. Read it aloud — the seam between
what the chord plants and what the man then "feels" is the entire sell, and it only
verifies out loud.*

### SOUND-DESIGN BRIEF *(the three streams — non-negotiable contrasts)*
- **SURFACE** *(spoken, in the room):* fully naturalistic. Real room tone, real
  acoustics, present and warm. Where the listener feels safe.
- **CHORD** *(the channel — telepathic):* **not** a single narrator. Two-or-more voices
  in near-unison, slightly staggered, close-mic'd, **no room reverb at all** (it isn't
  in the room; it's inside a skull). Cold, appraising, procedural. It calls the young
  woman *"the woman" / "the daughter"* — never "her," never a name. **NOTE: right now the
  chord is one voice short.** There is an audible *gap* where a third voice should sit.
  Do not fill it. Let it ache.
- **HIM** *(the man's interior):* a single warm voice, close, fully roomed — a real self.
  The horror is **delay**: the CHORD plants something; a beat (or longer) later, HIM
  blooms it as his own, with total conviction.
- **NARRATOR** *(our protagonist, years later, reconstructing):* intimate, dry,
  self-deprecating, close as a confession. She is the only family member who **cannot
  hear the CHORD** — then or now. When her narration drifts near it, she is *imagining*
  into a room she was always shut out of.

---

**RAIN, steady, against glass. A near-empty café-bar, late. A coffee machine sighs and
goes quiet. One chair scrapes. Room tone settles. Hold it — let the place be real before
anyone is.**

NARRATOR *(close, unhurried):* I have always met the wrong kind of man. My mother said
it like a weather report — *there she goes again* — and I believed her, because she was
never wrong about anything. *(a small breath)* This is the last man I ever brought home
to them. I didn't know that yet. What I remember isn't his face. It's that he folded his
receipt into a tiny square while he talked, smaller and smaller, like he was trying to
make it disappear before he had to ask for anything. I wasn't supposed to notice things
like that. Noticing things like that was exactly what was wrong with me.

**RAIN. A glass set down, gentle.**

NARRATOR: I brought him over with nothing but talk. That part was always mine. The rest
was never mine to do.

**The acoustic shifts subtly — we move to a table. Two more presences. A warmth in the
air that is almost too much, too soon.**

MAIDEN *(SURFACE — warm, easy):* So you're the one who's been keeping her all to
yourself.

DANIEL *(SURFACE — a laugh, a little nervous):* I — sorry, I didn't realize I was
keeping anyone.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless — the gap where a third voice should be):* He apologizes
for taking up space. Good. Easily moved. Bring the daughter into his eyeline. Let him
find her.

DANIEL *(HIM — interior, warm, slightly wondering):* ...God. The sister. I hadn't even —
how did I not notice her when we came in?

NARRATOR *(over it, dry):* I used to think it was me. That I'd warm them up and then,
every time, they'd turn — like a plant turning — toward whoever I'd brought them to. I
told myself I just wasn't the kind men stayed pointed at. *(beat)* I had the cause
backwards. But that took me thirty years and one funeral to work out.

MAIDEN *(SURFACE — softer now, leaning in):* What brings you here, then? Really.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* Honestly? Killing time. A friend bailed. *(self-deprecating)* Story
of my — you don't need my whole sad —

MOTHER *(SURFACE — first time she speaks; mild, grandmotherly, almost nothing):* We
don't mind a sad story. We've heard worse.

CHORD: The mother is correct. We have heard worse. *(a flat ripple — the nearest this
voice comes to amusement)* He responds to being needed. So. Need him.

MAIDEN *(SURFACE):* Stay. Sit with us a while. It's filthy out there and you'd be doing
us a kindness — it's been a hard week.

**RAIN, harder against the glass.**

DANIEL *(HIM — and this is the seam; let the listener hear the line above echo in his
own warm voice, a beat too soon to be his):* ...They've had a hard week. I could — yeah.
I could be useful. When's the last time anyone actually needed me to just *sit* with
them?

NARRATOR: He said yes to the rain, really. To not being alone in it. *(beat)* I want to
be clear that I knew exactly what was happening. I'd done it a hundred times. I just
couldn't, for the life of me, work out why this time it felt like watching someone walk
into traffic.

CHORD: The daughter has him. Step back. *(and now — to the surface, the one instruction
the listener understands has teeth)* Step back.

**A pause. A chair, very slightly, moves back from the table. The MAIDEN and DANIEL's
voices continue underneath now — warm, indistinct, a private weather forming without
our narrator in it.**

NARRATOR *(quieter, the dryness gone):* So I stepped back. The way you're meant to. And
I sat there in my own family like a coat someone had taken off and forgotten, and I
watched him fall for my sister with a look on his face like something good was finally
happening to him. *(beat)* And I was jealous. *(she catches it — almost laughs at
herself)* Jealous. Of course I was. After everything, *that* was the rot they could
never quite cut out of me.

**The RAIN. The two warm voices, close and happy and wrong. Hold.**

NARRATOR: I'm going to tell you what we were. I'm going to tell you what I am, and what
he was for, and what I did. *(beat)* But I want you to remember the receipt. The little
folded square. Because by the end of it I was the only person left alive who'd noticed it
at all —

**SNAP to silence. Rain only. Out.**

---

*Craft notes, for whoever produces it:*
- *The seam is demonstrated twice (CHORD "need him" → HIM "I could be useful"; CHORD
  "had a hard week" → HIM "they've had a hard week"). In production, vary the lag — one
  near-instant, one delayed — so it never feels mechanical (§8, §14 of the bible).*
- *The surprising-human beat against over-determination (Threat 4): the folded receipt.
  It serves no plot; it is pure useless human noticing — which is exactly the point, and
  exactly her "defect." It also becomes the episode's button. Let her have a few of these
  across the series that go nowhere.*
- *Everything is planted, nothing explained: the missing third voice (the dead Crone, the
  amputation, the reason they're hunting now); "the daughter" / "the woman" (the vessel
  the man is being steered toward); "one funeral"; that the narrator can't hear the cold
  voices we can. No listener needs the lore to feel the dread. (Bible §4: pattern, not lore.)*
- *Dramatic irony engine: the listener hears the CHORD; DANIEL and the NARRATOR do not.*

# 3 · FRAMING & HANDLING NOTE  *(how to talk about it, and protect it)*

*For anyone speaking about the project publicly — pitch, press, copy, interviews. The
SWOT's biggest non-craft risks are **misreading** and **density.** Both are solved with
discipline about language, not changes to the work.*

### THE ONE THING TO ALWAYS LEAD WITH
This is a story about **collectivity versus the individual self** — about the difference
between *merging* with people and actually *loving* them. It is **not** a story about
women preying on men, monstrous motherhood, or female collectivity as evil. When in
doubt, talk about *enmeshment and individuation* (a human, universal thing) and let the
horror be the amplifier.

### THE FOUR MISREADS, AND THE CORRECTIVE LINE FOR EACH
| If someone hears… | Say instead… |
|---|---|
| "Women prey on men" | "It's about a family that can't perceive *anyone* outside itself as real — the men, and its own daughter, equally." |
| "Motherhood is monstrous" | "The mother is never a villain. Her love is genuine — it simply cannot see the child as a separate person. That's the tragedy, and it's Lear, not horror-movie." |
| "Female collectivity is evil" | "It takes the sacred image of complete female selfhood and asks one hard question: what if a wholeness that total leaves no room for anyone else to be real?" |
| "Men contaminate women / gender war" | "Nobody's the enemy. It's about the cost of a closed system — and the one cast-out daughter who turns out to be the only one capable of love." |

### PROTECTING THE MAN *(SWOT Weakness 4)*
He is used, diminished, and not given conventional justice — and that honesty is
load-bearing; "rescuing" him would turn a tragedy into a thriller. **But he is not
disposable to the story.** The entire drama is the narrator, afterward, finally
*perceiving him as real* — reconstructing the inner life no one granted him while he
lived. **That reconstruction is his justice:** not survival or revenge, but being truly
seen, by the one person capable of it, too late. If a producer flinches at his fate, that
is the answer: *the form itself is the act of giving him back his personhood.*

### HANDLING THE SEXUAL / REPRODUCTIVE MATERIAL *(SWOT Weakness 2)*
- It is **consent-horror, not assault.** The violation is the *use of a person without
  his knowledge* while he is, on the surface, a joyful and willing participant. Frame it
  that way; never as predation-by-force.
- It stays **off-mic.** The seduction, the use, the diminishment — all rendered by
  *residue* (a flattened voice, an emptied room), never by depiction. Audio makes this
  natural; lean on it. Nothing graphic is required and nothing graphic should appear.
- In summary form, anchor to *the human nerve* (a family that consumes its members'
  selfhood — which enmeshing families do) so it never reads as exploitative shock.

### THE DENSITY DISCIPLINE *(SWOT Weakness 1 — the #1 commercial risk)*
The bible is intricate **on purpose, for the makers.** None of that intricacy belongs in
any public-facing sentence. **Pitch the feeling, never the system.** If a logline contains
the words *telepathic, lineage, triad, metabolic, suppression,* or *witch* — cut them. The
listener should be unable to fully explain the rules and unable to stop listening. That is
the design, applied to the sell.

### SAY THIS / NOT THAT
- **Say:** "You can hear a self being born." **Not:** "It uses a three-stream layered
  telepathic audio architecture."
- **Say:** "A family that shares one mind." **Not:** "A matrilineal birth-linked
  telepathic continuity."
- **Say:** "The thing she was taught was her brokenness is her capacity to love."
  **Not:** "Her switched-on oxytocin hardware inherited from the outside father."
- **Say:** "She lures the men her family needs." **Not:** "She procures reps for the
  Maiden's suppression-training and meals for the chord."

### THE CROSS-CHECK
The bible's **Appendix A** (the reviews we're writing toward) is the regression test: if a
draft or a cut can no longer earn one of those raves, something drifted — and the
annotation says which section to return to. Use it that way.
