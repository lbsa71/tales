# Episode 2: Reps

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` is spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` is the
channel — layered, roomless, cold, one voice short. `(HIM)` is Daniel's interior; his
feeling for Wren is the one thread no CHORD line ever precedes. `NARRATOR` is Wren, years
later, reconstructing; she cannot hear the CHORD. See the README.*

---

**A house at evening. The same old timber, the same unhurried clock. A kettle coming up to
the boil. Three women moving through rooms — you can hear it in the floor, the way they
never collide, never call out, never need to ask where another one is. Under it: the thin
layered hum, the hole in it. A door; a coat hung; the kettle clicks off.**

NARRATOR *(close):* He came on the Sunday. I want to tell you my family lived somewhere
grand, somewhere with a name, but it was just a house — too big for us, the way every house
we ever had was a little too big and a little too far from the next one. *(beat)* We moved
a lot. More than anyone I've ever met since. I used to think we were unlucky with
landlords. *(a small dry beat)* There was a fireplace in that house and we never once lit
it. My mother kept the matches in a drawer in the kitchen, at the back, behind things, the
way other people keep a thing they're frightened of where they won't come across it by
accident.

**A knock. A real knock, on real wood. Then warmth pouring out — LISS's voice, bright at
the door.**

LISS *(SURFACE — delighted):* You came! You actually came, Mum said you'd lose your nerve —

DANIEL *(SURFACE — laughing, off the back foot, charmed):* I — no, I'm — these are for you.
Both of you. All — however many of you there —

VALA *(SURFACE — from deeper in the house, mild):* Three. There are three of us. *(the
smallest pause — a thing she has had to start saying differently and is getting used to)*
There are three of us, dear, come in out of the cold.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless; and in the gap where the third voice should be, nothing —
the count is off and the channel notes it without feeling it):* Three. *(flat)* The number
is wrong. Proceed as if it is not. He must find a whole house. A house missing a voice asks
to be looked at. We do not get looked at.

DANIEL *(HIM — a flicker of cold he can't account for, then gone):* ...why have I got a
chill on me. *(beat)* The heating's on, the place is warm, and there's a draught coming off
it like a window's open somewhere I can't see. *(he shrugs it off, straight into gratitude)*
Big old house. Doesn't matter. *(warmer)* I've not been this glad to be let in anywhere in
years.

NARRATOR *(over it):* I carried the flowers to the kitchen. That was a job I could do.

---

**The table. Warm clatter — cups, a tin opened, biscuits. LISS and DANIEL close; their
voices easy, the rhythm of two people getting on. WREN further off — the scrape of her
chair is a half-second behind everyone else's, always.**

LISS *(SURFACE — warm, leaning in, that bright attention):* — no, but tell me the actual
thing, not the version you tell at parties. The friend who bailed. You do that, don't you,
you make everything into the funny short version so nobody has to —

DANIEL *(SURFACE — caught out, pleased to be caught):* God. Yeah. *(beat)* Okay. The actual
thing.

CHORD: She has him opening. Warm the words; keep nothing under them. *(precise, like a hand
on a dial)* Attend to him completely. Mean none of it. This is the skill. Run it warm. Do
not let it catch.

NARRATOR *(present-day, quiet):* My sister had a gift I used to envy so badly it kept me up
at night. She could give you her *whole* face. Every bit of her, turned on you like a lamp.
*(beat)* It took me half my life to work out what was wrong with the way she did it, and
when I finally did, I couldn't have told you in words. It was just — *(searching)* — you
know how a hotel room is warm? The heating's on, it's a perfectly warm room, and you still
wouldn't call it cosy, because nobody lives there. *(beat)* She was a warm room nobody lived
in. And men walked into her like coming in from the rain.

LISS *(SURFACE — soft, perfectly timed):* That's so sad. *(beat)* No — I mean it. That's
really sad, Daniel.

DANIEL *(HIM — and the bloom comes a beat late, and he owns it completely):* ...she *gets*
it. She actually — nobody's ever just said *that's sad,* nobody just lets it be sad, they
all try to fix it or top it. *(wondering)* I could fall for someone who just lets a thing be
sad.

NARRATOR: He didn't know he'd been handed the feeling. Neither did I, then. I just thought:
lucky her. Lucky him. *(beat)* I brought the tea over and nobody looked up, and I went back
for the milk nobody had asked for, so I'd have a reason to stand.

---

**A small thing now — and the listener must catch that no CHORD line precedes it. DANIEL,
mid-sentence to Liss, glances off — toward where Wren is standing with the milk.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE — to the room, to Wren really):* Sorry — Wren, is it? You've barely — come
and sit, you're hovering like you're on shift.

CHORD *(cold; a small recalculation):* He turns to the sister. Unhelpful. *(beat)* Draw him
back. Have the daughter laugh.

LISS *(SURFACE — laughing, reclaiming):* Oh, ignore her, she's happiest hovering, aren't
you — Daniel, tell me about the brother, you said there was a brother —

**And here — the resistance, and the patience. DANIEL lets himself be drawn back. But.**

DANIEL *(HIM — under it, true, un-planted):* ...she does look like she's on shift, though.
In her own kitchen. Who taught her to stand like that. *(a beat)* Like she's waiting to be
told she can sit down.

CHORD: He returns. *(flat — no alarm; the chord does not panic; the chord waits)* He
returns slowly. Note it. The sister holds his eye a half-beat past the others. We will not
seat them together again tonight.

NARRATOR *(present-day, and it lands quietly on her):* You hear that? *(beat)* He noticed how
I stood. *(a small broken sound that isn't quite a laugh)* I'd have given a finger, then, to
know a man had spent one second on how I stood in a kitchen. *(beat)* I'd have told you it
couldn't have happened. That nobody clocks the furniture. *(very quiet)* I have spent years
deciding whether I made that up. I've decided both ways. Tonight I've decided it was real.
Ask me tomorrow.

---

**The acoustic opens — a different memory pushing in. Daylight. A street, traffic, a
supermarket's automatic doors. WREN, present in the past, mid-errand. And then a voice she
half-knows — a man's, but wrong. Flattened. The colour turned down out of it, like a
photograph left in a window.**

NARRATOR *(quiet, present-day):* I told you I'd see them again. After. *(beat)* This is the
one I could never put back to sleep.

MAN *(SURFACE — and his voice should be audibly *diminished:* the words intact, the life
under them drained; warm content, no warmth):* — sorry, do I — you look so — have we —

WREN *(SURFACE — careful, kind, not placing him yet):* I think maybe? Sorry. I'm terrible
with —

MAN *(SURFACE, that flat patient drift):* No, it's — you've got one of those faces. *(beat)*
I don't really — I've not been sleeping great. Since. *(beat)* Since I don't know when,
actually. Funny. You lose track.

NARRATOR *(over it, and you can hear her not-letting-herself):* I knew him. I'd brought him
home. Not that spring — the spring before, maybe. He'd been — I remembered him *loud.* Full
of opinions about football and his ex-wife and a band he'd seen in '99. *(beat)* And here he
was, in the cereal aisle, and somebody had turned him *down.* Like a radio in another room.
All the words still there. No song in them.

MAN *(SURFACE):* Anyway. *(beat)* You take care. *(beat)* Whoever you are.

**His footsteps go. The automatic doors. WREN, not moving.**

NARRATOR: I stood there a long time. *(beat)* And I want you to watch what I did next,
because it's the whole of me, it's the thing I did my entire life until it killed the people
I loved. *(beat)* I thought: *poor man. Life does that to people.* And I picked up my
shopping. *(beat)* There was a question, right there, with its hand up. I could feel it. The
question was: *every man you have ever brought to that house — where are they now? What do
they sound like now?* *(beat)* And something in me reached out, very gently, the way you'd
settle a child, and put the question back to sleep. *(beat)* I called that being good. I'd
been trained to call it being good. The not-asking. I was so, so good at it.

CHORD *(cold, from memory, certain):* The drained do not return to us. They have nothing
left to give and no reason to come. *(flat)* This is tidy. We prefer tidy. *(beat)* The
sister sees one in the street and grieves him a little and forgets. She is built to forget
the thing she is built to do. It is why she is bearable to keep.

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I didn't hear that. *(beat)* But I lived it. Word for word.

---

**Back to the house. Night now. Daniel gone home — a car, a door, the gravel. The three
women alone. The thin hum, the hole in it. Vala in the kitchen; Liss somewhere above; the
floor telling us where everyone is. And here the CHORD is at its coldest and clearest,
because there is no man to perform for — this is the house talking to itself.**

CHORD *(layered, roomless, decided):* The man is good. *(beat)* Not for the table. *(a
weight comes into it — the nearest the channel gets to gravity)* This one is for the line.

NARRATOR *(over it; she is climbing the stairs in the memory, half-hearing the house go
still beneath her):* I came in from putting the cups away and the house had done the thing
again. Gone still. That held-breath stillness. *(beat)* My mother was standing in the
kitchen doorway — she'd started doing that since the funeral, standing in doorways — and she
looked at me, and for once she smiled, and said —

VALA *(SURFACE — warm, and it is the warmest she has been; the curdled-mother register, real
love bent into ownership):* You did well, love. You brought us a good one. *(beat)* You see?
You're not no use. When you're useful you're so —

**She doesn't finish it. She doesn't have to.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, and this is the wound the whole show turns on):* *You're not no use.*
*(beat)* You'd have to have grown up the way I grew up to know what that sentence does to a
body. I felt it go all the way down. I'd have brought her a hundred men to hear the back
half of it. *(beat)* The thing is — and I need you to hold both of these at once, because I
can't seem to put them down — the thing is, she *meant* it. That's not a monster talking.
That's my mother, who loved me, telling me I'd been good. *(beat)* It was the kindest she'd
been to me in a year. *(beat)* And I floated up those stairs on it like a girl who'd been
told she was pretty.

CHORD: The sister is useful and therefore content. *(flat)* Contentment in the sister is a
maintenance state. Maintain it. *(beat)* For the line, the budding will be soon. The mother
grieves; the grief makes her quick; quick is acceptable. The daughter is ripe. *(beat)* It
is time.

**The hum. The hole in it. The clock.**

NARRATOR: I lay in bed that night happier than I'd been since before the funeral. *(beat)*
A man had noticed how I stood. My mother had said I wasn't no use. *(beat)* And somewhere
under the floor of my own house, in the one room I was never allowed into, in the one
language I was born without — it had been decided what Daniel was for. *(beat)* He was for
the line. *(beat)* I didn't know the words. I want that understood. I didn't have the words.
*(very quiet)* But I'd felt the house go still like that before, every time, and every time,
a few weeks later, there'd be one fewer place set at the table, and a man somewhere in a
supermarket with the colour turned down out of him, saying *whoever you are* to a girl he
used to know loud.

**Silence opening under the words.**

NARRATOR: I knew. *(beat)* The way you know a thing you've decided your whole life not to
know. *(beat)* I'd have told you, that night, that I didn't have the first idea. *(beat)*
And I'd have been lying, the way I lied to myself — gently, lovingly, the way you settle a
child — every single day of my life.

**SNAP to silence. The clock, alone, three beats. Out.**
