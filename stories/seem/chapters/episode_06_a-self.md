# Episode 6: A Self

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` the channel —
layered, roomless, cold, and now fraying: a voice keeps pulling out of true. `(HIM)` Daniel's
interior, hollowing — the room around it draining. `NARRATOR` is Wren, reconstructing; she
cannot hear the CHORD. See the README.*

---

**The house, the days after. Wrong in a way that has no single sound — like a piece of music
played by a band that has stopped agreeing on the tempo. The thin hum is still there, but it
no longer breathes as one; under it, a voice keeps lifting out and being pulled back, lifting
out, pulled back. And over everything, a new quality in the rooms: a person who is grieving
something she will not name, moving through a house, opening doors and finding the wrong thing
behind every one.**

NARRATOR *(close):* My sister got sick. *(beat)* That's how it looked, from where I was —
from the outside, where I lived. She stopped coming down. She went off her food. She'd be
fine, ordinary, herself, and then her face would do a thing — go still, and frightened, like
a person who's just felt a lump that wasn't there last week. *(beat)* My mother said it was a
chill. My mother said it was the wedding nerves — she'd started calling it a wedding, by then,
though no one had said a word to Daniel, and Daniel had stopped calling. *(beat)* I believed
her, the way I believed her about everything. A chill. Nerves. *(beat)* I brought soup up to a
door that wouldn't open and I told myself it was a chill, while on the other side of it my
sister was being born, in agony, against her will, into a self she'd been raised her whole
life to think of as a tumour.

---

**Behind the door. And here we go where Wren could not — into the room, into the channel — and
the horror is that individuation, from the inside, is not freedom. It is a self growing in a
body that has the chord's values, and meets its own selfhood with the chord's revulsion.**

**The CHORD, fraying, and within it LISS's voice — flickering between *we* and *I,* gaining
and losing its little halo of room.**

CHORD *(layered, trying to be whole, cold but frightened):* Come back. *(beat)* Daughter. Come
back into the we. There is no out there. There has never been an out there. The thing you feel
is not real; smooth it; let it go flat; let us carry it for you —

LISS *(peeling out — roomed, separate, and *horrified at herself*):* — I can't. *(beat)* I
can't give it back. *(a rising panic)* There's a — there's a *me* in here, there's an *I,* I
can feel the edges of it, it has *edges,* oh God, it has an inside, why does it have an —

CHORD *(clamping, smoothing):* — it is a sickness. It is the sickness we have always guarded
against. You are not an *I.* You are the we, wearing this body for a while, as the we has worn
a hundred —

LISS *(out again, and now grief, the chord's own values turned on her own self):* — then cut
it out. *(begging)* Cut it out of me. I don't *want* it. I never wanted to be — I don't want to
be a — *(the word is the worst thing she knows)* — a *person.* People are *alone.* I can feel
how alone it is in here, I can feel the walls of me, I'm in a little room and there's no one
else in it and there has never in my life been no one else and I can't — *(breaking)* — make
it stop. Make me us again. *Please* make me us again.

NARRATOR *(present-day, over it, and it has taken her thirty years to be able to say this):*
You want me to tell you she was breaking free. *(beat)* Everyone wants that. The girl in the
tower, the chains coming off. *(beat)* It wasn't that. *(beat)* I have had to learn — slowly,
against everything that would have been easier to believe — that what happened to my sister
felt to her exactly like a disease. Because that's what she'd been taught a self was. She had
all their values and none of their company. *(beat)* She wasn't reaching for the door. *(beat)*
A wall was growing up through the middle of her, and she beat her fists on it and begged the
people on the other side to come and take it down, and they couldn't, because the wall was
*her,* the wall was the first her there'd ever been. *(very quiet)* She was the loneliest thing
that had ever lived in that house. And I'd been jealous of her. *(beat)* I'd lain in the dark
and envied the very thing that was now eating her alive — the thing I'd been born already
inside of, and called my defect, and apologised for, every day, for thirty years.

---

**The phone. It rings into the strange house — an ordinary, brutal sound. WREN answers,
because no one else will. And it is DANIEL, hollowed, the colour gone out of him. This must
be unbearable in its flatness.**

WREN *(SURFACE — careful, her heart going):* Daniel?

DANIEL *(SURFACE — flat now, the warmth a memory of warmth, the words right and the life
gone):* Wren. Hi. *(beat)* Is — could I talk to Liss. *(beat)* I keep — I've been meaning to.
I had this — *(he reaches for it and it isn't there)* — we had a really nice time. Didn't we.
*(beat)* I think we did. I can't quite — it's been a strange couple of weeks. I'm so tired,
Wren. I don't know what's the matter with me.

DANIEL *(HIM — and even the interior is dim now, the room around it nearly gone):* ...why
can't I picture it. *(beat)* The best night of my life and I can't — it's like a room with the
lights off. I know the furniture's there. I can't see it. *(beat)* What did I leave there. What
did I —

WREN *(SURFACE — and she's lying to him, gently, the only kindness she has left, and it's the
same kindness that damns him):* She's just got a chill, Daniel. *(beat)* She'll call you.
*(beat)* Get some rest. You sound — get some rest.

NARRATOR *(present-day):* He didn't call again after that. *(beat)* I used to wonder if he was
angry. *(beat)* He wasn't angry. There wasn't enough of him left to be angry. *(beat)* He just
— faded out of it, the way they all faded, off into a life with the colour turned down, and
somewhere in that life there's a man who sometimes gets a feeling he can't place, standing in a
kitchen, that he once had a night that mattered and left something behind in a house he can't
remember the road to. *(beat)* And here is the thing I heard in his voice on that phone — the
thing that finally, finally did it — *(beat)* — he sounded *like me.* *(beat)* That flat,
tired, can't-quite-feel-the-good-thing, what's-the-matter-with-me — *(beat)* — that was my
voice. That had always been my voice. *(very quiet)* I'd spent my life delivering men to be
made into the thing I already was. *(beat)* And I'd called it the family business. And I'd been
proud when I was good at it.

---

**And here it lands. No outside sound for this — it happens inside her, on the stairs, in the
hall, wherever she is when the floor finally goes. The guillotine that dropped a hundred times
does not drop. The thought completes.**

NARRATOR *(slow, and the whole architecture of her life coming down a beam at a time):* It
came all at once and it came in the wrong order, the way the truth does when you've held it
under for thirty years. *(beat)* It went: *the men go nowhere because I send them nowhere.*
*(beat)* It went: *I do the thing my mother does — I make a person feel chosen so that
something can be taken from them — I just do it by hand, because I never had the —* *(beat)* —
*because I'm the one who can't.* *(beat)* It went: *I am not the failed one in a family of the
whole. I am one of them. I learned it from them. I have been one of them all along.* *(a long,
terrible beat)* And then — and this is the part I'd kept the deepest, this is the one the
guillotine was really for — *(beat)* it went: *and the reason I am one of them, the reason I
could ever do it at all — the talking, the listening, the going-quiet-for-them — is the one
thing in me that was never theirs.* *(beat)* *They taught me to use it. But they didn't put it
there.* *(beat)* *They can't do it. Not one of them can do what I do in a café with nothing but
talk, because to do it you have to be able to —* *(she can barely)* — *you have to be able to
actually see the person. And they can't. They never could. That's the whole —* *(beat)* — *I
can see people. I'm the only one of us who can see people. And they took the one thing I had
that they didn't, and they bent it into a hook, and they hung men on it, and they told me it
was the only useful thing about me, and I believed them, because being useful was the only —*
*(she stops; she can't; and then, very small)* — oh. *(beat)* Oh, no. *(beat)* Oh, no, I'm not
the broken one. *(beat)* I was never the broken one.

**A silence. The fraying hum, under. A voice in it, lifting out, pulled back.**

NARRATOR *(barely):* And do you know what I felt, when it finally landed — the truth I'd run
from my whole life, the truth that should have set me free? *(beat)* I felt sick. I felt
*ashamed.* *(beat)* Because the floor that dropped wasn't *they were wrong about me.* *(beat)*
The floor that dropped was *they were right that there's something different in me — and they
spent my whole life turning the best thing I had into the worst thing I did.* *(beat)* There's
no liberation in that. There's no walking free. *(beat)* The verdict was correct. *(beat)* I
really was the one who couldn't merge. *(beat)* It's just that the thing I couldn't do — that's
the thing that means I can love. *(beat)* And I found that out the same week I worked out I'd
spent my whole life using it to feed people to a thing that can't.

---

**The house comes apart further. Above, LISS — the sound now genuinely frightening: a self
trying to hold itself together and the chord trying to dissolve it, in the same body, a
tug-of-war with no winner possible. And VALA — breaking. Not into rage. Into something worse:
a mother feeling a part of her own body die, who does not have the concept of "daughter as
separate person" to make sense of grief, and so experiences it as her own death, beginning.**

VALA *(SURFACE — and the grandmotherly mildness is gone; this is Lear on the heath, a love so
total it cannot tell itself from the beloved):* Stop it. *(beat)* Stop it, stop *doing* this to
us — you're tearing — I can feel you tearing and it's *me,* don't you understand, it's not
*you,* there's no *you,* you're tearing *me* — *(a sound of pure incomprehension and pure
love)* — give it *back,* give yourself back, come *home* —

**And then — VALA stops. Because she has turned, in the hall, and seen WREN. And something in
her, in extremity, perceives — for the first time in thirty years — that the dud has edges
too. That there is, and has always been, an *I* standing in her hallway. The disease, in the
one she'd kept and called family and never once seen. This is the dread-button: recognition
arriving the wrong way round.**

VALA *(SURFACE — quiet now, a terrible quiet, looking at Wren as if at a stranger who has been
in her house for thirty years):* ...you. *(beat)* You've got one too. *(beat)* Haven't you.
*(beat)* You've had one the whole time. *(a breath, and the whole long misunderstanding of
Wren's life turning over in it)* I used to think — when you were small — I used to think we
could melt it out of you, if we just loved you enough, if we held you enough — *(beat)* — and
you'd come right, you'd come in, you'd be *us.* *(beat)* And you never did. *(beat)* And I
stopped trying. *(very quiet, and it is the cruelest tender thing anyone says in the whole
play)* I'm sorry, love. *(beat)* I'm sorry we couldn't fix you.

**Silence. Above, Liss, flickering, in and out, in and out.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, and it is a wound and a key at once):* *I'm sorry we couldn't fix you.*
*(beat)* She meant it kindly. *(beat)* That's the thing I need you to hold, the thing the whole
story is — she has never once, in my whole life, been unkind to me. She loved me. *(beat)* She
just loved me the only way the thing inside her could: as a part of itself that wouldn't heal.
*(beat)* And in that hallway, with my sister coming apart upstairs and Daniel fading out
somewhere down a road none of us would ever drive again, my mother looked at me — *really*
looked, for the first time in thirty years — *(beat)* — and what she saw, the thing she finally
recognised, was the disease. *(beat)* She finally saw me. *(beat)* And she was sorry. Because to
her, seeing me was the same as seeing a tumour she should have caught earlier. *(very quiet)* I
had wanted, my whole life, for my mother to see me. *(beat)* And she did. *(beat)* And it turned
out that in my family, to be *seen* and to be *grieved as a thing that went wrong* were the same
sentence. There's no other kind of being-seen in there. *(beat)* That's the room I'd spent my
life trying to get into. *(beat)* That's what was on the other side of the door.

---

**Above, the sound peaks — LISS, no longer able to be a *we,* not yet able to bear being an
*I,* makes a sound that is past words: a person drowning in the air. The chord cannot hold
her and cannot let her go. And WREN, in the hall, does the thing she has never done in her
life — the thing the whole story has been walking toward.**

NARRATOR *(close, and steadier now, because something in her has been decided below the level
of deciding):* And I went up the stairs. *(beat)* You have to understand what that was. *(beat)*
There was a room in that house I had never been let into. Not once. Not in thirty years. *(beat)*
The room where they were *we.* The room where the language was. The room I'd stood outside of my
whole life with my ear against the door, trying to belong to a sound I'd been born without.
*(beat)* And my sister was in it now, alone for the first time in the history of our line, making
the sound of a creature who has just learned what alone is. *(beat)* And not one of the others
could go to her. *(beat)* You can't reach across a gap you've never had. *(beat)* The mother
couldn't go in. The mother could only call her *home,* call her back into the we, call her into
the thing that was killing her. *(beat)* There was exactly one person in that whole house — in
that whole long line, stretching back past every fire we'd ever run from — who knew how to walk
toward someone who is alone. *(beat)* Because I'd been alone my entire life. *(beat)* I was the
only one who knew the way in.

**Her hand on the door. The forbidden door. The handle.**

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* So I put my hand on the door of the room I was never allowed into.
*(beat)* And for the first time in my life — *(beat)* — I went in.

**SNAP to silence. Above, faintly, a single roomed voice, frightened, alone. Hold. Out.**
