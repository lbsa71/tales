# Episode 1: The Wrong Kind of Man

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` is spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` is the
channel — layered, roomless, cold; it is one voice short. `(HIM)` is Daniel's interior —
warm, roomed, his feelings arriving a beat after they are planted. `NARRATOR` is Wren,
years later, reconstructing; she cannot hear the CHORD. The listener hears everything.
Daniel hears only the room and himself. Wren hears only the room. See the README.*

---

**Silence. Not the absence of sound — the kind of silence that has a shape, that fits a
room the way water fits a glass. A house. Old timber settling. A clock somewhere,
unhurried. Underneath it, very faint, almost not there: a low, layered hum — voices, many,
breathing as one, with no words in them. And in that hum, a hole. A place where a voice
should sit and does not. Hold the hole. Let the ear lean toward it and find nothing.**

NARRATOR *(close, unhurried; a small dry warmth):* The week we buried my grandmother, the
house went quiet in a way I didn't have a word for. *(beat)* You have to understand — it
was never a loud house. My family didn't argue. They didn't need to. They'd settle a thing
between them before anyone got as far as a sentence, the way you don't argue with your own
hand about which way to turn a key.

**The hum, under. The hole in it.**

NARRATOR: But that week it went *thin.* Like a chord with a note lifted out of it. I kept
thinking the radiators had been turned down. I kept checking. They hadn't. *(beat)* I was
the only one in the house who couldn't feel what was actually missing. I just felt cold,
and assumed, the way I always assumed, that the cold was something the matter with me.

**A door. Footsteps — not hers; older, certain. A long pause in a doorway. Then the
footsteps move off. A breath let out that has too many breaths in it, layered, just under
hearing — and then only one woman breathing, in a kitchen, somewhere.**

NARRATOR: My mother stood in doorways that week. She'd never done that in her life. She'd
go to a room and then stop at the edge of it like she'd forgotten what rooms were *for.*
*(softer)* I brought her tea. I didn't know what else a person does. She looked at me —

**A pause. The clock.**

NARRATOR: — she looked at me like she was counting, and had come up one short, and was
trying to work out whether I was the one.

**The hum swells, very slightly. Hungry is the wrong word. But the room wants something.
Hold — then:**

NARRATOR: By the Thursday she sent me out. She didn't say *go and find someone.* She never
said it like that. She said, *you're no use to me moping, love, go and be among people.*
*(a small breath)* And I went, grateful, the way a dog is grateful to be given a job. I
didn't know I'd been given one. *(beat)* I never did, until it was far too late to give it
back.

---

**A cut. New acoustic — a bus, maybe, or the inside of a coat in the rain. Then a door, a
warm wash of café noise, a coffee machine. This memory comes fast and flat, almost bored.**

NARRATOR: Here. I'll show you the shape of it first, with one that doesn't matter, so you
don't have to take my word later. *(beat)* I don't remember his name. I want that on the
record — that I can stand here and tell you, in detail, the way a man I sat with for two
hours folded and unfolded a sugar packet, and I cannot give you his name, because I never
once needed it.

**Café. A man's voice, mid-thirties, lonely-friendly, the kind that's grateful to be
talked to.**

MAN *(SURFACE):* — no, you go ahead, I'm not — honestly I'm just glad of the company, it's
been a — it's been a week.

NARRATOR *(over it, flat):* It is always a week. Everyone you will ever lure has just had a
week. The trick — and it isn't a trick, it's just the only thing I've ever been good at —
is that you *listen.* Not the way people pretend to. All the way down. Until the man feels,
maybe for the first time since he was small, that there is a person across the table who
has gone quiet inside *for him.*

MAN *(SURFACE, lower, opening):* Can I tell you something I haven't — I don't know why I'm —

NARRATOR: And then you bring him home. And then —

**The café noise drops away abruptly, replaced by that cold, layered, roomless CHORD —
and the hole in it.**

CHORD *(cold, layered, close, no room around it; one voice short):* Adequate. Not for the
budding. For the table. Bring him to the daughter. The daughter will take it from here.

NARRATOR *(plain):* — and then it was never mine again. He turned, the way they all turned,
toward my sister, like a plant turning to a window. And I sat in my own family like a coat
somebody had taken off. *(beat)* That one went — wherever they go. I didn't ask. For thirty
years the not-asking felt like manners.

**The café fades entirely. Rain rises in its place — steady, against glass.**

NARRATOR: That's the shape. Now the one that mattered. The last man I ever brought home to
them. *(beat)* And remember, when I get there — I'm asking you a favour — remember that I
walked into it grateful. That's the part I can't make soft for you. Nobody made me. I went
because being sent was the closest thing to being wanted I had.

---

**RAIN, steady, against glass. A near-empty café-bar, late. A coffee machine sighs and
goes quiet. One chair scrapes. Room tone settles. Hold it — let the place be real before
anyone is.**

NARRATOR *(close):* I have always met the wrong kind of man. My mother said it like a
weather report — *there she goes again* — and I believed her, because she was never wrong
about anything. *(a small breath)* This is the last one. I didn't know that yet. What I
remember isn't his face. It's that he folded his receipt into a tiny square while he
talked, smaller and smaller, like he was trying to make it disappear before he had to ask
for anything. *(beat)* I wasn't supposed to notice things like that. Noticing things like
that was exactly what was wrong with me.

**A glass set down, gentle. The man's voice — DANIEL — close, a little tired, warm.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* You don't have to keep me company, you know. I'm a big believer in
letting people off the hook.

NARRATOR *(over it):* I brought him in with nothing but talk. That part was always mine. I
sat down across from a stranger in a dead bar in the rain and inside twenty minutes he was
telling me the true things. *(beat)* The rest — the part that came after the talk — was
never mine to do.

**The acoustic shifts subtly — we are at a table now. Two more presences arrive, settle. A
warmth comes into the air that is almost too much, too soon. A chair. A coat.**

LISS *(SURFACE — warm, easy, bright):* So you're the one who's been keeping her all to
yourself.

DANIEL *(SURFACE — a laugh, a little nervous):* I — sorry, I didn't realize I was keeping
anyone.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless — the gap where the third voice should be):* He apologizes
for taking up space. Good. Easily moved. Bring the daughter into his eyeline. Let him find
her.

DANIEL *(HIM — interior, warm, slightly wondering):* ...God. The sister. How did I not — she
was right there when we came in, how did I not *see* her?

NARRATOR *(over it, dry):* I used to think it was me. That I'd warm them up, and then, every
time, they'd turn — like that — toward whoever I'd brought them to. I told myself I just
wasn't the kind men stay pointed at. *(beat)* I had the cause backwards. But that took me
thirty years and one funeral to work out.

LISS *(SURFACE — softer now, leaning in):* What brings you here, then? Really.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* Honestly? Killing time. A friend bailed. *(self-deprecating)* Story of
my — you don't need my whole sad —

VALA *(SURFACE — first time she speaks; mild, grandmotherly, almost nothing there):* We
don't mind a sad story. We've heard worse.

CHORD: The mother is correct. We have heard worse. *(a flat ripple — the nearest this voice
comes to amusement)* He responds to being needed. So. Need him.

LISS *(SURFACE):* Then stay. Sit with us a while. It's filthy out there, and you'd be doing
us a kindness — it's been a hard week.

**RAIN, harder against the glass.**

DANIEL *(HIM — and this is the seam: let the line above arrive in his own warm voice, a
beat too soon to be his):* ...They've had a hard week. I could — yeah. I could be useful.
*(quieter, to himself)* When's the last time anyone actually needed me to just *sit* with
them.

NARRATOR: He said yes to the rain, really. To not being alone in it. *(beat)* I want to be
clear that I knew exactly what was happening. I'd done it a hundred times. I just couldn't,
for the life of me, work out why this time it felt like watching someone walk into traffic.

CHORD: The daughter has him. *(beat)* Step back. *(and now — the one instruction the
listener understands has teeth)* Step back.

**A pause. A chair, very slightly, moves back from the table. LISS and DANIEL's voices
continue underneath now — warm, indistinct, a private weather forming without our narrator
in it.**

NARRATOR *(quieter, the dryness gone):* So I stepped back. The way you're meant to. And I
sat there in my own family like a coat someone had taken off and forgotten, and I watched
him fall for my sister with a look on his face like something good was finally happening to
him. *(beat)* And I was jealous. *(she catches it — almost laughs at herself)* Jealous. Of
course I was. After everything, *that* was the rot they could never quite cut out of me.

---

**The two warm voices, close and happy, under. Rain. Then a small private sound: paper,
folded, refolded. The receipt. DANIEL, mid-laugh at something Liss has said, fingers
working at it without knowing.**

NARRATOR: While my sister did the thing she was made to do, I watched his hands. He'd
finished the receipt — folded down past the point where paper wants to fold, this hard
little square — and now he didn't know what to do with it, so he was turning it over and
over, this man who was, by every account including his own, having the best night of his
life. *(beat)* People who are happy don't do that with their hands. *(beat)* I noticed.
Nobody had asked me to notice. Nobody ever did.

**A burst of LISS's laughter, bright. DANIEL laughing too — but listen: under his laugh,
the HIM voice, low, not unhappy, just true.**

DANIEL *(HIM):* ...this is going to end. Things like this always — *(he stops himself)* No.
Be here. For once just *be* here.

CHORD: He guards himself. Naturally. *(flat)* The daughter will not rush. We have time.

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I didn't hear that, of course. The cold part. I never could. *(beat)*
But I'm going to tell it to you anyway, all the way through, because somebody should have
heard it, and I'm the only one left who'd want to. Even if I'm only guessing. Even if all I
ever had to go on was the look on his face, and what came after, and thirty years to be
sorry in.

**The clock from the opening, faint, returning under. The thin hum, the hole in it.**

NARRATOR: They didn't say it that night, but I know how my family talks when it's decided
something. There's a — settling. The way the house had gone thin all week, that Thursday it
went *still,* the way a held breath is still. *(beat)* I thought it was about my
grandmother. I thought we were grieving. *(beat)* We were. I just didn't understand that in
my family, grief and hunger are the same weather.

CHORD *(under, cold, certain):* This one. Not the table. *(beat)* This one is for the line.

NARRATOR: I'm guessing at that. But I'd bet my life on it. *(a breath)* I nearly did.

---

**Outside now. The café door, a bell. Rain on the street. DANIEL and WREN, walking, the
acoustic open and wet. Liss and Vala gone ahead or behind — for a moment it is just the two
of them, and this is the only stream the listener can fully trust.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE, warmer, looser, the wine in it):* Can I say a weird thing. *(beat)* Your
sister's — your whole family's lovely, they really are, they've been so —

WREN *(SURFACE — and this is the first we hear her *in scene,* younger, lighter, careful):*
But.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* No, not but. *(beat)* It's just — you're the one who talked to me. Like,
*talked.* And then you sort of — went away. And I keep — *(small laugh at himself)* I don't
know. I keep wanting to check you're still there.

**A beat. Rain. And here — crucially — no CHORD line comes before this. Nothing is planted.
It is only him.**

DANIEL *(HIM — quiet, unforced, and the listener must be able to tell this one is real):*
She's got the saddest, kindest face. Why would they keep someone like that at the edge of
everything.

WREN *(SURFACE — and she misreads it, the way she always will):* I'm always still here.
*(beat, dry, deflecting)* I'm the furniture. You'll learn.

NARRATOR *(over, present-day, and it costs her):* He said a true thing to me, in the rain,
and I made a joke, because a true thing said to me had to be a mistake. Someone had got the
wrong girl. *(beat)* I have gone back to that sentence — *I keep wanting to check you're
still there* — more nights than I'll admit. I've turned it over till it went soft, like his
receipt. *(very quiet)* I'll never know if it was real. I couldn't hear the part of him that
would've told me. *(beat)* I only ever got the room.

---

**RAIN. Then the warm voices fold back in — Liss returning, laughing, slipping her arm
through Daniel's. The private weather closing over again. WREN, half a step back, where she
belongs.**

LISS *(SURFACE, bright, drawing him on):* Come *on,* you two, you'll catch your deaths —
Daniel, you're staying for tea, I won't hear otherwise —

DANIEL *(SURFACE, swept, happy):* I — okay, yeah, okay —

CHORD: Good. He comes willingly. *(beat)* The daughter is ready. The mother is ready. *(and
then, into the gap where the third voice is not)* ...We are short. *(flat, not grief — a
hand reaching for a tool that is not on the bench)* We are short, and we will not stay short.

**The hole in the chord, loud now in its silence. Then the rain swallows it.**

NARRATOR: That's where I'll stop tonight. Him saying yes a second time — yes to the tea,
yes to the warmth, yes to all of it — and me a step behind, jealous, useless, *noticing.*
*(beat)* I'm going to tell you what we were. I'm going to tell you what I am, and what he
was for, and what I did. *(beat)* But I want you to remember the receipt. The little folded
square he made of it.

**The two warm voices, going away up the wet street. The thin hum under. The clock.**

NARRATOR: Because by the end of it, I was the only person left alive who'd ever noticed it
at all.

**SNAP to silence. Rain only. Out.**
