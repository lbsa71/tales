# Episode 1: The Wrong Kind of Man

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` is spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` is the
channel — layered, roomless, cold; it is one voice short. `(HIM)` is Daniel's interior —
warm, roomed, his feelings arriving a beat after they are planted. `NARRATOR` is Wren,
years later, reconstructing; she cannot hear the CHORD. The listener hears everything.
Daniel hears only the room and himself. Wren hears only the room. See the README.*

---

**Black. Then a man's voice, alone — close, ordinary, and wrong. The words are friendly.
The life under them is gone, like a recording of warmth played one too many times.**

MAN *(SURFACE — flat, patient, the colour turned down out of him):* No — no, you go on
ahead. I'm grand. *(beat)* I'm always grand. *(beat)* ...Funny. I used to be such good
company.

**Silence. Let that sit. Then NARRATOR — close, dry, certain. This is the contract; do not
hurry it.**

NARRATOR: That's a man I brought home to my mother. *(beat)* Years before. He was *loud*
when I found him — loud and lonely and full of himself, opinions on everything, a band he'd
seen, an ex-wife he wasn't over. He walked into my family one Sunday like a man coming in
out of the rain. *(beat)* And he walked out again, after. They always walked out after.
Alive. On their own two feet. *(beat)* Just — turned down. Like that. Like a radio in
another room. All the words still in them. No song left underneath.

**A breath.**

NARRATOR: For thirty years I brought lonely men home to my family. *(beat)* I thought it
was the one thing wrong with me — that I couldn't keep one. That I always picked the kind
who'd drift off toward somebody else the moment they got warm. *(a small dry sound)* *I
always meet the wrong kind of man.* My mother used to say it like a weather report, and I
believed her, because she was never wrong about anything. *(beat)* It took me thirty years
and one funeral to understand. I wasn't unlucky. I wasn't picking them wrong. *(very
quiet)* I was the bait.

**The faintest hum begins under her — low, layered, many voices breathing as one, no words
in them. And in the hum, a hole. A place a voice should sit, and doesn't.**

NARRATOR: It only ever worked because nobody looked at me twice. *(beat)* That was the gift,
they told me — the only one I had. I could go quiet inside for a man until he felt seen for
the first time in his life — and then, when my family came and took him, I could disappear
so completely he'd forget I was ever in the room. *(beat)* You can't bait a hook the fish
keeps staring at. *(beat)* I was good at not being seen. I'd had a lifetime's practice. In
my own house.

**A beat.**

NARRATOR: This is the last man I ever brought home. His name was Daniel. *(beat)* And I'm
going to tell you the worst of it now, so you don't sit there hoping: he walked out the way
they all walked out. Turned down. He's alive somewhere this minute, not knowing what he
lost, or that he lost it. *(beat)* He was the last because he was the only one who ever
turned it around. The only man who looked *back* at me. Who saw me — when seeing me was the
one thing the whole house was built to prevent. *(beat)* And that. Not a knife, not a drug,
nothing with a name. *That* is what brought it all down. *(very quiet)* My one good man. I
should have left him in the rain.

---

**The hum settles back, very faint. The house comes up around it — old timber settling, a
clock somewhere, unhurried. And under all of it, the layered breathing. The hole in it.
Hold the hole. Let the ear lean toward it and find nothing.**

NARRATOR *(close, unhurried):* It started the week we buried my grandmother. *(beat)* The
house went quiet in a way I didn't have a word for. It was never a loud house — my family
didn't argue, they never had to. They'd settle a thing between them before anyone got as
far as a sentence, the way you don't argue with your own hand about which way to turn a key.

**The hum, under. The hole in it.**

NARRATOR: But that week it went *thin.* Like a chord with a note lifted out of it. I kept
thinking the radiators had been turned down. I kept checking. They hadn't. *(beat)* I was
the only one in the house who couldn't feel what was actually missing. I just felt cold, and
assumed, the way I always assumed, that the cold was something the matter with me.

**A door. Footsteps — not hers; older, certain. A long pause in a doorway. Then the
footsteps move off. A breath let out that has too many breaths in it, layered, just under
hearing — and then only one woman breathing, in a kitchen, somewhere.**

NARRATOR: My mother stood in doorways that week. She'd never done that in her life. She'd go
to a room and then stop at the edge of it like she'd forgotten what rooms were *for.*
*(softer)* I brought her tea. I didn't know what else a person does. She looked at me —

**A pause. The clock.**

NARRATOR: — she looked at me like she was counting, and had come up one short, and was
trying to work out whether I was the one she could send.

**The hum swells, very slightly. Hungry is the wrong word. But the room wants something.
Hold — then:**

NARRATOR: By the Thursday she sent me out. She didn't say *go and find someone.* She never
said it like that. She said, *you're no use to me moping, love, go and be among people.* *(a
small breath)* And I went, grateful, the way a dog is grateful to be given a job. I didn't
know I'd been given one. *(beat)* I never did. Until it was far too late to give it back.

---

**A cut. New acoustic — a café, a warm wash of noise, a coffee machine. This memory comes
fast and flat, almost bored.**

NARRATOR: Let me show you how it works, with one who doesn't matter. So you don't have to
take my word for it later. *(beat)* I don't remember his name. I want that on the record —
that I can stand here and tell you exactly how a man I sat with for two hours folded and
unfolded a sugar packet, and I cannot give you his name, because I never once needed it.

**Café. A man's voice, mid-thirties, lonely-friendly, the kind that's grateful to be talked
to.**

MAN *(SURFACE):* — no, you go ahead, I'm not — honestly I'm just glad of the company, it's
been a — it's been a week.

NARRATOR *(over it, flat):* It's always been a week. Every man I ever brought home had just
had a week. *(beat)* And the only thing I was ever good at is that I'd listen. Not the way
people pretend to. All the way down. Until a man feels, maybe for the first time since he
was small, that there's a person across the table who's gone quiet inside *for him.*

MAN *(SURFACE, lower, opening):* Can I tell you something I haven't — I don't know why I'm —

NARRATOR: And then I'd bring him home. And then —

**The café noise drops away abruptly, replaced by that cold, layered, roomless CHORD — and
the hole in it. This is the first time the listener hears the channel. Make it feel like a
door opening onto a room with no air in it.**

CHORD *(cold, layered, close, no room around it; one voice short):* Adequate. *(beat)* Not
for the budding. For the table. Bring him to the daughter. The daughter will take it from
here.

NARRATOR *(plain):* — and then he stopped being mine. He turned, the way they all turned,
toward my sister, like a plant turning to a window. And I sat in my own family like a coat
somebody had taken off. *(beat)* That one I'd see again. Years later. A bus, a supermarket —
the colour turned down out of him, *whoever you are* to a girl he used to know loud. *(beat)*
And I'd think — *poor man, life does that to people* — and pick up my shopping, and go home
to the family that did it. *(very quiet)* Thirty years. I never once let myself finish the
question.

**The café fades entirely. Rain rises in its place — steady, against glass.**

NARRATOR: That's the shape. Now the one that mattered. The last man I ever brought home to
them. *(beat)* And remember, when I get there — I'm asking you a favour — remember that I
walked into it grateful. That's the part I can't make soft for you. Nobody made me. I went
because being sent was the closest thing to being wanted I had.

---

**RAIN, steady, against glass. A near-empty café-bar, late. A coffee machine sighs and goes
quiet. One chair scrapes. Room tone settles. Hold it — let the place be real before anyone
is.**

NARRATOR *(close):* This is the last one. I didn't know that yet. *(a small breath)* What I
remember isn't his face. It's that he folded his receipt into a tiny square while he talked,
smaller and smaller, like he was trying to make it disappear before he had to ask anyone for
anything. *(beat)* I wasn't supposed to notice things like that. Noticing things like that
was exactly what was wrong with me.

**A glass set down, gentle. The man's voice — DANIEL — close, a little tired, warm.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* You don't have to keep me company, you know. I'm a big believer in
letting people off the hook.

NARRATOR *(over it):* I brought him in with nothing but talk. That part was always mine. I
sat down across from a stranger in a dead bar in the rain, and inside twenty minutes he was
telling me the true things. *(beat)* The rest — the part that comes after the talk — was
never mine to do.

**The acoustic shifts subtly — we are at a table now. Two more presences arrive, settle. A
warmth comes into the air that is almost too much, too soon. A chair. A coat.**

LISS *(SURFACE — warm, easy, bright):* So you're the one who's been keeping her all to
yourself.

DANIEL *(SURFACE — a laugh, a little nervous):* I — sorry, I didn't realize I was keeping
anyone.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless — the gap where the third voice should be):* He apologizes
for taking up space. Good. Easily moved. Bring the daughter into his eyeline. Take him off
the sister. Let him find the right one.

NARRATOR *(over it, dry):* And every time — *every* time — I'd watch them turn. Like that.
The moment I'd warmed a man up, he'd swing toward whoever I'd brought him to, and I'd tell
myself I just wasn't the kind men stay pointed at. *(beat)* I had the cause backwards. But
that took me thirty years and one funeral to work out.

**A small thing — and the listener should clock that no CHORD line precedes it.**

DANIEL *(HIM — interior, warm, slightly wondering):* ...God. The sister. How did I not — she
was right there when we came in. How did I not *see* her?

CHORD *(a small recalculation, cold):* He drifts. Note it. *(flat)* Draw him forward. The
daughter holds his eye; the sister fades back. The sister is always to fade back. That is
what she is *for.*

LISS *(SURFACE — softer now, leaning in):* What brings you here, then? Really.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* Honestly? Killing time. A friend bailed. *(self-deprecating)* Story of
my — you don't need my whole sad —

VALA *(SURFACE — first time she speaks; mild, grandmotherly, almost nothing there):* We
don't mind a sad story. We've heard worse.

CHORD: The mother is right. We have heard worse. *(a flat ripple — the nearest this voice
comes to amusement)* He answers to being needed. So. Need him.

LISS *(SURFACE):* Then stay. Sit with us a while. It's filthy out there, and you'd be doing
us a kindness — it's been a hard week.

**RAIN, harder against the glass.**

DANIEL *(HIM — and this is the seam: let the line above arrive in his own warm voice, a beat
too soon to be his):* ...they've had a hard week. I could — yeah. I could be useful. *(quieter,
to himself)* When's the last time anyone actually needed me to just *sit* with them.

NARRATOR: He said yes to the rain, really. To not being alone in it. *(beat)* And I want to
be clear: I knew exactly what was happening. I'd done it a hundred times. I just couldn't,
for the life of me, work out why this time it felt like watching someone walk into traffic.

CHORD: The daughter has him. *(beat)* Step back. *(and now — the one instruction the listener
already understands has teeth)* Step back.

**A pause. A chair, very slightly, moves back from the table. LISS and DANIEL's voices
continue underneath now — warm, indistinct, a private weather forming without our narrator
in it.**

NARRATOR *(quieter, the dryness gone):* So I stepped back. The way you're meant to. And I sat
in my own family like a coat someone had taken off and forgotten, and I watched him fall for
my sister with a look on his face like something good was finally happening to him. *(beat)*
And I was jealous. *(she catches it — almost laughs at herself)* Jealous. Of course I was.
After everything, *that* was the rot they could never quite cut out of me.

---

**The two warm voices, close and happy, under. Rain. Then a small private sound: paper,
folded, refolded. The receipt. DANIEL, mid-laugh at something Liss has said, fingers working
at it without knowing.**

NARRATOR: While my sister did the thing she was made to do, I watched his hands. He'd
finished the receipt — folded it down past the point paper wants to fold, this hard little
square — and now he didn't know what to do with it, so he turned it over and over, this man
who was, by every account including his own, having the best night of his life. *(beat)*
People who are happy don't do that with their hands. *(beat)* I noticed. Nobody had asked me
to. Nobody ever did.

**A burst of LISS's laughter, bright. DANIEL laughing too — but listen: under his laugh, the
HIM voice, low, not unhappy, just true.**

DANIEL *(HIM):* ...this is going to end. Things like this always — *(he stops himself)* No.
Be here. For once just *be* here.

CHORD: He guards himself. Naturally. *(flat)* The daughter will not rush. We have time.

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I didn't hear that, of course. The cold part. I never could. *(beat)*
But I'm going to tell it to you anyway, all the way through, because somebody should have
heard it, and I'm the only one left who'd want to.

**The clock from the opening, faint, returning under. The thin hum, the hole in it.**

NARRATOR: They didn't say it out loud that night. But I know how my family talks when it's
decided something. There's a — settling. The way the house had gone thin all week, that
Thursday it went *still,* the way a held breath is still. *(beat)* I thought it was about my
grandmother. I thought we were grieving. *(beat)* We were. I just didn't understand that in
my family, grief and hunger are the same weather.

CHORD *(under, cold, certain):* This one. *(beat)* Not the table. *(beat)* This one is for
the line.

NARRATOR: I'm guessing at the words. But I'd bet my life on them. *(a breath)* I nearly did.

---

**Outside now. The café door, a bell. Rain on the street. DANIEL and WREN, walking, the
acoustic open and wet. Liss and Vala gone ahead or behind — for a moment it is just the two
of them, and this is the only stream the listener can fully trust.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE, warmer, looser, the wine in it):* Can I say a weird thing. *(beat)* Your
sister's — your whole family's lovely, they really are, they've been so —

WREN *(SURFACE — and this is the first we hear her *in scene,* younger, lighter, careful):*
But.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* No, not but. *(beat)* It's just — you're the one who talked to me. Like,
*talked.* And then you sort of — went away. And I keep — *(small laugh at himself)* I don't
know. I keep wanting to check you're still there.

**A beat. Rain. And here — crucially — no CHORD line comes before this. Nothing is planted.
It is only him.**

DANIEL *(HIM — quiet, unforced, and the listener must be able to tell this one is real):*
She's got the saddest, kindest face. Why would they keep someone like that at the edge of
everything.

WREN *(SURFACE — and she misreads it, the way she always will):* I'm always still here.
*(beat, dry, deflecting)* I'm the furniture. You'll learn.

NARRATOR *(over, present-day, and it costs her):* He said a true thing to me, in the rain,
and I made a joke, because a true thing said to me had to be a mistake. Someone had got the
wrong girl. *(beat)* I have gone back to that sentence — *I keep wanting to check you're still
there* — more nights than I'll admit. *(very quiet)* I'll never know if it was real. I
couldn't hear the part of him that would've told me. *(beat)* I only ever got the room.

---

**RAIN. Then the warm voices fold back in — Liss returning, laughing, slipping her arm
through Daniel's. The private weather closing over again. WREN, half a step back, where she
belongs.**

LISS *(SURFACE, bright, drawing him on):* Come *on,* you two, you'll catch your deaths —
Daniel, you're staying for tea, I won't hear otherwise —

DANIEL *(SURFACE, swept, happy):* I — okay, yeah, okay —

CHORD: Good. He comes willingly. *(beat)* But mark it: he turned to the sister, twice. He
*looks* at her. *(flat — and for the first time, something the listener should hear as a
threat)* That cannot be. The daughter will hold him closer. The sister will be kept further
back. No man we keep is ever to look at the sister twice.

**A beat. Then, into the gap where the third voice is not —**

CHORD: ...And we are short. *(flat, not grief — a hand reaching for a tool that is not on
the bench)* We are short, and we will not stay short.

**The hole in the chord, loud now in its silence. Then the rain swallows it.**

NARRATOR: That's where I'll stop tonight. Him saying yes a second time — yes to the tea, yes
to the warmth, yes to all of it — and me a step behind, jealous, useless, *noticing.*
*(beat)* They'd already decided what he was for. I didn't have the word for it yet. *(beat)*
All I knew was that he'd looked at me, once, in the rain, like I was a person. *(beat)* And
that being looked at was the one thing, in my whole family, I was never, ever meant to be.

**The two warm voices, going away up the wet street. The thin hum under. The clock.**

NARRATOR: Remember the receipt. The little folded square he made of it. *(beat)* Because by
the end of it, I was the only person left alive who'd ever noticed it at all.

**SNAP to silence. Rain only. Out.**
