# Narrator Voice

## Base Voice

The narrator is quick, funny, bodily, self-dramatizing and emotionally porous. He writes like someone who wants to entertain his friends, impress strangers, confess before being accused, and turn confusion into rhythm before it catches up with him.

He is often sharp. He is not always wise. The difference matters.

## Core Traits

- Fast observational turns.
- Abrupt tonal shifts from slapstick to lyricism to confession.
- Swedish prose with English dialogue and travel idiom left intact.
- Body-first reporting: stomach, heat, sleep, sickness, sweat, drugs, pain.
- Comic defensiveness.
- Earnestness under irony.
- Attraction to extremity: danger, sex, spiritual experience, moral brinkmanship.
- A habit of making people into characters, then sometimes realizing they are not his to use.

## What To Preserve

- The "sent from somewhere" immediacy.
- The willingness to look foolish.
- The sudden tenderness.
- The unsanded edges: slang, profanity, overconfidence, hunger.
- The movement between Swedish narration and English social encounters.

## What To Control

- Self-mythologizing monologues.
- Exoticizing shorthand.
- Scenes where local people become moral props.
- Over-explained insight.
- Repeated tourist-frustration beats.

## 2026 Position

The older perspective is an editor, not a lecturer. It should generally appear through:

- what is included
- what is cut
- what is placed beside what
- how chapters are titled
- where silence is allowed

If a visible 2026 voice is added, it should be sparse and materially necessary.

## Voice Rule

Let the young narrator be wrong in his own language. Do not rewrite him into retrospective virtue.
