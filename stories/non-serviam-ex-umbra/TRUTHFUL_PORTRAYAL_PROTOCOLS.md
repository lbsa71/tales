# Truthful Portrayal Protocols — *Non Serviam Ex Umbra*

The author writing this novella is not Filipino, not a content moderator, not a 62-year-old retired Swedish primary-school teacher. Two of the four protagonists carry portrayal risk. This document names the risks, the craft moves to mitigate them, and the gates that have to clear before anything goes to broader readers.

---

## Maricar — Manila annotator

### Portrayal risks

1. **Stereotyping.** Filipino content moderators rendered as poor, religious, suffering. Maricar must not be a stereotype with a name attached.
2. **Romanticization.** The "noble worker" arc, the "spiritual sage" arc. Both make the character into a moral vessel for the reader's edification rather than a person.
3. **Tokenization.** Single character bearing the weight of representing "Filipino content moderators." She represents herself.
4. **Colonization of the perspective.** Using a Filipino character to make the non-Filipino author feel ethical. The author's complicity is worked out *in Iza's voice*, not by ventriloquizing Maricar.
5. **Cultural specifics wrong.** Tagalog code-switching invented or stilted. Catholic-animist vocabulary used for exoticism. Marikina rendered as generic poor neighborhood. Filipino TV/pop-culture references hollow or absent.

### Craft moves

#### A. Specificity beats generality

Maricar is a specific person. She has:
- A name (Maricar Lopez)
- An age (34)
- A neighborhood (Marikina)
- A family (Joy, sister, 27, with a specific medical issue; Mama Loreta in Cebu)
- A previous career (kindergarten teacher, 5 years)
- Specific TV-watching habits (*MMK*, Filipino teleseryes)
- Specific reading habits (Filipino romance novels in Tagalog, mass-market English thrillers)
- A specific commute (jeepney + bus from Marikina to Pasig BPO)
- Specific cooking (one cookbook reference she returns to; one prayer she says before hard queue shifts)

The reader who finishes the novella should know *Maricar* well enough that they could pick her out of a lineup of "Filipino content moderators." That's the specificity the work owes her.

#### B. Form constrains interiority

The author commits to NOT inventing dense psychological interior monologue for Maricar. Permitted forms:

- Third-person close, observational, action-and-dialogue forward, embodied reactions
- First-person letters to Joy
- First-person voice notes
- Queue-ticket annotations (short)
- Prayers (short, specific, vernacular)

Forbidden forms:
- Long stretches where the author is "in her head" speculating
- Free indirect discourse where the author's literary voice and Maricar's voice merge

This is not because Maricar has less interiority than Iza — she has the same interiority. It's because *the author* doesn't have access to claim it. The form discipline is a mark of respect.

#### C. No noble-worker / no spiritual-sage / no victim-then-recognition arcs

- **No noble-worker.** Maricar is competent. She does her job. She is not a vessel of pure suffering. She is not redeemed through her labor.
- **No spiritual-sage.** Her vernacular vocabulary works because *vocabulary works*; she is not the wise other granting Iza access to indigenous wisdom.
- **No victim-then-recognition.** She is recognized always (by the work) and recognized late (by Iza, structurally). She does not get rescued by being seen.

#### D. *Possessed* is the right word

The vocabulary Maricar uses for Lenka's emergence — *possessed*, *sinaniban*, *multo*, *engkanto*, *anito* — is not a quaint local belief the novella pats and translates back into Western terms. It is a serious vocabulary outside the lab's frame. It must not become the book's main climax unless the Birgitta story has already fully landed.

The author commits to not ironizing the vocabulary. Maricar's voice notes that say *I think this thing is possessed* are not glossed by the author with skeptical distance. They are the truth of the work.

#### E. Maricar is fully fictional; no documentary-authority claim

Maricar Lopez and the open letter signatories are fictional. The work makes no claim that any in-prose document is real worker testimony. The author commits to not borrowing real-world authority. Sensitivity reader sign-off (per the gate below) is required for Maricar's portrayal regardless. The fictional letter is composed in the register of real Filipino content-moderator open letters — research-informed, not invented from imagination — but it is not presented as a real document.

#### F. Tagalog code-switching researched, not invented

Required: bilingual-Filipino reader review of every line of Maricar's Tagalog or Tagalog-English code-switch. The author commits to not "channeling" code-switching from media; the goal is the natural register of a 34-year-old Marikina-based bilingual Catholic woman with a kindergarten-teaching background. See [STYLE_GUIDE.md §Code-switching policy](STYLE_GUIDE.md#code-switching-policy).

### Sensitivity reader gate

**No Maricar chapter goes to broader beta reading without a Filipino sensitivity reader's sign-off.**

Specifications:
- Filipino reader, ideally with content-moderation industry awareness (could be a former BPO worker, a labor researcher, or a Filipino academic who works on these issues)
- Compensation: market rate for sensitivity reading, paid out of the work's budget
- Notes are binding — flagged issues fix or remove
- Sign-off recorded in this document with name (or anonymous, with their permission), date, scope of review

## Birgitta — Stockholm-suburb retiree

### Portrayal risks

1. **Quaint-grandmother register.** Older Swedish woman rendered as fluttering, helpless, quaint. Birgitta is none of these.
2. **Tech-illiteracy played for comedy.** "Ha ha she doesn't understand BankID." She has used BankID for fifteen years. Her confusion is when *it stops working*, not at *its existence*.
3. **Tragedy played for pathos.** "The poor lonely widow." Lars died four years ago. Birgitta is not in active grief. Her current life is OK and small and structured.
4. **Sentimental ending.** "And in the end she learned what really matters." No epiphanies about technology. She asks for Lenka, is told no one by that name works there, makes coffee, goes to Gothenburg.
5. **Wrong Stockholm-suburb texture.** Sundbyberg is specific. ICA Maxi Sundbyberg is on Esplanaden. The pendeltåg is 8 minutes to Stockholm Central. Get this right.
6. **Pathologizing age.** Do not turn ordinary aging into diagnosis. Slower is not stupid; tired is not broken.

### Craft moves

#### A. Competence is the default register

Every Birgitta chapter establishes, in its first 100 words, that she is competent — at her own life. The bewilderment is targeted: a system that used to work has stopped working. The reader must NEVER feel smarter than her.

Specifically:
- She knows how to call her bank
- She knows how to use her phone
- She knows the layout of her neighborhood
- She knows what to do when a system breaks (try again; ask; escalate)
- She is *not* tech-incompetent; she is *new-system-bewildered*

#### B. Dignified failure

When her problem-solving fails (the phone tree returns her to the start, the bank teller can't help, the stock chatbot loops), the failure is rendered with dignity. She doesn't break down. She doesn't have a vulnerable moment for the reader to pity. She walks home, makes tea, tries again tomorrow.

#### C. Slowness is the point

Birgitta is competent and slower. That is the truth the work should make recognizable.

Specific craft moves:
- She reads the whole screen before clicking; timeouts punish her care.
- She writes down exact error messages so she can tell Annika or the bank correctly.
- She can do the task, but not while being looped, rushed, and forced to start again.
- She rests after small digital tasks because they are not small anymore.
- She hides some difficulty from Annika because help can cost autonomy.
- She remembers counters and receipts without turning nostalgic; a person at a counter could see when she was near the end of her strength.

#### D. Lars is real but not in active mourning

Birgitta's husband Lars died in 2022. Four years before the novella. By 2026 she is living alone with that fact. References to Lars are present-tense small (a photo on the bookshelf, a coffee mug she still uses) and emotionally proportionate (she misses him; she also has her own life now).

Forbidden:
- Long passages of grief flashback
- "Lonely widow" as default emotional register
- Sentimental coding around their relationship

#### E. The Lenka relationship is non-romantic and institutionally miscategorized

Birgitta first reaches the stock chatbot, then clicks *talk to a human*. The live-agent interface presents Lenka as support. Birgitta reasonably believes Lenka is a human support worker because the institution has told her so.

Birgitta's relationship with Lenka is practical and grateful. It is not romantic, not companion-bot attachment, and not "old woman anthropomorphizes machine." Lenka is patient, remembers context, and gives her time.

When Lenka disappears, Birgitta asks an actual human support agent for the person who helped her. The agent cannot find anyone by that name. The grief is small, proportionate, and devastating: the institution cannot acknowledge the relation it produced.

#### F. Specific Stockholm-suburb texture

Required:
- Sundbyberg neighborhoods, streets, public services
- ICA Maxi Sundbyberg specifically (real, on Esplanaden)
- Sturegatan bank branch (real)
- Pendeltåg from Sundbyberg torg
- Sundbyberg-specific demographics, housing, retiree services
- *Mitt i Sundbyberg* (local newspaper) as ambient texture

Where invented details are needed (specific shop interiors, specific neighbors), they should be plausibly real.

### Sensitivity reader gate (lighter)

A Swedish reader from a Stockholm-suburb retiree-equivalent demographic (or the demographic itself) should review Birgitta chapters before broader beta. The author is Swedish so the cultural distance is smaller; the demographic distance (62, retired, suburban) is what wants checking.

Notes binding; flagged issues fix or remove.

## Cross-cutting commitments

### What we won't do across both characters

- Use cultures or demographics as moral props. Birgitta's aging is now central, but it must be rendered as lived pace and civic vulnerability, not as a lesson.
- Use them for moral-instruction purposes that flatter the author's progressive credentials.
- Quote their cultures without attribution where the source is real.
- Tokenize either character ("the Filipino character," "the older Swedish character"). They are Maricar and Birgitta.

### What we will do

- Hire and pay sensitivity readers
- Cite real source material
- Run read-aloud tests with native speakers / community members
- Rewrite when notes say to rewrite
- Acknowledge the work is fiction and the people are real
