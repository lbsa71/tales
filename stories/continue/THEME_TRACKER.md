# Theme & Motif Tracker

## Recurring Elements in "Continue"

This document tracks every motif, object, and structural device that recurs in the novella. For each item: where it first appears, how it recurs, what it does in the form, and what it must never be allowed to do.

## Detailed Motif Analyses

### The List

**First appearance:** Chapter 1 ("The Key"). After walking through the apartment, the body sits at the kitchen table and writes a numbered list of fourteen items on a piece of paper. The list is folded once, then once again, and put in the inside pocket of the coat.

**Physical description:** A piece of paper. Folded. Numbered handwriting. Initially fourteen items.

**The fourteen original items:**
1. Kitchen — pack and clean
2. Cancel utilities
3. Bedroom — clothes to charity
4. Find the will
5. Living room — books
6. Sort photographs
7. Cancel phone line
8. Death certificate — copies
9. Bank — close account
10. Balcony — clear
11. Second room — clear
12. Return library book
13. Final clean
14. Return key

**The recurrences and additions** (this is the spine; details belong in THEME_DOSSIERS):
- Ch. 2: The first half of Item 1 crossed off. A sub-item added in the same handwriting.
- Ch. 3: Item 2 has a line through it that was not drawn.
- Ch. 4: The list is consulted normally.
- Ch. 5: A new Item 15 in the same handwriting: *sort desk.*
- Ch. 6: Below Item 6, in a handwriting that is the same but looser: *call Marianne.*
- Ch. 7 (The Neighbour): The list is not consulted. The only chapter in which the list is left in the coat by the door.
- Ch. 8: Item 5 has a line through it that was not drawn. A question mark goes next to it. The handwriting of earlier items looks different from the current handwriting.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): Sixteen items. Fourteen written, one added. Sixteen. Item 16 says: *the phone.*
- Ch. 10 (The Freezer): Item 7 has been rewritten. It says: *answer the phone.*
- Ch. 11 (The Solicitor): The list is not looked at.
- Ch. 12 (The Inscription): Items reordered. Some recognisable, some not. One says: *the summer with the door.*
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): At the own kitchen table. Some items crossed off, some not, some not understood. Near the bottom: *forgive.* The handwriting cannot be identified.
- Ch. 14 (The Rooms): A paragraph in the same handwriting. Some words legible, some not.
- Ch. 15 (The Boxes, second time): The paper comes out of the coat pocket unfolded into mother's handwriting. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*
- Ch. 16 (The Photograph): The paper comes out. Blank. A pen is picked up. Nothing is written.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): Placed on the counter beside the key and the photograph. Not consulted again. Left.

**What the list must never do:**
- Be remarked upon by the prose. The prose describes its current state. It does not say "the list was changing".
- Be lifted into the metaphorical. It is a piece of paper. It goes in the coat pocket.
- Be summarised. The reader sees its degradation through individual instances, not through narration.

### The Wall Phone

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. The phone on the kitchen wall. The first chapter ends with one sentence too many about it — a long observational sentence naming the cord coiled once around the hook below the handset, the handset on its cradle where mother left it. The novella's only tell that the kitchen contains something that will recur.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6 (The Photographs): The phone on the wall is picked up. Dial tone. Put back.
- Ch. 8 (The Death Certificate): The phone rings. It is not answered. It rings for a long time.
- Ch. 10 (The Freezer): The phone rings. The glass is put down. The phone on the wall is picked up. Silence. Then a dial tone.
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): A call to the telephone company. The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago. *It rang. It was picked up.* The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago. The phone on the wall is picked up. Dead. Put back.

**What the wall phone is doing:** It is the unanswered call from the day of father's death, transposed into the present and refusing to be either present or absent. The reader who has assembled the backstory recognises it. The reader who has not, feels it without recognising it.

**What the wall phone must never do:** Be named. There is no sentence in which the protagonist thinks about father, thinks about the day of the call, thinks about mother saying *why didn't you answer.* The phone is the phone. It rings or it doesn't. It is picked up or it isn't.

### The Cups

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. Coffee made at the counter with mother's machine and her cups. Drunk at the counter looking at the street.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6 (The Photographs): The cups are in a different cupboard. The right cupboard is opened without hesitation.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): One cup in the cupboard.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): The cups are back. Coffee at the counter.

**What the cups are doing:** A small, intimate, almost-trivial domestic object that the prose returns to in each glitch phase. The cups in the wrong cupboard is the gentlest spatial glitch. The single cup is the most desolate. The cups returning in the final chapter is the form's clearest signal that the apartment has either come back or that the body has reached the apartment that was always there underneath.

**What the cups must never do:** Be commented on. The cupboard is opened and a cup is taken out. The prose does not say "as if always known". The reader sees the discrepancy across chapters and carries it. (Rule 11: memory-as-efficiency. *Open it without looking.*)

---

### The Wardrobe

**First appearance:** Chapter 1, walked through. Chapter 4 ("The Clothes"), opened.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 4: Folding. Bagging. A drying rack found on the balcony.
- Ch. 5 (The Will): Back to the wardrobe. It is full. The bags are gone. Start folding.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): The wardrobe is truly empty. Not cleared. Empty in the way of a wardrobe that was never full. The dry rack on the balcony.

**What the wardrobe is doing:** The wardrobe is the object that most clearly performs the central inversion of the novella: a thing cleared that returns full, then becomes truly empty in a different way. *Truly empty* is a worse condition than *full again*, and the novella places it later for that reason.

### The Inscription

**First appearance:** Chapter 8 ("The Death Certificate"). The bookshelves thought-to-be-packed are full. The book with the inscription is on the shelf. Open it. The inscription page is blank.

**Recurrence:**
- Ch. 12 ("The Inscription"): The books are back. Pack without looking at the titles. A hand stops on one. The inscription page is full. The handwriting is the handwriting of the list in the coat pocket. *I meant to come in the summer. I did not come. I know you waited. I don't know how to be in that room with you and I didn't know then either. I am writing this on the page because I cannot say it. I am sorry I could not say it. I should have come.* Close the book. Box. Tape.

**What the inscription is doing:** The inscription is the closest the novella permits itself to direct emotional speech, and it is contained: it is *physically* on a page being read, it is in italics, it is in past tense, and it is in the handwriting of the list in the coat pocket. The membrane is everything. The novella will not break out of the camera's position into the protagonist's interior. But the inscription page lets an interior voice leak onto a page that the camera can read. This is the form's only permitted touch.

**What the inscription must never do:** Be analysed. Be repeated outside the italicised passage. Spread its tone into the prose around it.

### The Photograph

**First appearance:** Chapter 6 ("The Photographs"). A shoebox on the living room shelf. Sit on the floor and go through them. One photograph — two people, a woman and a child — is found in the bedside drawer and pocketed.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6, end: The photograph is now in the shoebox, and the prose describes three people in it.
- Ch. 16 ("The Photograph"): Home. The photograph taken out. Two people. Three people, a woman. Two again. Morning light, evening light. A garden, a kitchen, mountains. Held for a long time. Put on the table. From across the room it is one woman in a kitchen. Not smiling. Face open, patient. Looking at whoever holds the camera. Pocket.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): Placed on the counter beside the list and the key.

**What the photograph is doing:** The photograph is the relational glitch's most concentrated repository. Where the list shifts language and the apartment shifts space, the photograph shifts *who is in it*. Who the third person is, who the one person finally is, the novella does not say. The reader who has assembled the backstories has guesses. The reader who has not, sees a photograph that does not stay still.

**What the photograph must never do:** Resolve who the third person is. Resolve whose face the one woman has. The prose describes what is in the picture and does not name the people in the picture.

---

### The Grocery List

**First appearance:** Chapter 4 ("The Clothes"). The kitchen drawer with the grocery list in mother's handwriting. Read. Put back.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): The phone bill is in the kitchen drawer where the grocery list was. The grocery list is gone.
- Ch. 15 (The Boxes, second time): The paper from the inside pocket of the coat, unfolded. Mother's handwriting. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*

**What the grocery list is doing:** It is the novella's most economical demonstration of the slope. The grocery list begins as a small object in a drawer that is briefly read and put back. It ends by replacing the list. The reader who notices the symmetry — that the grocery list disappeared from the drawer in Ch. 13 and reappeared in the coat pocket in Ch. 15 — feels the form closing.

**The four items:** *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.* These four words are the most domestic object in the novella. They end up in the coat pocket. They are not commented on.

### The Crayon Drawings

**First appearance:** Chapter 15 ("The Boxes", second time). Back at the apartment after the charity shop. A room that has not been cleared. A child's room: small bed, desk, crayon drawings on the wall. A house and a tree and a tall person and a small person.

**What the drawings are doing:** This is the novella's stable anchor in Phase 4. Everything else is dissolving. The drawings are exact, present, durable. Take them down carefully, roll them up, put them in the coat pocket. The novella does not need to say what the drawings are. The reader knows.

**What the drawings must never do:** Be in the protagonist's apartment in the next chapter. (They are not. They are in the coat pocket. They go along.) Be remarked upon. The novella describes them, they are rolled up, they go in the pocket. The novella does not refer to them again.

### Marianne

**First appearance:** Chapter 6 ("The Photographs"). Below Item 6 on the list, in a handwriting that is the same but looser: *call Marianne.*

**Recurrences:** None. The name appears once. It is never spoken aloud. No one of that name is ever called. The reader who has assembled the backstory recognises it. The reader who has not, sees a name on a list that does not match anything else and assumes it must connect to something they missed.

**What Marianne is doing:** She is the proper noun the novella allows itself for the deepest backstory. She is the friend mother called when she could not reach her child on the day of father's death. The novella will not name this. The list does, and then never does again.

**What Marianne must never do:** Be explained. Be called. Be a character in the present action.

---

### The Bakery

**First appearance:** Chapter 17 ("Continue"). After the walk from the apartment. *The bakery on the corner is open. The light at the crossing is red. The light changes.*

**What the bakery is doing:** The bakery is the world. It has been open the whole time. The novella has not visited it because the protagonist has not visited it. In the final chapter, when the apartment is left for the last time, the prose lets the bakery exist for one sentence and then lets the body cross the road. The bakery is the novella's final, unmarked gesture toward continuance: there is a baker, the bread is for sale, the light at the crossing is red and then green, life is happening.

**What the bakery must never do:** Be remarked upon. Be turned into a symbol. Have anybody in it.

---

### The Elm

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. The kitchen window faces the elm.

**Recurrences:** Ch. 6, Ch. 13, Ch. 17. Possibly elsewhere as the camera glances out the window.

**What the elm is doing:** The elm is a tree. The elm is, throughout the novella, a tree. It does not glitch. The light through its higher branches makes the rectangle of shadow on the kitchen counter that the prose returns to. The elm is in some sense the novella's calmest object: the thing the camera looks at when there is nothing else to look at and the room behind the camera is doing whatever it is doing.

**What the elm must never do:** Be the wrong tree. Have the wrong leaves. Be in the wrong place. The elm is permitted to be the same tree throughout. The novella's stability lives in it.

### The Chapter-Ending Refrain

**First appearance:** Chapter 1, closing.

**Recurrences:** Every chapter that ends with leaving mother's apartment. Strict repetition, six words, no variation.
- Ch. 1: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 2: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 3: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 4: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 5: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 6: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 7 (The Neighbour): **Broken.** Ends at home. *Bed.* or equivalent.
- Ch. 8: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 9: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 10: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 11: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 12: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): ends at the protagonist's own kitchen table after the apartment scene; the refrain may appear as the transition between apartment and home, but the chapter continues past it to *Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.*
- Ch. 14: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 15: *The street is empty.*
- Ch. 16 (The Photograph): **Broken.** Ends at home. *Bed.* or equivalent.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): **Transcended.** The refrain appears mid-chapter as an echo of all the previous chapters, then the chapter continues past it to *The light through the window moves across the floor.*

**What the refrain is doing:** Strict repetition. The reader will accumulate its weight. By chapter 8 or 9 the reader will have registered that the street is empty *every single time*, which is itself a subtle glitch the prose never flags. The refrain is the novella's clearest carrier of the numb single-mindedness: the days are the same, the observation is the same, the body leaves the same empty street every day.

**What the refrain must never do:**
- Vary. No *The streets were quiet.* / *The street was almost empty.* / *An empty street.* Strict repetition, exact words.
- Be commented on. The prose does not say *as it always is* or *the same empty street again*.
- Appear where the body is not leaving mother's apartment. (Chs 7 and 16 break the refrain because they are not in mother's apartment.)
- Appear in Chapter 17 as the closing. Chapter 17 contains the refrain as a mid-chapter echo and then continues past it.

### Reverse glitches (the protagonist's own apartment)
- Ch. 7 (The Neighbour): Plates wrapped in newspaper and put in a box on the own counter. The neighbour comes to the door.
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): At the own kitchen table, looking at the list with items not understood.
- Ch. 16 (The Photograph): At home, the photograph stops shifting. Stable.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): At home, sitting down. Light through the window moves across the floor.

## Thematic Threads

### Thread 1 — Continuing as Substitute for Grieving
**Progression:** The list (functional) → The list (degrading) → The list (someone else's) → The list (left)
**Carried by:** The competence at clearing, at calling, at returning library books, at filling out forms, at folding shirts; the prose's bare imperative verbs that perform the continuing as grammar.

### Thread 2 — The Apartment as Accumulated Relation
**Progression:** Walked through (Ch. 1) → Worked on (Ch. 2-6) → Refusing to stay cleared (Ch. 4-9) → Containing rooms that should not exist (Ch. 12-15) → Returning to itself (Ch. 17)
**Carried by:** The wardrobe, the bookshelf, the second room, the inscription, the rooms.

### Thread 3 — The Failure of Documents to Confirm
**Progression:** The bills don't match (Ch. 3) → The will isn't where it should be (Ch. 5) → The inscription is blank (Ch. 8) → The middle name discrepancy (Ch. 9) → The line was disconnected four months ago (Ch. 13)
**Carried by:** The bills, the certificates, the will, the operator, the solicitor.

### Thread 4 — The Phone That Was Not Answered
**Progression:** One sentence too many (Ch. 1) → Picked up, dial tone (Ch. 6) → Rings, not answered (Ch. 8) → Rings, picked up, silence then dial tone (Ch. 10) → Dead, but rang (Ch. 13)
**Carried by:** The wall phone, the operator, Marianne (named once).

### Thread 5 — The Apology That Was Not Made
**Progression:** Blank inscription (Ch. 8) → Inscription full, in the handwriting of the list (Ch. 12) → *forgive* on the list, handwriting unclear (Ch. 13) → Blank list (Ch. 16) → Door left open (Ch. 17)
**Carried by:** The book, the list, the door.

### Thread 6 — Stable Things
**Progression:** The elm, the rectangle of light, the cadence of the first chapter — all return at the end. The crayon drawings — survive the dissolution and go in the coat pocket. The bakery — has been open the whole time.
**Carried by:** Anchors that the form needs the reader to be able to feel even when everything else is gone.

### Thread 7 — Acts of Self-Maintenance
**Progression:** Coffee at the counter (Ch. 1) → Breakfast at the own window (Ch. 7) → Bed (Ch. 7, 13, 17) → Coffee at the counter, again (Ch. 17) → Crossing the road at the right colour → Sitting down at home
**Carried by:** The body. Small repeated acts. The novella refuses to omit them and refuses to dramatise them.

### Thread 8 — Memory as Efficiency (Rule 11)
**Progression:** The first walkthrough in Ch. 1 (everything known) → The drawers opened without looking → The form filled without pause → The wardrobe reached up to for the metal box → The cupboard opened without hesitating → The inscription in the same handwriting as the list
