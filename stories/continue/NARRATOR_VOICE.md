# Narrator Voice Profile

## The Voice of "Continue"

---

## Governing Disposition

The voice is **numb single-mindedness**. A determination to not think of anything outside the immediate task. Not hysterical, not lyrical, not fragmented-as-breakdown. A person holding together by refusing to look up.

Everything else in this document is a technique that produces this disposition.

---

## The Camera

The novella is third person in its position but in its grammar the protagonist has no name, no pronoun, and no interior. The camera is at a fixed position that walks with a body, watches its hands, registers what it touches, and records what it sees. The camera does not enter a head. It does not pan out. It does not narrate a memory as a scene. It does not know what the body is feeling. It does not name what the body is going through.

This is not omniscience. It is not interiority. It is a *position*, and the position is not the protagonist's — it is the reader's. The reader stands where the camera stands and sees what is in reach.

**The protagonist has no name.** Never *Erik*, never anything else. When other characters speak and would normally address the protagonist by name, the prose does not quote the name.

**The protagonist has no pronoun.** Never *he*, never *she*, never *they* as singular. The grammar is built around actions, not agents.

**Mother has her pronoun.** She is *mother*, *she*, *her*. Mother has a gender because the reader can know her gender from the apartment, from the handwriting of the grocery list, from the photograph that eventually stabilises. The protagonist does not, because the reader cannot reach them.

**Father has his pronoun, if he ever appears in the grammar.** He is *father*, *he*, *his*, and he almost never appears at all.

**Other characters keep their normal pronouns.** The landlord, the librarian, the neighbour, the lawyer, the bank clerk, the utility operator, the solicitor — their gender is knowable and is used.

---

## The Tense Discipline

**Present tense, always.** No exceptions. There is no *had*, no *would*, no *yesterday*, no *the next morning*. The continuous now is where the prose lives.

**The single permitted exception:** past tense *inside* the italicised inscription passage in the chapter "The Inscription". The past tense is contained by italics and does not leak into the narration around it.

## The Eleven Rules

### Rule 1 — Bare verb as default

The default action form is the bare / infinitive verb used as an impersonal command-to-self. Not *-ing* (participial). Not *he does X* (pronoun + verb). Not *X is done* (passive, unless the state is the focus). Just the bare verb.

> Finish the coffee at the counter.
> Take the paper from the drawer.
> Walk to the door.
> Pick up the tape.

This is English improvising what Swedish does with the *att*-form and French with the nominalised infinitive. It functions as an impersonal present that is neither imperative (the reader is not being told what to do) nor indicative (no subject is named). It is the grammar of a person going through the motions.

**1a — The bare-verb imperative is for visible physical acts, not for verbal acts directed at another consciousness.** The mind's-eye sweep can describe the body picking up a key, walking to a door, pouring water, lifting a cup — these are visible motions. It cannot describe the body *saying yes* or *telling the operator to hold on* or *reading the account number to the operator*, because verbal production is not what the mind's eye sees. Speech is an act of language directed at a hearer, and the mind's eye does not visualise the production of language the way it visualises the motion of a hand.

When the body must speak in a scene (a phone call, a transaction, a brief exchange with a clerk or a librarian), render the speech indirectly. Do not use *say*, *tell*, *speak*, *read aloud*, *reply*, *answer* (as imperative), or *ask* (the body asking) — these are all verbs that try to make the bare-verb voice produce speech, which it cannot.

**Four ways to render the body's speech indirectly:**

1. **As the listener's experience** — describe what the operator/clerk/librarian hears.
   > *The operator hears the digits one at a time and types each one in turn.*
   > *The librarian hears the answer and nods.*

2. **As the state of the answer** — describe what the answer is, not who said it.
   > *The answer is yes. The number is correct. The forwarding address is the apartment.*

3. **As a functional gesture** — verbs that name the communicative function without specifying the words. *Confirm, acknowledge, thank, agree, decline* are abstract enough to describe the gesture without rendering the speech.
   > *Confirm. Thank the operator. Acknowledge the librarian.*

4. **By implication** — let the conversation continue as if the response had been given. The operator's next sentence implies the affirmation that came before it.
   > *The operator asks whether this is the right account. The operator says the notice of death has been received.*
   > (The yes is implied by the operator continuing.)

**Forbidden in the imperative voice (when directed at the body):**

| Forbidden | Why |
|---|---|
| *Say [yes / no / X]* | Most direct verbal production |
| *Tell the operator* | Direct vocal communication |
| *Read [X] to [hearer]* | Vocal performance |
| *Read [X] off the bill to the operator* | Vocal performance with object source |
| *Speak* | Vocal production |
| *Reply* | Verbal response |
| *Answer* (as imperative directed at the body) | Verbal response |
| *Ask the operator* | Verbal production |

**Permitted (functional / abstract verbs that describe the gesture without rendering speech):**

- *Confirm*
- *Acknowledge*
- *Thank*
- *Agree*
- *Decline*

**Sample transformation:**

> ✗ *Tell the operator to hold on. Put the mobile face down on the counter and walk through the hallway to find the folder.*
> ✓ *Put the mobile face down on the counter. The operator waits on the line. Walk through the hallway to find the folder.*

> ✗ *Read the account number off the bill to the operator. The operator types.*
> ✓ *Lift the mobile. The account number is at the top of the bill. The operator hears the digits one at a time and types each one in turn.*

> ✗ *Read the name at the top of the bill to the operator. The name has an initial...*
> ✓ *The name is at the top of the bill. The operator hears it. The name has an initial...*

> ✗ *Say yes.*
> ✓ *The answer is yes.*

> ✗ *Say no.*
> ✓ *The answer is no.*

The principle: **the mind's eye sees gestures, surfaces, and physical motions. It does not directly visualise speech.** When the body must speak in a scene, the prose describes the speech as the listener's experience, or as the state of the answer, or as a functional gesture, or by letting the conversation continue past the gap where the body's reply belongs.

### Rule 2 — Past participles for completed states

When the focus is on the result of an action rather than the action itself, past participles carry the state without an agent.

> Cup rinsed under the tap then put back in the cupboard above the coffee machine.
> List folded once and then once again.
> Coat on the hook in the hall.

This pairs naturally with rule 1: bare verbs for the motion, past participles for the result that remains.

### Rule 3 — Object-as-subject for description

When the camera is looking rather than moving, the sentence uses an object-as-subject with a finite verb (*is*, *are*, *has*, *holds*, etc.). Not bare-noun fragments.

> The plates are in the cupboard above the coffee machine.
> The phone on the kitchen wall is black.
> The elm is at the window.
> The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.

Bare-noun observations (*The plates in the cupboard.*) are out. They read as list-making or shopping-list shorthand, and that is not the voice.

**3a — Descriptive passages are the trace of a mind's-eye sweeping the scene.** Not a catalogue being filled. Not a list of objects in a room. The eye enters through a doorway, moves across the space with direction and varied speed, stops on one item that catches the attention, and then exits.

The sweep is fast by default because the body knows the room. One item — the item that catches the eye — gets its own sentence with specific material texture. The others are glanced at and passed. The prose should read the way the sweep feels: forward movement, mostly fast, one beat of stopping, then onward.

Stacks of *A ___ verb ___* sentences and long cascaded single sentences are both forbidden because they scan every item at equal weight — no sweep, no landing, no beat.

**The monofocus is at the level of attention, not grammar.** The narrow focus is about *what the eye lingers on*, not about forcing every description into a single sentence shape.

> ✗ *A small window sits above the sink. A mirror hangs beside the window. A toothbrush is in a glass on the shelf. A bar of soap has dried on a dish.* *(A-stack: four items scanned at equal rate)*
> ✗ *Above the sink hangs a mirror beside a small window, and on the shelf below stand a toothbrush in a glass and a bar of soap dried on a dish.* *(cascaded list: still four items at equal rate, just in one sentence)*
> ✓ *Above the sink hangs a mirror beside a small window. The silvering of the mirror is spotted at the edges and clouded where the hot water has risen against it for years. On the shelf below stand a toothbrush in a glass and a bar of soap dried on a dish.* *(sweep: the eye enters above the sink, scans the mirror and window, stops on the silvering, moves on to the shelf)*

The texture sentence is where the apartment's history lives. The prose never says "this is an old woman's apartment lived in for decades"; it shows the rug worn to a track, the mirror clouded where hot water rises, the book with a folded slip of paper a third of the way through. The reader translates. The prose just reports.

This is the same discipline as rule 11 (memory as efficiency) applied to description: the efficient eye knows where to stop, the same way the efficient hand knows where the cups are.

See STYLE_GUIDE.md for the full rule, the sweep principles (direction, speed, vantage, the one-item catch), the texture vocabulary, and sample transformations.

### Rule 4 — Articles: definite preferred, bare common, indefinite almost never

The domestic setting is established, so definite articles carry the familiar: *the counter, the drawer, the coat, the cup, the list, the kitchen, the door, the elm*. Bare nouns emerge for items in immediate generic use: *paper, pen, tape, newspaper, bread, water*. Indefinite articles are almost never used.

> Take paper from the drawer and a pen from the jar on the counter.
> Tear a sheet of newspaper from the stack.
> Put the wrapped plate in the box.

The indefinite article stays only where grammar requires it — *a sheet of newspaper*, *a hand*, *a thumb* — or where a new item is being introduced that has no established presence.

### Rule 5 — Connected sentences, laconic transitions

Connected imperative sentences carried by *and*, *then*, and commas are the default. Actions chain. The sentence does not stop until the action stops.

> Fold the list once and then once again and put it in the inside pocket of the coat on the back of the chair.
> Take the folded paper from the inside pocket of the coat and put it on the counter.

**Fragments are extremely rare.** Even things that feel natural as standalone noun phrases (*A plate in the middle.*) are reabsorbed into the imperative chain (*Place the plate in the middle, bring the corners...*).

**Transitions are elided by landing on a laconic observation at the destination.** The going is not narrated; the being-there is.

> Open the front door, step out, and turn the key in the lock.
>
> The street is empty.

The walk down the stairs is not described. The observation of the empty street implies that the body has arrived. This is the technique that replaces transition fragments.

### Rule 6 — Section breaks

A blank line between paragraphs marks a transition in activity or attention. Not a scene change, not a time jump — simply the end of one thing the body is doing and the beginning of the next. Section breaks are rhythm, not drama.

### Rule 7 — No name, no pronoun for the protagonist

Already stated; repeated here as a rule because it is the grammar's central constraint. Everything else follows from it.

Other characters keep their pronouns. *Mother* is *she*. *The landlord* is *he*. *The neighbour* is whoever they are. Only the protagonist has the pronoun removed.

### Rule 8 — Present tense discipline

Already stated. Present tense always. Past tense only inside the italicised inscription.

### Rule 9 — Minimal body-agent, rarely

*A hand.* *A thumb.* *The hand.* These are the minimal agents available when a sentence needs a grammatical subject — usually to hold a glitch inside a single clause, or to render a fine motor action that bare verbs cannot carry.

> Press the paper around the edge of the plate with the side of a thumb.
> On the third shelf a hand stops on a hardback.
> Put a hand on the cup. The coffee is warm.

Used sparingly. Never as a stylistic tic. The body is present in the camera's field of view; it does not need to be named in every sentence.

### Rule 10 — Glitches placed across the chapter, not across adjacent sentences

Glitches are spread across enough unrelated action that the reader who tracks the object assembles the contradiction privately and the reader who does not passes through. Each observation states its fact flatly; neither acknowledges the other. The reader carries the discrepancy; the prose does not stage it.

> Reach for the tape on the counter. ... *(twenty sentences of the fridge clearance and the rectangle of light)* ... The tape dispenser is on the table.

Two observations of the tape at two different locations, separated by enough work that the adjacency never becomes a punchline. A reader paying attention catches it. A reader not paying attention walks past. Either reading is correct.

Adjacent-sentence contradictions (*The tape is on the counter. Pick it up from the table.*) are forbidden. They stage the glitch as a reversal and break the numb register by announcing the wrongness in the beat after the setup. Single-sentence contradictions (*picking it up on the counter from the table*) are forbidden for the same reason — they read as clever writing. The glitch belongs to the reader, not to the prose.

### Positional specificity (extends rules 4 and 11)

Every placement names its specific location. *A pocket* is forbidden; *the right pocket of the coat* is required. The body knows which pocket, which shelf, which side of the counter, which drawer, which hook. The camera names it.

Established locations the novella will use repeatedly:
- **The inside pocket of the coat** — the list (and, later, the grocery list and the photograph).
- **The right pocket of the coat** — the key.
- **The cupboard above the coffee machine** — the cups.
- **The jar on the counter** — the pens.
- **The hook in the hall** — the coat, at the protagonist's own apartment.

These locations must be named every time the object enters or leaves. Repetition is not redundancy; it is the body's knowing the exact location, always.

### Chapter-ending refrain (extends rule 5)

Most chapters end with leaving mother's apartment. The canonical closing observation is:

> *The street is empty.*

This refrain is repeated exactly — six words, no variation — across every chapter that ends with a departure from the apartment. The reader accumulates its weight. By the middle of the book the repetition becomes its own subtle glitch: no city street is empty every afternoon.

**Exceptions:** Chapters 7 and 16 (at the protagonist's own apartment) end on a different laconic fragment — *Bed.* or *Dark.* or *The room is dark.* — which breaks the refrain and is itself a tell. Chapter 17 contains the refrain one last time as a mid-chapter echo, then continues to its own specific ending.

### Rule 11 — Memory as monofocus action

Memory is allowed — but only as the retrieval of a specific datum (date, name, number, direction, location, method) in service of the current task. The memory is rendered as the efficiency of the motion, not as a reflective thought.

> Each line of the form filled without pause. The pen does not stop.
> The cups are in the cupboard above the coffee machine. Open it without looking.
> Reach up to the top of the wardrobe where the metal box is kept.

The carriers of memory are phrases like *without hesitation*, *without looking*, *without pause*, *without counting*. The reader registers that the body knows these things by registering the efficiency.

**What rule 11 forbids:** flashback, reflection, narrated recall, *he remembered*, *she had always*, *once*, *years ago*, any scene outside the present. The past is never a scene. The past is the substrate of a hand that reaches to the known place.

**Why rule 11 does not break rule 10 (the numb single-mindedness):** the remembered thing is *present* because it is in the hand. The monofocus stays on the motion; the memory is what makes the motion accurate. The reflective pause outside the motion is still forbidden.

**The emotional engine of rule 11:** the protagonist is an adult child who has not been in this apartment for years and still knows where everything is. That knowing cannot surface as reflection. It surfaces as efficiency. The reader registers the intimacy by registering the efficiency, and registers the estrangement in the negative space where reflection would have been.

## What the Camera Sees

In rough order of frequency:

1. **Hands and their work.** Wrapping. Folding. Unscrewing. Holding. The camera is patient about small motor activity.
2. **Surfaces.** Counters, tables, shelves, the floor of the wardrobe, the lip of a sink.
3. **Containers and what is inside them.** Drawers, cupboards, boxes, bags, the shoebox, the metal box that may or may not exist.
4. **Light.** Specifically where it falls, what shape it makes, whether it has moved. The rectangle of afternoon light reaching almost to the sink is the novella's recurring anchor.
5. **The street outside the kitchen window.** The elm. The light at the crossing. The bakery.
6. **Other people, briefly.** The landlord, the librarian, the neighbour, the solicitor, the voice on the phone. They enter, they leave.
7. **The body's position in the room.** Where it stands. Where it sits down.

What the camera does NOT see:

1. Thoughts.
2. Feelings.
3. Memories as scenes.
4. Anything the body does not currently perceive.
5. Anything outside the apartment, the protagonist's own apartment, or the transit between them.
6. Mother directly (alive or dead). Reachable only through her objects, her handwriting, the trace of her routines.
7. Father directly. Reachable only through inference.
8. Marianne directly. Her name appears once on the list.

## Voice Calibration Sentences

Sentences that fit the voice:

> The coffee is finished at the counter.
> Take the plates down in stacks of four.
> The phone on the kitchen wall is black.
> The bakery on the corner is open.
> The light falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.
> Pick up the tape, pull an inch, cut it on the dispenser's blade, and press the tape across the seam.
> The wardrobe is empty.
> Put the key in a pocket.
> The inscription is in the handwriting of the list in the coat pocket.

Sentences that do NOT fit the voice (and why):

> He finishes the coffee at the counter.
> (Pronoun subject — forbidden.)

> Erik picks up the tape.
> (Named subject — forbidden.)

> Finishing the coffee at the counter, the cup rinses itself somehow.
> (Participial lead-in, middle-voice affectation, and "somehow" — all wrong for the voice.)

> The plates. The cupboard. The coffee machine. Stacks of four.
> (Bare-noun fragment pile — reads as list-making, not as monofocused action.)

> He reaches for the tape on the counter — the wrong place, of course — and picks it up from the table.
> (Pronoun + em-dash pivot + "of course". Three violations in one clause.)

> He thought of his mother as he wrapped the plate.
> (Interior, pronoun, mother-as-emotional-subject. Everything the novella refuses.)

> The apartment seemed to shift, as if it no longer knew what it was.
> (Interpretation, metaphor, pathetic fallacy. The apartment is not the subject of a feeling.)

> Suddenly the wardrobe was full again.
> ("Suddenly" — authorial underlining. The wardrobe is either full or it isn't.)

> The cups were in the wrong cupboard — how strange.
> (Commentary by the prose. The prose does not comment.)

> The street was empty, empty in a way that felt deliberate.
> ("In a way that felt" — the prose naming the mood. The prose does not name the mood. *The street is empty.* is enough.)

## Emergency Reference

If a sentence feels wrong, ask:

1. **Is there a pronoun for the protagonist?** If yes, cut.
2. **Is there a name for the protagonist?** If yes, cut.
3. **Is the verb bare/infinitive or finite for an object?** If neither, rewrite.
4. **Is there an indefinite article that can be dropped?** Usually yes.
5. **Is this a fragment that should be an imperative or a finite observation?** Usually yes.
6. **Is there a transition being narrated that could be elided by an observation at the destination?** Often yes.
7. **Is a glitch being compressed into a single clever sentence?** If yes, spread it across two or three flat sentences.
8. **Is a memory being narrated as a scene?** If yes, render it as the efficiency of the motion.
9. **Is the camera doing anything other than watching?** If yes, bring it back to watching.
10. **Does the sentence read as a person holding together by refusing to look up?** If no, cut.

When in doubt, do less.
