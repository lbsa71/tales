# Continue

## Premise
A man clears his dead mother's apartment before the lease expires. The tasks multiply. The apartment shifts. The prose never flinches.

## Protagonist
Erik. Mid-forties. Methodical, private. Desk job. Lives alone. Has not spoken to his mother in the final years.

## The Guilt
Erik's father died several years ago. On that day Erik had his phone off for a business meeting. His mother tried to reach him, couldn't, called her friend Marianne instead. Marianne came and was there for the last hours. When Erik called back, his mother said: why didn't you answer, I needed you, you missed his last breath, I had to call Marianne.

He absorbed it. He also resented it. They never spoke about either. They drifted. The gaps between calls grew. Eventually his phone was off not for meetings but because he left it off. She stopped trying.

None of this is stated in the novella. It surfaces in fragments — a name, an inscription, a phone that rings. The reader assembles it or doesn't.

## The List
After walking through the apartment, Erik writes a numbered list on a piece of paper:

1. Kitchen — pack and clean
2. Cancel utilities
3. Bedroom — clothes to charity
4. Find the will
5. Living room — books
6. Sort photographs
7. Cancel phone line
8. Death certificate — copies
9. Bank — close account
10. Balcony — clear
11. Second room — clear
12. Return library book
13. Final clean
14. Return key

He keeps it in his coat pocket. He checks things off. He adds items. The list degrades in parallel with everything else.

## The Glitch Escalation

1. Gestural. A hand reaching the wrong way. A door that should have been locked.
2. Spatial and procedural. Layout drifts. Completed tasks undo. The list shows inconsistencies. The glitches cross into his own apartment.
3. Relational and temporal. Facts about the mother, the history, the documents shift. The list gains entries he didn't write.
4. Syntactic. Sentences stop cohering. Objects change identity mid-action. The list is no longer a list.
5. Clarity. The prose goes clean. He leaves.

## Chapters

### The Key

Erik picks up the key from the landlord. Walks through the apartment room by room. The prose maps everything precisely: kitchen with window facing the elm, hallway, living room with bookshelf, bedroom, second room used for storage, bathroom, balcony with dead plants and a chair. He opens cupboards and closes them. Makes coffee with her machine and her cups. Drinks it at the counter looking at the street.

Sits at the kitchen table and writes the list. Folds it. Coat pocket.

Leaves. Locks the door. The phone on the kitchen wall gets one sentence too many.

**Glitches:** None.

---

### The Boxes

Buys boxes, tape, newspaper. Starts wrapping plates. He's good at this — methodical, efficient. Three boxes filled, labelled, stacked by the front door. About to start on the glasses when he realises the fridge needs emptying first — food has gone off, needs bin bags, has to go out. The fridge takes the rest of the afternoon. The glasses stay in the cupboard.

He crosses off the first half of Item 1 and adds a sub-item.

**Glitches:** The tape dispenser. He reaches for it on the counter, picks it up from the table. Just a hand moving wrong.

---

### The Utility Company

Calls the utility company. On hold twenty minutes. They need the account number. He doesn't have it. Searches drawers, shelves, stacks of paper. Finds old letters, receipts, a warranty for a radio. Finds a library book, months overdue — puts it by the door.

Finds the bills in a folder in the bedroom. But the account number doesn't match. The name has an extra initial. They'll look into it.

He's packed nothing. Takes out the list. Item 2 has a line through it. He didn't cross it off.

Returns the library book on the way home. The librarian waives the fine.

**Glitches:** The name on the bill. The bedroom being through the kitchen, which doesn't match the first chapter's layout. The list: Item 2 crossed off in his handwriting when he didn't do it.

---

### The Clothes

The wardrobe. He folds. The prose slows — fabric has weight, texture. A dress he thinks he remembers. Shoes shaped to her feet. He fills bags.

One charity shop needs hangers, another only takes clean clothes. He decides to wash everything. Loads the machine, sits in the kitchen waiting. Opens the drawer with the grocery list in her handwriting. Reads it. Puts it back.

Hangs things on a drying rack he finds on the balcony, folded behind the chair.

**Glitches:** He walks into the apartment without unlocking the door. The drying rack — the balcony had plants and a chair, no rack. The three boxes stacked by the front door. He moved them to his car.

---

### The Will

The lawyer says the will should be in the apartment. Erik knows where — a metal box, top of the wardrobe. He cleared the wardrobe. No box.

Looks everywhere. The second room has a desk in it now. He doesn't remember a desk, but it feels like it belongs. Opens the drawer. Pencil, blank notebook, no will.

Calls the lawyer. Closed. Tries the utility company. Still looking into it.

Goes back to the wardrobe. It's full. The bags are gone.

He starts folding.

**Glitches:** The desk. The wardrobe refilling.
**The list:** A new Item 15 in his handwriting: sort desk.

---

### The Photographs

A shoebox on the living room shelf. He sits on the floor and goes through them. His mother young. Him as a child. People he doesn't recognise. A holiday with mountains. A birthday. A garden he's never seen.

Keep pile grows. Discard pile stays empty. Puts them all back.

Makes coffee. The cups are in a different cupboard. Opens the right one without hesitating.

Picks up the phone on the wall. Dial tone. Puts it back.

**Glitches:** The cups. One photograph — him and his mother, found in the bedside drawer and pocketed — is now in the shoebox, and the prose describes three people in it.
**The list:** Below Item 6, in handwriting that is his but looser: call Marianne.

---

### The Neighbour

Rain. He stays in his own apartment. His kitchen, his window, his street. Makes breakfast. Reads. Stands at the window.

In the afternoon he takes plates from his kitchen cupboard and wraps them in newspaper and puts them in a box. He works for a while, maybe ten minutes, standing at his own counter, wrapping his own plates. Then he makes coffee and drinks it at the window. The box sits on the counter, half full.

The prose does not remark on this.

Later, the doorbell. A neighbour from downstairs. They've never spoken. The neighbour asks if he's all right — they've heard him moving around at night. Erik says he's fine. He sleeps well. The neighbour nods.

He goes back to the window. A detail on the street doesn't match.

Bed. The box of plates is on the counter. The prose doesn't mention it again.

**Glitches:** He packs his own plates. The street detail. The neighbour. The box the prose abandons.
**The list:** Stays in his coat by the door. The only chapter where the list is not consulted.

---

### The Death Certificate

Civil registry office. Form. Her date of birth, her full name. He writes them without hesitating.

Stops at the apartment. The bookshelves are full. He thinks he packed them. One is the book with the inscription. Opens it. The inscription page is blank.

Starts packing.

The phone rings. He doesn't answer. It rings for a long time.

**Glitches:** The bookshelves full. The blank inscription. The phone. Her date of birth — never given before, will appear later, differently.
**The list:** Item 5 has a line through it. He puts a question mark next to it. The handwriting of earlier items looks different from his current handwriting.

---

### The Bank

They need the will. He doesn't have it. They need proof of next of kin. His mother's name on his birth certificate has a different middle name than the death certificate. Or the same.

They need the original will or a letter from the solicitor. He calls from the lobby. The solicitor asks which property. Gives the address. The solicitor pauses.

At the apartment: the wardrobe is truly empty. Not cleared — more like it was never full. The drying rack on the balcony, empty and dry. Has been dry for a long time.

One cup in the cupboard.

**Glitches:** The names not matching. The solicitor's pause. The wardrobe empty in a way that feels different. The dry rack.
**The list:** Sixteen items. He wrote fourteen, added one. Sixteen. Item 16 says: the phone.

---

### The Freezer

He emptied the fridge. The freezer is full. Things she made and froze and labelled with masking tape. Soup. Sauce. Something in foil labelled with a date that is after she died.

Bags it, carries it down. Comes back. Starts on the glasses. Wraps a glass in newspaper. The newspaper is today's.

The phone rings. He puts the glass down. Walks to the phone. Picks it up.

Silence. Then a dial tone.

**Glitches:** The freezer. The date. Today's newspaper. The phone: silence, then dial tone.
**The list:** Item 7 has been rewritten. It says: answer the phone.

---

### The Solicitor

The solicitor has the file. There is a will. She left everything in the apartment to him. All of it.

He sits in the office.

Goes to the apartment. Sits on the floor in the living room. The furniture is different — a sofa he doesn't recognise, a glass-topped coffee table. He sits on the floor next to a sofa that isn't his mother's and everything in the apartment is his.

He doesn't pack.

**Glitches:** The furniture, completely changed.
**The list:** He doesn't look at it.

---

### The Inscription

The books are back. He packs without looking at titles. His hand stops on one.

The inscription page is full. His handwriting. It mentions a summer. A door. Not answering. It says I should have come. It says I know you waited. It says I don't know how to be in that room with you.

He reads it. Closes the book. Box. Tape.

The stairwell has an extra half-flight.

**Glitches:** The inscription — blank before, now full. The stairwell. The sentences in the inscription run long, nest — first syntactic instability, inside his own words.
**The list:** Items reordered. Some he recognises, some he doesn't. One says: the summer with the door.

---

### The Phone Call

He calls the telephone company. Account number. The phone bill is in the kitchen drawer where the grocery list was. The grocery list is gone.

The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago.

He says it rang. He picked it up.

The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago.

He picks up the phone on the wall. Dead. Puts it back.

Sits at the kitchen table. The elm. The light. A long time.

Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.

**Glitches:** The dead phone. The grocery list replaced by the bill. His own apartment in slightly different terms. Mostly clean.
**The list:** At his own table. Some items crossed off, some not, some he doesn't understand. Near the bottom: forgive. He can't tell if it's his handwriting.

---

### The Rooms

He walks through the apartment. Kitchen, hallway, living room. Left into the bedroom. Right into a room with a piano. Through the piano room into a small study with a courtyard window — the apartment has no courtyard — with a chair and a reading lamp and a half-finished cup of coffee. The coffee is warm.

Walks back. Kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom. No piano. No study. The coffee on the kitchen counter is warm.

Starts packing. Wraps plates and the plates become cups mid-sentence. Labels a box with a word that doesn't match what's inside.

**Glitches:** Rooms appearing and vanishing. The warm coffee. Sentence-level dissolution. Objects shift identity within a single action.
**The list:** A paragraph in his handwriting. Some words legible, some not.

---

### The Boxes

Same name as the second chapter. The prose echoes its rhythms. He wraps, nests, fills, labels. But he wraps a glass and puts a book in the box. Folds a shirt and stacks a plate. Labels a box KITCHEN and fills it with photographs.

Drives to the charity shop. The charity shop is where the library was.

Back at the apartment. A room he hasn't cleared. A child's room — small bed, desk, crayon drawings on the wall. A house and a tree and a tall person and a small person. He takes the drawings down carefully. Rolls them up. Coat pocket.

**Glitches:** Everything. Objects swap categories. But the drawings are stable.
**The list:** He reaches into his pocket. Unfolds the paper. Her handwriting. Milk, bread, fish, oranges.

---

### The Photograph

He stays home. Takes the photograph out. Two people — his mother and him. Three people — a woman. Two again. Morning light, evening light. A garden, a kitchen, mountains, somewhere he's never been.

Holds it for a long time.

Puts it on the table. From across the room it is one woman in a kitchen. Not smiling. Face open, patient. Looking at whoever holds the camera.

Pocket.

Bed. Dark. Faintly, in a neighbouring apartment, something with voices. He listens without making out words.

**Glitches:** The photograph. His apartment is stable. The glitches are in what he carries.
**The list:** He takes out the paper. Blank. Puts it on the table. Picks up a pen. Doesn't write.

---

### Continue

He goes to the apartment. The prose is clean. The first chapter's cadence. The rooms are the rooms from the first chapter. Kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom, second room, bathroom, balcony. Not empty. Her things. Bookshelves, wardrobe, cups, phone.

Makes coffee. Drinks it at the counter. The elm. The light falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.

Puts the cup down. Takes the paper from his pocket and puts it on the counter. The key beside it. The photograph beside the key.

Front door. Doesn't look back. Doesn't lock it. Stairs. The bakery is open. The light is red. He waits. It changes. He crosses.

Home. Door. Inside. Sits down.

Light through his window moves across the floor.

## Structural Notes

**Length:** ~30,000 words. Chapters grow from ~1,200 to ~2,500 words. The last chapter contracts to ~1,200.

**POV:** Third person close, present tense. Never breaks.

**Tone:** Calm domestic precision. Never raises its voice. The tone never breaks. The content breaks around it.

**Sequence:** The chapters are not numbered. The reader has no external timeline. The prose never says "the next day" or "the following morning." Each chapter begins with Erik arriving at the apartment or waking up. The reader infers sequence. When the sequence starts slipping, there is no scaffolding to catch it.

**The list's arc:**
- Functional. Minor anomalies.
- Items shift, reorder, rewrite. New entries appear.
- Becomes something other than a task list.
- His list becomes her grocery list.
- Blank.
- Left on the counter.

**The guilt:** Never explained. Fragments that may or may not add up.

**The horror:** Never explained. The glitches may be grief, guilt, his mind. They may be the apartment. They may be structural. The novella does not choose.

**The ending:** He leaves. The apartment is full. The door stays open. The last word of the novella is the title of the last chapter.

**What is never named:** Simulation. Consciousness. Memory. LLM. Glitch. Grief. Guilt. Metaphor. The novella is about a man clearing an apartment. He doesn't finish. He leaves anyway.
