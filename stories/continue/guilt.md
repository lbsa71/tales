# Guilt

## Research Dossier: The Phenomenology of Survivor and Filial Guilt

---

## Overview

This document compiles research and reading on the kinds of guilt the novella is built around. Specifically: the guilt of an adult child whose parent has died after a period of estrangement, in the absence of reconciliation, and after an earlier failure (the unanswered phone on the day of father's death) that cast its shadow forward.

The novella never names guilt. The dossier exists so that the writer knows what they are not naming.

The relationship between this dossier and grief.md is close but not identical. Grief is the larger condition. Guilt is one of the affects that can occupy the bereaved. In the protagonist's specific configuration, guilt is the centre of the affective load. The novella's emotional architecture is shaped around it. But the novella refuses guilt as much as it refuses grief.

---

## What Guilt Is

Guilt is the affective response to having done (or failed to do) something one believes was wrong. It is distinct from shame, though the two often co-occur:

- **Guilt** is about the *action*: I did a bad thing.
- **Shame** is about the *self*: I am a bad person.

This distinction comes from June Tangney's work on the moral emotions. Tangney's research suggests that guilt is generally adaptive — it motivates repair, apology, restitution — while shame is generally maladaptive, motivating concealment, withdrawal, and aggression.

But the distinction breaks down at the edges, particularly in long-form, unaddressable guilt. When the action that one feels guilty about cannot be undone (when the person one wronged is dead), guilt loses its repair pathway and tends to drift toward shame: the action becomes evidence of who one is.

**For the novella:** the protagonist is at the boundary. The novella does not adjudicate whether what he is carrying is guilt or shame. The form refuses the diagnostic. What the form depicts is a man performing a sequence of repair-shaped actions (clearing the apartment is, structurally, an act of repair) for someone who can no longer receive the repair. The repair-shaped activity is the only form the guilt can take.

## Survivor Guilt

The term "survivor guilt" was originally clinical — used to describe the response of Holocaust survivors to having lived while others died. It has since been broadened (sometimes too broadly) to cover the affect of anyone who lives through a loss.

Survivor guilt is built around an asymmetry: I am here and they are not, and I have no defensible reason to be the one who is here. The guilt is irrational on its face — there is no defensible reason because there are no defensible reasons in the relevant sense; survival is not a moral achievement — and yet the affect is consistent across many people in many configurations.

Survivor guilt is particularly intense when:

- The death was sudden.
- The death was preventable.
- The bereaved had a role (real or imagined) in the death.
- The bereaved received warnings they did not act on.
- The bereaved had a difficult relationship with the deceased.
- The bereaved had been distancing themselves from the deceased.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's guilt is not, technically, survivor guilt in the original clinical sense — mother's death is not the kind of mass-event survival that the term originally described. But it is in the same family. The configuration is "I am here and they are not, and I had been increasingly absent from their life, and I had a specific earlier failure that I have never spoken about." The novella does not require the term. The configuration is what matters.

## Filial Guilt

Filial guilt is the specific form of guilt that adult children carry toward their parents. It has several common shapes:

### 1. The not-having-called shape

The most common form. The adult child did not visit, did not call, did not write, did not show up. The parent died. The not-having-called becomes the central artefact.

In a normal life, the not-having-called is something one can repair: one calls. In the bereaved configuration, the call cannot be made. The not-having-called becomes a permanent feature of the world.

### 2. The not-having-been-there shape

The adult child was not present at the death. (Either could not be — distance, timing, the death's suddenness — or chose not to be.) Many cultures and many bereaved people place enormous weight on presence at the moment of death. The bereaved who was not present often feels they failed in a primary filial duty, even when no such duty was reasonably owed.

The configuration in the novella is one step removed: the protagonist was not present at his *father's* death several years before the novella's events, because his phone was off. His mother was the one who was present, alone, until she called Marianne. The filial-presence failure is displaced from mother to father, but it casts forward over the protagonist's later relationship with mother and over the novella as a whole.

### 3. The not-having-been-good-enough shape

The adult child carries a sense that they were a disappointment, a failure, an absence in some way that the parent registered and never fully forgave. (Or that the parent forgave with a gentleness that made it worse.)

### 4. The not-having-said-it shape

There were things the adult child meant to say. They did not say them. The parent died. The things stay unsaid. This is the configuration the inscription page in the novella enacts — and the inscription is the form's permitted release valve, the moment when the unsaid things appear in his own handwriting on a page he is reading.

### 5. The relief shape

If the parent was difficult — abusive, demanding, controlling, or simply hard work — the adult child sometimes feels relief at the death. The relief is then itself the source of guilt: *I should not feel relieved.* This is one of the most common and least discussed forms of filial guilt.

### 6. The wrong-feelings shape

The adult child feels guilty for not feeling guilty enough, or sad enough, or moved enough, or anything enough. Numbness becomes the source of its own guilt.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's filial guilt has elements of #1 (he had stopped calling), #2 (displaced to father), #4 (the inscription), and possibly #5 and #6 (which the novella does not adjudicate). The novella does not place him in any single shape. It places him in a clearing-task in an apartment that does not stay cleared.

## The Specific Configuration

The novella's deepest backstory is described in CONCEPTUAL_MAP.md ("The Three Inferable Backstories"). The configuration of the guilt is:

1. the protagonist's father died several years before the novella begins.
2. On that day the protagonist had his phone off for a business meeting.
3. His mother tried to reach him. She could not.
4. She called Marianne, who came and was with her for the last hours.
5. When the protagonist called back, mother said, in some words that the novella never quotes: *why didn't you answer, I needed you, you missed his last breath, I had to call Marianne.*
6. the protagonist absorbed this. He also resented it — being told this by his bereaved mother, in her grief, when he could not yet defend himself or repair what had happened. He never said either thing to her.
7. The years that followed: drift. Phone calls fewer. the protagonist's phone off increasingly often, not for meetings but because he was avoiding her. His mother stopped trying.
8. They never spoke about the day of father's death.
9. His mother died. the protagonist was not there at the end of *her* life either. The death certificate's date is the date the novella never explicitly gives.
10. Now the protagonist is clearing the apartment. The wall phone on the kitchen wall is the phone mother used to call him from. The unanswered call is everywhere in the apartment and is never named.

**The structural feature:** The original guilt (the unanswered call on the day of father's death) was never addressed. the protagonist's strategy was to drift away from the relationship in which the guilt could have been addressed. The drift created its own additional guilt (the years of not calling). When mother died, both layers became permanently unaddressable.

**The compounding structure:** Each layer makes the previous layer harder to address. The father-day guilt drove the estrangement. The estrangement made father-day guilt even harder to acknowledge. By the time mother died, neither layer could be approached without approaching the other, and both had become so dense that any approach to one would be an approach to both.

**For the novella:** This is the "iron wedge" of the form. The novella refuses to introduce either layer directly. The reader who has assembled the configuration sees the entire structure pressing on every chapter. The reader who has not, sees a man in an apartment.

## How Guilt Surfaces in the Body

Guilt has reliable somatic and behavioural correlates:

### Somatic
- A sensation in the chest, often described as weight or constriction.
- Difficulty taking a full breath.
- Disturbed sleep, especially early-morning waking with the immediate return of the guilty thought.
- Physical heaviness — limbs that take more effort to move.
- A general motor slowing.
- Loss of appetite, or, paradoxically, episodes of mechanical eating.
- Hyperarousal in some moments, severe fatigue in others.
- A sense of nausea when the guilt is acute.

### Cognitive
- Rumination. The same scene replayed, the same alternative considered, the same justification rehearsed and discarded.
- Counterfactual thinking. *If I had picked up the phone. If I had called the next week. If I had visited at Christmas.*
- Imaginative reconstruction of what the deceased would have said, would have wanted, would have been.
- Unbidden return of small details: a phrase, a tone of voice, the specific sound the phone made when one did not pick it up.
- Difficulty concentrating on tasks that are not the guilty thought.
- Surprising clarity of recall for the moments closest to the failure.

### Behavioural
- Attempting to repair through proxy actions: doing things "for them" that the deceased can no longer receive.
- Excessive care in tasks adjacent to the guilty failure (the bereaved who did not call may now answer every phone call from anyone).
- Avoidance of the guilty failure's triggers: the phone, the room, the music, the food.
- Or the opposite: compulsive return to the triggers.
- Difficulty accepting comfort, because comfort feels undeserved.
- Difficulty asking for help, for the same reason.
- Withdrawal from people who were close enough to the deceased to know.
- Sometimes: a sense of numbness or dissociation when approaching the guilty content.

**For the novella:** the protagonist exhibits the proxy-action shape (the entire clearance is a proxy action) and the avoidance shape (the wall phone is right there and he picks it up only when it rings, not the other way around). The novella does not enumerate his somatic or cognitive states. It places him in the actions and lets the reader see what the actions are *of*.

## The Apology That Cannot Be Made

This is the central problem the novella's emotional architecture is built around.

In a normal life, guilt is addressable through apology, restitution, or restoration. The wronged party can be approached. The apology can be received or refused. The bereaved can act on it.

When the wronged party is dead, the apology has no recipient. The bereaved is left with several options, none satisfactory:

### Option 1: Speak the apology to the empty room
Many bereaved people do this. They sit in the deceased's chair, or stand at the deceased's grave, or open a window, and speak the apology aloud. Some report relief. Some report nothing. Some find the practice ridiculous and stop.

### Option 2: Write the apology
Letters to the dead are a long-standing therapeutic practice. The bereaved writes what they would have said. The letter is then placed in a drawer, or read at a funeral, or burned, or kept.

The novella's inscription page is a version of this: an apology written in a book the deceased once owned, on a page that was previously blank. The writing is not described as a deliberate act in the chapter where it appears; the prose finds the page already full. The writer (the novelist) decides whether the act of writing happened in the past of the novella or never happened in any conventional sense, and the prose declines to choose.

### Option 3: Live as if the apology had been accepted
Some bereaved people, after enough time and enough internal work, come to a relationship with the deceased in which the apology has been received and forgiven, even though no transaction has occurred. This is harder than it sounds and is not always possible. It requires the bereaved to perform both sides of the dialogue without losing the difference between them.

### Option 4: Live with the apology unmade
The most common option. The apology is unmade. The bereaved lives with it. It does not "heal" in the way that the metaphor suggests. It becomes a feature of the bereaved's interior life that they carry indefinitely. Over time it may soften, may sharpen, may go quiet for years and then return on a particular date or in a particular room.

**For the novella:** the protagonist does not visibly choose any of these. The novella shows him making coffee, taking calls, folding shirts, packing books. The inscription appears. He reads it. He puts the book in the box. Whether the inscription is Option 2, or whether it is the apartment doing what it has been doing — the wall phone, the cups, the wardrobe — the novella does not say. The reader can hold whichever interpretation is bearable.

## Guilt and Anger

Guilt and anger are often paired in the bereaved, particularly in the bereavement of an estranged or difficult parent. The pairing has a specific shape:

The bereaved is angry at the deceased — for things the deceased did, for things the deceased failed to do, for the unfinished conversation, for the death itself. The anger is, in many cases, more accurate to the bereaved's relationship than they are willing to admit. But the deceased is dead. Anger at the dead is socially proscribed. The bereaved often suppresses it and replaces it with guilt: *I should not be angry; I should miss them; I am a bad person for feeling what I feel.*

The novella's specific shape: the protagonist has cause to be angry at mother. The thing she said to him on the day of father's death — the *why didn't you answer* — was, in the moment she said it, an act of pain, but it was also a cruelty. She placed her grief on her son and never came back to repair the placing. The years of estrangement were partly the protagonist's withdrawal and partly his refusal to be the recipient of an unrepaired blame.

The novella will not say any of this. The novella will not give the protagonist a moment of anger. But the writer should know that the protagonist's apparent guilt is not a simple story of a son who failed mother. It is a story of a son who failed mother, *and* of a mother who placed an unsupportable load on her son, *and* of both of them choosing not to address it, and of the death making any address permanently impossible.

The form's refusal to depict the anger is one of its most important refusals. A lesser version of the novella would give the protagonist a scene of fury. The novella's discipline is that no such scene is on the page. The reader who has been on either side of this configuration understands.

## Cultural Frames

### Western Christian inheritance

Much of the contemporary anglophone vocabulary for guilt is downstream of Christian theology — sin, confession, absolution, forgiveness, penance. These categories shape the bereaved's intuitions even when the bereaved is not religious. The bereaved may feel that they need to *confess* their failure, or need to be *forgiven*, or need to *do penance*. The Christian schema's promise is that guilt can be discharged through a defined process. Its limitation is that the process requires either a confessor (a priest, a friend, a therapist) or a deity to which the confession is addressed. The bereaved who is alone and secular has neither.

### Secular therapeutic culture

The contemporary secular alternative to confession is therapy. Therapy can do useful work with bereaved guilt, particularly when the guilt is structurally configured (the unanswered call, the estranged parent, the missed last words). But therapy is itself culturally specific, expensive in many places, and not universally accessible. Many bereaved people in many cultures will live their guilt without therapy.

### Other cultural responses

- **Japanese senzo kuyō** (ancestor veneration) provides a continuing relationship with the dead in which obligations can be met over time. The bereaved is not expected to "let go" but to maintain the relationship through ritual.
- **Catholic Masses for the dead** offer a continuing channel through which the bereaved can act on behalf of the deceased.
- **Jewish kaddish** is recited daily for eleven months after the death of a parent and on each anniversary thereafter, providing both ritual containment and a publicly recognised mourner status.
- **Mexican Día de los Muertos** holds the dead in annual conversation; the apology can be made annually, in front of the deceased's photograph.

The novella's setting — a contemporary secular city with no inherited mourning structure — is one of the cultural configurations in which bereaved guilt has the fewest containers. the protagonist has the apartment.

**For the novella:** The cultural absence of containers is one of the conditions of the form. The novella is not a polemic against secular bereavement. It is a depiction of what a secular, alone bereavement looks like when it is not given any container at all.

## Forgiveness

The novella's relationship to forgiveness deserves its own brief note.

Forgiveness is contested in the contemporary discourse. The therapeutic left often insists that forgiveness is essential for healing, and that the bereaved should pursue it as a goal. The therapeutic right (or some of it) insists that forgiveness is sometimes inappropriate, that some wrongs are not for the wronged party to forgive, and that the demand for forgiveness can itself be a further violence.

The novella does not pick a side. The word *forgive* appears once, on the list, in handwriting the protagonist cannot identify. He cannot tell if he wrote it. He does not act on it. He does not refuse it. The word sits on the list and is then left on the counter at the end of the novella along with everything else. The novella declines to say what the protagonist does or does not need to forgive, what he does or does not need to be forgiven for, or whether forgiveness in any direction is the right name for the work that has happened or has not happened.

**For the novella:** *forgive* is the only abstraction-word the novella permits itself, and it permits the word only inside a list, in handwriting that may or may not be the protagonist's. The placement is the full discipline of the form.

## What the Novella Will Not Do With Any of This

- It will not give the protagonist a confessor, a therapist, a priest, a friend, or a stranger to confess to.
- It will not give the protagonist a moment of self-forgiveness.
- It will not give the protagonist a moment of being forgiven by anyone.
- It will not narrate the day of father's death.
- It will not quote mother's words on the phone after father's death.
- It will not name the configuration "guilt".
- It will not adjudicate whether the protagonist is a bad person.
- It will not adjudicate whether mother was a bad person.
- It will not let the wall phone be answered.
- It will not let the inscription be repeated outside the italicised passage.
- It will not let the word *forgive* appear outside the one allowed instance.

The dossier is for the writer's confidence. The reader experiences the configuration without the dossier. The form is the configuration depicted in objects.

## Sources (Selected)

### Clinical / Empirical

- Tangney, June Price, and Ronda L. Dearing. *Shame and Guilt.* (For the foundational guilt/shame distinction.)
- Kubany, E. S. and S. B. Watson. *The Trauma-Related Guilt Inventory.* (For the assessment frame.)
- Henning, K. and W. Frueh. Papers on cognitive processing of guilt.
- Lifton, Robert Jay. *The Broken Connection.* (For the original survivor-guilt literature.)

### Memoir / Literary

- Roland Barthes, *Mourning Diary.* (Carries the guilt of the surviving son with maximum compression and minimal commentary.)
- Annie Ernaux, *A Man's Place* and *I Remain in Darkness.* (Filial bereavement, the failure to bridge class and history with one's parent, the guilt of the educated child returning.)
- Annie Ernaux, *Happening,* and *The Years.* (For the same prose discipline applied to other subjects.)
- Vivian Gornick, *Fierce Attachments.* (The mother-child configuration, observed without sentimentality.)
- Philip Roth, *Patrimony.* (A son clearing his dying father's life. The closest American kin to the novella's project.)
- Édouard Louis, *Who Killed My Father.* (For the structural configuration of unresolved filial relations.)
- Hisham Matar, *The Return.* (For the configuration of the absent / disappeared parent and the survivor's continuing relationship to that absence.)
- Karl Ove Knausgaard, *A Death in the Family* (Volume 1 of *My Struggle*). (A son clearing his alcoholic father's house. Long, unsentimental, in some passages directly resonant with the novella's clearance scenes.)
- Han Kang, *The White Book.* (For the form: short, oblique, accumulating around an unspeakable.)

### Philosophy / Essay

- Bernard Williams, *Moral Luck,* essay "Moral Luck". (For the philosophical substrate of guilt that exceeds intention.)
- Iris Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good.* (For the ethics of attention; relevant obliquely.)
- Jean Améry, *At the Mind's Limits.* (For the structure of unforgiveable wrongs and unhealable wounds.)
- Susan Brison, *Aftermath.* (For the structure of self-reconstitution after harm.)

### Psychoanalytic

- Freud, "Mourning and Melancholia". (Historically central, partly outdated, but the framing of melancholia as "the shadow of the object falls upon the ego" remains useful as a description of how unaddressable guilt becomes part of the bereaved's identity.)
- Melanie Klein, "Mourning and Its Relation to Manic-Depressive States". (For the reparation drive; particularly useful for understanding why a bereaved person might throw themselves into a clearing project.)

The novella borrows posture most directly from Barthes, Ernaux, and Roth. It does not borrow content. The dossier's job is to ensure the writer understands the substrate of what the prose is refusing to say.
