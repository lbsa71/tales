## Executive Summary

*Continue* is a ~30,000-word novella in 17 chapters about the work of clearing a dead parent's apartment. Its governing formal commitments are:

- A protagonist with no name and no pronoun
- Third-person position, present tense
- Bare-verb imperatives as the default grammar
- Refusal of interiority, metaphor, interpretation, and consolation
- A governing disposition of *numb single-mindedness* — a body holding together by refusing to look up
- Slowly escalating "glitches" in the apartment, never named, never explained
- A recurring chapter-end refrain (*The street is empty.*) that both marks the days and accumulates as a subtle wrongness
- An inscription passage that is the only permitted break in the form, contained in italics, in past tense, in the protagonist's handwriting

The novella is about grief, guilt, and an unmade apology, and it never names those words. It is technically demanding, deliberately unresolved, and not commercially oriented.

**Current state:** Foundation complete. All planning artifacts written and voice-migrated. First draft not yet begun.

**Next step:** Draft Chapter 1 ("The Key") and Chapter 17 ("Continue") as bookends, then Chapter 14 ("The Rooms") and Chapter 12 ("The Inscription") as the form's most demanding chapters, then fill in chronologically.

### What is settled (and should not be reopened without cause)

The following are decisions that have been made across multiple iterations and should be treated as stable unless the manuscript itself demands reconsideration:

1. **The protagonist has no name, no pronoun, no interior.** The camera is at a fixed position that walks with a body. Everything else in the voice follows.
2. **Mother and father are bare nouns** (*mother*, *father*), treated almost as proper nouns. Not *the mother*.
3. **Bare-verb imperatives are the default grammar.** *Finish the coffee. Take the paper.*
4. **Object-as-subject observations are full sentences with finite verbs.** *The plates are in the cupboard.*
5. **Glitches are placed across sentences, not compressed into clever single sentences.**
6. **The chapter-ending refrain is *The street is empty.*** Strict repetition. Broken only in Chs 7 and 16 (at the protagonist's own apartment) and transcended in Ch 17.
7. **Positional specificity** — every placement names the specific location. *The right pocket of the coat*, not *a pocket*.
8. **Present tense throughout**, except inside the italicised inscription passage in Ch 12.
9. **Memory allowed as monofocus action** — carried by *without hesitation*, *without looking*, never as reflection.
10. **The governing disposition is numb single-mindedness**, not hysterical fragmentation.
11. **The novella never names** grief, guilt, mourning, loss, memory-as-topic, dissociation, forgiveness (except once on the list), metaphor, simulation, or glitch.

These eleven are the form. Reopening them would not be a refinement; it would be a different novella.

## Phase 2: Artistic Merit Assessment

### What the novella is trying to do

*Continue* is attempting a specific kind of literary difficulty: depicting the interior of a bereaved body from outside that body, without a single interior sentence on the page. The reader experiences the interior entirely by inference from what the camera records: where the hands go, what is not said, which pocket the key is dropped into, the accumulated weight of a refrain that repeats without comment.

This is not an original ambition — Ishiguro, Ernaux, Barthes, Han Kang, and a handful of others have attempted adjacent disciplines. What is specific to *Continue* is the combination:

- The **no-pronoun protagonist** (more formally restrictive than any of the named precedents)
- The **bare-verb imperative** as the default grammar (closer to recipe or stage direction than literary prose, yet sustained across 30,000 words)
- The **glitch architecture** that gradually makes the apartment unclearable without ever explaining why
- The **sustained Todorovian hesitation** — the reader is never released into either a psychological or a supernatural reading, and the novella does not adjudicate
- The **chapter-ending refrain** as both rhythmic anchor and accumulating subtle wrongness
- The **inscription passage** as the single contained break in the form

Each of these individually is defensible. The combination is the bet. Whether the combination holds across 30,000 words is what the drafting process will discover.

### Literary precedents and kin

(See [the_uncanny.md](the_uncanny.md) for the fuller literature.)

Closest formal kin (not imitated, but posture-sharing):

- **Kazuo Ishiguro,** *The Remains of the Day* — refused interiority in a first-person narrator who cannot say what the novel is clearly about. *Continue* extends this discipline into the third person with no protagonist pronoun.
- **Annie Ernaux,** *I Remain in Darkness* and *A Man's Place* — flat declarative sentences about a parent's life and death, refusal of literary ornament. *Continue* shares the sentence-level discipline.
- **Roland Barthes,** *Mourning Diary* — the fragments Barthes wrote after his mother's death, refusing to interpret what he recorded. Closest stylistic neighbour.
- **Han Kang,** *The White Book* — oblique accumulation around an unspeakable loss. *Continue* shares the accumulation mode.
- **Karl Ove Knausgaard,** *A Death in the Family* — clearance scenes in a deceased parent's house with granular attention to small physical work. *Continue*'s practical surface borrows from this.
- **Alain Robbe-Grillet,** *La Jalousie* — a protagonist inferable only from the camera's position; no name, no first-person, no interior. *Continue*'s no-pronoun discipline is closest to this (though the subject matter could not be more different).
- **Marguerite Duras,** *The Lover* and *The Malady of Death* — short, fragmented, refusing to explain.

Distant kin:

- **Thomas Bernhard** — the obsessive repetitive voice; *Continue* is more restrained but shares the patience.
- **W.G. Sebald** — the calm camera in front of devastating material; *Continue* inherits the tone.
- **Lydia Davis** — sentence-level economy; *Continue* borrows the trust in the literal.
- **Claire Keegan,** *Small Things Like These* — the Irish minimalist register; *Continue* is flatter but adjacent.

### What could succeed

The form could succeed if:

- The voice holds across 30,000 words without a single interiority slip
- The glitches are placed at the right density, spread across the right sentences
- The chapter-ending refrain lands as ritual rather than gimmick
- The inscription passage carries the emotional weight the form has been withholding
- The last chapter's cadence genuinely rhymes with the first chapter's
- Readers who finish the book are unsettled in a way they cannot quickly articulate
- At least one reader has to put the book down for a while during Chapter 14
- At least one reader rereads the inscription
- The novella is recognised (by the few readers who will read it carefully) as formally unusual in a way that justifies its difficulty

### What could fail

Honest assessment of the risks:

1. **Readers bounce off page 3.** The no-pronoun grammar is demanding. Some readers will give up before the form starts doing its work. Mitigation: the first chapter has to be strong enough on its mundane surface to hold the reader for ten pages, after which the form is bearable.

2. **The voice becomes its own tic.** Bare-verb imperatives sustained for 30,000 words risk becoming monotonous. Mitigation: the three grammatical tools (bare verbs, past participles, object-as-subject) must actually vary across sentences. If they don't, the prose becomes a single-note exercise. The calibration excerpts in EXAMPLE_DRAFT.md need to be re-read constantly during drafting to check that the three tools are all being used.

3. **The glitches read as supernatural horror.** Readers unfamiliar with literary-fantastic-mode may interpret the wall phone ringing on a disconnected line, the freezer containing an impossibly dated meal, and the piano room appearing in a three-room apartment as a ghost story. The novella does not want this reading (or rather, does not want to be reduced to it). Mitigation: the calm register has to be absolutely held, and the final chapter has to clear the dissolution without explaining it. The reader should feel the psychological-dissociative reading available alongside the uncanny-apartment reading, and neither should win.

4. **The refrain becomes precious.** *The street is empty.* at every chapter ending could feel like an MFA tic if the rest of the prose is not disciplined enough. Mitigation: strict repetition, exact words, never varied, and the rest of the prose earning the refrain's weight chapter by chapter.

5. **The inscription passage is either too much or too little.** Six sentences. Any more and it becomes a scene. Any fewer and it doesn't carry. Mitigation: this is the single sentence-level moment where the writer will likely need multiple revision passes. It's worth spending a week on.

6. **The ending is mistaken for a lack of ending.** Readers expect resolution. The novella ends with light moving across a floor. Some readers will feel cheated. This is not fixable and not a failure — it is the form. But it should be understood as a real risk and the novella's reception will reflect it.

7. **The translation problem.** The bare-verb imperative moves differently in different languages. Some languages (Romance) have cleaner nominal-infinitive constructions that would actually suit the voice better than English. Other languages (German) may struggle. This is deferred until after the English manuscript exists, but worth noting as a forthcoming problem.

8. **The hyper-restraint reads as "nothing happens."** Some readers will find the novella boring. For them, the form is not doing its work. This is unavoidable — the novella is specifically not for every reader. The question is whether enough readers of the right kind find it.

### Honest assessment of ambition vs. capability

*Continue* is a more formally ambitious novella than *A Rabbit's Life*. Its refusals are deeper, its voice is more unusual, and its reader address is more demanding. It is also structurally more complex — the glitch escalation runs across five phases with specific calibrated transitions, the list's arc is the novella's spine, and the chapter-ending refrain creates a rhythmic architecture that has to be built from the first chapter onward.

This is worth attempting precisely because the form is unusual. The risk is that the form is too unusual to cohere. The drafting process will reveal which it is.

The form's central bet: *that a novella about clearing a dead parent's apartment, written entirely in bare-verb imperatives with no protagonist pronoun, refusing every interior sentence, can carry more emotional weight than the same story told with conventional interiority*. This bet is testable only by writing the draft and seeing if it holds.

## Phase 3: Reader and Comparative Position

### Who this novella is for

Not for every reader. Specifically for:

- Readers who have read Ernaux, Barthes, Han, Ishiguro, Sebald, Knausgaard, and were changed by one or more of them
- Readers who tolerate (and prefer) oblique emotional writing over declarative
- Readers who have lost a parent and are prepared to sit with material that approaches the loss sideways
- Readers who notice form and are rewarded by form
- Readers who read slowly, who reread, who close a book and sit with it
- Readers interested in the formal limits of what can be said about grief without saying *grief*
- Readers willing to be held in Todorovian hesitation without release

The novella will not find its way to most of these readers through commercial channels. It will find them through word of mouth among small readerships, through reviews in literary journals, through recommendations by the handful of booksellers and librarians who read attentively, and over time.

### Who this novella is not for

- Readers who want a plot
- Readers who want a protagonist to identify with by name
- Readers who want emotions named on the page
- Readers who want ambiguity resolved by the ending
- Readers who want comfort or catharsis
- Readers who read fast
- Readers who read for escape
- Readers who read for the supernatural (the glitches are not ghosts)
- Readers who read for procedural / bureaucratic satisfaction (the novella has this surface but does not pay it off)

### Reader experience to expect

(See [EMOTIONAL_ARC.md](EMOTIONAL_ARC.md) for the fuller map.)

A reader who finishes the novella well will have moved through: trust (Chs 1-3) → suspicion (Chs 4-6) → active dread (Ch 7) → carrying (Chs 8-15) → stilled (Chs 16-17). They will finish the book unsettled in a way they cannot articulate, and they will not be able to summarise what the novella was about without summarising the entire synopsis.

Some percentage of readers — honestly, possibly the majority of readers who pick it up — will not finish the book. This is not a failure of the form; it is a consequence of its demands. The form is calibrated for the readers who will finish, not for everyone who picks up.

### Comparative market position

*Continue* sits in a small niche of contemporary literary novellas that:

- Are under 40,000 words
- Are about grief or loss
- Are formally unusual
- Expect the reader to do significant interpretive work
- Are published by small literary houses or self-published by disciplined authors

Reference points for positioning (not comparable titles for marketing):

- **Claire Keegan,** *Small Things Like These* (~30,000 words, widely reviewed, Booker-shortlisted 2022) — a precedent for short, oblique literary fiction breaking through
- **Han Kang,** *The White Book* (~160 pages, accumulation-by-fragment)
- **Annie Ernaux,** *A Man's Place* and *I Remain in Darkness* (short, declarative, about a parent)
- **Marilynne Robinson,** *Housekeeping* (larger, but the same patient domestic register)
- **Denise Riley,** *Time Lived, Without Its Flow* (prose on grief and time after the death of a son, formally unusual)
- **Édouard Levé,** *Suicide* (oblique posthumous accounting, short, fragmentary in effect)

None of these are *Continue*'s competitors. They are its ancestors and its fellow travellers.

### Non-monetary measures of success

The novella's success should be measured in:

- **Whether it exists** — the first and hardest measure. Most novellas of this ambition are abandoned.
- **Whether it holds its form** — whether the voice stays consistent, whether the glitch architecture coheres, whether the refrain lands.
- **Whether a handful of readers are moved by it** — not thousands. A handful.
- **Whether those readers recommend it to the kind of reader who would also be moved** — word of mouth in small circles.
- **Whether it gets reviewed in at least one serious literary venue** — Bookforum, LRB, TLS, Music & Literature, Asymptote, 3:AM, a few others.
- **Whether it stays in print** — longevity matters more than launch for work of this register.
- **Whether the writer thinks, at the end, that the novella is the thing that was trying to get written** — the private measure that no one else can take.

Commercial success is incidental. It is welcomed if it arrives but it is not a target. The novella is not built to maximise sales; it is built to maximise the specific experience it is trying to produce.

### Order of composition

Four passes interleaved, not a strict linear sequence.

#### Pass 1: Bookends (Chapters 1 and 17)

**Duration estimate:** 2-3 weeks of writing time, not including rest days.

**Why first:** The last chapter has to rhyme with the first at the sentence level, specifically the *rectangle of light* sentence and the *cups in the cupboard* sentence. The rest of the novella is calibrated against these two chapters as anchors. The last chapter cannot be drafted before the first, and the middle chapters cannot be drafted before both anchors exist.

**Sub-order:**
1. Chapter 1 ("The Key") — the baseline. Every subsequent chapter's glitches will be judged against this chapter's stability. No glitches in this chapter (except the *one sentence too many* about the wall phone). The list is quoted in full. The chapter ends with *The street is empty.* — which establishes the refrain.
2. Chapter 17 ("Continue") — the return. Restores Chapter 1's cadence. Contains the refrain one last time mid-chapter, then continues past it to *The light through the window moves across the floor.*

**Output:** Two chapters, ~1,200 words each, ~2,400 total.

**Quality gate:** The two chapters must share their cadence. Specifically:
- The *rectangle of light* sentence must be identical in both chapters.
- The kitchen walkthrough must match in room order (kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom, second room, bathroom, balcony).
- The cups must be in the same cupboard in both.
- The coat must be on the same hook (in the protagonist's apartment for Ch 17).
- The first and last chapter's registers must be indistinguishable from each other on read-aloud.

#### Pass 2: The hardest chapters (Chapters 12, 14, 7)

**Duration estimate:** 4-6 weeks.

**Why second:** These three chapters contain the form's most demanding moments. They are drafted before the surrounding chapters because the surrounding chapters need to know what they are calibrated against.

**Sub-order:**
1. Chapter 12 ("The Inscription") — the italicised passage. The single permitted break in the form. Sit with the inscription text. Draft, revise, rewrite, revise again. Six sentences. The surrounding prose must return to baseline immediately after the italics close.
2. Chapter 14 ("The Rooms") — the syntactic dissolution. The piano room and the small study that cannot exist. The plate-cup-plate sentence. The chapter's length target is ~2,300 words, the longest in the book. The prose has to hold the baseline for most of its length and then allow the permitted sentence-level dissolution in one or two specific places.
3. Chapter 7 ("The Neighbour") — the first major rupture. The protagonist is at their own apartment. The protagonist is packing their own plates. The neighbour comes to the door. This chapter has to read at the same register as the mother's apartment chapters even though it is in a different apartment — the continuity of register is the chapter's central effect. Ends with a broken refrain (*Bed.* or equivalent).

**Output:** Three chapters, ~1,800 + ~2,300 + ~1,700 = ~5,800 words.

**Quality gate:**
- The inscription passage is contained inside italics; the prose before and after is baseline.
- The Rooms syntactic dissolution is used exactly twice in the chapter (the courtyard sentence and the plate-cup-plate sentence); everywhere else the prose holds baseline.
- The Neighbour chapter's register is indistinguishable from the mother's-apartment chapters. The reader should only notice that it's a different apartment through the content (Erik's own plates, the neighbour at the door), not through any shift in the prose.

#### Pass 3: The early chapters (Chapters 2-6)

**Duration estimate:** 4-6 weeks.

**Why third:** The early chapters establish the reader's trust in the baseline register and start accumulating the Phase 1 and Phase 2 glitches. They need to know what Chapter 7 looks like (because Chapter 7 is the first rupture) and what Chapter 12 looks like (because Chapter 12 is the first prose-level break).

**Sub-order:**
1. Chapter 2 ("The Boxes") — the tape dispenser glitch (placed across three sentences per rule 10). Ends with *The street is empty.*
2. Chapter 3 ("The Utility Company") — the first procedural glitch, the bedroom-through-the-kitchen spatial glitch, the library book returned. Ends with *The street is empty.*
3. Chapter 4 ("The Clothes") — the wardrobe. The drying rack. The grocery list in the drawer. Ends with *The street is empty.*
4. Chapter 5 ("The Will") — the metal box not on top of the wardrobe. The desk in the second room. The wardrobe refilled. Item 15 added. Ends with *The street is empty.*
5. Chapter 6 ("The Photographs") — the cups in the wrong cupboard, the wall phone first picked up, the three-people photograph, Marianne on the list. Ends with *The street is empty.*

**Output:** Five chapters, averaging ~1,600 words each, ~8,000 total.

**Quality gate:**
- The list's state is correct for each chapter (see THEME_TRACKER.md's list arc)
- The wall phone's state is correct
- Every required glitch from THEME_DOSSIERS.md is present and placed across sentences
- Every chapter ends with *The street is empty.*
- The prose baseline is unbroken; no sentence draws attention to itself

#### Pass 4: The late chapters (Chapters 8-11, 13, 15, 16)

**Duration estimate:** 6-8 weeks.

**Why fourth:** The late chapters depend on the bookends (Pass 1), the hardest chapters (Pass 2), and the early chapters (Pass 3) all existing. They are where the form's glitch escalation is at its most demanding and where the refrain's weight is at its highest.

**Sub-order (approximately chronological):**
1. Chapter 8 ("The Death Certificate") — the registry form, the blank inscription page, the wall phone ringing. Rule 11 (memory as efficiency) carries the form-filling. Ends with *The street is empty.*
2. Chapter 9 ("The Bank") — the middle-name discrepancy, the solicitor pause, the wardrobe truly empty, one cup in the cupboard. Item 16 *the phone*. Ends with *The street is empty.*
3. Chapter 10 ("The Freezer") — the freezer full, the date after she died, today's newspaper, the phone ringing then silence then dial tone. Item 7 rewritten. Ends with *The street is empty.*
4. Chapter 11 ("The Solicitor") — the will finally pinned, the furniture changed, sitting on the floor. The list is not consulted. Ends with *The street is empty.*
5. Chapter 13 ("The Phone Call") — the line disconnected four months ago, the grocery list gone from the drawer, the phone bill in its place. Contains the refrain mid-chapter (after leaving the apartment) and then continues to *Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.*
6. Chapter 15 ("The Boxes" second time) — the box labels wrong, the charity shop where the library was, the child's room with the crayon drawings, the grocery list replacing the list in the coat pocket. Ends with *The street is empty.*
7. Chapter 16 ("The Photograph") — the photograph stabilising, the blank list, the pen picked up and not used. The protagonist at home. Ends with a broken refrain (*Dark.* or equivalent).

**Output:** Seven chapters, ~12,500 words.

**Quality gate:**
- The glitch escalation runs smoothly across the seven chapters
- The refrain is strict in the five chapters that end with leaving the apartment
- The refrain is broken in Chapter 16 (protagonist at home)
- Chapter 13 has the refrain mid-chapter, not as closing
- The inscription (already drafted in Pass 2) is consistent with the surrounding prose

### Pace realism

500-1,000 words per drafting day, sustained. Some chapters will take a week; some will take two; Chapter 12 and Chapter 14 may take longer. The order-of-composition above assumes approximately four months of active drafting if the writer works most weekdays. Six months is more realistic with rest days, life interruptions, and the inevitable chapters that resist.

**The chapters that resist are usually the chapters that are doing the most work.** Do not push through resistance without diagnosis. If Chapter 12 is taking three weeks, that is probably correct. If Chapter 3 is taking three weeks, something is wrong with Chapter 2 or with the voice calibration.

### What to do when stuck

1. Re-read the relevant calibration excerpt in [EXAMPLE_DRAFT.md](EXAMPLE_DRAFT.md)
2. Re-read the previous chapter's last paragraph aloud
3. Re-read the "Emergency Reference" in [NARRATOR_VOICE.md](NARRATOR_VOICE.md)
4. Read a page of Ernaux or Barthes
5. Stop. Do something else for a day.
6. Return and write the sentence that comes next, whatever it is
7. If none of the above works within a week, diagnose: is the chapter's THEME_DOSSIERS entry correct? Does the chapter actually have somewhere to go? Is there a glitch missing that the chapter needs? Does the list state make sense?

### When to stop drafting and revise

The rule: finish a full first draft of all 17 chapters before any systematic revision pass. Individual chapters may be sent back for small adjustments (a wrong glitch, a missing refrain), but large revision work waits until the complete first draft exists.

The reason: the voice is learned by drafting. Chapters drafted later in the process will be better-voiced than chapters drafted earlier, and systematic revision after the complete draft will bring the earlier chapters up to the level of the later ones. Revising Chapter 1 in isolation, before Chapter 17 exists, produces a Chapter 1 that will need to be revised again.

## Phase 5: Beta Readers (Sketch)

(Fuller guidance in [IMPLEMENTATION_STRATEGY.md](IMPLEMENTATION_STRATEGY.md). This section is the project-level framing only.)

### Who to ask

Six to eight readers, distributed across:

- **Two readers who read literary fiction widely** and will recognise the form. They will be able to locate the novella in its tradition and say whether it holds.
- **Two readers who do not read literary fiction widely** and will be unguarded. They will tell the writer whether the form is bearable for readers not already trained in this register.
- **One reader who has recently lost a parent**, with permission sought first and with explicit understanding that the material may be difficult. Their feedback is not about whether the novella is "correct" about grief (it refuses to be correct about anything) but whether it is bearable.
- **One reader who has not lost a parent**, to test whether the inference architecture works for a reader without personal access to the configuration.
- **Optional: one reader who works in translation** to flag early any sentences that would be particularly difficult in another language.

### When to ask

After the first draft is complete, the Voice Pass (IMPLEMENTATION_STRATEGY.md Phase 3) has been run, and the Glitch Calibration Pass (Phase 4) has been run. Not before. The beta readers should see something approximately correct at the sentence level and approximately placed at the structure level. They should not be doing voice calibration for the writer.

### What to ask

- Did you finish the book? (Not whether you enjoyed it. Whether you finished.)
- Was there a chapter you put the book down at? Which one?
- Was there a sentence you reread? Which one?
- Did you understand what was happening in the apartment?
- Did you understand what was happening to the body doing the clearing?
- Was there anything you wanted the book to say that it did not?
- If you had to describe the book in one sentence to someone else, what would you say?

### What not to ask

- Did you like it?
- What is the book about?
- Was it too sad?
- What did you think of the ending?
- What would you change?

The first set of questions is diagnostic. The second set invites the reader to become a co-author and is actively harmful to a novella of this register.

### How to integrate feedback

Three-pile sort:

1. **Signal pile:** patterns that multiple readers reported independently. These are worth acting on.
2. **Noise pile:** individual reactions, idiosyncratic preferences, requests for the novella to be a different novella. These are to be noted and set aside.
3. **Diagnostic pile:** places where a reader was confused in a way that suggests the inference architecture did not work as intended. These are worth investigating even if only one reader reported them.

The goal of beta readers is not to make the novella more pleasing. It is to make the novella do what it is trying to do, and to learn which parts of the form are working and which are not.

## Phase 6: Post-Draft Decision Points (Placeholder)

After the first draft exists, revision is complete, and beta readers have been integrated, the following decisions will have to be made. They are deferred until the manuscript exists because they depend on what the manuscript turns out to be.

### Deferred: Publishing route

Three options, to be evaluated with a real manuscript in hand:

1. **Traditional literary-house submission.** A small set of houses publish work of this register: New Directions, Fitzcarraldo Editions, And Other Stories, Archipelago, Dalkey Archive, Melville House, NYRB Classics (for reprints), Graywolf, Coffee House, Deep Vellum, Charco Press. Some of these accept direct submissions; most require an agent. This route would take 1-3 years from completed manuscript to published book if it succeeds, and it will not succeed for most submissions.

2. **Agent submission then traditional publishing.** Finding an agent who understands the register is the hardest step; once found, the path to a small literary house becomes more viable. Agents for this kind of work exist but are few.

3. **Independent / self-publishing**, as zelda did. Fast, full creative control, no institutional validation, limited distribution. The novella would rely entirely on word of mouth and independent reviews.

The choice between these depends on:
- The quality of the completed manuscript (a genuinely strong manuscript of this register might find a home at a literary house; a merely-finished manuscript probably will not)
- The writer's appetite for multi-year submission cycles
- Whether any early readers or advocates can offer introductions to literary-house editors
- Whether an agent is interested

**Decision deferred.** Revisit after the manuscript exists.

### Deferred: Translation

*Continue* is being drafted in English. A Swedish translation may follow. Other languages will be considered if the English edition finds readers.

The voice discipline translates unevenly:

- The **bare-verb imperative** works well in languages with nominal infinitives (French, Italian) and struggles in languages without (German).
- The **no-pronoun protagonist** is easiest in languages that drop subject pronouns naturally (Italian, Japanese, Spanish) and hardest in English (which is why the discipline is load-bearing in the English draft).
- The **article-drop** (*mother* without *the*) works in languages without definite articles (Russian, Japanese) and requires conscious effort in languages with them.
- The **chapter-ending refrain** translates cleanly as long as the six-word count is approximately preserved.
- The **inscription passage** (italicised, past tense) translates cleanly.

**Decision deferred.** Translation planning begins when the English manuscript is stable.

### Deferred: Production and distribution

Format, cover, typesetting, platform, pricing, metadata — all dependent on publishing route and all deferred until a publishing route is chosen.

### Deferred: Marketing

Pre-launch foundation, review strategy, launch campaign, sustained marketing, long-tail — all dependent on the publishing route and on the writer's appetite for the marketing work. For a novella of this register, marketing is largely a matter of getting the book in front of the small number of readers and reviewers who will actually read it attentively, rather than a matter of volume or conversion.

**Decision deferred.**

## Phase 8: Success Metrics

### Artistic success

The novella is artistically successful if:

1. The voice holds across all 17 chapters without a single pronoun slip, interior verb, metaphor, em-dash pivot, or abstraction name.
2. The glitch architecture coheres: the reader who follows the five phases experiences the escalation as designed, and the reader who doesn't feels the wrongness anyway.
3. The inscription passage earns its weight and is remembered by readers who finish the book.
4. The final chapter's rhyme with the first chapter lands.
5. The chapter-ending refrain accumulates rather than flattening into tic.
6. The form succeeds at its central bet: that bare-verb imperative prose with no protagonist pronoun can carry more emotional weight than conventional interiority would.

### Reader success

The novella is a success with its readers if:

1. At least a handful of careful readers finish it and are moved in ways they cannot quickly articulate.
2. At least one reader has to put the book down at Chapter 14.
3. At least one reader rereads the inscription.
4. At least one reader asks, weeks after reading, whether the protagonist has a specific gender. (The correct answer: no.)
5. At least one reader tells the writer that the novella is the thing it was trying to be.

### Writer success

The novella is a success for the writer if, at the end, the writer can look at the manuscript and believe that the form they were reaching for was reached. This is the private measure and the one that finally matters. It is also the measure that cannot be judged before the manuscript exists.

### Decision point after draft

When the first draft is stable, the writer will know more about what the novella is than is currently knowable. At that point, three decisions will have to be made in sequence:

1. **Is this the novella that was trying to get written?** If yes, proceed to publishing decisions. If not, decide whether to revise further, set aside, or abandon.
2. **What is this novella's publishing route?** (Decision deferred until the manuscript exists; see Phase 6 above.)
3. **What happens next?** Translation, next project, rest.

These are not decisions to agonize about now. They are flagged here so that future-self knows they are coming.
