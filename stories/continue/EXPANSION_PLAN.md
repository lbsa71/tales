# Expansion Plan

## Emotional Density and the Path to ~28,500 Words

This document combines two things that turned out to be the same thing:

1. **The diagnosis** of the emotional-engagement concern both publishers raised ("readers may not feel enough", "middle chapters risk losing a reader"), and
2. **The expansion plan** for bringing the v0.1 draft from 16,294 words toward the 28,500 target.

They turned out to be the same problem because the thing the draft needs more of, emotionally, is also the thing the draft needs more of, structurally. Both are answered by **deploying existing permitted techniques more densely, across more chapters, at more moments per chapter**.

This plan is the roadmap for the revision phase. It should be read alongside [FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md](FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md) (which contains the smaller priority-1 through priority-4 revisions) and [PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT.md](PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT.md) (which contains the broader revision candidates).

## Part 1: The Diagnosis

### The problem is density, not form

The form's bet is that refused interiority can produce stronger emotion than named interiority. Both publishers confirm the bet is working *at the landing points*:

- The inscription in Ch 12 (publisher 1: *lands hard*)
- The word *forgive* on the list in Ch 13 (publisher 1: *lands hard*)
- The crayon drawings in Ch 15 (publisher 1: *lands hard*)
- The final chapter's structural echo (publisher 1: *quietly devastating structural move*)

The problem is the **spaces between landings**. For a short novella where every chapter is about grief, there are only ~6-8 major emotional landing points across 17 chapters. That is approximately one every 2-3 chapters. For a reader not already inside the form's discipline, the gaps between landings read as cold procedure rather than as patient accumulation.

**The diagnosis is not:** *the form is too restrained*.
**The diagnosis is:** *the landing density is too low for the book's length and the reader's patience*.

The difference matters because it points to the solution. Fewer chapters of higher intensity would work (but destroys the book's current architecture). **More landings of the same intensity** would also work, and is achievable without changing the form.

### The "corny vs. rich" distinction

The risk the writer is worried about — making the form corny by reaching for easy emotional solutions — is real. It would happen if we reached for the forbidden techniques:

**Forbidden (these would make the book corny):**

- Interiority verbs (*Erik felt, Erik thought, Erik remembered*)
- Direct emotion (*grief, loss, longing, sorrow, loneliness*)
- Interpretive metaphor (*like a hollow chamber, as if the apartment itself were mourning*)
- Adjective-of-feeling descriptions (*the cold empty kitchen, the unbearable silence*)
- Authorial commentary (*he would never, not yet, somehow, finally*)
- Scene-to-memory transitions (*he remembered the time when...*)
- Emotion assigned to objects (*the lonely wardrobe, the patient sofa*)

None of these can be added. Any of them would turn the book into a different book — one any literary writer could write.

**Permitted and currently underused (these are rich, not corny):**

1. Rule 11 (memory as monofocus action)
2. Texture landings per rule 3a (sweep with material-history details)
3. Physical sensation — the body as a sensing thing
4. Micro-pauses — physical hesitation without interior content
5. Stable anchors referenced more often
6. Self-maintenance beats — the body keeping itself alive
7. Learned rituals from the mother

All seven are already in the form's vocabulary. All seven are currently deployed sparsely. **Deploying all seven more densely is the salvage.** The form does not change; only the density of what it already does changes.

## Part 2: The Seven Techniques in Detail

### Technique 1 — Rule 11 (memory as efficiency), deployed more consistently

**What it does:** The body knows the apartment because it has been here before. That knowing surfaces as efficiency of motion — *the hand that reaches to the known cupboard without looking, the pen that fills in the form without pause, the finger that goes to the exact drawer*.

**Why it is the form's primary emotional engine:** Every rule 11 moment carries years of the mother through one present-tense action. The reader translates *body knows where the cups are kept* into *the body has been in this kitchen many times with the mother*. The translation is the reader's work, which means the reader is doing interpretive labour, which means the reader is engaged. Rule 11 is how the form gets readers to do the emotional work *inside* the form rather than *outside* it.

**Current deployment:** Ch 6 (*open it without looking*), Ch 8 (*each line filled without pause, the pen does not stop*), Ch 17 (*Take a cup down from the cupboard above the coffee machine without looking*). Three explicit moments across 17 chapters. Too sparse.

**Target deployment:** At least one rule 11 moment per chapter, and two or three in the busier chapters. The efficiencies should be small and specific — *the drawer that opens without looking, the chair that the body knows the exact position of, the route through the hallway that the feet already know*.

**Signal words:** *without hesitation, without looking, without counting, without pausing, straight to, without searching, in the usual place, where they are kept, without thinking about it*.

### Technique 2 — Texture landings (rule 3a applied more thoroughly)

**What it does:** One specific item per descriptive passage gets a sentence of material-history detail. The rug frayed at the centre where the path from the sofa to the door has worn a track. The mirror clouded where hot water has risen for years. The book with a paper slip marking a place a third of the way through. The cardigan with sleeves worn thin at the cuffs.

**Why it carries emotion:** Each texture landing is a compressed piece of the mother's life. The reader sees the object's wear and translates that wear into years of use. The translation is silent; the prose never interprets.

**Current deployment:** After the v0.1.2/3 sweep audit, most chapters now have one landing per descriptive passage. Coverage is good but not complete.

**Target deployment:** Two or three texture landings per chapter, not one — one on a structural object (wardrobe, shelf, table) and one or two on **small objects the body actually touches** (a specific plate, a specific button, a specific sheet of paper, a specific doorknob). The small-object landings carry more emotional density because they are at the scale of the body's contact with the apartment.

**Texture vocabulary to draw from:** *frayed, creased, spotted, clouded, chipped, cracked, yellowed, faded, worn, split, warped, stained, dented, scratched, dried, smoothed, dog-eared, indented, rubbed, polished, sunken, peeling, bleached, darkened, thinned at the creases, warm at the handle, soft at the corners, held open more than once*.

### Technique 3 — Physical sensation, the body as a sensing thing

**What it does:** The body is having physical experiences constantly — the weight of the coat, the warmth of the kitchen, the cold of the hallway, the texture of fabric, the smell of the wardrobe, the hum of the fridge, the tick of the kettle. The form permits all of these because they are *present-tense observations of physical facts*.

**Why it carries emotion:** Sensation is what a body has before it has thoughts. When the prose renders a body's sensations without interpretation, the reader recognises the body as a body and connects to it at the physical level — the level that does not require emotion-naming to engage.

**Current deployment:** Very sparse. *The cup is warm in a hand* (Ch 1 and Ch 17) is one of the only explicit sensory-physical moments in the whole draft. This is a massive missed opportunity.

**Target deployment:** One physical-sensation beat per chapter at minimum, and more in scenes where the body is handling objects. The body should be felt as a body in every chapter.

**Examples of what to add:**
- The coat's weight when put on
- The kitchen warm from the radiator; the hallway colder
- The keys in the right pocket felt through the fabric of the coat
- The list in the inside pocket felt against the chest
- The wardrobe's smell on opening the doors
- The sound of the kettle beginning to tick before it clicks off
- The weight of a book in a hand
- The smooth dusty top of the wardrobe when a hand reaches up
- The grain of the kitchen table under the pen when writing the list
- The cold of the fridge door when it opens
- The softness of the rug underfoot when sitting on the floor
- The hard wood of the bench at the civil registry under the thighs
- The chill of the metal handrail on the stairs

These are mundane. That is the point. The body is a body going through a day at a body's speed.

### Technique 4 — Micro-pauses (physical hesitation without interior content)

**What it does:** The body sometimes stops. Not because of a thought — the prose cannot render thoughts. The body stops because its hand is holding something and the hand has not yet put it down. Or because its feet are at a threshold and the feet have not yet crossed.

**Why it carries emotion:** A pause without an explanation is a moment the reader fills in. The reader supplies what the body is feeling by supplying what the body *might be* feeling during the pause. This is the same mechanism as the rule 11 translation — reader labour is reader engagement.

**Current deployment:** Minimal. The *long time* sitting at the kitchen table after the phone call (Ch 13) is the clearest example. There are a few others (*hold the list, read it, hold it*) but most chapters move smoothly from action to action.

**Target deployment:** One micro-pause per chapter, placed at a moment where stopping would be physically natural — before putting something down, before opening a door, before answering a question, before turning the page of the form, before pressing a button.

**Forms the pause can take:**
- Repetition of an action word (*hold the letter. read it. hold it.*)
- A brief observation during the pause (*stand at the counter with the glass in the hand. the water in the glass settles.*)
- An extended action rendered in small steps (*put the key in the lock. turn the key. pause with the door unlocked. open the door.*)
- A numeric pause (*count to three. open the door.*) — though this edges toward interpretive
- The body stopping and another action resuming (*stop wrapping. drink the glass of water at the sink. start wrapping again.*)

### Technique 5 — Stable anchors referenced more often

**What it does:** The elm, the rectangle of light on the counter, the bakery on the corner, the crayon drawings in the child's room (once they appear in Ch 15). These are the things the form has designated as *not glitching*. Their emotional function is to give the reader something that can be trusted.

**Why it carries emotion:** In a form where everything else is drifting, the stable anchors are where the reader's attention rests. Each reference to the elm is a small reassurance: *this is still here*. The emotional weight comes from the stability, not from interpretation of the stability.

**Current deployment:** The elm appears in Ch 1, Ch 6, Ch 13, Ch 17. The rectangle of light in the same four chapters. The bakery only in Ch 17. The drawings only in Ch 15. The references are concentrated at the beginning and the end.

**Target deployment:** Brief anchor references scattered across the middle chapters. Not substantial — a single sentence. The body glimpses the elm through the window while on hold in Ch 3. The body passes the bakery on the walk to the civil registry in Ch 8. The rectangle of light on the counter briefly registered in Ch 5 while drinking water. These cost almost nothing but reinforce the reader's grip on the book.

### Technique 6 — Self-maintenance beats

**What it does:** The body eats, drinks, sleeps, washes, dresses. A body in grief still does these things. Showing them — without interpretation — is a way of saying *the body is continuing to be a body* without saying *the body is grieving*.

**Why it carries emotion:** The continuation itself is the emotional content. A body that is still eating after a parent has died is a body that is going on. The prose does not have to say *the body continues*; the prose just has to *show* the body continuing.

**Current deployment:** Compressed or offscreen across most chapters. Ch 13's *Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.* is the clearest moment of self-maintenance in the draft. Ch 7 and Ch 16 (at the own apartment) have some self-maintenance. Most middle chapters elide it entirely.

**Target deployment:** A brief self-maintenance beat per chapter, or every other chapter. These can be tiny — one or two sentences. The body washes its hands after the fridge clearance in Ch 2. The body drinks a glass of water after the phone call in Ch 3. The body sits on the balcony chair for a minute after folding the last dress in Ch 4. The body eats a biscuit from a tin found in a cupboard in Ch 5. The body washes its face at the bathroom sink in Ch 6. Each beat is *still here*.

### Technique 7 — Learned rituals from the mother

**What it does:** The body has learned things from the mother over the course of a life — how to make coffee, how to fold clothes, how to stir a pot, how to wrap a parcel, how to make a bed, how to rinse a cup. When the body performs these learned rituals in the mother's apartment, it is performing the mother's hand.

**Why it is the most delicate of the techniques and the most potent:** The procedure itself is the intimacy. The prose cannot say *mother taught this*; the prose can only describe the procedure with such specificity that the reader recognises it as a *learned* procedure rather than a generic action. The specificity is the intimacy.

**Current deployment:** Ch 1's coffee-making scene has a faint rule 11 signal (*spoon two spoonfuls into the cup* — *two*, a learned measurement). Most other chapters do not lean on this.

**Target deployment:** One learned-ritual moment per chapter where the body is doing domestic work. Render the actions with the specificity that implies *this is how it has always been done here*.

**Examples:**
- Ch 2: the body wraps plates in a specific order (big plates first, small plates on top, saucers in a separate layer)
- Ch 4: the body folds clothes with a specific method (fold in thirds, not in half; lay flat before stacking)
- Ch 8: the body fills out the civil registry form in a specific order (last line first, then top line, then middle, because that is the order mother always filled forms)
- Ch 10: the body clears a freezer by taking the top shelf first (warmest first), then the second (colder), then the door (hardest to dislodge)

Each of these is a procedural detail that carries *from the mother* without saying so.

## Part 3: The Expansion Plan

The expansion target is approximately 28,500 words (the original IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN target). The current draft is 16,294 words. The gap is ~12,200 words.

**This is achievable through the seven techniques, chapter by chapter.** What follows is the per-chapter breakdown with word-count targets and specific techniques to deploy. Each chapter target assumes:

- **Rule 11 moments:** 1-3 per chapter (each ~20-40 words)
- **Texture landings:** 2-3 per chapter (each ~30-80 words)
- **Physical sensation:** 1-2 per chapter (each ~15-40 words)
- **Micro-pauses:** 1-2 per chapter (each ~15-30 words)
- **Stable anchor references:** 1 per chapter (each ~10-20 words)
- **Self-maintenance beats:** 1 per chapter (each ~30-80 words)
- **Learned rituals:** 1 per chapter (each ~20-50 words)
- **Procedural expansion:** the existing procedural scenes given more time and texture (50-300 words per chapter)

Not every chapter deploys all seven. Some chapters lean on two or three. The distribution is chapter-specific.

### What the expansion is not

**It is not:** adding new plot material, adding new dialogue, adding new characters, adding memory passages, adding explanation, adding commentary, adding metaphor, adding interiority, adding emotion-naming, or adding anything from the forbidden list in Part 1.

**It is:** slowing the existing scenes, adding texture to the existing descriptions, rendering the existing actions with more specificity, giving the existing moments more weight through the seven permitted techniques. The chapters after expansion will cover the *same ground* as the current chapters. They will just cover it more slowly and with more density.

### The risk of the expansion

**Editorial fragility** (publisher 2's warning) is the single largest risk. Every added sentence has to be evaluated against the form's discipline:

- Does it break the bare-verb imperative default?
- Does it introduce interiority?
- Does it name an emotion?
- Does it add a metaphor or comparison?
- Does it commit to interpretation?
- Does it disturb the calm register?
- Does it cascade multiple observations in a list?
- Does it sweep without landing?

If any answer is yes, the sentence is wrong and must be rewritten. If all answers are no, the sentence is within the form.

**The second risk** is that added density flattens the voice — too many landings per chapter becomes its own kind of list. The mitigation is variation: rule 11 moments in some places, physical sensation in others, self-maintenance beats in others, texture landings in still others. No one technique dominates any chapter. The chapter reads as a sweep through *multiple* permitted techniques rather than as a pile of one technique.

**The third risk** is that the expansion turns the novella into a longer but flatter book. The current compression has its own intensity. Losing compression in exchange for length is a bad trade. The mitigation is to let the landing density vary across the expanded length — some paragraphs dense with texture, some paragraphs faster scans between landings. The book should breathe.

### Order of operations

The expansion should not be done as a single pass across all 17 chapters. That risks building up drift. Instead:

1. **Test the form on one chapter.** Expand Ch 3 (The Utility Company) — the chapter most at risk of reading as cold procedure — from 899 to ~1,400 words using 4-5 of the seven techniques. Read the result end-to-end against the current version. Confirm the expansion feels like *the same chapter but richer* rather than *a different chapter*.

2. **If Ch 3 works, expand Ch 4 (The Clothes) and Ch 5 (The Will).** These are the other middle chapters most in need of engagement work. Check the total word count progression and the voice consistency.

3. **Expand the bureaucratic chapters (Ch 8, 9, 10, 11).** These are the ones most at risk of reading as flat procedure. They are also the ones where the seven techniques can add the most — rule 11 on route knowledge, learned ritual on form-filling, texture on the objects of bureaucracy.

4. **Expand the hardest chapters (Ch 12, 14, 15).** These already have strong landings; the expansion is about giving the existing landings more room rather than adding new ones.

5. **Expand the own-apartment chapters (Ch 7, 13, 16).** These are the chapters with permission to dwell on self-maintenance. The expansion is about letting the body be a body at home.

6. **Expand the bookends (Ch 1, 2, 17).** These are the most voice-load-bearing. Expand them last, after the form of the expansion has been established.

7. **Read the whole draft end-to-end.** Confirm the expansion has not flattened the voice. If it has, some additions should be rolled back.

8. **Run the voice pass (Strategy Phase 3) on the expanded draft.** Check every new sentence against the forbidden-word list and the six-point checklist.

### Time estimate

The expansion is a substantial piece of work — approximately the scope of drafting the original middle chapters, because what is being added is equivalent to drafting new material (even though the new material is interleaved with the existing).

Realistic estimate:
- Ch 3 test expansion: 1 session
- Ch 4, 5 expansions: 2 sessions
- Ch 8, 9, 10, 11 expansions: 2-3 sessions
- Ch 12, 14, 15 expansions: 2 sessions
- Ch 7, 13, 16 expansions: 2 sessions
- Ch 1, 2, 17 expansions: 1-2 sessions
- Read-through + voice pass: 1 session
- **Total: approximately 11-13 sessions of focused work.**

For context, the original drafting of the 17 chapters (Sessions 4, 5, 6) took three sessions. The expansion is roughly 3-4x that work because it is more careful — each added sentence has to be evaluated for form compliance and for emotional fit.

### The three priority FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md items

They are all chapter-specific applications of the seven techniques. Doing them inside the expansion is more efficient than doing them as a separate pass.

However, **if the decision is to NOT expand to 28,500 words**, the three priority items should be done as a standalone small revision pass. See FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md for the standalone version.

## Part 4: The Two Paths

At this point there is a binary decision:

### Path A: Small revision only (~16,500-17,000 words)

- Do the three priority items from FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md
- Do the inscription text revision
- Accept the compression as form-appropriate
- Submit to novella-friendly publishers (Fitzcarraldo, Dorothy, Dalkey, Transit)
- The book remains ~16-17k words, a short novella

**Pros:** Faster to completion. Preserves the compression as a deliberate formal choice. Lower risk of flattening the voice.

**Cons:** Leaves the middle-chapter engagement concern less thoroughly addressed. Leaves the novella-length industry concern unaddressed. Constrains the publisher target set to short-form houses.

### Path B: Full expansion (~28,500 words) — this document's recommendation

- Do the expansion as laid out in Part 3
- Do the Ch 13 photograph beat and other FEEDBACK_RESPONSE.md items inside the expansion
- Do the inscription text revision during the Ch 12 expansion
- Submit to a broader publisher target set
- The book is a full novella at ~28-30k words

**Pros:** Addresses both publisher concerns directly (middle engagement + novella length). Opens the publisher target set beyond the specifically-short-form houses. Gives the form room to breathe at its natural pace rather than at compressed pace. Deploys the seven underused techniques that both publishers implicitly asked for.

**Cons:** Substantial revision work (11-13 sessions). Risk of flattening the voice during expansion. Requires careful preservation of the compression's intensity inside the expanded length.

### The recommendation

**Path B, with Ch 3 as the test chapter.**

Rationale:
- Both publishers flagged middle-chapter engagement. Expansion addresses this directly.
- Both publishers flagged novella-length industry concerns. Expansion addresses this directly.
- The seven techniques are all already in the form. The expansion is continuous with the work already done, not a new direction.
- The Ch 3 test allows for early validation. If the Ch 3 expansion reads as the same chapter but richer, the approach is confirmed. If it reads as a different chapter or as a flattened version, the approach needs reconsidering before committing to the full pass.
- The form's gamble — that refused interiority can produce strong emotion — is more likely to pay out at full density than at current sparse density. The publisher feedback is the signal that the density should be higher.

## Summary

**The emotional engagement concern both publishers raised is a density problem, not a form problem.** The form is working where it is allowed to work — at the landing points. The spaces between landings are too sparse, which makes the middle of the book read as cold procedure.

**The solution is to deploy seven already-permitted techniques more densely across all chapters:** rule 11 (memory as efficiency), texture landings (per rule 3a), physical sensation (body as sensing thing), micro-pauses (physical hesitation), stable anchors (referenced more often), self-maintenance beats (body continuing to be a body), and learned rituals from the mother (procedural specificity as intimacy).

**None of the seven techniques breaks the form.** All seven are already in the form's vocabulary. All seven are currently underused.

**Deploying the seven techniques at target density expands the draft from 16,294 to approximately 28,500 words.** This matches the original IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN word-count target, addresses the novella-length industry concern, and provides the emotional engagement the publisher feedback asked for — all while preserving the form's discipline.

**The expansion is not corny.** Corny would require reaching for forbidden techniques (interiority, metaphor, named emotion, interpretation). The seven techniques above are all physical, material, efficient, or procedural — not interpretive.

**The recommended next step** is to test-expand Ch 3 to ~1,400 words and confirm that the expansion reads as the same chapter but richer. If the test works, the plan scales across the 17 chapters.
