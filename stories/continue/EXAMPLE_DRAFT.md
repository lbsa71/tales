# Example Draft

## Calibration Excerpts for "Continue"

These are short draft fragments used to test and calibrate the voice against NARRATOR_VOICE.md and STYLE_GUIDE.md. They are not the novella. They are tests of whether the eleven rules can hold across actual sentences before the full first draft begins.

Each excerpt names the chapter and the specific formal decision it is trying to demonstrate. Use them as calibration, not as canon. Anything here is revisable.

---

## 1. Chapter 1 — The Key (closing)

**Demonstrating:** Baseline register. The original 14-item list placed verbatim so that every later deviation is legible. The single "one sentence too many" about the wall phone that is Chapter 1's only tell. Rule 5 transition by observation at the destination.

---

Finish the coffee at the counter. Rinse the cup under the tap and put it back in the cupboard above the coffee machine.

Take paper from the drawer and a pen from the jar on the counter. Sit down at the kitchen table and write the list:

1. Kitchen — pack and clean
2. Cancel utilities
3. Bedroom — clothes to charity
4. Find the will
5. Living room — books
6. Sort photographs
7. Cancel phone line
8. Death certificate — copies
9. Bank — close account
10. Balcony — clear
11. Second room — clear
12. Return library book
13. Final clean
14. Return key

Fold the list once and then once again and put it in the inside pocket of the coat on the back of the chair. Put the coat on and walk to the door.

The phone on the kitchen wall is black, the kind with a cord, the cord coiled once around the hook below the handset, the handset on its cradle where mother left it.

Open the front door, step out, and turn the key in the lock. Drop the key into the right pocket of the coat.

The street is empty.

## 2. Chapter 2 — The Boxes (the tape dispenser glitch)

**Demonstrating:** Rule 10 — glitches placed across sentences, not compressed. The tape-on-counter / picked-up-from-table contradiction sits between two adjacent sentences; neither acknowledges the other; the third sentence continues past without pause.

---

The plates are in the cupboard above the coffee machine. Take them down in stacks of four and set them on the counter.

Tear a sheet of newspaper from the stack and lay it flat. Place the plate in the middle, bring the corners of the paper up and over, and press them around the edge of the plate with the side of a thumb. Pick up the tape, pull an inch, cut it on the dispenser's blade, and press the tape across the seam.

Put the wrapped plate in the box at the feet. Take another sheet of newspaper.

At twelve plates, fold the flaps of the newspaper over the top layer, close the box, run a strip of tape along the seam, and write KITCHEN on the side with the marker from the counter.

---

## 3. Chapter 12 — The Inscription

**Demonstrating:** Rule 8 — past tense and first-person are permitted only inside the italicised inscription passage, contained by italics. The prose around it stays in the present and does not react. Rule 11 in action through *without looking at the titles*. Rule 9 minimal body-agent (*a hand stops*).

---

The books are on the shelves. Take them down four at a time without looking at the titles, wrap them in newspaper, and put them in the boxes.

On the third shelf a hand stops on a hardback. Take it down. The cover is dark cloth, no dust jacket. Turn it to read the spine. Open it at the front.

On the page before the title page is an inscription. The handwriting is the handwriting of the list in the coat pocket.

*I meant to come in the summer. I did not come. I know you waited. I don't know how to be in that room with you and I didn't know then either. I am writing this on the page because I cannot say it. I am sorry I could not say it. I should have come.*

Close the book. Wrap it in a sheet of newspaper and put it in the box with the others. Run a strip of tape along the seam.

Take down the next four books.

## 4. Chapter 14 — The Rooms (the syntactic dissolution)

**Demonstrating:** The first permitted prose-level dissolution. Rooms that should not exist. The courtyard-that-isn't sentence placed flat. The permitted plate-cup-plate sentence using *and* chains. A mislabelled box at the end.

---

Walk from the kitchen through the hallway into the living room, turn left into the bedroom, and turn right into a room with a piano.

The piano is an upright in dark wood. The lid is closed and the stool is tucked in. A music stand holds no music. The room is small. The ceiling is high.

Through the piano room into a small study. A chair is by the wall. A reading lamp is on a side table. A cup of coffee is on the side table next to the lamp, half-drunk. The window looks onto a courtyard. The apartment has no courtyard.

Put a hand on the cup. The coffee is warm.

Turn around. The bedroom is through the doorway. Walk through the bedroom into the living room, the hallway, and the kitchen. The study is not there. The piano room is not there. On the kitchen counter a cup of coffee is warm. Put a hand on it.

Back to the boxes and the newspaper.

Wrap a plate and the plate is a cup and wrap the cup and the cup is a plate and put the plate in the box. Take a cup from the cupboard, wrap it, and put it in the box. Label the box with the marker. The word on the side of the box is WARDROBE.

Take another plate from the cupboard.

## 5. Chapter 17 — Continue (closing)

**Demonstrating:** The first chapter's cadence restored. The three objects (paper, key, photograph) placed on the counter. The unlocked door. The bakery, the red light, the crossing, all rendered as laconic observations at destinations. The final sentence about light moving across a floor.

---

Open the front door with the key and go in.

Walk through the apartment: kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom, second room, bathroom, and balcony. Her things are in their places. The bookshelves are full. The wardrobe is closed. The cups are in the cupboard above the coffee machine. The phone is on the wall.

Fill the kettle and put it on. Take a cup down from the cupboard. Wait for the kettle, pour the water over the coffee, and stir the cup.

The elm is at the window. The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.

Drink the coffee at the counter.

Put the cup in the sink. Take the folded paper from the inside pocket of the coat and put it on the counter. Take the key from the right pocket of the coat and put it beside the paper. Take the photograph from the inside pocket of the coat and put it beside the key.

Walk to the front door. Open it. Do not look back. Do not lock it.

The bakery on the corner is open. The light at the crossing is red. The light changes.

Walk home. Unlock the door and hang the coat on the hook in the hall. Sit down in the chair by the window.

The light through the window moves across the floor.

## Notes on Decisions Made

These are the calibration choices in the excerpts above. They are revisable; if any of them is the wrong call, the correction ripples into THEME_DOSSIERS.md and possibly into STYLE_GUIDE.md.

### Chapter 1

- **The list is quoted in full.** Every later deviation (crossed off without being crossed off, reordered, rewritten, replaced) needs the original 14 items to be legible. An abbreviated list would weaken every subsequent list glitch.
- **The "one sentence too many" is the phone sentence** with the coiled cord and the final clause *where mother left it*. Denotative rather than emotional — it states facts about the phone's physical configuration — but it is the only sentence in the closing that lingers on a detail the chapter did not need to linger on. It is the tell.
- **The chapter ends on *The street is empty.*** This is the canonical form of rule 5's transition-by-observation: the walk down the stairs is not narrated; the observation at the destination implies the going. The emptiness is neutral — a quiet street in the afternoon — not a glitch. Chapter 1 has no glitches.

### Chapter 2

- **The glitch is spread across the chapter, not across adjacent sentences.** The tape dispenser's location is observed twice: *Reach for the tape on the counter.* during the first packing sequence, and *The tape dispenser is on the table.* in the end-of-chapter room-state scan, separated by roughly twenty sentences of unrelated work (the fridge clearance, the bin run, the rectangle of light). Neither observation acknowledges the other. A reader tracking the tape assembles the displacement privately. A reader not tracking it passes through. The earlier draft's *The tape is on the counter. Pick it up from the table.* is superseded: the adjacency staged the glitch as a punchline and broke the numb register.
- **KITCHEN is written in capitals.** The capitals represent the physical label. This also sets up the later chapters' mislabelled boxes as typographically visible.
- **The wrapping sequence is one connected sentence** carrying three actions (place, bring, press) through commas + *and*. This is the rule 5 default rhythm.

### Chapter 12

- **The inscription is six sentences.** Longer would tip into speech. Shorter would not hold the weight. It includes both a statement of what was not done (*I did not come*) and a statement of the writing itself (*I am writing this on the page because I cannot say it*) so that the reader understands the inscription is the displaced speech act it is.
- ***I cannot say it* is present tense; *I could not say it* is past.** The only place in the novella where the two tenses sit inside the same small passage. The pairing is deliberate: the form's single permitted admission that the unspoken thing is both then and now.
- **The handwriting is identified by reference to the list.** *The handwriting is the handwriting of the list in the coat pocket.* This ties two established objects (the inscription and the list) together without using the forbidden *his handwriting*. The reader who has been tracking the list since chapter 1 makes the connection.
- **The prose does not react to the inscription.** After the italics close, the book is closed, wrapped, taped. The refusal of reaction is the point.

### Chapter 14

- **The plate-cup-plate sentence uses *and* chains rather than commas.** Commas would feel witty — Lydia-Davis-like. The *and...and...and* construction is what a body continuing its work produces if the continuation itself is breaking. The sentence of someone not stopping.
- ***The apartment has no courtyard* is a single flat sentence after the description.** Not commented on. The next sentence is the warm cup.
- **The warm coffee appears twice** — in the impossible study and then on the kitchen counter after the walk back and the rooms are gone. The doubling is the chapter's most compact temporal glitch and requires no gloss.
- **The mislabelled box says WARDROBE.** A box full of plates labelled with the name of a room of clothes. The label can change; what matters is that the word on the side of the box does not match what is inside.

### Chapter 17

- **The rectangle-of-light sentence is the one that must be word-for-word what Chapter 1 also says.** *The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.* The reader's recognition of the sentence is the chapter's largest unspoken gesture.
- **The three objects are placed in a specific order:** paper, then key, then photograph. The order is the order of what the coat pocket has been carrying through the novella. The photograph is placed last because it is the most recent addition (Chapter 16) and the most weighted.
- ***Walk home.*** This is a single imperative sentence for the transition. The alternative — elision by observation at the destination — would be *The street is empty again* or similar, but Chapter 1's closing already uses that technique and repeating it would flatten both. A bare imperative carries the walk.
- **The final sentence is *The light through the window moves across the floor.*** A sentence about light moving across a floor, exactly as the synopsis specifies. It does not try to be a final sentence. It is the next sentence.
