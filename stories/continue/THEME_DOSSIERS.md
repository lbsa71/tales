# Theme Dossiers / Stage-Specific Notes

## Per-Chapter Construction Notes for "Continue"

This document expands SYNOPSIS.md chapter by chapter. For each chapter:

- **Position in arc:** which glitch phase, which threads it advances
- **Camera focus:** what the prose lingers on
- **Sensory palette:** the materials and textures the chapter is made of
- **Procedural detail:** the bureaucracy or work that organises the chapter
- **Glitches:** every glitch in the chapter, by category, with construction notes
- **List state:** what the list is doing in this chapter
- **Wall phone state:** silent / picked up / ringing / dead
- **What must NOT be said:** sentences and constructions specifically forbidden in this chapter
- **What the reader should infer:** the load the chapter is asking the reader to carry without help
- **Prose register:** any chapter-specific calibration

Read this in conjunction with NARRATOR_VOICE.md, STYLE_GUIDE.md, THEME_TRACKER.md, and CONCEPTUAL_MAP.md.

**Voice note:** This document is partially scrubbed for the voice migration. All italicised voice examples have been rewritten to the new voice (bare imperatives, object-as-subject observations, no protagonist pronoun). The meta-discussion around the italicised examples may still occasionally use *he/him/his* as shorthand for the protagonist — this is meta-description only, and the actual prose of the novella follows NARRATOR_VOICE.md and STYLE_GUIDE.md rigorously. If a *he* appears outside italics in this file, read it as "the protagonist" in meta and then forget it when drafting.

## Chapter 1 — The Key

**Position in arc:** Phase 1 baseline. The chapter the reader will later use as their reference for what "the apartment" means. The novella's foundation. Length target: ~1,200 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist's hands receiving the key from the landlord. the protagonist's body moving through the rooms. The kitchen counter. The cup. The kitchen window. The elm. The afternoon light. The kitchen table. The piece of paper. The fold. The coat pocket. The key turning in the lock on the way out.

**Sensory palette:**
- The weight of the key.
- The hallway's smell of an apartment that has not been opened for a few days.
- The kitchen window facing the elm; the rectangle of afternoon light on the counter; the shape of the rectangle.
- The cups in the cupboard. The coffee machine. The cup in hand at the counter.
- The dead plants on the balcony. The chair on the balcony.
- The bookshelf in the living room.
- The wardrobe in the bedroom (closed).
- The second room used for storage (boxes, a folded camp bed, things she did not throw out).
- The bathroom (one paragraph at most).
- The kitchen table at which he sits to write the list.
- The coat pocket.

**Procedural detail:**
- The handover with the landlord (a few sentences; the landlord is polite; the lease term is mentioned; the protagonist is told when the apartment must be empty).

**Glitches:** None. This is the absence-of-glitches chapter. The reader's baseline. The only thing that approaches anomaly is the closing inflection: *the phone on the kitchen wall gets one sentence too many.* This is the novella's first tell, and it must be small enough that a first-time reader does not register it as anything more than a slightly long sentence.

**List state:** Written for the first time, at the kitchen table. Fourteen items, in order, in the handwriting of the list. Folded once. Coat pocket. Quoted in full in this chapter. (The reader needs to know the original list precisely so that subsequent degradations are recognisable.)

**Wall phone state:** Silent. On the wall. The chapter ends with one sentence too many about it. Canonical version (see EXAMPLE_DRAFT.md): *The phone on the kitchen wall is black, the kind with a cord, the cord coiled once around the hook below the handset, the handset on its cradle where mother left it.* The principle: it is one beat too many, and it does not name what it is doing.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the protagonist has not been here for a long time. (The reader will infer this from his ease and his ignorance both — see "infer" below.)
- That the cups were mother's. ("Her cups" is allowed in flat denotation; the prose does not lean on it.)
- Anything about mother's death except as the practical condition of being here. There is no funeral scene in the chapter. There is no obituary scene. There is no thinking-about-her scene.
- That the list is a survival mechanism, an attempt at control, etc. The list is a list.
- That the apartment "feels" anything (silent, empty, abandoned, watching, etc.). It is an apartment. the protagonist walks through it.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That a parent of the protagonist's has recently died.
- That the protagonist is competent and methodical.
- That the protagonist is alone in this. (No partner, no sibling, no friend present.)
- That he is going to do tthe work.

**Prose register:** This chapter is the *baseline*. Every sentence is the calmest version of itself. The reader will later, retrospectively, read this chapter for clues. The chapter must reward that re-reading without containing planted clues. Everything is what it is.

## Chapter 2 — The Boxes

**Position in arc:** Phase 1. The first glitch. Length target: ~1,300 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist buying boxes at the hardware shop. The boxes flat-packed. Tape. Newspaper. The walk back. Unfolding the boxes. The first plate. The wrapping. The labelling. The fridge. The bin bags. The going out with the bin bags. The fridge taking the rest of the afternoon. The list, taken out at the kitchen table, the first half of Item 1 crossed off, a sub-item added in the handwriting of the list.

**Sensory palette:**
- Cardboard. The smell of new cardboard. The crease of the fold. The shape of the assembled box.
- The thunk of the tape gun. The pull of the tape. The cut.
- Newsprint smell. The grey of the newspaper.
- The first plate. The weight. Wrapping it. Setting it in the box.
- The fridge interior: smells, the things that have gone off, the things still in date, the things wrapped in foil.
- Bin bags. The trip down to the bins.
- The kitchen counter at the end. The list. Pen.

**Procedural detail:**
- Buying supplies. The hardware shop. The number of boxes (specific number). Tape. The receipt.
- The fridge clearance. The categories he sorts food into.

**Glitches:** One. **Gestural, placed across three sentences per rule 10.** *The tape is on the counter. Pick it up from the table. Pull an inch, cut it on the dispenser's blade, and press the tape across the seam.* Two flat statements contradicting each other, and a third that continues past. The prose does not stop. The next sentence is the next plate.

**List state:** The list is consulted at the kitchen table at the end of the chapter. The first half of Item 1 is crossed off (or some sub-marker; the writer's choice of how exactly partial completion is tracked). A sub-item is added in the same handwriting: *glasses (tomorrow)*, or *finish kitchen*, or similar small clarification. The list is functional. Only one hand has written on it.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. Possibly noticed in passing as the protagonist moves through the kitchen. Not described in any detail.

**What must NOT be said:**
- *Wasn't the tape dispenser on the counter? Pick it up from the table anyway.* Forbidden. The glitch is not commented on, even as a question.
- *The tape dispenser on the counter. Strange. Pick it up from the table.* Forbidden. The word *strange* is forbidden.
- Any thought about the food in the fridge as "her food", "the last food she", etc. The food is food. It goes in the bin bags.
- Any commentary about how "good he is at this".

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the protagonist has done physical work like this before, that he has competence, that he is reliable.
- That he is not going to finish today and he knows it and he is not anxious about it.

**Prose register:** Light momentum. The chapter has a rhythm of cleared work. The reader is allowed to settle into the satisfying-domestic register. The single gestural glitch sits inside that satisfaction without disturbing it consciously.

## Chapter 3 — The Utility Company

**Position in arc:** Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition. The first procedural glitch and the first list glitch in a single chapter. Length target: ~1,400 words.

**Camera focus:** The phone (the mobile, not the wall phone). The hold music. Drawers. Shelves. Stacks of paper. The folder of bills. The letterhead. The library book on the way out the door. The walk to the library. The librarian. The walk home.

**Sensory palette:**
- The phone against an ear during the hold.
- The grain of the wood in the drawer fronts.
- Old paper, receipts, warranty cards, the radio warranty, a phone bill from years ago.
- The folder of bills. The plastic sleeves. The order of the bills.
- The library book in hand. The fine the librarian waives. The librarian's small smile.

**Procedural detail:**
- The call to the utility company. Hold time (twenty minutes, specified). They need the account number. The polite voice. *We're looking into it. We'll get back to you.* the protagonist gives a phone number. He is the reasonable customer.

**Glitches (multiple, transitional chapter):**

1. **Procedural:** The utility company is *looking into it*. The name on the bill has an extra initial. This is the first time bureaucracy in the novella fails to confirm reality.

2. **Spatial:** The bills are in a folder *in the bedroom*. But to get to the bedroom, the route goes *through the kitchen*. The first chapter mapped the apartment with kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom — bedroom off the hallway, not through the kitchen. The chapter does not announce the contradiction. The prose simply says: *Walk through the kitchen into the bedroom.* The reader who is paying attention catches the wrongness; the reader who is not, doesn't.

3. **List glitch:** Item 2 has a line through it. the protagonist did not cross it off. He puts the list back. The chapter does not say *he frowned* or *he hesitated*. He puts it back.

**List state:** Item 2 (Cancel utilities) is crossed off in the handwriting of the list, by no hand of his. He looks at it, puts it back. The list is otherwise unchanged.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. the protagonist's call to the utility company is from the mobile.

**What must NOT be said:**
- "The bedroom was on the wrong side of the apartment." Forbidden. The bedroom is through the kitchen. That is the sentence.
- "He stared at the line through Item 2 and felt a small chill." Forbidden.
- Any comment on the librarian as a kind person. The librarian waives the fine. That is what happens.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the apartment the protagonist mapped in Chapter 1 and the apartment he is moving through now are not the same apartment in some small way. The reader cannot yet tell whether it is them, the writer, or the apartment.
- That the list is not entirely under the protagonist's control.
- That the bureaucratic apparatus around the death is going to be more complicated than the practical work of clearing.

**Prose register:** Slightly busier than Chapter 2 because of the call. But the sentences hold their length. The phone hold is given weight: *On hold twenty minutes.* The prose is allowed to sit with the wait.

## Chapter 4 — The Clothes

**Position in arc:** Phase 2 establishing. Length target: ~1,800 words. This chapter has the first sustained slowness and the first object glitch.

**Camera focus:** The wardrobe's interior. Hangers. The shoulders of dresses. The shoulders of coats. The shoes shaped to her feet. Folded clothing in piles on the bed. Bin bags filling. The trip to the charity shop and the discovery that they need hangers; the call to another charity shop. The washing machine. The kitchen drawer with the grocery list in her handwriting. The drying rack on the balcony.

**Sensory palette:**
- Wool. Linen. Cotton. The shoulders of garments. The smell of the wardrobe interior — slightly her, slightly cedar, slightly mothball.
- The shoes on the floor of the wardrobe. The shape they hold.
- The bag in hand, fillable.
- The kitchen drawer: pens, tape, batteries, the small notebook with the grocery list.
- The grocery list, in her handwriting: *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.* (Quoted, italicised, briefly.)
- The washing machine starting.
- The drying rack on the balcony.

**Procedural detail:**
- Calls to two charity shops. One needs hangers. The other only takes clean clothes. He decides to wash everything.
- The washing machine cycle. He sits at the kitchen table and waits.

**Glitches:**

1. **Spatial:** *Walk back into the apartment. The door was not unlocked.* Two sentences per rule 10. Placed inside an unremarked transition between leaving for the charity shop and returning. The chapter does not draw attention to it.

2. **Object:** The drying rack on the balcony. The first chapter described the balcony as having dead plants and a chair. *Folded behind the chair* — the protagonist finds a drying rack. This is a small thing. The reader who remembers the first chapter notices.

3. **Object:** *The three boxes stacked by the front door. He moved them to the car.* The reader is told that the boxes the protagonist packed in Chapter 2 and stacked by the front door are now (a) still by the front door in the present sentence, and (b) already in the car. The chapter does not resolve the contradiction. The next sentence is the next thing.

**List state:** Consulted normally. the protagonist crosses off something on Item 3 (Bedroom — clothes to charity). The list is otherwise stable in this chapter.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. Possibly seen as the protagonist passes through the kitchen.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That he remembered her wearing the dress. That he held it up. That he inhaled it. None of this. He folds. He puts it in the bag.
- That the grocery list was the last thing she wrote. That she had not had time to buy fish. That she would never buy oranges again. None of this. The grocery list is in the drawer. He reads it. He puts it back.
- "Almost as if she had just stepped out." Forbidden. The novella does not do this.
- Any narration of the boxes-in-the-car contradiction. The boxes are by the door. He moved them to the car. Two consecutive sentences. The reader carries the discrepancy.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the protagonist has been here long enough to know the rhythms of the wardrobe.
- That the grocery list in the drawer is one of the most intimate objects in the novella, and that he has just read it and put it back.
- That something is happening with the boxes that he is either not noticing or not commenting on. The reader cannot tell which.

**Prose register:** The slowest chapter so far. Folding clothes is the kind of physical work the prose was made for. The reader should find this chapter quietly satisfying to read. The glitches sit inside that satisfaction.

## Chapter 5 — The Will

**Position in arc:** Phase 2. The chapter that introduces the Will thread. Length target: ~1,500 words.

**Camera focus:** The lawyer on the phone. The metal box on top of the wardrobe (which is not there). Searching everywhere. The second room (now with a desk in it). The drawer of the desk. The blank notebook. The pencil. The wardrobe (full again). The bags (gone). His hands beginning to fold again.

**Sensory palette:**
- The lawyer's voice on the phone: practical, distant.
- The top of the wardrobe (he reaches up). The dust. The absence of the metal box.
- The second room: the things that were stored in it, plus a desk that wasn't there, plus the desk drawer's interior — pencil, blank notebook, nothing else.
- The wardrobe interior: full of clothes again. The same clothes? The prose does not say.
- His hands: folding.

**Procedural detail:**
- The call with the lawyer (asking after the will). The lawyer says it should be in the apartment. the protagonist thanks them.
- Calls the utility company again. *Still looking into it.*
- The lawyer's office is closed by the time he tries to call back.

**Glitches:**

1. **Object:** The metal box on top of the wardrobe is not there. (He had cleared the wardrobe in the previous chapter; this absence could be either the clearing or the apartment removing the box. The novella does not say which.)

2. **Spatial:** The desk in the second room. *The second room has a desk in it.* One flat sentence. The chapter does not say whether the desk was remembered or not — that would be interior commentary. The desk is there.

3. **Object:** The wardrobe is full. The bags are gone.

4. **List glitch:** A new Item 15 in the handwriting of the list: *sort desk.* The reader is given two possible readings: the protagonist has unconsciously added the item, or the list has gained an item. The prose does not adjudicate.

**List state:** Sixteen items (fourteen plus two additions: the sub-item from Ch. 2 if it counts, plus the new Item 15: *sort desk*). Or fifteen, if the Ch. 2 sub-item is treated as a sub-item. The exact count is for the writer to keep track of, but the reader is meant to start losing track.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. the protagonist makes outgoing calls but the wall phone is not used.

**What must NOT be said:**
- "Had he added the item himself?" Forbidden interior question.
- "The desk almost looked familiar." Forbidden "almost".
- Any acknowledgement that the wardrobe being full again is impossible.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the apartment is no longer a fixed background; that the work of clearing is up against something.
- That the protagonist is not going to comment on this.
- That the will is going to be a recurring problem.

**Prose register:** Slightly busier — phone calls, searches. The chapter contains the most "walking around the apartment" of any chapter so far, and the apartment uses it.

## Chapter 6 — The Photographs

**Position in arc:** Phase 2. The first relational glitch and the first appearance of the wall phone in active mode. Length target: ~1,800 words.

**Camera focus:** The shoebox. the protagonist on the floor. The photographs going through a hands. The piles he makes (and unmakes). The cups in the wrong cupboard. The wall phone. The list, with *call Marianne* below Item 6.

**Sensory palette:**
- The shoebox: cardboard, lid, contents.
- The photographs: the texture of old prints, the white borders, the small handwritten dates on the back of some, the curling corners of some.
- The floor of the living room.
- The cups in the new cupboard.
- The wall phone: the receiver in hand, the dial tone.

**Procedural detail:**
- None. This is the chapter with the least procedural content. It is the most purely domestic chapter so far. Photographs, coffee, a phone picked up and put down.

**Glitches:**

1. **Object/spatial:** The cups are in a different cupboard. He opens the right one without hesitating.

2. **Relational:** The photograph of him and mother, found in the bedside drawer in this chapter and pocketed, *is now in the shoebox*, and the prose describes three people in it. (Construction: at the beginning of the photograph sequence, the protagonist finds the photograph in the bedside drawer and puts it in the coat pocket. Later in the same chapter, while going through the shoebox, the prose lists what is in the shoebox and includes "the photograph of him and mother and a woman". The reader who is paying attention catches it. The reader who is not, will catch it next chapter or not at all.)

3. **List glitch:** Below Item 6 (Sort photographs), in handwriting that is his but looser, the line: *call Marianne.*

**List state:** New entry below Item 6: *call Marianne.* In handwriting the protagonist recognises as his but slightly loose. He does not call anyone of that name. He puts the list back.

**Wall phone state:** First active appearance. He picks up the wall phone. There is a dial tone. He puts it back.

**What must NOT be said:**
- Who Marianne is. The name is on the list once. The novella does not explain it.
- That the protagonist recognised the name. (He may or may not. The prose does not say.)
- That the third person in the photograph could be Marianne. (Possibly. The prose does not say.)
- Any thought about father.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the photographs are intimate and that the protagonist is going through them without (visible) emotion.
- That a name that is not the protagonist's mother's has just appeared in the novella, and that it is in the singular handwriting of someone close enough to know, and that it is on a list of practical tasks that contains nothing else of that kind.
- That the photograph of two people now contains three.

**Prose register:** The slowest chapter so far. The going-through-photographs sequence wants to take its time. The wall phone moment is brief and unweighted; one paragraph at most.

## Chapter 7 — The Neighbour

**Position in arc:** Phase 3 opening. The chapter where the glitches cross into the protagonist's own apartment. Length target: ~1,700 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist at home. The protagonist's own kitchen. The protagonist's own window. The protagonist's own street. The packing of *the protagonist's own plates*. The doorbell. The neighbour. The conversation. The street detail that doesn't match. Bed.

**Sensory palette:**
- the protagonist's kitchen: cleaner, brighter, less full than mother's kitchen.
- The protagonist's own breakfast — what he eats, how he eats it.
- The protagonist's own street: the buildings opposite, the cars, a corner shop, a tree (not the elm).
- The protagonist's own kitchen cupboards: the plates, the wrapping paper or newspaper, the box on the counter.
- The doorbell: the specific sound of the doorbell of the protagonist's own apartment.
- The neighbour: described in physical terms, age, posture, the way they fill the doorframe, the way they stand back when they speak.
- The protagonist's own bed.

**Procedural detail:** None of clearing-the-mother's-apartment kind. The chapter is *not* in mother's apartment. This is the form's first major rupture: a clearing chapter that does not contain the apartment being cleared.

**Glitches:**

1. **Reverse / object:** the protagonist takes plates from the protagonist's own kitchen cupboard and wraps them in newspaper and puts them in a box on the protagonist's own counter. He works for ten minutes. He makes coffee. He drinks it at the window. The box sits on the counter, half full. *The prose does not remark on this.*

2. **Relational/spatial:** A detail on the protagonist's own street doesn't match. (Specific choice: a sign that should say one thing says another; or a building opposite has the wrong number of windows; or a tree he has looked at every day his whole adult life is a different species. Choose one. Make it small and specific. Do not announce it.)

3. **Relational:** The neighbour. the protagonist and the neighbour have never spoken. The neighbour has heard him moving around at night. The neighbour asks if he is all right. the protagonist says he is fine. He sleeps well. The neighbour nods.

**List state:** *The list stays in the coat by the door.* The only chapter in which the list is not consulted at all. The prose may name this once: *The list stays in the coat by the door.* That is the entire treatment.

**Wall phone state:** Not present. (the protagonist is not in mother's apartment in this chapter.)

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the protagonist is unsettled by the neighbour's question.
- That the neighbour is the first person to register that something is happening to him.
- That he packed the protagonist's own plates because the work has overflowed its container.
- "He could not say why he was packing the protagonist's own plates." Forbidden interior question.
- That the box on the counter at the end of the chapter is something the reader should pay attention to. The prose says the box is on the counter. That is what the prose says.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the work has crossed a boundary that the work itself does not respect.
- That the protagonist is *being heard* moving around at night by his downstairs neighbour, that this means he is not sleeping the way he says he is, that none of this is on the page directly.
- That the person closest to him in space (the downstairs neighbour, never spoken to before) is the only person who has registered, however indirectly, what is happening.

**Prose register:** Quietly devastating. This chapter's entire job is to destabilise the reader's sense that the apartment was the problem. The prose stays calm. It is the calmest chapter since the first. The discrepancy between calm and content is at its highest.

## Chapter 8 — The Death Certificate

**Position in arc:** Phase 3 fully. Length target: ~1,800 words.

**Camera focus:** The civil registry office. The form. Filling it in. Her date of birth, her full name. Walking out. The apartment. The bookshelves (full). The book with the inscription. The blank inscription page. The wall phone ringing. the protagonist not answering.

**Sensory palette:**
- The registry office: the bench in the waiting area, the form on a clipboard, the pen, the small window with the clerk.
- Filling in the form: each line, the pen scratching, the date.
- The walk back to the apartment: a street, a corner.
- The bookshelves: full. The novella's first explicit recurrence of "things he has cleared, returning".
- The book in hand: a particular book — specifiable, e.g., a hardback novel or a hardback poetry collection. Heavy.
- The inscription page: blank.
- The wall phone: ringing, ringing, ringing.

**Procedural detail:**
- Filling out the death certificate form. (One short paragraph at most. The novella does not linger on the form's content. the protagonist knows the answers and writes them.)
- Number of copies requested. Possibly the fee.

**Glitches:**

1. **Object:** The bookshelves are full. The three boxes of books are stacked and taped by the front door. Two present-tense declarations. The reader supplies the contradiction (the books were packed, the books are back) without the prose ever reaching for a past tense.
   *Cleaner construction: He packed the bookshelves. The bookshelves are full.*

2. **Documentary:** The book with the inscription. *The inscription page is blank.* The inscription is the inscription page that has, until this chapter, been understood (by the protagonist, if he were to think about it; by the reader, who infers it) to contain something. It is blank.

3. **Procedural:** *The phone on the kitchen wall rings. It rings for a long time. It rings and then it stops.* The phone is not answered. The prose does not say so directly; the three sentences carry the not-answering by describing only the ringing.

4. **List glitch:** Item 5 has a line through it that he didn't draw. He puts a question mark next to it. The handwriting of earlier items looks different from his current handwriting.

5. **Temporal anticipation (subtle):** Her date of birth, written by the protagonist on the form, is the first time the reader has seen the date. The chapter does not flag the date. The reader will see the date again, *differently*, in the documentary glitch chapters that follow. Place it now without weight.

**List state:** Item 5 (Living room — books) crossed off, by no hand of his. Question mark next to it. The handwriting of the earlier items looks different. The list is no longer entirely his.

**Wall phone state:** Rings. the protagonist does not answer. It rings for a long time. (How long? The prose can specify a number of rings: "It rings twelve times. It rings fifteen times. He does not count.")

**What must NOT be said:**
- That he could not pick up the phone.
- That the ringing reminded him of the day father died. (The novella's central forbidden sentence.)
- That the inscription must have been written and erased.
- That the bookshelves being full was strange. (They are. The prose says they are.)
- That her date of birth came easily to him. (It did. The prose says he writes them without hesitating.)

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the protagonist knows mother's birthdate without thinking.
- That the wall phone is doing something the novella will not name.
- That the inscription matters. (The reader does not yet know what it is.)
- That the apartment is undoing the work as fast as he can do it.

**Prose register:** Slow. The form-filling sequence is given full attention. The phone-ringing sequence is given white space. The chapter is one of the longer chapters in the book.

## Chapter 9 — The Bank

**Position in arc:** Phase 3. The most procedurally dense chapter. Length target: ~1,700 words.

**Camera focus:** The bank lobby. The clerk. The documents. The discrepancy. The phone call from the lobby to the solicitor. The pause on the line. The walk back to the apartment. The wardrobe (truly empty). The drying rack (dry). The cup (one). The list (sixteen items, Item 16 says: *the phone*).

**Sensory palette:**
- The bank: marble, plastic, the queue, the ticket, the seat, the desk.
- The clerk: their hands, their politeness, the documents they ask for.
- The documents: the death certificate, the birth certificate, an ID. Spread on the desk.
- The discrepancy: her name on the birth certificate. Her name on the death certificate. *Different middle name. Or the same.*
- The lobby. The mobile in hand. The solicitor's number. The pause.
- The apartment: the wardrobe, looking at it. Empty in a way that is *not the way he left it.*
- The kitchen: one cup in the cupboard.
- The kitchen table: the list.

**Procedural detail:**
- The bank's process: what the protagonist needs (will, proof of next of kin, account number, ID). What he has. What he doesn't. What they tell him to come back with.
- The phone call to the solicitor. The solicitor asks which property. the protagonist gives the address. The solicitor pauses.

**Glitches:**

1. **Documentary:** Her name on the birth certificate has a different middle name than the death certificate. *Or the same.* The prose's *or the same* is the chapter's most carefully placed sentence. It is not the protagonist's hesitation; it is the prose's. (This is the most explicit prose-level wrongness yet, and it is permitted because the prose is reporting what is on a document the protagonist is currently looking at.)

2. **Procedural:** The solicitor pauses on the line.

3. **Object:** The wardrobe is empty. *Not cleared. Empty in the way of a wardrobe that has been empty for a long time.* (Borderline construction; acceptable because it is naming a physical state rather than a feeling.)

4. **Object:** The dry rack on the balcony.

5. **Object:** One cup in the cupboard.

6. **List glitch:** Sixteen items. the protagonist wrote fourteen. He added one (the *sort desk* of Chapter 5). Sixteen. He doesn't try to count what's missing. Item 16 says: *the phone.*

**List state:** Sixteen items. Item 16 (in handwriting that may not be his): *the phone.*

**Wall phone state:** Silent. the protagonist is in the apartment and looking at the kitchen but not picking up the wall phone. The list now contains *the phone* as Item 16, and the wall phone is on the wall, and these two facts sit next to each other on the page without comment.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the discrepancy in middle names was unsettling.
- That the solicitor's pause was significant.
- That the wardrobe being truly empty was worse than the wardrobe being full.
- That Item 16 was an instruction to him.
- The word *the phone* outside the list.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the documents are no longer reliable.
- That the solicitor knows something.
- That the protagonist has been doing tthe work for an unspecified length of time and the work has unmade itself enough that he is now down to one cup.
- That Item 16 is going to be relevant.

**Prose register:** Procedural in the bank scene; slower in the apartment scene. The transition from the bank lobby to the apartment is by observation at the destination, per rule 5. (*The wardrobe is empty.*) The novella refuses to bridge.

## Chapter 10 — The Freezer

**Position in arc:** Phase 3. Most temporally distorted chapter. Length target: ~1,600 words.

**Camera focus:** The freezer. The contents of the freezer. The labels on the contents. The bagging of the contents. The trip down to the bins. The return. The first glass. The newspaper around the glass. The newspaper's date. The wall phone ringing. The pickup. The silence. The dial tone. The list with Item 7 rewritten.

**Sensory palette:**
- The freezer: the cold air rolling out, the labels in masking tape and biro, her handwriting on the labels.
- Soup. Sauce. The thing in foil with a date on it.
- The bag he carries down to the bins.
- The kitchen, after the freezer. The first glass. The newspaper. The way the glass slips into the cone of newspaper.
- The wall phone: ringing. The walk to it. The receiver. The silence. The dial tone.

**Procedural detail:**
- Defrosting / clearing the freezer is technically a continuation of the kitchen task. The chapter does not call it that. It is just the freezer.

**Glitches:**

1. **Temporal/object:** *The freezer is full.* A single declarative. The reader remembers Chapter 2's fridge clearance (*Bag the contents of the fridge. Carry it down to the bins.*) and notes that the freezer is full in Chapter 10, and carries the contradiction.

2. **Temporal:** Something in foil labelled with a date *that is after she died.* The chapter does not say "after she died". It says: *Something in foil labelled with a date.* The date is given. The reader, who has the protagonist's mother's date of death from the registry chapter (or who infers it from context), recognises the discrepancy.

3. **Temporal:** *Wrap a glass in a sheet of newspaper. The newspaper is today's.* Two sentences; the second states the date flatly.

4. **Procedural:** The wall phone rings. He picks it up. *Silence. Then a dial tone.*

5. **List glitch:** Item 7 has been rewritten. It says: *answer the phone.*

**List state:** Item 7 (Cancel phone line) has been rewritten as *answer the phone.* The handwriting may or may not be his. The chapter does not say.

**Wall phone state:** Rings. the protagonist picks up. Silence. Dial tone. the protagonist puts it back.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the date on the foil meant she had been there after.
- That the newspaper being today's was impossible.
- That the silence on the phone was meaningful.
- "Answer the phone" being read as a command to him from anyone.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the temporal architecture of the apartment is broken.
- That a phone he does not pick up rings, and a phone he does pick up is silent.
- That the list is now writing him.

**Prose register:** This is the chapter where the temporal pressure is highest. The prose holds. The sentences stay in present tense. The freezer scene is given the full slow rendering of any clearance scene. The phone moment is given exactly four sentences and no more.

## Chapter 11 — The Solicitor

**Position in arc:** Phase 3, near the end. Length target: ~1,400 words.

**Camera focus:** The solicitor's office. The folder on the desk. The will. The solicitor's voice. the protagonist sitting in the office. The walk to the apartment. The living room. The sofa he doesn't recognise. The glass-topped coffee table. the protagonist on the floor.

**Sensory palette:**
- The solicitor's office: bookshelves, the lamp, the chair, the framed certificates.
- The folder on the desk. The pages.
- The will: a few sentences. *She left everything in the apartment to him. All of it.*
- The street, briefly, between office and apartment.
- The apartment's living room: the new sofa, the coffee table, the absence of mother's furniture.
- The floor.

**Procedural detail:**
- The will is read aloud, or the protagonist reads it. The prose handles it briefly. The terms are simple.

**Glitches:**

1. **Object/relational:** The furniture in the apartment is completely different. A sofa that is not mother's. A glass-topped coffee table. *Sit on the floor next to the sofa. The apartment is legally the protagonist's now. None of the furniture in the apartment is mother's.* Three flat sentences; the contradiction (the apartment has been inherited; its contents are no longer recognisable) is not named.

2. The list is not consulted. This is the second chapter (after The Neighbour) in which the list is not used. The list is in the coat pocket; the absence of consultation is itself the chapter's smallest tell.

**List state:** Not consulted. Implicitly: still sixteen items, still as it was in Ch. 10. (The reader does not see it in this chapter.)

**Wall phone state:** Silent. Possibly unmentioned.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the will was a relief or a burden.
- That the sofa was wrong.
- That the apartment had become a stage set.
- "Everything in the apartment was his — and everything in the apartment was unrecognisable." Forbidden em-dash pivot.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the legal architecture of the death has now resolved (the will exists, it is found, its terms are clear) at exactly the same time the physical architecture of the apartment has stopped resolving.
- That the protagonist sits on the floor of an apartment that is now legally his, and that he does not pack.
- That this is the lowest point in the chapter sequence so far.

**Prose register:** Quiet. The solicitor's office is rendered in clean sentences. The apartment is rendered in fewer sentences than usual. The chapter ends without consolation.

## Chapter 12 — The Inscription

**Position in arc:** Phase 4 opening. The first appearance of the prose's permitted dissolution. Length target: ~1,800 words.

**Camera focus:** The bookshelves (back, again). the protagonist packing without looking at titles. His hand on one book. Opening to the inscription page. Reading. Closing the book. The box. Tape. The stairwell. The list, reordered.

**Sensory palette:**
- The bookshelves: the spines, the order, the rhythm of the arm taking books down.
- The book that stops him: a particular weight in the hand.
- The inscription page.
- The tape gun.
- The stairwell: the steps, the worn paint on the wall, the half-flight that is one half-flight too long.

**Procedural detail:**
- Packing books. (The procedure is the procedure of any book-packing task: rows, sizes, orientations.)

**Glitches:**

1. **Object:** The bookshelves are back. (Already an established recurrence; placed without comment.)

2. **Documentary/relational:** *The inscription page is full. His handwriting.* The inscription itself: the closest thing to direct emotional speech in the novella. Italicised. Past tense. Several sentences. *I should have come. I know you waited. I don't know how to be in that room with you.* (The exact text is for the writer to find. The principle: it is short, plain, declarative; it does not contain metaphor; it is the apology that was not made.)

3. **Spatial:** *The stairwell has an extra half-flight.*

4. **Syntactic precursor:** *The sentences in the inscription run long, nest.* (The first syntactic instability, contained inside the protagonist's own words on the inscription page.)

5. **List glitch:** Items reordered. Some he recognises, some he doesn't. One says: *the summer with the door.*

**List state:** Reordered. New entry: *the summer with the door.* (A reference whose meaning the novella does not unlock — it gestures at backstory the reader cannot reach, which is part of how the form refuses interpretation.)

**Wall phone state:** Silent.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the inscription "spilled out of him". The inscription is on a page in a book. He reads it.
- That writing the inscription was impossible. (He didn't, in the present tense of the novella; the inscription is on the page already; the prose does not investigate when or how it got there.)
- That the half-flight was uncanny.
- That *the summer with the door* meant something specific.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the protagonist (somehow, somewhere, at some point) wrote those words on the inscription page of a book mother kept on her shelf, and that the words are an apology, and that the apology is one the novella has been organised around.
- That the membrane between the protagonist's interior and the page has been broken once, in a contained, italicised, past-tense passage, and the prose around the passage stays calm.
- That a phrase from a part of his life the novella will never describe (*the summer with the door*) has just appeared on the list.

**Prose register:** This is the chapter the entire form has been waiting to reach. The prose around the inscription is *cleaner* than any prose so far, because the inscription is doing the work the prose has been holding back. After the inscription passage, the prose returns to baseline. The reader is left with the inscription's voice ringing inside their head and the prose refusing to repeat any of it.

## Chapter 13 — The Phone Call

**Position in arc:** Phase 4. The chapter where the wall phone is finally addressed (and refuses to be). Length target: ~1,600 words.

**Camera focus:** The kitchen drawer. The phone bill. The grocery list (gone). The call to the telephone company. The operator's voice. The wall phone, dead, on the wall. Sitting at the kitchen table. The elm. The light. A long time. Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.

**Sensory palette:**
- The kitchen drawer: pens, batteries, where the grocery list was, a phone bill instead.
- The phone bill: the account number.
- His mobile in hand: dialling, the operator's hold music, the operator.
- The wall phone: silent. Receiver. Dead line.
- The kitchen table. The elm. The angle of the light.
- The protagonist's own apartment. His kitchen. His counter. His dinner. His plate. His bed.

**Procedural detail:**
- Calling the telephone company. The operator looks up the account. *The line was disconnected four months ago.* He says it rang. He picked it up. *The line was disconnected four months ago.* The repetition is the chapter's load.

**Glitches:**

1. **Object/temporal:** The grocery list is gone from the drawer. The phone bill is in its place.

2. **Procedural:** *The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago.* Twice. The prose lets the operator say it twice. The intervention between the two repetitions is one sentence: *The phone rang. The phone was picked up.*

3. **Object:** He picks up the wall phone after the call. *Dead.*

4. **List glitch:** At the protagonist's own table (he has gone home). Some items crossed off, some not, some he doesn't understand. Near the bottom: *forgive.* He cannot tell if it is the handwriting of the list.

**List state:** At the protagonist's own kitchen table, looking at it. *Forgive*, near the bottom, in handwriting he cannot identify. He puts the list down.

**Wall phone state:** Dead. (Confirmed.)

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the line being disconnected four months ago meant mother had been dead for four months. (The reader assembles this. The prose does not.)
- That *forgive* was a command.
- That the protagonist's own kitchen, after the apartment, was different.
- That sitting at the kitchen table was sitting with her.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the wall phone has been ringing, picking up, going silent, and is in fact (according to the only documentary record the novella permits) dead.
- That something on the list says *forgive*.
- That the protagonist now sits at the protagonist's own table and the list is in hand.
- That dinner happens. Counter. Plate. Bed. the protagonist's body keeps eating.

**Prose register:** The chapter contains the operator's repeated sentence as its only doubled line. After the apartment scene, the chapter ends in a sequence of fragments at the protagonist's own apartment: *Home. Dinner. Counter. Plate. Bed.* This is the closest the early chapters' rhythms come to fragmenting; the fragmentation is permitted because the protagonist's body is doing things in sequence and the prose is recording them.

## Chapter 14 — The Rooms

**Position in arc:** Phase 4 deepest. The chapter with the highest motif density and the first overt syntactic dissolution. Length target: ~2,300 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist walking through the apartment. Kitchen, hallway, living room. Left into the bedroom. Right into a room with a piano. Through the piano room into a small study with a courtyard window. The chair. The reading lamp. The half-finished cup of coffee, warm. Walking back. No piano room. No study. The coffee on the kitchen counter, warm. Packing. The plates and the cups swapping inside sentences.

**Sensory palette:**
- The hallway. The known doors.
- The piano in the piano room: an upright, brown wood, the keys, the music stand empty.
- The small study: the chair, the reading lamp, the courtyard window (the apartment has no courtyard; the prose says, neutrally, "the courtyard window"), the half-finished cup of coffee, warm.
- The walk back: the same sequence in reverse, with the piano room and the study no longer present.
- The kitchen: a cup of coffee on the counter, warm.
- The packing: a plate, then a cup, then a plate.

**Procedural detail:**
- Packing. The procedure is recognisable. The objects are not.

**Glitches:**

1. **Spatial:** *Right into a room with a piano.* The room has not existed before in the novella. The piano room is given full sensory rendering — the upright, the keys, the music stand. The novella spends one paragraph on it.

2. **Spatial:** *Through the piano room into a small study with a courtyard window — the apartment has no courtyard.* This is the chapter's most overt spatial impossibility. The prose names it, neutrally, in a single clause. (Note: this is one of the few permitted uses of an em-dash-equivalent — but in this style guide, even here, it should be a comma or a period: *Through the piano room into a small study with a courtyard window. The apartment has no courtyard.* Two sentences. Flat.)

3. **Temporal/object:** The half-finished cup of coffee in the small study. *The coffee is warm.*

4. **Spatial:** Walking back, the rooms are gone.

5. **Object:** *The coffee on the kitchen counter is warm.* (The coffee from the small study has somehow remained — or the warm coffee has migrated to the kitchen — or there is and was always a warm coffee on the kitchen counter. The novella does not say. It says: *The coffee on the kitchen counter is warm.*)

6. **Syntactic:** *Wrap a plate and the plate is a cup and wrap the cup and the cup is a plate.* The novella's first overt sentence-level dissolution. One sentence using bare-verb imperatives linked by *and*. Permitted because the form has earned it through 13 chapters of refusal.

7. **Syntactic:** *Labels a box with a word that doesn't match what's inside.*

8. **List glitch:** *A paragraph in the handwriting of the list. Some words legible, some not.* The list is no longer a list.

**List state:** A paragraph. Not numbered. Not items. His handwriting. Some words legible, some not. The list is moving toward its end-state.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. The chapter does not visit the wall phone. (After Chapter 13's confirmation that the line is dead, the wall phone disappears from the novella's foreground. It is in the kitchen. It is not picked up.)

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the rooms shouldn't exist.
- That the protagonist was hallucinating.
- "It was as though the apartment had grown rooms." Forbidden.
- "He could no longer trust his senses." Forbidden interior question.
- That the warm coffee was impossible.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the apartment is no longer the apartment.
- That the protagonist is still walking through it methodically.
- That the prose has begun to dissolve at the sentence level, and that this is happening to the *novella*, not to the protagonist.

**Prose register:** The longest chapter and the most charged. Most of its sentences are still in baseline register. The dissolution is permitted in two specific places: the *the apartment has no courtyard* clause and the *plate-cup-plate* sentence. Everywhere else, the prose holds. The contrast is the form.

## Chapter 15 — The Boxes (second time)

**Position in arc:** Phase 4 sustained. The chapter that quotes the second chapter's title and dismantles its competence. Length target: ~2,000 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist wrapping. the protagonist labelling. the protagonist filling. The mismatches inside actions. The drive to the charity shop. The charity shop, which is where the library was. The drive back. The room he hasn't cleared. The child's room. The drawings. Rolling them. Coat pocket. Reaching into the pocket. The grocery list. Her handwriting.

**Sensory palette:**
- Cardboard, tape, paper. The same sensory palette as Chapter 2, exactly. The rhythms are quoted.
- The shirt that becomes a plate. The plate that becomes a shirt.
- The car. The road. The charity shop.
- The room he hasn't cleared: small bed, desk, drawings.
- The drawings: a house, a tree, a tall person, a small person.
- The roll. The pocket.
- The grocery list. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*

**Procedural detail:**
- Driving to the charity shop. The discovery of where the charity shop is.

**Glitches:**

1. **Syntactic:** *Wrap a glass and put a book in the box. Fold a shirt and stack a plate. Label a box KITCHEN and fill it with photographs.* (Chapter 14 introduced the swap. Chapter 15 repeats it without commentary.)

2. **Spatial/temporal:** The charity shop is where the library was. (One sentence. Not commented on. The reader who returns to Chapter 3 finds that the library was where the library was, and now it is the charity shop, and the charity shop is the charity shop, and the geography of the city has shifted in the same way the apartment has.)

3. **Spatial:** The room he hasn't cleared. The child's room. (A room that has not been in any earlier walkthrough. Drawings on the wall. Bed. Desk.)

4. **Stable anchor:** The drawings. *Take the drawings down carefully and roll them up. Put the roll in the inside pocket of the coat.* The drawings do not glitch. They are stable. They are the chapter's anchor.

5. **List → grocery list substitution:** *Reach into the coat pocket and take out the folded paper. The paper is in mother's handwriting.* *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*

**List state:** Replaced. The paper in the coat pocket is no longer the list. It is her grocery list, in her handwriting. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*

**Wall phone state:** Silent. Possibly seen on the way out of the kitchen.

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the child's room was his room.
- That the drawings were his drawings.
- That the grocery list was a message.
- That the substitution of the list by the grocery list was a relief or a horror.
- That the charity shop being where the library was meant the city had become the apartment.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That something the protagonist made when he was small is on the wall of a room he has not cleared, and that he rolls the drawings up and puts them in the coat pocket.
- That the list has been replaced by her grocery list.
- That the glitches have crossed out of the apartment into the city.

**Prose register:** Quotes the second chapter's rhythms. A reader who remembers the second chapter feels the echo. The chapter is the longest in the novella alongside Chapter 14. The drawings sequence is the slowest passage in the chapter and the most stable; it is a small resting place inside the dissolution.

## Chapter 16 — The Photograph

**Position in arc:** Phase 5 opening. The chapter where the photograph stops shifting. Length target: ~1,400 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist at home. The photograph in hand. The photograph on the table. The photograph from across the room. Bed. Listening.

**Sensory palette:**
- The photograph: held, set down, walked away from.
- The morning light, the evening light. (The chapter spans more than one moment of looking; the prose moves between them by simply describing them.)
- A garden, a kitchen, mountains, somewhere he's never been. (The shifting backgrounds of the photograph as it shifts.)
- The table.
- His bed in the dark. The faint sound of voices in a neighbouring apartment.

**Procedural detail:** None. The chapter is purely contemplative — but the protagonist does not contemplate. He looks. He puts down. He picks up. He moves across the room. He goes to bed.

**Glitches:**

1. **Relational:** Two people. Three people, a woman. Two again. Morning light, evening light. A garden, a kitchen, mountains, somewhere he's never been. (The photograph runs through its variations in the chapter's first half.)

2. **Stabilisation:** *Holds it for a long time. Puts it on the table. From across the room it is one woman in a kitchen. Not smiling. Face open, patient. Looking at whoever holds the camera.* (The photograph stops shifting. The chapter does not say "stops shifting". It says: *From across the room it is one woman in a kitchen.* The reader who has been tracking the photograph through the novella sees the resolution; the reader who has not, sees a small, intimate description of a single image.)

3. **Stable anchor:** *His apartment is stable. The glitches are in what he carries.* (This is the chapter's small private declaration. It is the closest the prose comes to commentary on its own architecture, and it sits inside the chapter as a single short paragraph. It does not interpret. It states.)

4. **List glitch:** *Take the paper out of the inside pocket of the coat. The paper is blank. Put it on the table. Pick up a pen. Do not write.*

**List state:** Blank. The paper that was once the list, then the grocery list, then a paragraph, is now blank. He picks up a pen. He doesn't write.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. (the protagonist is not in mother's apartment.)

**What must NOT be said:**
- Who the one woman in the kitchen is.
- That the photograph "found her face" or "settled".
- That the blank list was a relief.
- That picking up the pen and not writing was a refusal or a peace.
- That the voices in the neighbouring apartment were anything to do with anything.

**What the reader should infer:**
- That the relational glitch has resolved into a single image of a woman, looking at whoever holds the camera.
- That whoever holds the camera is, presumably, the protagonist or father, and that the novella will not say which.
- That the protagonist is not yet finished but the form is approaching stillness.
- That the list is blank and he is not going to write on it.

**Prose register:** The shortest chapter so far. The slowest. The cleanest. The novella is preparing the reader for the final chapter's return-to-cadence by clearing the prose of its remaining noise. Almost no glitches in the chapter — only the photograph's run-through and stabilisation. the protagonist's apartment is not glitching. The prose's final-chapter posture is being assembled here.

## Chapter 17 — Continue

**Position in arc:** Phase 5 closing. The chapter the entire novella has been moving toward. Length target: ~1,200 words.

**Camera focus:** the protagonist going to the apartment one last time. Walking through the rooms. Kitchen, hallway, living room, bedroom, second room, bathroom, balcony. Not empty. Her things. Bookshelves, wardrobe, cups, phone. Coffee at the counter. The elm. The light. The rectangle. The paper, the key, the photograph on the counter. The door, not locked. The stairs. The bakery. The light at the crossing. Crossing. Home. The door. Inside. Sitting down. The light through the window moving across the floor.

**Sensory palette:**
- All the first chapter's sensory palette, returned.
- The kettle. The cup. The coffee. The counter.
- The elm. The light through the elm. The rectangle on the counter. *The light falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.*
- The folded paper. The key. The photograph. The arrangement on the counter.
- The hallway. The front door. The handle. The unlocked door.
- The stairwell. The street. The bakery. The red light. The crossing.
- The protagonist's own front door. The protagonist's own apartment. The chair. Sitting down. The window. The floor. The light.

**Procedural detail:** None. The chapter has no bureaucracy. It is the chapter that ends the work without finishing it.

**Glitches:**

1. **Return of stable anchors:** All of them. The cups in the cupboard. The wardrobe full of her clothes. The bookshelves. The wall phone, on the wall, silent. The elm. The rectangle of light reaching almost to the sink. *The first chapter's cadence.*

2. **Single sentence of returned ease:** *The light falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.* (This is a sentence the novella's reader has been carrying since Chapter 1. Its return is the chapter's quietest and largest gesture. It is not announced. It is the same sentence.)

3. **The leaving:** *Front door. Doesn't look back. Doesn't lock it. Stairs.* The unlocked door is the only act in the chapter that the reader is asked to weigh. The novella does not weigh it.

4. **The world:** *The bakery is open. The light is red. He waits. It changes. He crosses.* The bakery is the world. It has been open the whole time. The novella visits it in this one sentence sequence and lets it stand for everything outside the apartment that has been outside the apartment and continues to be outside the apartment.

5. **Home:** *Home. Door. Inside. Sits down. Light through the window moves across the floor.*

**List state:** *Takes the paper from a pocket and puts it on the counter. The key beside it. The photograph beside the key.* The list is left. It is not consulted. It is not closed. It is on the counter beside the key and the photograph.

**Wall phone state:** Silent. On the wall. The novella does not return to it. (The reader does. The novella does not.)

**What must NOT be said:**
- That the protagonist had reached an understanding.
- That the apartment was peaceful.
- That leaving the door open was forgiveness.
- That the photograph on the counter was the photograph of the woman he had finally recognised.
- That the rectangle of light "felt" anything.
- That the chapter title was a verb addressed to him.

**What the reader should infer:**
- Whatever the reader infers. The novella has finished asking.

**Prose register:** First chapter's cadence, restored. The reader who is paying attention will recognise the cadence. The reader who is not, will read the chapter as a calm, slightly strange final scene of a man making coffee, leaving an apartment, going home, and sitting down. The chapter ends without resolution. The last sentence is a sentence about light moving across a floor. The book ends.

## Construction Checklist (per chapter)

For every chapter, before declaring it written:

- [ ] Position in glitch phase is correct
- [ ] Camera never leaves the protagonist's shoulder
- [ ] No interior verbs (think, feel, remember, realise, wonder)
- [ ] No abstractions named (grief, guilt, mourning, etc.)
- [ ] No metaphors, no "as if", no "almost" except in physical positioning
- [ ] No em-dash pivots
- [ ] No foreshadowing
- [ ] No retrospective
- [ ] All glitches present at baseline register, not announced
- [ ] List state for the chapter is correct
- [ ] Wall phone state for the chapter is correct
- [ ] Required motifs from THEME_TRACKER appear at correct pressure
- [ ] Stable anchors that should be stable have not glitched
- [ ] What-must-NOT-be-said list has been checked
- [ ] the protagonist eats / drinks / sleeps if the chapter spans the moment for it
- [ ] Chapter ends without resolution
- [ ] Chapter does not bridge to the next chapter
