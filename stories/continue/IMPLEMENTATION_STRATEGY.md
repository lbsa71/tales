# Implementation Strategy

## Writing Process Guide for "Continue"

---

## Overview

This document is the practical companion to the planning artifacts. It describes how the novella gets written, in what order, with what checks, and how to know when each phase is done.

The novella is unusually demanding in voice discipline. The single most important thing this document can do is name the failure modes early and the order of operations clearly.

## Phase 2 — First Draft

### Order of Composition

The novella is written in three passes that interleave:

**Pass A — The opening and the closing first.**

Chapter 1 ("The Key") and Chapter 17 ("Continue") are written first, in that order. The first chapter establishes the baseline register and the cadence the last chapter restores. The last chapter cannot be written until the first chapter is written, but it must be drafted *before* any of the middle chapters, because every middle chapter is calibrated to a final chapter that already exists.

**Pass B — The most-difficult chapters next.**

Chapter 12 ("The Inscription") and Chapter 14 ("The Rooms") are the chapters where the form's discipline is hardest to hold. They are drafted second. The inscription text is the central problem of Chapter 12; the syntactic-dissolution sentences are the central problem of Chapter 14. Both are written at length and revised before the surrounding chapters are drafted, because the surrounding chapters need to know what they are calibrated against.

Chapter 7 ("The Neighbour") is drafted in the same group: it is the form's first major rupture and it sets the discipline for "the protagonist in the protagonist's own apartment" chapters that follow.

**Pass C — Fill in chronologically.**

Chapters 2 through 6, then 8 through 11, then 13, then 15 and 16. Written in order. Each chapter built against THEME_DOSSIERS.md.

### Daily Practice

Each writing session:

1. Read NARRATOR_VOICE.md's "Emergency Reference" section.
2. Read the relevant THEME_DOSSIERS.md entry for the chapter being written.
3. Read the previous chapter's last paragraph aloud.
4. Write.
5. After writing, run the six-point sentence checklist (STYLE_GUIDE.md) on the day's pages.
6. Mark anything uncertain. Do not fix it now.

### Daily Pace

500–1,000 words per day, sustained. Some chapters are intentionally slow to compose. Chapter 12 may take a week. Chapter 14 may take two. Do not push through. The chapters that resist composition are usually the chapters that are doing the most work.

## Phase 3 — Voice Pass

### Focus

This pass is *only* about voice. It is not about plot, structure, or motif placement. It is the pass that catches every interiority slip, every metaphor, every em-dash pivot, every named abstraction.

### Method

1. Read the entire draft aloud, slowly, in one or two sessions.
2. After each chapter, run the forbidden-word search (see below).
3. After each chapter, run the six-point sentence checklist.
4. Mark every sentence that fails. Do not fix yet.
5. After the read-through, return to the marked sentences in order. Cut, replace, or rebuild.

### Forbidden-Word Search Commands

These should be run as text searches against each chapter file. The presence of any of these in the draft is a flag for review (most are forbidden; some require context check).

```

feel

suddenly

indeed
somehow

the strangeness

grief
mourning

shame

dissociation
memory (as topic)
glitch
simulation
metaphor
mother (followed by any feeling word)
forgive (outside the one allowed instance)
—
```

The em-dash is the most important search. Almost every em-dash will need to be removed.

### Voice Pass Quality Gate

The chapter passes when:

- [ ] No forbidden words remain (or each remaining instance is justified)
- [ ] No metaphor or simile remains
- [ ] No interior verb remains except in dialogue or inscription
- [ ] No abstraction is named
- [ ] No em-dash pivots
- [ ] No "almost" outside physical positioning
- [ ] The chapter reads aloud without any sentence drawing attention to itself as "literary"

## Phase 4 — Glitch Calibration Pass

### Focus

This pass checks that every glitch is in the right chapter, in the right phase, at the right pressure, and not announced.

### Method

1. Open THEME_TRACKER.md and THEME_DOSSIERS.md.
2. For each chapter, verify every required glitch is present.
3. For each glitch, verify:
   - It is at baseline prose register (not announced).
   - It is not commented on by the prose.
   - the protagonist does not have an internal reaction described.
   - The next sentence continues without bridge.
4. For each motif, verify it is in the right state for the chapter (the list, the wall phone, the cups, the wardrobe, the bookshelves, etc.).
5. Verify the stable anchors *have not glitched*. (The crayon drawings, the elm, the rectangle of light, the bakery, the first-chapter cadence in the last chapter.)

### Glitch Calibration Quality Gate

- [ ] Every glitch in THEME_DOSSIERS.md appears in the chapter
- [ ] No glitch is announced
- [ ] No glitch is interpreted by the prose
- [ ] All motif states are correct for the chapter
- [ ] All stable anchors are stable
- [ ] No bridge sentence between chapters

## Phase 5 — Pace and White-Space Pass

### Focus

This pass is about the rhythm of the novella as an object the reader holds in their hands. It is about which chapters need more room and which need less.

### Method

1. Read the novella in one sitting, if possible.
2. Mark places where the prose seems to be hurrying past something the reader needs to sit with.
3. Mark places where the prose is lingering on something the reader has already understood.
4. Add white space (paragraph breaks, section breaks) where the reader needs to be held.
5. Cut transitional sentences whose only purpose is to move from one beat to the next.

### Specific Rhythms to Check

- The first chapter's opening and closing rhythms. They will be quoted in the last chapter; they need to be the rhythms the writer wants the last chapter to inherit.
- The folding-clothes sequence in Chapter 4. Should be slow.
- The wall phone moments in Chapters 6, 8, 10, and 13. Each is short. Each is unweighted.
- The inscription passage in Chapter 12. Italicised. Past tense. The prose around it returns to baseline immediately.
- The plate-cup-plate sentence in Chapter 14. Stands alone. Has its own paragraph or near to it.
- The grocery list moment in Chapter 15. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.* Italicised. Followed by nothing.
- The crayon drawings sequence in Chapter 15. Slow.
- The leaving sequence in Chapter 17. *Walk to the front door. Open it. Do not look back. Do not lock it.* The bare imperatives here carry the leaving; fragments are not needed.
- The very last sentence of the novella. Should be a sentence about light moving across a floor. Should not be a sentence that calls attention to itself as a final sentence.

### Pace Quality Gate

- [ ] No chapter feels rushed
- [ ] No chapter feels padded
- [ ] White space is used to hold the reader where the prose cannot
- [ ] The first and last chapters share their cadence
- [ ] The novella reads in a single sitting without the reader putting it down for the wrong reason

## Phase 6 — Final Sweep

### Focus

A final consistency pass. Names, dates, room counts, the order of the original list items, the wall phone status across chapters.

### Specific Checks

- [ ] The original 14 items of the list (Chapter 1) match the items as they appear in subsequent chapters
- [ ] Every list state in THEME_DOSSIERS.md is reflected in the corresponding chapter
- [ ] Every wall phone state is reflected
- [ ] Every cups state is reflected
- [ ] Every wardrobe state is reflected
- [ ] Every bookshelves state is reflected
- [ ] Every photograph state is reflected
- [ ] The grocery list, *Milk, bread, fish, oranges*, appears in its three required places (Ch. 4 in the drawer, Ch. 13 absent from the drawer, Ch. 15 in the coat pocket)
- [ ] The crayon drawings appear once, in Ch. 15, and in the coat pocket thereafter (but are not named again)
- [ ] The elm is not glitched at any point
- [ ] The first-chapter cadence is recognisably restored in the last chapter
- [ ] *Marianne* appears exactly once
- [ ] *forgive* appears exactly once (on the list, in Ch. 13)
- [ ] No forbidden word remains (final search pass)
- [ ] No em-dash remains except where explicitly justified
- [ ] No accidental past tense
- [ ] No accidental break of camera (no scene the protagonist is not physically present in)

### Final Sweep Quality Gate

The novella is complete when:

- [ ] All consistency checks pass
- [ ] A reader who has not seen any of the planning documents can read the novella through and finish it
- [ ] At least three readers (not the writer) have reported that they were unsettled and could not say exactly why
- [ ] At least one reader has told the writer that they had to put the book down at Chapter 14
- [ ] At least one reader has told the writer that they had to read the inscription twice
- [ ] No reader has asked the writer to clarify what was happening (if any reader does, the form has not held)

## Common Failure Modes (Anticipated)

### Failure 1 — Interiority Leakage

The writer adds a sentence telling the reader what the protagonist is thinking or feeling. The sentence reads like something a careful literary novelist would write. It is wrong for *this* novella.

**How to catch:** The forbidden-word search. The read-aloud test. Any sentence that uses a pronoun for the protagonist or a verb of cognition.

**How to fix:** Cut the interior sentence. Replace with action, position, or nothing.

---

### Failure 2 — Glitch Announcement

The writer wants to make sure the reader notices a glitch. They add a sentence emphasising it. ("The wardrobe was full again, and he had no idea how.") This is wrong.

**How to catch:** Read-aloud. The voice sounds different in the announcing sentence.

**How to fix:** Cut the announcing sentence. The glitch goes back to baseline.

---

### Failure 3 — Em-dash Cleverness

The writer falls into the contemporary-literary tic of em-dash subordinate-clause pivots. (*Pick up the cup — the wrong cup — and put it down.*)

**How to catch:** Search for em-dashes.

**How to fix:** Periods or commas. Often the pithy aside can simply be cut.

---

### Failure 4 — Backstory Spillage

The writer wants the reader to know that the father died, that the phone was off, that mother said *why didn't you answer*. The writer puts a memory passage on the page. This is the failure mode the form was built to refuse.

**How to catch:** Any passage that describes a moment that is not happening on the page right now.

**How to fix:** Cut. Trust the inference. The reader is doing the work and the work is the form.

---

### Failure 5 — Sentimentality

The writer writes a sentence that makes them, personally, feel something. This is often the worst sentence in the chapter.

**How to catch:** Any sentence the writer would put on a postcard.

**How to fix:** Cut. The novella is not interested in postcards.

---

### Failure 6 — Decoration

The writer wants the prose to be beautiful. The writer adds an adjective, a comparison, a small flourish. The flourish is what the form forbids.

**How to catch:** Any sentence that is beautiful instead of accurate.

**How to fix:** Replace with the literal version. The literal version, in this novella, is the beautiful version.

---

### Failure 7 — Resolution

The writer wants to give the reader a moment of relief, a small acknowledgement, a faint indication that the novella is going to be all right. The novella is not going to be all right. The novella is going to continue.

**How to catch:** Any sentence whose function is to console the reader.

**How to fix:** Cut. The novella does not console.

---

### Failure 8 — Naming the Abstractions

The word *grief* slips into the prose. Or *memory*. Or *guilt*. Or *the loss*.

**How to catch:** The forbidden-word search.

**How to fix:** Find the thing the abstraction would have named, in its physical particulars. The rectangle of light. The cup in the cupboard. The phone on the wall. The list in the coat pocket.

## Beta Readers

### Who to ask

- Two readers who read literary fiction widely and will recognise the form.
- Two readers who do not read literary fiction widely and will be unguarded.
- One reader who has recently lost a parent.
- One reader who has not.

### What to ask them

- Did you finish the book? (Not whether they enjoyed it. Whether they finished it.)
- Was there a chapter you put the book down at?
- Was there a sentence you reread?
- Did you understand what was happening in the apartment?
- Did you understand what was happening to the protagonist?
- Was there anything you wanted the book to say that it did not?

### What not to ask them

- Did you like the book?
- What is the book about?
- Was it too sad?
- What did you think of the ending?

The reader's experience is the form. The form does not need defending. The writer needs to know whether the form did its work, not whether it pleased.

## Translation

When the novella is finished, a translation plan should be drafted. The form's discipline travels in some languages and breaks in others. Specifically:

- The present-tense discipline holds in any language with a continuous present.
- The interiority refusal holds in any language whose third-person past does not require interior verbs to function.
- The em-dash discipline holds in any language whose punctuation does not require dashes for clauses.
- The italicised inscription will need careful handling in languages without italic conventions.
- The grocery list (*Milk, bread, fish, oranges*) translates without difficulty but the four-word rhythm needs to be preserved.
- The wall phone discipline depends on a language in which "wall phone" is one phrase, not two. Some languages will not have this word; the writer should consider what the equivalent is.

These are problems for after the novella exists. They are noted here as a flag.

## Success Criteria

The novella is complete when:

1. The voice is held throughout. Not a single interiority slip.
2. The glitches are placed and uncommented.
3. The list arc is complete and recognisable.
4. The first chapter and the last chapter share their cadence.
5. A reader who finishes the book cannot summarise what it was about without summarising the synopsis.
6. A reader who finishes the book is unsettled and held.
7. At least one reader has told the writer that they had to sit with the book for a while.
8. The writer can no longer tell which sentences are working and which are not. (This is a sign that the form has done its work to the writer too.)
