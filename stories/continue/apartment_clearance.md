# Apartment Clearance

## Research Dossier: The Material and Procedural Reality of Clearing a Deceased Parent's Home

---

## Overview

This document compiles practical, procedural, and material research on the work of clearing a deceased parent's apartment. Most of the novella's surface is made of this work. The glitches sit inside it. The writer needs to know exactly how the work is done so that the deviations from how the work is done are visible to the reader who is paying attention and inferable to the reader who is not.

A note on usage: this dossier is denser in practical detail than the grief and guilt dossiers, because the prose surfaces of the novella draw directly on practical detail. The grief and guilt dossiers describe what the prose refuses to name; this dossier describes what the prose actively shows.

---

## The Shape of the Work

Clearing a deceased parent's apartment is a project with three intertwined dimensions:

1. **Physical:** The objects in the apartment must be sorted, packed, removed, donated, sold, given away, or thrown out. The apartment must be left in a defined condition (typically: empty, clean, in some configurations: in original condition with damages repaired).

2. **Bureaucratic / Administrative:** The death has triggered a cascade of procedural obligations: utilities, banks, insurance, the estate, the will, taxes, the lease (if rented), the mortgage (if owned), the death certificate copies, the funeral provider, social security or its equivalent, the employer (if employed), pension funds, healthcare providers, possibly the courts.

3. **Relational:** The objects in the apartment are not neutral. They carry the deceased's life. Decisions about what to keep, what to give to whom, what to throw out, are also decisions about what is allowed to survive of the deceased's presence in the world. This is the dimension the novella's surface holds most invisibly: every box the protagonist tapes is also a decision about what is now permanently gone.

The novella foregrounds the physical and the bureaucratic. The relational dimension is everywhere but never named.

---

## Time and Pressure

Clearing an apartment is almost always done under time pressure. The kinds of pressure include:

- **Lease termination.** A rented apartment must be empty by a date. The bereaved typically learns the date within days of the death.
- **The next tenant.** The landlord has often already arranged for new tenants to move in.
- **The estate.** Probate can require the apartment to be valued, sold, or transferred by a certain date.
- **The cost of holding.** Rent, mortgage, utilities, insurance — these continue to be due. The longer the bereaved holds the apartment, the more it costs.
- **Family pressure.** Other family members may have opinions about what should be kept and how quickly things should move.
- **The bereaved's own pressure.** Some bereaved want to be done. Some bereaved want it never to end. Both push toward unhealthy speeds.

The novella's the protagonist appears to be working under lease pressure. The synopsis specifies "before the lease expires". The novella does not give a number of days, weeks, or months. The pressure is ambient. the protagonist knows when the apartment must be empty. The reader is not told.

**For the novella:** The lease pressure is the only external clock the form has. Even this clock is not announced. The reader infers urgency from the protagonist's continuous work and from the absence of any sign that he is taking time off.

## The First Walkthrough

The first walkthrough of a deceased parent's apartment is, for many bereaved adult children, a particular and unrepeatable event. It has features:

- **The smell.** Apartments that have been closed for days carry a specific air. The bereaved registers it within seconds. It is the smell of the apartment without its inhabitant. It is not unpleasant. It is wrong.
- **The temperature.** The thermostat may have been left on or off. The apartment is colder or warmer than it would be if someone lived in it.
- **The light.** Mail on the floor may indicate the angles the bereaved had not noticed before — the door's relation to the morning sun, the kitchen window's afternoon light.
- **The arrangement.** Things are where the deceased left them. The half-full glass on the table. The book open beside the chair. The cardigan over the back of the chair. (Or, if the death was anticipated and the deceased had time to arrange, things may be arranged with eerie deliberation.)
- **The sound.** The apartment is quieter than the bereaved expects. No appliance running. No radio. No water in the pipes. The fridge hum.
- **The familiar that has become strange.** The bereaved recognises everything and recognises that the recognising no longer reaches anyone.

**For the novella:** Chapter 1 is the first walkthrough. The novella renders it without commentary. The reader is given the rooms in the order the protagonist walks them, the cup he takes down, the coffee at the counter, the elm, the rectangle of light, and the sitting at the kitchen table to write the list. The first chapter is the novella's most stable chapter and contains nothing the reader will later distrust. The walkthrough is the novella's anchor to the rest of the form.

## Tools and Supplies

The bereaved typically acquires the following early in the project:

- **Boxes.** Small for books, medium for kitchen and household, large for clothes and bedding. Some bereaved buy boxes by the dozen and accumulate more than needed; some buy a few at a time.
- **Tape.** Brown packing tape. The tape gun is a small acquisition that becomes central. The thunk of the tape gun is the project's percussion.
- **Newspaper or wrapping paper** for fragile items.
- **Bin bags.** Heavy-duty. Black. Many.
- **Markers.** For labelling boxes. Black. Wide-tip.
- **Cleaning supplies.** Sponges, cloths, bleach, surface cleaner, glass cleaner, oven cleaner, drain cleaner, a mop, a bucket. Some of these the deceased already had.
- **Tools.** A screwdriver (Phillips and flathead). An Allen key. Pliers. For dismantling furniture. Some of these the deceased already had.
- **A vehicle.** For trips to the charity shop, the dump, the recycling centre, the storage facility, the bereaved's own home.

The novella's the protagonist buys boxes, tape, newspaper at the start of Chapter 2. He uses his car for the trips. The novella does not enumerate his other supplies; the reader infers them.

## The Categories

Almost every clearance project develops a working set of categories. The bereaved sorts each room's contents into them:

1. **Keep — for the bereaved.** Things the bereaved is taking home.
2. **Keep — for other family members.** Things to be passed to siblings, children, cousins. (Not all bereaved have such recipients.)
3. **Donate to charity.** Things in good condition with no specific recipient.
4. **Sell.** Furniture, appliances, art, books that have value. (Often more trouble than the value warrants. Many bereaved end up donating things they had planned to sell.)
5. **Recycle.** Paper, glass, electronics.
6. **Throw away.** Things in poor condition or with no remaining use.
7. **Confused.** The bereaved cannot decide. The category accumulates and becomes its own problem.

The categories are leaky. A book moves from "keep" to "donate" to "keep" across multiple revisits. A piece of jewellery is for the bereaved, then for a sibling, then back. The clearance project, in practice, is less linear than a category system suggests.

**For the novella:** The novella does not give the protagonist a category system in any explicit way. The list is not a category system; it is a sequence of tasks. The reader sees the protagonist wrap plates, fold clothes, fill bags, tape boxes. The destination of each item is mostly unspecified. The unspecified destination is part of the novella's neutrality: the protagonist is doing the work; the reader is not asked to track what is going where.

## The Kitchen

The kitchen is, in many clearances, the room with the most physical objects per square metre and the most decisions per object. It contains:

- **Pots, pans, utensils.** Often older, often well-seasoned, often more than needed.
- **Dishes, glasses, mugs, cutlery.** Often more than the deceased used. Often more than the bereaved can use.
- **Small appliances.** Toaster, kettle, coffee machine, food processor, blender. Often older. Sometimes broken. Sometimes the deceased's favourite item, used daily.
- **Pantry.** Dry goods, spices, tinned food, packets. Some still in date, some not.
- **Fridge.** Perishables in various states. Items the deceased had bought just before. Items that have gone off.
- **Freezer.** Frozen meals, sometimes labelled in the deceased's handwriting with the date of preparation. The freezer often contains the most poignant artefacts of the clearance: meals the deceased made in the expectation of eating them later.
- **The drawers.** Cutlery in the cutlery drawer. Tea towels in the tea towel drawer. The "junk drawer" with batteries, pens, take-away menus, instruction manuals, tape, twine, the small notebook that the deceased used for grocery lists.
- **The cleaning cupboard.** Bleach, sponges, dishwasher tablets, some of which the bereaved will use to clean the apartment they are clearing.
- **The grocery list.** Often on the fridge or in a drawer. The last list. The bereaved finds it. Reads it. Does something with it.

**For the novella:**
- Chapter 2 is the kitchen's first major day: boxes, the plates, the fridge.
- The fridge is cleared in Chapter 2.
- Chapter 4 visits the kitchen drawer with the grocery list (and the prose treats it neutrally: he reads it, puts it back).
- Chapter 10 is the freezer chapter. The freezer is full. There is a thing in foil with a date after she died.
- Chapter 6 has the cups in a different cupboard.
- Chapter 9 has one cup in the cupboard.
- Chapter 17 has the cups back, and the coffee at the counter, and the rectangle of light reaching almost to the sink.

The kitchen is the novella's most worked-over room.

### The Grocery List Specifically

The grocery list is one of the most consistent objects in any clearance. It is what the deceased was going to buy before they died. It is short. It is in their handwriting. It contains items that are domestic and uninflected: milk, bread, fish, oranges, soap, tea bags, washing-up liquid.

The grocery list is one of the objects bereaved people most often report being struck by. It is a record of intention. It says: this person was planning to be here next week. They were going to need oranges.

Many bereaved people keep the grocery list. Some put it in a frame. Some carry it folded in a wallet. Some take a photograph and throw the original away. Some do nothing with it.

**For the novella:** The grocery list is in the drawer in Chapter 4. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.* (Provisional; see IMPLEMENTATION_STRATEGY.md for the note on whether to revisit.) the protagonist reads it and puts it back. In Chapter 13, when he opens the same drawer, the grocery list is gone and a phone bill is in its place. In Chapter 15, he reaches into his coat pocket and unfolds the paper that was once his list, and it is her grocery list. The four words are the same. The novella does not say how it got into his pocket. The reader carries the substitution.

## The Wardrobe

The wardrobe is, in many clearances, the most affectively dense room. Clothes hold the body. Folding a parent's clothes is, for many bereaved, the moment the project becomes hardest.

- **Hanging clothes.** Coats, dresses, suits, shirts. Often in a particular order the deceased maintained.
- **Folded clothes.** Sweaters, T-shirts, trousers. In drawers or on shelves.
- **Underwear.** Often the most private. Many bereaved bag this category quickly without sorting.
- **Shoes.** On the floor of the wardrobe. Each pair shaped to the deceased's feet. The shape persists after the foot is gone.
- **Accessories.** Belts, scarves, ties, hats, jewellery, handbags. Some valuable, some sentimental, most neither.
- **The smell.** The wardrobe holds the deceased's scent. For some bereaved this is a comfort. For others it is a knife.

### Charity shop logistics

Charity shops have requirements that the bereaved learns by trial and error:

- Many will not take stained or damaged items.
- Many will not take items that need cleaning. (The bereaved is expected to wash clothes before donating.)
- Some accept hangers, some do not.
- Some accept furniture, some do not.
- Some send a van, some do not.
- Most have specific opening hours and quantity limits.

The bereaved often makes more trips to the charity shop than expected.

**For the novella:** Chapter 4 is the clothes chapter. the protagonist discovers he needs hangers (one shop), then that he needs clean clothes (another shop), then decides to wash everything. The washing machine. The drying rack on the balcony (which was not on the balcony in Chapter 1). The chapter contains the first object glitch.

## The Books and the Bookshelf

Books are slow. They are heavy. There are many of them. They are also the most personal objects in many homes — the bereaved learns about the deceased through the books even if they thought they knew the deceased well.

Common features of book clearance:

- **The bookshelf order.** The deceased had a system. The bereaved may or may not understand it.
- **The dust at the top.** Books at the top have not been touched in years. Books in the middle have. The deceased's actual reading life is visible from where the dust is.
- **Inscriptions.** Some books are inscribed — by the giver, by the deceased to themselves, by the bereaved to the deceased years ago, by people the bereaved does not recognise. Inscribed books are slower to handle.
- **Bookmarks.** Receipts, postcards, train tickets, pressed leaves, scraps of paper with phone numbers. The bereaved finds these inside books. Each is a small artefact of a life.
- **Margin notes.** The deceased's handwriting, if they were a writer in margins. Sometimes the bereaved learns more about the deceased from margin notes than from any conversation they had.

Books almost always overwhelm the bereaved's planned packing pace.

**For the novella:** Chapter 8 ("The Death Certificate") opens the bookshelves. the protagonist thinks he packed them. They are full. He finds the book with the inscription. The inscription page is blank. Chapter 12 ("The Inscription") opens them again. The inscription page is full. His handwriting. The novella places the inscription inside an italicised passage and lets it stand. The book goes into the box. The box is taped.

## The Photographs

Photographs are the slowest objects in any clearance. The bereaved sits down to "go through them quickly" and is still on the floor an hour later.

- **Albums.** Old photos, often arranged. The bereaved discovers people they do not recognise. The bereaved discovers themselves at ages they do not remember.
- **Loose photos.** In shoeboxes, in drawers, in envelopes from the developer. Often unsorted.
- **The deceased's phone.** Recent photos. The last photo. (The novella does not visit this directly; mobile phones are largely outside its surface.)
- **Photographs of the deceased.** The bereaved sees their parent young, before they were a parent.
- **Photographs of the bereaved as a child.** The bereaved sees themselves as the parent saw them.
- **Photographs of people the bereaved cannot identify.** Friends of the deceased the bereaved did not know about. Holidays the bereaved was not part of. A garden the bereaved has never seen.

**For the novella:** Chapter 6 is the photographs chapter. the protagonist sits on the floor with the shoebox. The keep pile grows; the discard pile stays empty; he puts them all back. One photograph of him and mother is found in the bedside drawer and pocketed. By the end of the chapter the photograph is in the shoebox again, and the prose says it has three people in it. Chapter 16 is the photograph (singular) chapter, in which the photograph is held and put down and seen from across the room as one woman in a kitchen.

## The Documents

Every clearance contains a documentary substrate. The deceased's papers are spread across drawers, folders, file boxes, the bottoms of cupboards, and (if the deceased was old enough or organised enough) a single labelled binder somewhere obvious.

The bereaved is looking for:

- **The will.** Often kept with a solicitor; often a copy is at home; often the bereaved does not know where; often the apartment contains a metal box at the top of a wardrobe in which the will may or may not be.
- **The death certificate.** Issued by the civil registry. Several copies needed (for the bank, the pension, the insurance, the lawyer, the registry itself).
- **Identification.** The deceased's passport, ID, driving licence. Often needs to be returned or destroyed.
- **The birth certificate.** Sometimes needed for proof of next-of-kin.
- **The marriage certificate.** Sometimes needed for the same.
- **Bank statements.** For the estate accounting.
- **Tax records.** For the final tax return.
- **Pension documents.** For pension cessation.
- **Insurance policies.** For claims.
- **Title deeds, mortgage documents.** For property.
- **Utility bills.** For account numbers and termination.
- **Medical records.** Sometimes needed for the certificate.
- **The address book.** For knowing whom to inform.
- **Letters.** Personal correspondence. Almost always slows the bereaved.
- **Diaries, notebooks, journals.** Almost always slow the bereaved further.

The bereaved learns to keep two distinct piles: documents needed for the procedural work, and documents that are too personal to handle right now and are placed in a box "for later".

**For the novella:**
- Chapter 3: the protagonist searches drawers and shelves for the utility account number. He finds old letters, receipts, a warranty, the library book.
- Chapter 5: He searches for the will. The metal box that should be on top of the wardrobe is not there.
- Chapter 8: Civil registry office. The form for the death certificate.
- Chapter 9: The bank. The middle name discrepancy on the birth certificate vs. the death certificate.
- Chapter 11: The solicitor's office. The will is finally found. Its terms are simple.
- Chapter 13: The phone bill is in the kitchen drawer where the grocery list was.

The documents are the novella's procedural chapters. They are also the chapters where the most documentary glitches occur.

## The Bureaucracy: A Fuller List

A typical clearance generates correspondence with most of the following parties:

- **The landlord** (notice of termination, deposit return, key handover)
- **The utility companies** (electric, gas, water, possibly heating, possibly waste, possibly internet, possibly phone)
- **The phone company** (mobile and/or landline)
- **The bank(s)** (account closure, last balance, transfer of funds to estate)
- **The solicitor / lawyer** (will, probate, estate)
- **The pension provider** (cessation, possibly survivor benefits)
- **The social security / state pension office**
- **The employer** (if employed at the time of death)
- **The insurance company / companies** (life, home, auto, health)
- **The civil registry** (death certificate)
- **The funeral provider** (arrangements, payment)
- **The cemetery / crematorium** (burial or cremation, plot, urn)
- **The doctor / GP** (medical certificate, removal from records)
- **The dentist**
- **Subscriptions** (newspapers, magazines, streaming services, gym, club memberships)
- **Memberships** (libraries, professional associations, religious organisations, political parties)
- **Charities** (the deceased may have been a regular donor; the standing orders need to be cancelled)
- **Tax authorities** (final tax return, possibly inheritance tax)
- **The post office** (mail forwarding or stoppage)
- **Online accounts** (email, social media, cloud storage, banking, shopping)

Most bereaved do not know most of this is needed. They learn it. The learning takes weeks or months and is almost always punctuated by a moment of realising they have missed something.

**For the novella:** The novella shows a small subset:
- The landlord (Ch. 1, key handover)
- The utility company (Ch. 3)
- The library (Ch. 3)
- The civil registry (Ch. 8)
- The bank (Ch. 9)
- The solicitor (Ch. 5, 9, 11)
- The phone company (Ch. 13)

The other items are present in the world the novella implies but not on the page. The novella does not need to enumerate them. the protagonist is doing them off-screen, or not doing them, and the reader extrapolates.

## The Charity Shop, the Dump, the Storage Unit

The physical destinations of the cleared objects:

### The charity shop
- Variable opening hours.
- Variable acceptance criteria.
- Variable temperaments of the volunteers behind the counter.
- The bereaved makes multiple trips, often more than expected.
- Some bereaved discover that a charity shop is run from a building they have known their whole lives without ever entering.
- Some bereaved discover that the charity shop is in the location where another business used to be.

### The dump / household recycling centre
- Often a drive out of town.
- Containers for: general waste, garden waste, wood, metal, glass, paper, electronics, paint, batteries, white goods.
- Strict rules. The bereaved often gets one rule wrong on the first visit and learns.
- The dump is one of the few places where the work feels physically conclusive: the thing leaves the bereaved's hands and is gone.

### The storage unit
- Some bereaved cannot decide. They rent a storage unit and put the undecided things in it.
- The storage unit then becomes its own problem. Many bereaved keep storage units for years.

### The skip / dumpster
- For larger clearances, the bereaved hires a skip and fills it. The skip is on the street outside the apartment. The bereaved carries things down to it.
- Filling a skip is heavy, public, sometimes embarrassing work.

**For the novella:** the protagonist uses the charity shop. He drives there. In Chapter 15, the charity shop is *where the library was*. The novella does not visit the dump explicitly; if the protagonist makes those trips, they are off-page. The novella does not visit the storage unit. There is no skip. The clearance is, in the novella, smaller-scale and more domestic than a typical clearance might be — partly because the novella's focus is on the rooms themselves and the objects in them, not on the vehicles in and out.

## The Final Clean

Most leases require the apartment to be left clean. The bereaved cleans the apartment themselves or hires a service. Cleaning is the last task before the keys are returned.

- **The kitchen.** Oven (often the worst). Hob. Extractor fan. Fridge interior. Freezer interior. Cupboards. Sink. Drains. Floor.
- **The bathroom.** Toilet. Bath/shower. Sink. Tiles. Mirror. Floor.
- **The floors.** Vacuumed and mopped throughout.
- **The windows.** Inside and (sometimes) outside.
- **The walls.** Marks from removed furniture, picture hooks. Sometimes a coat of paint.
- **The light fittings.** Bulbs. Shades.

The final clean is, for many bereaved, the first time the apartment looks like an empty rental rather than the deceased's home. It is the moment when the deceased is, in some sense, removed from the space the deceased lived in.

**For the novella:** Item 13 of the original list is "Final clean". Item 14 is "Return key". The novella does not depict the final clean. the protagonist does not finish. He leaves the apartment full and the door open. The list's last items are not enacted. The reader who notices the un-enactment understands the entire form's refusal to "complete" the work.

## Time of Day, Light, Weather

A practical observation: clearance work has a particular relationship to the day's light. The bereaved typically:

- Arrives mid-morning. (Avoiding rush hour, having had coffee.)
- Works through the afternoon.
- Loses light in winter at four; in summer can stay until nine.
- Goes home in the evening, exhausted.
- Does not work at night except for unusual reasons (deadline, cannot sleep).

The light through the apartment changes the work. Mid-morning is for kitchen. Afternoon is for clothes (the wardrobe needs the south-facing light). Evening is for sitting on the floor with photographs.

**For the novella:** The novella does not name times of day in absolute terms. It refers to morning, afternoon, evening, night. The light is the novella's primary clock. The rectangle of afternoon light on the kitchen counter is the recurring marker. The novella's reader feels the light moving across the apartment without being given a clock.

## The Things That Are Not Worth Anything

A category that most bereaved have to confront and that is rarely discussed in advice articles:

The vast majority of the things in a deceased parent's apartment are worth nothing financially. Furniture from the 1980s. Crockery. Linens. Books that were read once. Clothes from a decade the bereaved cannot date. Appliances that work but are old. Things bought from catalogues. Things received as gifts and never used. Things made by the deceased that the deceased never showed to anyone. Things kept for sentimental reasons by the deceased for reasons the bereaved cannot reconstruct.

The bereaved discovers that almost everything is worth nothing. The "worth" is the relational dimension only. A chipped mug from a holiday in 1987 has no value to anyone except the people who were on the holiday, and the people who were on the holiday include one person who is dead and one person who is the bereaved, and the chipped mug now has either total value or no value depending on who is in the room.

**For the novella:** the protagonist does not seem to be making valuation decisions. He wraps. He folds. He bags. He labels. The novella does not differentiate the valuable from the worthless. The reader is not told which objects matter. The reader assumes, correctly or not, that they all matter.

## What Other Bereaved Have Reported

A list of recurring observations from accounts of clearance work, both literary and procedural:

- **"I learned what they ate."** The fridge and the pantry tell the bereaved what the deceased ate, in what quantities, with what variety.
- **"I learned what they read at night."** The book on the bedside table.
- **"I learned what they kept."** The objects that turned out to have meaning the bereaved did not know about — the postcards, the letters, the envelope of school reports, the lock of hair.
- **"I learned what they had not told me."** The friend whose name appears in the address book whom the bereaved has never heard of. The hospital appointment letters. The reading list from a course the deceased had taken without mentioning.
- **"I had to stop several times."** Most clearances are not done in one sustained stretch. The bereaved stops, sits, drinks coffee, leaves and comes back, decides to come back tomorrow, decides not to.
- **"I left things until the end that I could not face."** Almost universally, the bereaved leaves one room, one drawer, one box for the very last day, knowing it is the hardest. Sometimes it is not opened at all.
- **"I left some things behind."** Some bereaved leave things in the apartment for the next tenant or the landlord to dispose of, because they cannot bring themselves to handle them.
- **"I took home something small."** A pen. A single book. A teaspoon. A photograph. The thing that goes home with the bereaved is often not the thing they planned to take.
- **"I drove home slowly."** The drive home from the last day at the apartment is, for many bereaved, the moment the project ends.

**For the novella:**
- the protagonist learns what she ate (Ch. 2 fridge, Ch. 10 freezer).
- the protagonist learns what she kept (Ch. 6 photographs, Ch. 8 inscription, Ch. 15 the child's room).
- the protagonist learns what she had not told him (Marianne, possibly Ch. 6).
- the protagonist stops, makes coffee, sits at the counter (Ch. 1, Ch. 17).
- the protagonist leaves things behind (Ch. 17, where he leaves the apartment full and does not lock the door).
- the protagonist takes home something small (the photograph, in Ch. 17 — though by then the small thing has accumulated weight).
- the protagonist drives home slowly is replaced by the protagonist walking home, crossing at the red light (Ch. 17).

## The Specific Apartment

The novella's apartment has the following spatial features (collated from SYNOPSIS.md and the chapter dossiers):

- **Kitchen** with a window facing an elm. Counter. Sink. Cupboards. Coffee machine. Cups. Plates. Glasses. Cutlery. Drawer with the grocery list. Wall phone on the kitchen wall. Kitchen table. Fridge. Freezer.
- **Hallway** connecting the front door to the rest of the apartment.
- **Living room** with bookshelves. Sofa (her sofa, not the glass-topped one that arrives in Ch. 11). Coffee table.
- **Bedroom** off the hallway in Ch. 1; through the kitchen in Ch. 3 onward. Wardrobe (top of which the metal box should be on). Bedside drawer (where the protagonist finds one photograph).
- **Second room** used for storage in Ch. 1; with a desk in it from Ch. 5; the child's room in Ch. 15.
- **Bathroom.** Visited briefly in Ch. 1; barely in the novella thereafter.
- **Balcony.** Dead plants and a chair in Ch. 1. Drying rack from Ch. 4.

**Rooms that should not exist:**
- **Piano room.** Appears in Ch. 14.
- **Small study with a courtyard window.** Appears in Ch. 14. The apartment has no courtyard.

The writer's job is to know the floor plan well enough that the changes are precise. The reader should not have to consciously map the apartment, but the reader's unconscious is doing exactly that, and the changes need to be where the unconscious can feel them.

## A Note on the City and the Practical Surface

The novella does not name its city. The bureaucratic surface — utility companies, banks, civil registries, library returns, charity shops — is consistent with much of urban Northern Europe and parts of the anglophone world.

Specific notes:

- **The wall phone.** A landline wall-mounted phone in a kitchen is uncommon in 2020s urban apartments but not impossible. A reader will read it as slightly old-fashioned, slightly inherited. This works for the form: the apartment is the apartment of an older woman, and the wall phone is the kind of object that has been on the wall for decades.
- **The library book.** Returned in Chapter 3, fine waived. The librarian's small kindness is one of the novella's few warm gestures and should be calibrated carefully — present, not sentimental.
- **The civil registry office.** A counter with a clerk and a form. Universally recognisable.
- **The bank.** Marble or glass, polite, slow. Universally recognisable.
- **The solicitor's office.** Bookshelves, lamp, framed certificates. Universally recognisable.
- **The bakery.** Open. The light at the crossing. The street outside the apartment building. The walk home. The novella's only depiction of the city's continuing life.

The writer should know where the apartment is, what the city looks like, how long the walk to the bakery is, what the bakery sells, what the elm looks like at this latitude, what the crossing looks like, what the bereaved's own apartment looks like in relation to mother's apartment. The reader does not need to know any of this. The writer must.

## What the Novella Will Not Show

- **The funeral.** No funeral scene. No memorial. No graveside.
- **The mother in life or in death.** Her body is not in the apartment. She is not in the novella.
- **The father.** Not mentioned by name. Not seen. Inferable only.
- **The hospital.** Not visited.
- **The undertaker.** Not visited.
- **Any moment of breakdown by the protagonist.** Not depicted.
- **Any moment of breakthrough by the protagonist.** Not depicted.
- **Anyone helping the protagonist.** Not depicted.
- **A friend, partner, sibling, child.** None of these.
- **A pet.** No dog. No cat. The apartment has no animal life.
- **A plant indoors.** The plants on the balcony are dead. There are no living plants in the apartment.

The novella's surface is austere by design. The austerity is part of what allows the smallest objects (the cup, the elm, the rectangle of light, the grocery list) to carry the weight they do.

## Sources (Selected)

### Practical / How-To

- The advice columns and pamphlets produced by hospices, funeral providers, and bereavement organisations are the most reliable sources for the procedural details. Specific titles are less important than the texture of any half-dozen of them.
- *What to Do When Someone Dies* (UK, Which? Books, multiple editions) and equivalents in other countries.
- The websites of national bereavement charities (Cruse Bereavement Support, Hospice Foundation of America, Marie Curie, etc.) carry checklists.
- The websites of solicitors specialising in probate carry detailed walkthroughs.

### Literary

- Philip Roth, *Patrimony.* (The single most directly relevant text. A son helping his father die and clearing his life.)
- Karl Ove Knausgaard, *A Death in the Family* (Volume 1 of *My Struggle*). (The clearance scenes in the second half of the book are the closest literary kin to the novella's clearance work. The cleaning scenes especially.)
- Helen Garner, *The Spare Room.* (The texture of caring for someone dying in your house. Not a clearance book per se but adjacent.)
- Annie Ernaux, *I Remain in Darkness* and *A Man's Place.* (For the texture of clearing a parent's life and the small details that survive.)
- Lydia Davis, various stories on parents and apartments. (For the prose discipline.)
- W.G. Sebald, *The Rings of Saturn* and *Vertigo.* (For the practice of attention to small material details that carry historical and emotional weight.)
- Édouard Levé, *Suicide.* (For the form of an oblique posthumous accounting of a life.)
- Marie Kondo, *The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up.* (For the cultural framing of clearance as a project; the novella is in some ways a refutation.)

### Non-fiction on Stuff

- Frank Trentmann, *Empire of Things.* (For the broader history of consumer accumulation in the 20th century.)
- Daniel Miller, *The Comfort of Things.* (Anthropology of household objects. Particularly useful for understanding why a chipped mug from 1987 carries the weight it carries.)
- Russell Belk, "Possessions and the Extended Self" (Journal of Consumer Research, 1988). (Foundational paper on the way objects extend the person who owns them.)

The novella borrows posture most directly from Roth, Knausgaard, and Ernaux. The dossier's job is to ensure the writer can render the practical surface with absolute confidence, so that the deviations from the practical surface are visible against an unimpeachable ground.
