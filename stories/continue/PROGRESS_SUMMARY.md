## Revision Notes

### v0.1 — First-pass bookends (Pass 1)

**Objective:** Establish the baseline voice in Chapter 1 and the restored cadence in Chapter 17, so that the middle chapters have anchors to calibrate against.

**Approach:**
- Chapter 1 drafted end-to-end.
- Chapter 17 drafted end-to-end immediately after.
- Both chapters use identical phrasing for the kitchen walkthrough, the cup description, and the rectangle-of-light observational paragraph (seven sentences verbatim between the two).
- Chapter 17 includes a single rule 11 efficiency marker (*Take a cup down from the cupboard above the coffee machine without looking.*) that Chapter 1 does not include.
- Chapter 17 final line user-revised from *The light through the window moves across the floor.* to *See the light through the window move across the floor.* The revised imperative form shifts the closing from observation to direction-of-attention.

**Protected passages (DO NOT shorten in future revision):**
- The fourteen items of the original list in Ch 1 (must be quoted verbatim)
- The phone sentence in Ch 1 (*The phone on the kitchen wall is black, the kind with a cord...*)
- The rectangle of light sentence in Ch 1 and Ch 17 (*The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.*)
- The closing refrain *The street is empty.* in every chapter that ends with leaving mother's apartment
- The final sentence of Ch 17: *See the light through the window move across the floor.*
- The inscription passage in Ch 12 (italicised, past tense, contained)

### v0.1 — Pass 2 hardest chapters

**Objective:** Draft the three chapters where the form's discipline is most demanding: the contained break (Ch 12), the syntactic dissolution (Ch 14), and the cross-contamination rupture (Ch 7).

**Approach:**
- Drafted in the IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN order: Ch 12 → Ch 14 → Ch 7.
- Ch 12 uses the EXAMPLE_DRAFT provisional inscription text. This is canonical for v0.1 but should be revisited during Pass 2 revision; the six sentences carry the form's emotional load and deserve a dedicated revision session.
- Ch 14 delivers the syntactic dissolution through a single *and*-chain sentence (*Wrap a plate and the plate is a cup and wrap the cup and the cup is a plate and put the plate in the box.*) followed by additional object-swap sentences that extend the dissolution without formally breaking further.
- Ch 7 keeps the baseline register of the mother's-apartment chapters even while set at the protagonist's own apartment. The three unwrapped plates, the untaped box on the counter, and the tape dispenser still out — none of these are commented on. The neighbour's visit is rendered in paraphrase (no direct-quote italics).

**Glitch placements:**
- **Ch 12:** Bookshelves full again (recurrence from Ch 8, placed without comment). Inscription page full, in the handwriting of the list (the form's single permitted interior release). Extra half-flight and narrow landing in the stairwell (placed as flat observation: *A half-flight between the second-floor landing and the ground floor.*). List state: items reordered, a new entry *the summer with the door*, a paragraph that grows across chapters.
- **Ch 14:** Cup of coffee on the kitchen counter visible on arrival (anticipating the warm-coffee glitch). Piano room through the bedroom's back corner. Small study beyond the piano room, with a courtyard window; *The apartment has no courtyard.* Warm coffee in the impossible study; warm coffee on the kitchen counter after walking back; the two cups are the same cup. Syntactic dissolution in one *and*-chain sentence. Box labelled WARDROBE containing cups, plates, and a shirt (plate becomes shirt mid-paragraph). Box labelled KITCHEN filled with photographs from the shoebox. List: *Marianne* appears and becomes illegible again.
- **Ch 7:** Packing the protagonist's own plates on the counter — three wrapped, three unwrapped, the box abandoned half-full. Opposite building window-count glitch placed across two sentences (earlier: *The opposite building has six windows on the second floor of its grey facade.* later: *The opposite building has five windows on the second floor of its grey facade.*). The neighbour's *we've heard you moving around at night*. Chapter ends on *Dark.* — the broken refrain.

**Voice discipline self-check:**
- No protagonist pronouns in any of the three chapters
- No direct interior verbs
- No em-dash pivots (em-dashes used only in list items for formatting)
- No metaphors or similes
- No abstraction names
- No past-tense narration outside the italicised inscription
- After first draft, one pass identified and fixed five past-tense slips in Ch 12 and Ch 14 (was/were in comparison constructions) and one *wondered* in Ch 7 dialogue (converted to paraphrase)

**Known issues to address in revision:**
- Ch 7 (~923 words) slightly under the 1,700-word target for the chapter. The chapter is structurally quiet by design (no clearing work, no apartment to walk through, just the rain, the breakfast, the neighbour, the bed) but revision could expand the standing-at-the-window sequences and the evening beats.
- Ch 12 (~1141 words) under the 1,800-word target. The packing sequence could be extended; the six-book inscription moment could be given slightly more weight; the three trips down the stairs could each carry slightly more detail.
- Ch 14 (~1204 words) under the 2,300-word target, the longest chapter in the book. The rooms-that-do-not-exist sequence, the walk back, and the packing dissolution could all be slower. This is the chapter most likely to need significant expansion in revision.
- The inscription text in Ch 12 is provisional and should be revisited as its own piece of work.

## Notes and Observations

The voice held without visible effort during the Ch 1 and Ch 17 drafting. The bare-verb imperative mode reads naturally once it is calibrated, and the refusal of pronouns for the protagonist is now internalised enough that it does not require constant vigilance. The one passage that required multiple attempts was the "one sentence too many" about the phone at the end of Chapter 1 — several versions were tried before settling on the canonical form. The five-clause sentence with the cord coiled once around the hook below the handset feels right because each clause is a slightly unnecessary addition, and the final clause (*where mother left it*) is the quiet place the sentence has been approaching.

Chapter 17's echo of Chapter 1 is at this point almost verbatim in the kitchen-walkthrough and coffee-making sequences. This may turn out to be too on-the-nose; during revision, some Ch 17 phrasings may need slight variation to keep the echo from reading as copy-paste. But for the first draft the verbatim echo is the safer choice — it guarantees the cadence match that Pass 1's whole purpose is to establish.
