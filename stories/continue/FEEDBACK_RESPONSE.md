# Feedback Response Plan

## Early Publisher Feedback on v0.1 — What to Extract and Act On

Two publisher responses received on the v0.1 draft of *Continue* (shared as early draft, no expectation of commitment). Both are literary-house-oriented readers. Both take the work seriously, identify the lineage accurately, and converge on a small set of specific concerns. This document is the extraction of what is worth acting on, what is worth remembering for later, and what to leave alone.

It is a **response plan**, not a to-do list. The revisions it proposes are ordered by priority and should be considered in sequence with the other revision work already flagged in [PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT.md](PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT.md).

---

## Executive Summary

**Strong overall signal.** Both publishers read the form correctly, identify the lineage, and consider the manuscript *real literature* (publisher 2's phrase) with prize potential. Neither dismisses it, neither misreads it as amateur, neither asks for a conventional rewrite. This is approximately the best possible outcome from an early-draft review.

**Three concrete things to act on** (both publishers converge on these):

1. **Middle chapter engagement** — the uncanny escalation may be too slow; risk of losing readers in Chs 3-6.
2. **One emotional throughline could be sharper** — publisher 1 specifically names the Marianne / third-woman-in-the-photograph thread; publisher 2 asks for sharpening generally.
3. **The ending may read as drift** to some readers (publisher 2's concern; publisher 1 considers the ending a strength). Mixed signal, but worth a careful look.

**Seven positioning-intelligence items to capture** for the deferred publishing phase.

**Three things to note and NOT act on** — publisher misreadings and category-resolution requests the form correctly refuses.

**One structural decision the feedback informs** — the novella-length problem, already flagged in PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT and IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN as "compression vs. expansion." The feedback leans the decision toward expansion.

## Part 1: What the Feedback Validates

These are the elements both publishers identified as working. **They must not be damaged in revision.** Publisher 2 explicitly warns: *Bad edits could destroy the rhythm entirely.* Editorial fragility is real. Any revision pass should preserve these elements first and foremost.

### The voice discipline is working

- Publisher 1: "*almost procedural, second-person imperative voice*" — they read the bare-verb imperative as second-person addressed to the reader (a reader-side projection; see Part 4 below), but the reading is consistent and the *effect* is working.
- Publisher 2: "*Control of tone: extremely disciplined*" and "*Consistency of device: the procedural voice never breaks*."
- Publisher 1: "*The prose control is remarkable — the repetition is deliberate and accumulative rather than lazy.*"

**Preserve:** the bare-verb imperative, the absence of protagonist pronoun/name, the refusal of interiority. These are the central formal decisions and both publishers confirm they produce the intended effect.

### The list as central metaphor is working

- Publisher 1: "*The central conceit — a to-do list for clearing a dead mother's apartment that slowly becomes haunted, items rewriting themselves...*"
- Publisher 2: "*the list mutating is a strong central metaphor*" and names it as one of the *four things that work very well*.

**Preserve:** the list's entire arc from Ch 1 to Ch 17 as currently staged. Every list state described in THEME_TRACKER and every list appearance in the drafted chapters is working. The degradation is legible.

### The emotional architecture — grief leaking through procedure — is working

- Publisher 1: "*Grief here isn't expressed; it leaks through the cracks in the procedural surface.*" (This is almost word-for-word the form's thesis statement.)
- Publisher 2: "*Ritual / bureaucracy of grief... grief rendered as task management.*"

**Preserve:** every refusal-to-name in the draft. The word *grief* never appears, and neither publisher needs it to. The form's central discipline is confirmed.

### The ending's structural echo is working (to publisher 1; see Part 3 for publisher 2)

- Publisher 1: "*The final chapter's near-exact repetition of Chapter 1, but with the protagonist choosing to leave the key, the list, and the photograph behind, is a quietly devastating structural move.*"

**Preserve:** the Ch 17 / Ch 1 cadence match, the rectangle-of-light sentence verbatim, the three-objects-on-the-counter gesture, the unlocked door, the mid-chapter refrain, the final light-across-floor closing.

### The restrained emotional moments are landing

- Publisher 1: "*The emotional restraint makes the few moments of direct feeling — the inscription in the book, the word 'forgive,' the children's drawings — land hard.*"

**Preserve:** the Ch 12 inscription passage (all six italicised sentences), the *forgive* on the list in Ch 13, the Ch 15 crayon drawings in the child's room. All three are doing what they were designed to do. The inscription text — currently still provisional per PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT — is confirmed as landing; the revision should sharpen rather than replace it.

### Specific glitches are being noticed

- Publisher 1 explicitly mentions: "*the wardrobe refilling (Chapter 5), the desk appearing, the photograph changing its contents depending on distance.*"
- Publisher 2: "*The world behaves like a corrupted save file.*" — this is an elegant external articulation of the entire glitch architecture.

**Preserve:** these specific moments and the general glitch discipline (across-sentence placement per rule 10, no commentary, no explanation).

### The ambiguity around Marianne is working (as ambiguity)

- Publisher 1: "*the relationship with 'Marianne' and the third woman in the photograph could use slightly more anchoring — **though the ambiguity is also clearly intentional**.*"

**Preserve:** the ambiguity. See Part 3 for the anchoring question.

## Part 2: Convergent Concerns — Things Both Publishers Flag

These are the places where both readers, independently, point at the same issue. Convergent concerns are high-priority because they are unlikely to be idiosyncratic.

### Concern 1: Middle chapter engagement / uncanny escalation timing

**Publisher 1:** *the middle chapters risk losing a reader before the uncanny elements fully take hold.*

**Publisher 2:** *Slight escalation of anomalies earlier* — listed as the first of three editorial interventions.

**What they mean:** Chapters 3-6 have glitches but those glitches are too subtle, too quiet, or too far between. A reader who is not already inside the form may disengage before the form starts doing its work. By the time the Phase 3 and Phase 4 machinery kicks in (Ch 7's rupture, Ch 8's ringing phone, Ch 9's middle-name discrepancy, Ch 10's dated freezer package), some readers will have put the book down.

**The tension with the form's discipline:** the glitch escalation was designed to be slow and accumulative. Making the early glitches louder risks breaking the calm baseline that the whole form depends on. But the publishers are saying the baseline may be *too* calm for its first third.

**Possible interventions** (to be evaluated, not committed to):

- **Amplify the existing Ch 3 glitches.** The bedroom-through-the-kitchen spatial glitch is very quiet. It could be given one extra beat of pressure — the body pausing briefly at the threshold because the route doesn't match, then continuing. (Risk: this edges toward commentary. Would need careful wording.)
- **Make the Ch 4 boxes-by-door / boxes-in-car contradiction more visible.** Currently it is two adjacent sentences with no framing. It could be set up with a clearer spatial cue so the contradiction lands harder.
- **Bring one visceral glitch forward.** The wall phone currently has its first active pickup in Ch 6 (dial tone) and first ring in Ch 8. Having it ring once in Ch 5 — briefly, unanswered, without comment — would give the reader a visceral moment earlier. This has to be done without breaking the form; it would be a single beat, not a new subplot.
- **Rule 11 moments as engagement.** The memory-as-efficiency rule is currently deployed most visibly in Chs 6, 8, and 17. Deploying it more in Chs 3 and 4 would give the reader something to hold onto — the small intimacies of a body that knows the apartment too well — while the glitches are still quiet. This is additive, not structural.
- **The grocery list drawer.** The Ch 4 grocery-list-in-the-drawer moment is currently a single observation (body reads it, puts it back). This is one of the novella's most emotionally-charged objects and it gets one sentence of weight. It could be given texture (per rule 3a's landing discipline) — a beat where the handwriting is described more specifically, where the slip of paper is held for a moment longer.

**Recommended revision priority: HIGH.** Begin with the softest interventions (rule 11 moments and grocery list texture) and only escalate to structural changes if those are insufficient.

### Concern 2: Sharpening one emotional throughline

**Publisher 1:** *the relationship with 'Marianne' and the third woman in the photograph could use slightly more anchoring.*

**Publisher 2:** *Sharpening one emotional throughline (even subtly).*

**What they mean:** The emotional architecture is working at the level of individual moments (inscription, forgive, drawings) but the *connective tissue* between moments could be stronger. Publisher 1 is specific: the Marianne / third-woman thread is currently two beats (Ch 6 list, Ch 6 photograph) plus a brief reappearance in Ch 14 and a faint echo in Ch 16. The thread is there but thin across the middle of the book.

**The tension with the form's discipline:** the planning documents strictly state *Marianne appears exactly once*. Adding a beat relaxes this rule. But publisher 1 explicitly says the ambiguity is intentional and should be preserved — so the anchoring doesn't need to *explain* Marianne, it just needs to give the reader *more moments to hold onto*.

**Possible interventions:**

- **Ch 13 photograph beat.** Between the phone-company call scene and the list check with *forgive*, the body could briefly take the photograph out of the coat pocket and look at it. No interpretation, no movement. The photograph is still what it is. The beat lasts two or three sentences. This reinforces both the photograph thread and the forgive thread in the same chapter, without naming Marianne or explaining anything. **My recommended intervention.**
- **Ch 11 photograph beat.** During the sitting-on-the-floor scene after the solicitor, the body could take the photograph out briefly. Similar function but in a different chapter.
- **A second Marianne beat.** If publisher 1's specific suggestion is taken literally, Marianne could appear a second time — perhaps in a document (a name on the mother's address book, a name on a medical form from the father's death a few years ago, a name on the utility company's emergency contact). This would relax the "exactly once" rule.
- **The woman-in-the-grass beat.** The Ch 6 photograph has "a woman standing behind them in the grass, her face out of focus." This woman could briefly reappear — perhaps in a different photograph in Ch 6 that the body doesn't pocket, or as a half-glimpse in the Ch 16 photograph-stabilisation sequence.

**Recommended revision priority: HIGH.** Start with the Ch 13 photograph beat (softest intervention, highest return). Only add a second Marianne beat if the Ch 13 photograph doesn't sufficiently anchor the thread.

### Concern 3: Ensuring the ending lands as culmination, not drift

**Publisher 2:** *Ensuring the ending lands as culmination, not drift* — listed as the third of three editorial interventions.

**Publisher 1 (contrary):** *The final chapter's near-exact repetition of Chapter 1, but with the protagonist choosing to leave the key, the list, and the photograph behind, is a quietly devastating structural move.*

**Mixed signal.** Publisher 1 considers the ending a clear strength. Publisher 2 worries about it. This is a reader-experience divergence: publisher 1 is reading with the form's discipline, publisher 2 is warning based on what "some readers" might feel.

**What to do:** Do not break the form's refusal-of-closure. The ending's point is that the body leaves without finishing, and the door stays open. Adding resolution would destroy the form.

**But** there is a minor interventions that could make the ending *land more clearly as deliberate* without adding closure:

- **Slow the three-object placement in Ch 17.** Currently the paper, key, and photograph are placed on the counter in three brisk imperative sentences. The placement is the book's central gesture. It could be slightly slower — a beat of looking at each object as it is set down, or a moment where the three objects are observed in their row before the body turns away. This would make the placement feel like an *act* rather than an *activity*.
- **One small landing moment at the own apartment.** Currently the arrival home is compressed: *Walk three blocks home. Unlock the door and go in. Hang the coat on the hook in the hall. Sit down in the chair by the window.* Four sentences of procedure. Could have one sentence of arrival (the quiet of the own apartment, the light on the floor, the chair waiting) before the final sentence. This would signal that the arrival is the ending, not a stopping point.

**Recommended revision priority: MEDIUM.** Publisher 1's reading is closer to the form's intent. The intervention should be small and in the direction of *making the intended effect more legible* — not in the direction of adding closure.

## Part 3: Things to Note and NOT Act On

These are publisher responses that represent misreadings or requests the form correctly refuses. They should be logged here so that future revision work doesn't accidentally drift toward them.

### "Second-person imperative" — a reader-side projection

Publisher 1 reads the voice as *second-person imperative*, as if the bare verbs are addressing the reader directly. The actual discipline is impersonal — the bare verbs have no subject and no addressee; they are the grammar of a body going through motions without naming itself. But the reader's experience of the voice as "second-person" is a natural projection (it is the most familiar grammatical container for bare imperatives in English), and it is not wrong.

**What not to do:** Do not change the voice to be more explicitly second-person. Do not add *you*. Do not lean into the instructional-writing register. The voice's impersonal nature is load-bearing; if it becomes overt second-person, the novella becomes a different thing.

**What to note:** The reader-experience of the voice is second-person-like. In pitch materials and positioning, this is worth referencing — it is how the voice *reads*, even if it is not how the voice *is*.

### "Is it grief? haunting? dementia? simulation?" — category-resolution requests

Publisher 2 asks whether the form is grief, haunting, dementia, or simulation — framing these as alternatives the manuscript should resolve. The form deliberately refuses to resolve; the whole point of the Todorovian sustained hesitation is that the novella does not choose a frame and does not tell the reader which frame is correct.

**What not to do:** Do not add explanation. Do not add genre signals (not ghost, not dementia, not simulation). The refusal is the form.

**What to note:** Publisher 2 is flagging this as a marketing/positioning concern — a reader who cannot place the book in a genre may disengage. This is a real concern but it is not fixable by editorial revision; it is fixable only by positioning the book correctly at publication time. The pitch line *literary ghost story told through bureaucracy* (publisher 1) does the positioning work: it names a frame (ghost story) while the book itself refuses to confirm or deny the frame.

### "Too experimental" sales-team categorisation

Publisher 1 flags the risk that sales teams will categorise the book as too experimental for commercial traction. This is a market-side concern, not a craft-side concern. It is not fixable by revising the book.

**What not to do:** Do not tone down the form to chase commercial categorisation. The book's identity is its experimental form; softening that identity would destroy the book without gaining the commercial audience.

**What to note:** Target publishers that specifically publish formally unusual work (Fitzcarraldo, Dorothy, Dalkey, New Directions, And Other Stories, Galley Beggar). Don't waste submission attempts on publishers who need commercial crossover.

**What to do with this:** When the revision phases begin, every revision pass should be *against* the existing form (preserving voice, preserving discipline, preserving glitch architecture) rather than *toward* a different form. Small, specific, preservational revisions. No structural rewrites unless explicitly necessary. The revisions proposed in Part 2 above should all be evaluated against this warning.

## Part 5: Concrete Revision Priorities (Ordered)

In sequence, from highest-value-lowest-risk to highest-risk-highest-value:

### Priority 1 (high value, low risk): The Ch 13 photograph beat

**What:** Add a two-or-three-sentence beat where the body takes the photograph out of the coat pocket between the phone-company call and the list check with *forgive*. The photograph is still what it is (still mother and child, or mother and child and third woman — whatever the Ch 13 state should be). The beat is wordless; no interpretation, no comment. The photograph goes back in the pocket and the list check follows.

**Why:** Addresses concern 2 (emotional throughline anchoring) with the softest possible intervention. Reinforces the photograph thread and the forgive thread in the same chapter without explaining anything. Does not relax the "Marianne exactly once" rule.

**Risk:** Very low. A small addition to an already quiet chapter.

### Priority 2 (high value, low risk): Rule 11 amplification in Chs 3-4

**What:** Deploy rule 11 (memory as efficiency) more visibly in Chs 3 and 4 — the efficient opening of drawers without looking, the knowing of where bills are kept, the familiarity of the wardrobe's layout. Small additions. No structural change.

**Why:** Addresses concern 1 (middle chapter engagement) by giving the reader small intimacies to hold onto while the glitches are still quiet. The body's pre-drafted knowledge of the apartment carries weight that the reader can feel even if they don't articulate it.

**Risk:** Low. Adds texture; does not change structure.

### Priority 3 (high value, medium risk): Grocery list landing in Ch 4

**What:** Add texture to the Ch 4 grocery-list-in-the-drawer moment. Currently: *The folded piece of paper is a grocery list in mother's handwriting. Milk, bread, fish, oranges. The handwriting is careful and slightly slanted. Read the grocery list once and put it back in the drawer.* This is already quiet and intimate but has not been given a sweep-rule landing moment. Possible landing: *The paper is warm, as if it has just been held* — but this edges toward a glitch. Alternative: *The paper is yellowed at the creases from being folded and unfolded many times* — this carries years of mother reading-and-rewriting her own list. Simpler and keeps the domestic register.

**Why:** Addresses concern 1 (middle chapter engagement) and gives the grocery list — which will replace the list in Ch 15 — more initial weight.

**Risk:** Medium. If the landing is too loud, it becomes an announcement. If it is too subtle, it disappears. Needs careful wording.

### Priority 4 (medium value, low risk): Ch 17 three-objects placement slowed

**What:** Slow the Ch 17 placement of the paper, key, and photograph on the counter. Currently four imperative sentences. Could be five or six, with a small beat of looking at each object as it is set down, or a final sentence observing the three objects in a row before the body turns away.

**Why:** Addresses concern 3 (ending drift risk) by making the placement feel more deliberate. Does not add closure.

**Risk:** Low. Additive, not structural.

### Priority 5 (medium value, medium risk): Early glitch amplification (Ch 3 or Ch 5)

**What:** One small additional glitch beat in Ch 3 or Ch 5. Options:
- A brief wall phone ring in Ch 5 (unanswered, not commented on)
- A small documentary anomaly in Ch 3's utility call (a second name on the account that doesn't match anyone)
- A cup in the wrong cupboard in Ch 4 (currently this glitch first appears in Ch 6)

**Why:** Addresses concern 1 more directly than priorities 2-3. Brings visceral glitch material earlier.

**Risk:** Medium. Risks breaking the planned glitch escalation. Should only be attempted if priorities 2-3 do not sufficiently address the middle-chapter engagement concern.

### Priority 6 (deferred): Inscription text revision

**What:** A dedicated revision session on the six italicised sentences in Ch 12.

**Why:** Publisher 1 confirms the inscription lands hard, which supports the current version. But the inscription was flagged as provisional in PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT even before the publisher feedback. The feedback raises the stakes — the inscription is one of the book's most effective moments and deserves its own session.

**Risk:** The inscription is working. A revision session should be *sharpening* rather than *replacing*. Could easily be made worse by a heavy hand.

### Priority 7 (deferred, structural): Compression vs expansion decision

**What:** The PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT question of whether to accept ~16k words as form-appropriate or expand toward the 28,500 target. The publisher feedback leans toward expansion for industry-length reasons but also because several chapters read as under-drafted.

**Why:** Deferred per PROGRESS_ASSESSMENT, but the feedback provides new information.

**Risk:** High. Wrong expansion could bloat the form. Right expansion could give the middle chapters room to breathe and address concern 1 organically.

**Recommendation:** Do not make this decision until priorities 1-4 have been completed and the revised draft read end-to-end. The compression may read differently once the Ch 13 photograph beat, rule 11 amplifications, and Ch 4 grocery list landing are in place.
