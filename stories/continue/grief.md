# Grief

## Research Dossier: The Phenomenology and Pathology of Bereavement

---

## Overview

This document compiles research on grief — particularly the kinds of grief most relevant to "Continue": the loss of a parent, the loss of a parent with whom the relationship was unfinished, complicated grief, prolonged grief disorder, and the day-to-day phenomenology of bereaved cognition. The novella never names grief. This document exists so that the writer knows precisely what they are not naming.

A note on usage: nothing in this dossier is for paraphrasing into the prose. The dossier exists to give the writer a private map of what is happening to the protagonist that the prose will not make available to the reader. The reader experiences the map by inference. The writer must hold the whole map.

---

## What "Grief" Is (Briefly)

Grief is the constellation of physical, cognitive, behavioural, and existential responses to the loss of a person, a relationship, a future, or an identity. It is not a single emotion. It is a reorganisation of the bereaved person's relationship to reality, occurring in the body and the mind simultaneously, over a period that contemporary research describes in terms of months and years rather than days and weeks.

Grief is not depression, though it overlaps with depressive symptoms. It is not trauma, though it can co-occur with trauma. It is not anxiety, though it produces anxious states. It is its own thing. It is also one of the most universal experiences a human will have, and one of the least well described in language.

The novella's central technical problem is to depict grief without naming it and without reaching for the abstractions that grief literature has accumulated. The dossier collects what the writer needs to know in order to depict it through specifics.

## Models of Grief

### The Stages Model (Kübler-Ross, 1969) and Its Misuse

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross described five stages — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — based on her work with terminally ill patients (not bereaved survivors). The stages were originally about people anticipating their *own* death.

The model's popularisation reframed it as a five-stage model of bereavement. This reframing is empirically poor and has been broadly rejected by contemporary grief researchers. Bereaved people do not move through stages in order. They move through states that recur, overlap, and resist sequencing. The five-stage model's continued popularity in lay culture is itself a phenomenon worth understanding: it is the kind of clean model bereaved people reach for because it offers the promise of a sequence with an end. The novella's the protagonist is exactly the kind of person who might privately want such a model and would never speak of it.

**For the novella:** the Kübler-Ross model is something the novella does not endorse and does not refute. It is a thing in the cultural air. the protagonist is not visibly using it.

### The Dual Process Model (Stroebe and Schut, 1999)

Stroebe and Schut proposed that bereavement involves an oscillation between two orientations:

1. **Loss-orientation:** confronting the loss, grieving, missing the deceased, dealing with the emotional reality.
2. **Restoration-orientation:** doing the practical work of reorganising one's life — bills, paperwork, household, social roles, identity.

The bereaved person *oscillates* between the two. They cannot stay in either indefinitely. The work of restoration provides necessary breaks from the work of loss; the work of loss provides the affective ground that gives restoration its purpose.

**For the novella:** This is the model most useful for understanding the protagonist. He is doing restoration-orientation work — clearing the apartment, calling the utility company, finding the will, returning the library book — at extreme length. His oscillation back into loss-orientation is what the novella refuses to depict directly. The glitches are arguably the loss-orientation pressing on the restoration-orientation work without ever being given its own scenes. The Dual Process Model gives the writer a frame for what the protagonist is *not* doing on the page.

### Continuing Bonds (Klass, Silverman, Nickman, 1996)

Earlier grief models, including Freud's, emphasised "letting go" and "decathexis" — withdrawing emotional investment from the deceased and reinvesting it elsewhere. The Continuing Bonds school proposed instead that bereaved people typically maintain an ongoing relationship with the dead — by talking to them, keeping their possessions, dreaming about them, sensing their presence, asking them for advice, marking their absences in routines.

Contemporary research has largely supported this view. Continuing bonds are not pathological; they are the norm. People talk to dead parents for the rest of their lives. People keep a coat in the closet. People walk into a room and notice the chair.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's continuing bond with mother is unspoken and unobservable. The novella does not give him a scene of talking to her. It gives him a wall phone that rings. It gives him a grocery list in his coat pocket. The continuing bond exists in the apartment's refusal to be cleared.

### Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)

Added to the ICD-11 in 2018 and to the DSM-5-TR in 2022, Prolonged Grief Disorder is the formal diagnostic category for grief that persists with high intensity beyond what would be expected. The criteria include:

- Persistent yearning or longing for the deceased.
- Preoccupation with the deceased.
- Identity disruption.
- A sense of disbelief about the death.
- Avoidance of reminders of the loss.
- Intense emotional pain (anger, bitterness, sorrow).
- Difficulty re-engaging.
- Emotional numbness.
- A sense that life is meaningless.
- Persistent loneliness.
- Symptoms persisting more than 6–12 months after the loss (criteria vary).

PGD is not the same as depression and is not the same as PTSD, though it co-occurs with both. The diagnostic category is contested for several reasons: it pathologises a normal experience; it imposes timelines that vary by culture; it can be used to push bereaved people into treatment they do not need.

**For the novella:** PGD is one frame the novella refuses. The novella does not place the protagonist on a diagnostic axis. He is not given a label. He is given a list and an apartment and a sequence of tasks. The reader who knows the diagnostic literature can apply it; the reader who does not, sees a man clearing an apartment.

### Complicated Grief

Before PGD was formalised, "complicated grief" was the term used for grief that became stuck — that did not move through the expected processes of integration. Complicated grief is associated with:

- Sudden or violent loss.
- Loss of a child.
- Loss in the context of an unresolved relationship.
- Loss in the context of guilt.
- Loss in the context of estrangement.
- Loss in the context of unfinished business.
- Loss in the absence of social support.

the protagonist's loss carries several of these markers: an unresolved relationship, guilt, estrangement, unfinished business, and (as far as the novella shows) no social support. The novella is depicting, without naming, a grief structurally configured to become complicated.

**For the novella:** the writer should know that what the protagonist is going through has a name, has a literature, has a treatment, and has nothing to do with the novella's prose. The prose is about a man clearing an apartment. The complicated grief is the reader's possible explanation; the novella does not adopt it.

## The Phenomenology of Acute Grief

A bereaved person, especially in the first months, reports:

### Bodily Symptoms

- Heaviness in the chest, the sensation of a weight on the sternum (sometimes called "the iron weight" in older literature).
- A constricted throat.
- Sighing breath. Inability to take a full breath.
- Dry mouth.
- Hollow stomach. Loss of appetite, sometimes alternating with sudden hunger.
- Sleep disturbance — early waking is more common than insomnia at sleep onset.
- Fatigue disproportionate to activity.
- Cold hands and feet.
- Aches in muscles that have not been worked.
- Increased risk of cardiovascular events. (Studies have shown elevated risk of myocardial infarction in the first weeks after the death of a spouse or close relative.)
- Compromised immune function.
- The startle response heightened.
- A strange new body, in general — the body that the bereaved person inhabits after a major loss is often unfamiliar to them.

**For the novella:** the protagonist is a body. The novella does not enumerate his bodily symptoms; it shows him eating, drinking, sleeping (or claiming to). The reader who knows the somatic phenomenology of grief reads the eating and the sleeping at full attention. The novella permits the protagonist to be tired without saying so by giving his actions a slightly slower rhythm in middle chapters and by placing him at the kitchen table for "a long time" after the phone-company call.

### Cognitive Symptoms

- Difficulty concentrating. Reading the same paragraph three times.
- Forgetfulness about minor things — keys, names, appointments.
- A strange clarity about other things — the bereaved person can recall the smallest details of the dead person's last week and forget what they ate for lunch yesterday.
- Time distortion. An hour feels like a day, a week feels like a month. Or the reverse: weeks pass without registering.
- A sense of unreality, sometimes bordering on dissociation. Things look slightly wrong. The kitchen looks like a kitchen but also like a stage set of a kitchen.
- Searching behaviour. Without intending to, the bereaved person scans rooms for the deceased. Looks at chairs. Listens for footsteps. Reaches for the phone to call them.
- "Ghosts in the home." Many bereaved people report sensing the presence of the deceased — hearing them in another room, glimpsing them, dreaming of them with unusual vividness.
- Numbness alternating with sharp pangs.
- Difficulty making decisions, even small ones.
- A reduced sense that time has a future.

**For the novella:** This list is the novella's secret architecture. *Time distortion. A sense of unreality. Searching behaviour. Ghosts in the home.* The glitches in the apartment are, at one possible reading, the somatic and cognitive symptoms of acute grief presented without their somatic and cognitive labels. The cups in the wrong cupboard is a small instance of the kitchen looking slightly wrong. The wall phone ringing when it has been dead for four months is a maximally vivid instance of "reaching for the phone to call them". The novella does not say any of this. The dossier's job is to ensure the writer knows.

### Emotional Symptoms

- Sadness. (The most expected, often the least dominant.)
- Anger. At the deceased, at oneself, at the doctors, at God, at the universe, at strangers who have not had this happen to them.
- Guilt. (See guilt.md for the dedicated dossier.)
- Relief. (Especially after a long illness or a difficult relationship — and especially shameful for the bereaved to admit.)
- Fear. Of one's own mortality, of further losses, of the future, of the empty house, of being alone.
- Loneliness. A particular and absolute loneliness; the realisation that one specific other person is no longer reachable.
- Yearning. The pull toward the deceased. The wanting to call them. The wanting to ask them something.
- Numbness. The absence of expected feeling. The bereaved person often reports being unable to feel what they "should" be feeling, especially in early days.
- Disbelief. The mind's refusal to accept the news. Returning, over and over, to the question of whether it is real.
- Bewilderment. A general sense of "I do not understand what is happening to me."

**For the novella:** the protagonist is not given any of these as named feelings. The reader is invited to attribute as much or as little as they want. The form's discipline is that whatever the protagonist is feeling is *not on the page*. The reader who has been bereaved will attribute generously. The reader who has not, will see a man clearing an apartment and feel an unease they cannot place.

### Behavioural Symptoms

- Withdrawal from social contact, even from people who are trying to help.
- A particular kind of competence at small tasks, alternating with a complete inability to do other small tasks. (The bereaved can phone the gas company but cannot answer their own phone. Or vice versa.)
- Avoidance of specific places, objects, music, foods.
- Obsessive return to specific places, objects, music, foods.
- Repetitive talking about the deceased (with the few people the bereaved will speak to).
- Repetitive *not* talking about the deceased (with most other people).
- Uncharacteristic risk-taking or uncharacteristic caution.
- Sleep displacement: the bereaved person stays up later, sleeps badly, wakes earlier.
- Walking around at night.

**For the novella:** "Walking around at night" is the chapter "The Neighbour". The neighbour says: *we've heard him moving around at night.* the protagonist says he sleeps fine. The reader who knows that bereaved people walk around at night sees the entire chapter at full pressure.

## The Specific Configuration: Loss of an Estranged Parent

The novella's grief is specifically the grief of an adult son for mother, after a period of estrangement, in the absence of any reconciliation.

This configuration carries specific features:

### The Unfinished Conversation

Estranged adult children often live with the assumption that the relationship will be repaired *eventually*. The eventually is what the death removes. The bereaved is left with a conversation that has been postponed for years and is now permanently unhad.

The unfinished conversation has its own grammar. The bereaved repeats it to themselves, in the kitchen, on the train, while doing dishes. Sometimes they begin to write letters they will not send. Sometimes they speak the dead parent's part as well as their own. Sometimes they discover they no longer know what they wanted to say.

**For the novella:** The unfinished conversation is the wall phone. It is the inscription that was blank and is now full. It is the entire reason there is a phone on the kitchen wall and a one-too-many sentence about it in the first chapter. The novella does not say "the unfinished conversation". It places the conversation's surface: the phone, the inscription, the list with *forgive*.

### The Last Years

In an estrangement, the last years of the parent's life are often a particular blur for the adult child. They did not see them. They received occasional updates from third parties. They did not know what the parent's daily life looked like. The clearance of the apartment is, at one level, the first time the bereaved sees how their parent was actually living in those years.

This experience is often disorienting. Things have changed in the apartment that the bereaved cannot account for. The parent has bought a new appliance the bereaved did not know about. The parent has moved a piece of furniture. The parent has acquired a friend the bereaved has never heard of. (Marianne, in the novella, may be one such friend.) The bereaved encounters their parent's *unknown life* and recognises that it was a life and that they were absent from it.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's competence in clearing the apartment is, at one reading, the competence of someone who knows the apartment well from before the estrangement. The novella does not say so. The reader who has lived this experience will recognise it.

### The Apology That Cannot Be Made

In the absence of reconciliation, the bereaved often discovers that they were carrying an apology they had never quite formulated. The death makes the apology impossible to deliver and impossible to retract. Some bereaved people write letters and burn them. Some speak the apology in empty rooms. Some never apologise and live with the not-apologising for the rest of their lives. None of these is a moral failure; all are responses to a situation in which the apology has lost its possible recipient.

The therapeutic literature on this is large. The literary tradition is larger. (See the_uncanny.md for the literary side; see guilt.md for the affective side.)

**For the novella:** The inscription page in Chapter 12 is the apology. It is in the protagonist's handwriting. It appears on a page that was previously blank. It says, in a few sentences, what cannot be said. The novella permits this — and only this — and contains it inside the italicised passage. After the inscription, the protagonist closes the book and tapes the box.

### The Phone That Was Not Answered

One specific configuration that recurs in the literature on complicated grief: the bereaved missed the deceased's last call, last text, last attempted contact. The bereaved was busy. Or the bereaved was avoiding. Or the bereaved did not know what the call would be. The deceased then died, and the missed contact becomes the bereaved's central artefact of guilt.

This is the configuration the novella is built around, displaced one generation backward. the protagonist's guilt about *mother* is structured by his earlier failure to answer the phone on the day his *father* died. The novella never says this. The reader assembles it from: the wall phone, the one sentence too many in Chapter 1, the name *Marianne* on the list, the operator's *the line was disconnected four months ago*, the inscription's *I should have come*.

**For the novella:** This configuration is the deepest backstory. The novella has been built so that the reader *can* assemble it and is never told it. See CONCEPTUAL_MAP.md "The Three Inferable Backstories".

## Cultural and Historical Frames

### The Victorian Mourning Code

In the nineteenth century in much of Europe and the United States, mourning was an externally regulated practice. Widows wore black for a year. Households suspended social activity. Letters were on bordered paper. Funerals were elaborate and public. The bereaved had a script. The script was confining and frequently inhumane, but it was also a structure that took some of the work off the bereaved and put it onto the social code.

The twentieth century dismantled the script. The mid-twentieth century medicalised grief. The late twentieth century individualised it. The early twenty-first century has, in some quarters, begun to reach for ritual again, though without the consensus to make rituals collective.

**For the novella:** the protagonist has no script. There is no funeral scene in the novella. There is no period of formal mourning. There is no friend who "drops by". The absence of script is one of the conditions that gives the novella its emptiness. The clearing of the apartment is the only ritual the protagonist has access to, and the novella shows it being slowly dismantled around him.

### Anglophone Grief Avoidance

Contemporary anglophone culture is, broadly, avoidant of grief. Bereaved people are given a few days off work. They are expected to "be all right" after a few weeks. They are praised for "staying strong" — which is often understood to mean "not crying in front of others". Friends and family do not know what to say and so often say nothing or say the wrong thing.

This avoidance is not malicious; it is structural. It is what most people in most workplaces have learned to do. It is also what bereaved people often internalise: the bereaved person decides, on day three, that they should be "fine by now", and acts fine, and then is ambushed by grief two months later in a supermarket.

**For the novella:** the protagonist is, on the visible surface, doing what an avoidant culture asks bereaved people to do. He is functioning. He is making the calls. He is filing the forms. The novella's reader, who can see what the surface conceals (the wall phone, the list, the apartment), can also see that the avoidance is the crisis. The novella does not editorialise.

### Other Cultural Models (Briefly)

- **Jewish mourning** has a defined structure: shiva (seven days), shloshim (thirty days), the year of mourning, the kaddish. The structure provides container and ritual.
- **Islamic mourning** also has defined periods, particularly for widows.
- **Mexican Día de los Muertos** holds the dead in a continued ritual relationship across generations, modelling continuing bonds explicitly.
- **East Asian ancestor practices** maintain continuing bonds through household altars and seasonal observances.
- **Northern European secular cultures** (the novella's likely setting) have largely lost their inherited mourning structures and have not consistently replaced them.

**For the novella:** Whatever city the novella is set in, it is a city where the protagonist has no inherited structure to lean on. He has a list. He has an apartment. He has the bakery on the corner.

## What Researchers Currently Know About Helping

The therapeutic interventions that have evidence behind them include:

- **Complicated Grief Therapy (CGT)** developed by Katherine Shear: a structured 16-session protocol that combines elements of interpersonal therapy and cognitive-behavioural therapy specifically for complicated grief.
- **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy** for grief, with adaptations.
- **Group bereavement support**, particularly for specific kinds of loss (loss of a child, loss to suicide, loss to overdose).
- **Letters to the deceased** as a guided practice.
- **Continuing bonds work**, in the right framework, to legitimise the ongoing relationship rather than push for "letting go".
- **Walking, exercise, sleep regulation, basic somatic care.**

What is *not* generally found to help:

- Telling the bereaved person to "move on".
- Telling the bereaved person they are in a particular stage of a five-stage model.
- Pressuring the bereaved person into talking before they are ready.
- Suppressing the bereaved person's continuing bond.
- Giving the bereaved person large amounts of unsolicited advice.

**For the novella:** the protagonist is not in any of these therapeutic structures. He is alone. He has no therapist. He has no group. He has the apartment. The novella is not a recommendation against therapy; it is a depiction of a man who is not in any. The reader who wishes the novella had a therapy scene is feeling, accurately, that one might have helped — and the novella's refusal to provide one is part of its honesty about the kind of bereaved life many people actually live.

## What the Novella Will Not Do With Any of This

The novella will not name any of the categories in this dossier. It will not depict a stage. It will not depict a diagnostic process. It will not offer a therapeutic recommendation. It will not show the protagonist researching anything. It will not show the protagonist considering whether what he is going through has a name. It will not show the protagonist talking to anyone about it. It will not have a wise friend, a wise priest, a wise therapist, or a wise stranger who delivers the framework the dossier provides.

The dossier's contents are *the writer's*. They give the writer the confidence to depict, in physical particulars, what the writer knows is happening at the level of the somatic, cognitive, and affective phenomenology of acute grief. The reader experiences the phenomenology by inference. The form is the inference.

## Sources (Selected)

These are the kinds of works the writer should have at least passed through before drafting the novella. Specific editions are not necessary; the goal is calibration of intuition.

### Clinical / Empirical

- Bonanno, George A. *The Other Side of Sadness: What the New Science of Bereavement Tells Us About Life After Loss.* (For the empirical correction to the stages model.)
- Shear, M. Katherine et al. Papers on Complicated Grief Therapy.
- Stroebe, Margaret S. and Henk Schut. Papers on the Dual Process Model of Coping with Bereavement.
- Klass, Dennis, Phyllis R. Silverman, Steven L. Nickman (eds.). *Continuing Bonds: New Understandings of Grief.*
- Worden, J. William. *Grief Counseling and Grief Therapy.* (For the four "tasks of mourning" model — dated but still useful as a frame.)

### Memoir / Literary

- C.S. Lewis, *A Grief Observed.* (The classic short text on the disorientation of acute grief.)
- Joan Didion, *The Year of Magical Thinking.* (Dual-orientation grief in real time, in literary prose. The "magical thinking" of the title is the Continuing Bond pressed to its phenomenological limit.)
- Joan Didion, *Blue Nights.* (The grief of a parent for a child, with the same observational discipline, taken further into the unsayable.)
- Helen Macdonald, *H is for Hawk.* (Grief for a father, displaced into another activity entirely. Very useful as a structural model: the displacement *is* the grieving.)
- Julian Barnes, *Levels of Life,* third section. (The widower's account.)
- Max Porter, *Grief is the Thing with Feathers.* (Grief depicted with formal invention; the opposite formal solution to the one *Continue* takes, but worth knowing.)
- Denise Riley, *Time Lived, Without Its Flow.* (On grief and time after the death of a son. Particularly important for the time-distortion section.)
- Anne Carson, *Nox.* (Grief for a brother. The form is the carrying.)
- Roland Barthes, *Mourning Diary.* (Daily fragments after the death of mother. The closest stylistic neighbour to *Continue* in the literature on bereaved cognition.)

### Medical Anthropology / Sociology

- Walter, Tony. *On Bereavement: The Culture of Grief.*
- Doka, Kenneth J. *Disenfranchised Grief: Recognising Hidden Sorrow.* (Particularly relevant for grief in the context of estrangement.)

The novella borrows posture from Barthes and Didion most directly. It does not borrow content. The dossier's job is to ensure the writer is calibrated, not to provide material for paraphrase.
