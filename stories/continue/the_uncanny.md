# The Uncanny

## Research Dossier: Theoretical and Literary References for the Form of "Continue"

---

## Overview

This document compiles theoretical, literary, and formal references for the kind of writing the novella attempts. It is the most explicitly *literary* of the four research dossiers — concerned with form, posture, prose models, and the tradition of describing the strange-inside-the-domestic without naming the strange.

The novella does not borrow content from any of the works named here. It borrows posture from a few of them, partially. The dossier's purpose is to give the writer a calibrated relationship with the tradition the form sits inside.

---

## Freud's Unheimlich

The starting point for almost any contemporary discussion of "the uncanny" is Freud's 1919 essay "Das Unheimliche" — usually translated as "The Uncanny", though more literally "the un-home-like". Freud's essay is a notoriously meandering and unfinished piece of thinking, and its central observation is more interesting than its theory.

The observation: *unheimlich* (un-home-like, unfamiliar, eerie) is a word whose meaning shades into its opposite, *heimlich* (home-like, familiar, hidden). What is most familiar — the home, the body, the mother — becomes the most uncanny when it is encountered in a slightly wrong configuration. The uncanny is not the foreign or the monstrous; it is the domestic in a state of small displacement.

Freud's main examples include:
- Hoffmann's "The Sandman" (the doll Olympia, the eyes).
- The double, the doppelgänger.
- The repetition compulsion (a number that recurs, a street one keeps returning to).
- The animation of the inanimate.
- Premature burial.
- The fear of the female genitals as the place from which one came.

Freud's psychoanalytic explanation — that the uncanny is the return of the repressed — is less interesting and less universally accepted than the observation. The observation, stripped of the explanation, is enormously useful for the kind of writing the novella attempts: *the wrongness inside the familiar is more disturbing than any monster from outside*.

**For the novella:** The novella is, in the most literal sense, an unheimlich novel. It is set in a home (or two homes, if we count the protagonist's). Its disturbances are of the home: a cup in the wrong cupboard, a wardrobe full again, a phone that rings. The novella does not need to introduce anything supernatural. The home is enough.

The novella does not, however, narrate Freud. It does not have a moment in which the protagonist is troubled by the doubleness of the apartment. It does not have a doppelgänger. It uses the *structural* observation about the unheimlich without taking on the psychoanalytic frame.

## Heidegger's Unheimlichkeit

Heidegger uses *Unheimlichkeit* in a different but related sense in *Being and Time*. For Heidegger, *Unheimlichkeit* is the condition of *not-being-at-home* that anxiety reveals. Anxiety, in Heidegger's account, strips away Dasein's everyday absorption in the world and reveals that Dasein is not, in some deep sense, at home anywhere — that being-in-the-world is fundamentally a being-not-at-home.

Heidegger's *Unheimlichkeit* is closer to existential displacement than to Freudian repression. It is the condition of having no home in the first place, rather than the state of finding one's home suddenly strange.

**For the novella:** the protagonist, by the late chapters, is a man who is not at home anywhere. The mother's apartment is unhandleable. His own apartment is no longer a refuge: he packs his own plates in Chapter 7. By Chapter 13, he is sitting at his own kitchen table looking at a list with words he does not recognise. Heidegger's frame is, in some ways, a more accurate description of the protagonist's late-novella condition than Freud's. The writer can hold both frames without choosing between them.

## Tzvetan Todorov: The Fantastic

In *The Fantastic: A Structural Approach to a Literary Genre* (1970), Todorov proposes that the fantastic is the literature of *hesitation*: the reader (and often a character) hesitates between a natural and a supernatural explanation of an event. As soon as the hesitation is resolved — the event is explained either as supernatural (Todorov's "marvellous") or as a hallucination, a dream, a delusion (his "uncanny" in a more limited sense) — the fantastic ends.

The pure fantastic is a knife-edge: the reader cannot decide. The novella sustains the hesitation indefinitely.

**For the novella:** *Continue* lives almost entirely in Todorov's "fantastic". The reader is never told whether the glitches are supernatural, psychological, or formal. The novella refuses to resolve the hesitation. This is one of the form's primary technical features. The reader is held in the hesitation for the entire book and is not released into either explanation at the end.

Todorov's framework is not the novella's only frame, but it is one of the cleanest descriptions of the *technical* effect the novella is producing. The form is fantastic-mode in Todorov's sense, sustained.

## The Domestic Uncanny in 20th- and 21st-Century Fiction

A partial tradition. None of these is the novella's source. Several of them are its kin.

### Shirley Jackson, *We Have Always Lived in the Castle* (1962) and "The Lottery"

Jackson's mode is the domestic-eerie: a house, a kitchen, a sister, a small ritual, all rendered in calm prose. The horror is not invasion from outside; it is what is already inside the rooms. Jackson's tone is light to the point of seeming pleasant; the content is what gives weight.

**Distance from the novella:** Jackson's third-person camera moves more than the novella's. Her prose is more inflected with character voice. Her endings, while ambiguous, often resolve in some way the novella's would not.

**Useful for:** The lesson that horror in the domestic does not require any voice-raising. Jackson never raises her voice.

### Mariana Enríquez, *Things We Lost in the Fire* (2016) and *The Dangers of Smoking in Bed*

Enríquez writes a contemporary South American gothic in which the disturbances are inside the houses, the streets, the apartments of recognisable cities. The disturbances may or may not be ghosts.

**Distance from the novella:** Enríquez allows herself more visible horror. The novella does not.

**Useful for:** A model of contemporary urban uncanny that does not require nineteenth-century furnishings.

### Han Kang, *The White Book* (2016) and *Human Acts*

Han's prose is the closest direct ancestor of the kind of restraint the novella attempts. *The White Book* is a series of short, oblique paragraphs about whiteness and the death of a sister the narrator never met. Each entry is small. Each entry refuses to interpret. The accumulation is what carries the weight.

**Distance from the novella:** Han is more lyrical. Her prose is more openly poetic. The novella's prose is flatter.

**Useful for:** The model of accumulation. Each chapter of *Continue* should feel like one of Han's paragraphs in the way it refuses to interpret what it is showing.

### Kazuo Ishiguro, *The Remains of the Day* (1989)

Ishiguro's Stevens is the master of refused interiority. The first-person narration is structurally incapable of saying what it is most clearly about (Stevens's love for Miss Kenton, his complicity in his employer's politics). The novel works *because* of this incapacity, not despite it.

**Distance from the novella:** Ishiguro is in the first person. The novella is in the third person close. Ishiguro's discipline is character-shaped (Stevens cannot say it because Stevens is who he is). The novella's discipline is form-shaped (the novella cannot say it because the form refuses to).

**Useful for:** The proof that a long literary work can be entirely organised around the refusal of a saying, and that the refusal can produce more emotional power than the saying would.

### Marilynne Robinson, *Housekeeping* (1980)

Robinson's *Housekeeping* contains the domestic surface raised to the level of the metaphysical without ever raising its voice. The water under the floorboards. The room in the dark. The sister who cannot be kept in the house.

**Distance from the novella:** Robinson is more lyrical. Her prose is decoratively beautiful in a way the novella's must not be. Her novel has a first-person narrator who *does* think and feel.

**Useful for:** The lesson that the domestic surface can be the entire novel.

### Anne Carson, *Nox* (2010)

*Nox* is a single accordion-fold book in a box, made of fragments — a translation of Catullus 101, photographs, scraps, definitions, partial sentences — about the death of Carson's brother. The form is the carrying. There is no through-line. There is no resolution.

**Distance from the novella:** *Nox* is in many ways the formal opposite of *Continue*: it is openly fragmented, openly visual, openly an artefact rather than a novel. *Continue* hides its fragmentation inside the apparent continuity of a narrative.

**Useful for:** The lesson that grief can be a form rather than a content. *Nox* is one of the few works in which the formal and the affective are completely fused. The novella aspires to a similar fusion in a different mode.

### Roland Barthes, *Mourning Diary* (published posthumously, 2009; written 1977-79)

The fragments Barthes wrote on small slips of paper after the death of the mother. Each is short. Each refuses to do anything with the material it presents. Each accumulates with the others into a record of what bereavement actually feels like, day by day.

**Distance from the novella:** Barthes is in the first person. He is writing for himself, not for a reader. His fragments do not have plot.

**Useful for:** The closest stylistic neighbour to the novella in the literature of bereaved cognition. Barthes's *posture* — the refusal of the literary, the precision of the small observation, the patience with the unsayable — is one of the postures the novella reaches for.

### Annie Ernaux, *A Man's Place* (1983), *I Remain in Darkness* (1997), *A Woman's Story* (1988)

Ernaux's books on her parents are the strongest model in contemporary French literature for the prose the novella attempts. *A Man's Place* is the chronicle of her father's life and death; *A Woman's Story* is the chronicle of her mother's. *I Remain in Darkness* is the journal of her mother's descent into Alzheimer's, written without literary distance.

Ernaux's discipline: write the facts, the sentences, the small specific details, without metaphor, without sentimentality, without the rhetorical apparatus of "literature". The result is one of the great bodies of work in twentieth-century European prose.

**Distance from the novella:** Ernaux is in the first person and is openly autobiographical. *Continue* is in the third person close and is fictional.

**Useful for:** Ernaux's flat sentences are the closest model for the novella's flat sentences. Her refusal to ornament is the same refusal. Her trust that the literal is enough is the same trust.

### László Krasznahorkai (especially the more domestic passages of *The Melancholy of Resistance* and *Seiobo There Below*)

Krasznahorkai is famous for paragraph-long sentences and for a prose mode that does not let the reader stop. His attention is patient to the point of being almost unbearable.

**Distance from the novella:** Krasznahorkai's sentences are far longer than the novella's. His prose is more openly metaphysical. His patience runs in a different direction.

**Useful for:** The model of patience. The novella shares the *patience* with Krasznahorkai even though it does not share the sentence length. The reader of *Continue* has to be willing to stay in the room with the protagonist for a long time. Krasznahorkai is the writer who has most thoroughly proven that this is something a reader can be asked to do.

### Thomas Bernhard (especially *Concrete*, *The Loser*, *Wittgenstein's Nephew*)

Bernhard's narrators are obsessive, repetitive, and incapable of letting anything go. The prose is in long unbroken paragraphs. The voice is single and continuous and never silent.

**Distance from the novella:** Bernhard's voice is intensely interior. The novella's is intensely exterior. Bernhard's narrators talk, the novella's protagonist barely does.

**Useful for:** The example that prose can sustain itself across a whole book on the strength of a single posture, even an extreme one.

### W.G. Sebald (*The Rings of Saturn*, *Austerlitz*, *Vertigo*)

Sebald's prose is calm in the face of devastating material. His narrators walk, observe, photograph, accumulate, refuse to dramatise. His sentences are long but never showy. He is the German-language writer most clearly working in the same tradition the novella is working in.

**Distance from the novella:** Sebald is more roaming, more historical, more discursive. His narrators travel; the novella's protagonist does not.

**Useful for:** The model of the calm camera in front of unbearable material. Sebald is one of the writers who taught the contemporary literary world how to do this. The novella inherits the technique.

### Lydia Davis (*Varieties of Disturbance*, *Can't and Won't*, *The End of the Story*)

Davis writes very short stories, often one paragraph, that observe a small phenomenon with surgical precision. She is the writer who has most thoroughly proven that the literal can be the entire mode.

**Distance from the novella:** Davis is shorter and more aphoristic. The novella is longer and more sustained.

**Useful for:** The lesson that observational discipline can be the entire literary value. The novella's individual sentences should be Davis-like in their refusal to ornament.

### Cormac McCarthy in his cleaner mode (especially *The Crossing*'s framing passages)

McCarthy's late style is best known for its biblical cadences and its violence. But there are passages in *The Crossing* and elsewhere where the prose goes clean — short declarative sentences, no subordinate clauses, the camera at the boy's shoulder — and these passages are some of the most patient and powerful in contemporary American prose.

**Distance from the novella:** McCarthy's content is action, landscape, animals, blood. The novella's is none of these.

**Useful for:** The proof that a third-person camera at a man's shoulder, in clean declarative sentences, can carry an entire long work.

### Marguerite Duras (*The Lover*, *The Malady of Death*)

Duras's prose is short, fragmented, and deeply unwilling to explain itself. Her sentences are calibrated to the centimetre. Her form is built around what is not said.

**Distance from the novella:** Duras is in the first person and is openly autobiographical. Her fragmentation is more visible than the novella's.

**Useful for:** The lesson that a short, refused-explanation work can carry a whole life.

### Jenny Offill (*Dept. of Speculation*, *Weather*)

Offill writes in short numbered paragraphs that look like aphorisms but are actually a continuous internal monologue with the connective tissue removed. The form refuses to deliver the things conventional prose would deliver.

**Distance from the novella:** Offill is openly fragmented and openly first-person. The novella is hiding its fragmentation inside continuous prose.

**Useful for:** The proof that white space carries weight, and that a contemporary literary novel can be made of pieces small enough to read in a breath each.

### Mark Z. Danielewski (*House of Leaves*, 2000)

A counter-example. *House of Leaves* is a novel about a house that is bigger on the inside than on the outside, and about the readers who try to make sense of it. It uses extreme typographic invention to enact its dissolution.

**Distance from the novella:** Maximally far. Danielewski's solution to the problem the novella addresses (a house that does not behave like a house) is to make the prose visibly insane. The novella's solution is the opposite: the prose stays calm and the house misbehaves quietly.

**Useful for:** As a counter-example. *Continue* is the version of *House of Leaves* in which the prose refuses to participate. Knowing this helps the writer avoid the mistakes that come from reaching for typographic invention when the form requires restraint.

### Other distant kin (briefer)

- **Don DeLillo,** *The Body Artist.* Short, slow, oblique, about grief and a strange presence in a house.
- **Jesse Ball,** *A Cure for Suicide.* Short, controlled, oblique.
- **Jon McGregor,** *Reservoir 13.* Multi-year quiet observation of a village. Patience.
- **Penelope Fitzgerald,** *The Blue Flower.* Restraint, the small detail, the unspoken.
- **Yoko Ogawa,** *The Memory Police.* Things disappear from the world. The prose stays calm.
- **Magda Szabó,** *The Door.* A relationship with an older woman, observed in calm prose, with a refusal to look directly at what is most central.
- **Claire Keegan,** *Small Things Like These* and *Foster.* Irish minimalism; the calm camera; the refusal to dramatise.
- **Cesare Pavese,** *The Moon and the Bonfires.* Returning to a place that is no longer the place.
- **Rachel Cusk,** *Outline* trilogy. The protagonist who barely speaks; the world that comes to her in other people's voices.
- **Édouard Levé,** *Suicide* and *Autoportrait.* Lists, refusals, the anti-narrative.

## Theoretical Frames Beyond Freud

### Mark Fisher, *The Weird and the Eerie* (2016)

Fisher distinguishes the *weird* (something that is *here* that should not be) from the *eerie* (something that is *not here* that should be, or whose presence cannot be explained). His distinction is more useful than Freud's *unheimlich* for some contemporary purposes.

The eerie, in Fisher's sense: a deserted landscape, an empty house, a phone that does not ring, a phone that *does* ring when it should not. The eerie is about absence and the strangeness of presence in places where presence has no agent.

**For the novella:** *Continue* is more eerie than weird, in Fisher's sense. The wall phone that rings when no one can be calling. The freezer with the date after she died. The crayon drawings on the wall of a room he does not remember. These are all eerie phenomena: a presence whose source cannot be identified, an absence that is not quite absent.

Fisher's frame is more useful for the novella than Freud's because Fisher is interested in the specifically *spatial* and *agentive* dimensions of the strange — who is doing this, and where — and the novella's whole architecture is about who-is-doing-this-and-where.

### Avery Gordon, *Ghostly Matters: Haunting and the Sociological Imagination* (1997)

Gordon's argument is that haunting is a social phenomenon, not a supernatural one. A haunting is what happens when a society or a person has not addressed something it should have addressed. The ghost is the unaddressed.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's apartment is haunted in Gordon's sense. The haunting is not by mother. It is by everything that was not addressed in the relationship. The wall phone is the haunting; the inscription is the haunting; the photograph that runs through its variations is the haunting. None of this requires the supernatural.

### Jacques Derrida, *Specters of Marx* (1993)

Derrida's *hauntology* — a pun on ontology — is the philosophy of the trace, the not-yet, the no-longer. Hauntology has been picked up in cultural criticism as a frame for contemporary art that is haunted by lost futures and lost pasts.

**For the novella:** Useful as a distant frame. The novella is in some sense a hauntological novel: the protagonist is in an apartment haunted by a relationship that did not happen, a conversation that was not had, a self he might have been. But the novella does not need Derrida's vocabulary. The dossier mentions it for completeness.

### Sianne Ngai, *Ugly Feelings* (2005)

Ngai catalogues the affects that contemporary literary theory has tended to ignore: irritation, envy, anxiety, paranoia, *stuplimity* (a coinage combining stupor and sublimity). Her work makes room for the kinds of affect that are not heroic and not tragic and not catharsis-shaped.

**For the novella:** the protagonist's affects are largely Ngai-shaped. He is not tragically grieving and he is not heroically processing. He is doing the work, in a state that does not have a clean name in the older literary vocabulary. Ngai gives the writer permission to not name it.

### Lauren Berlant, *Cruel Optimism* (2011) and *The Hundreds* (with Kathleen Stewart, 2019)

Berlant's work on attachment and affect is useful for understanding the kind of love-and-not-love that the novella is about. Her later collaborative work *The Hundreds* (paragraphs of one hundred words each) is also a model of the constrained-form literary essay.

## The Specific Tradition the Novella Sits In

If the novella has a tradition, it is the tradition of the *quietly devastating short literary work about the death of a parent or the failure of a familial relationship, written in the third person close, in clean prose, refusing both interiority and supernatural explanation*. This tradition is small but real. Its members include:

- Some chapters of Annie Ernaux.
- Some chapters of Marilynne Robinson.
- Han Kang's *The White Book* (in a tangential sense).
- Jenny Offill's *Dept. of Speculation* (in another tangential sense).
- Han Kang's *Human Acts* (the short chapters, not the longer ones).
- Claire Keegan's *Small Things Like These*.
- Marguerite Duras's *The Lover*.
- Édouard Levé's *Suicide*.
- The cleaner short stories of Mavis Gallant and Alice Munro (not their longer ones).
- The cleaner short stories of William Trevor.
- Late Tove Jansson (*The Summer Book*, *The True Deceiver*).
- Late Penelope Fitzgerald (*The Blue Flower*, *The Bookshop*).
- The early chapters of Karl Ove Knausgaard's *A Death in the Family*.

The novella is closest in posture to Ernaux, Han, Keegan, and Knausgaard — but it is more formally restricted than any of them. It refuses the first person (Ernaux, Han, Knausgaard). It refuses the village (Keegan). It refuses the autobiographical key (all four). It is, in some sense, a *more austere* version of what these writers have already done.

## Prose Models: A Calibration Exercise

Before drafting any chapter, the writer should read aloud one of the following passages and try to match its register. The match is not for content; it is for cadence and posture.

- The opening pages of Ishiguro's *The Remains of the Day*: Stevens at his desk, planning the trip. The exact tone of competence held in front of a feeling Stevens will not name.
- The "Year One" chapter of Joan Didion's *The Year of Magical Thinking*: the morning after, what Didion does, what Didion thinks she is doing, what Didion is actually doing.
- The early pages of Ernaux's *I Remain in Darkness*: the visits to her mother in the care home, the entries that refuse interpretation.
- A few pages of Han Kang's *The White Book*, almost any: the small paragraphs each held to a single observation.
- The opening of Sebald's *The Rings of Saturn*: the walk along the Suffolk coast, the calm camera in front of accumulating historical material.
- A page of Lydia Davis's stories: any of them, for the precision.
- A clearance scene from Knausgaard's *A Death in the Family*: the cleaning of the grandmother's house, the description of what is in each room, the patience.
- A section of Anne Carson's *Nox*: any of them, for the model of fragmentation that is also a form.
- A page of Roland Barthes's *Mourning Diary*: any of them, for the patient refusal to interpret.

The writer should read at least one of these before starting any difficult chapter. The reading is calibration. It is not material.

## What the Novella Is Not

It is worth being clear about what the novella is *not* doing, in relation to the tradition:

- **Not horror.** The novella is not in the horror genre. Even though it has eerie elements, it does not deliver horror's effects (shock, the jump, the monster, the chase).
- **Not magical realism.** Even though it has unexplained phenomena, it does not have the magical realism mode in which the strange is integrated into the everyday with a kind of warmth or wonder. The novella's strange is colder.
- **Not surrealism.** The novella does not foreground the irrational. The prose stays rational. The content does not.
- **Not science fiction.** The novella does not have a hidden technological or speculative explanation. It does not turn out that the apartment is a simulation. The novella is not playing in that key at all.
- **Not literary fantastic in the Borgesian sense.** Borges's puzzles are intellectual; the novella's are affective.
- **Not autofiction.** The novella is fictional. It is not the writer's life.
- **Not minimalism in the Carver sense.** Carver's minimalism foregrounds working-class American voice and dialogue. The novella is not interested in voice in that sense; its restraint is at the level of interiority and metaphor, not at the level of vocabulary or social class.
- **Not a horror-of-grief novel.** Even though it depicts the somatic and cognitive symptoms of grief, it does not rhetorically frame grief as a horror. It frames it as *what is happening*.

The novella is its own thing. The dossier exists so that the writer can place it accurately in the literary field — close enough to the kin to inherit posture, far enough from the obvious neighbours to avoid being read as one of them.

## A Note on the Word "Glitch"

The dossier and the planning artifacts use the word *glitch* freely. The novella does not use it. The word is borrowed from contemporary digital culture (where a glitch is a small visual or behavioural anomaly in a video game or simulation) and is convenient for the writer because it names the *character* of the novella's anomalies — small, low-key, unexplained, sitting inside an otherwise functional surface.

The reader of the novella may, or may not, reach for the word *glitch* on their own. Some will. Some will read the novella as a description of grief-induced derealisation and will not reach for the word. Some will reach for *uncanny* instead. Some will not reach for any word.

The word being available in the cultural air, but not on the page, is exactly the position the novella wants the word to be in. If the novella named *glitch*, the reader who knew the word would have the reading handed to them and would be released from the work of inference. The reading is more powerful because it is the reader's.

## What the Novella Will Borrow and What It Will Not

### What it will borrow

- The patience of Sebald and Krasznahorkai.
- The flat-sentence discipline of Ernaux, Davis, and McCarthy in his cleaner mode.
- The refusal of interiority of Ishiguro.
- The posture of Barthes in *Mourning Diary*.
- The accumulation-by-small-paragraphs of Han.
- The trust that the literal is enough, from all of the above.
- The Todorovian sustained hesitation.

### What it will not borrow

- The first person (Ernaux, Barthes, Han, Knausgaard, Didion, Carson).
- The lyricism (Robinson, Carson, Han).
- The fragment as visible form (Carson, Offill, Davis).
- The historical-discursive material (Sebald, Krasznahorkai).
- The sentence length of Krasznahorkai or Bernhard.
- The autobiographical key.
- The metaphor.
- The supernatural mode of Jackson, Enríquez, Danielewski.
- The typographic invention of Danielewski, Carson.

The novella's particular thing is to be in the third person close, present tense, fictional, calmly written, sustained for ~30,000 words, around a man clearing an apartment, refusing both interiority and supernatural explanation, holding the reader inside Todorovian hesitation for the entire book, and ending without resolution.

This particular thing is, as far as the writer knows, not exactly what any of the named writers have done. It is what the novella is for.
