# Episode 1: The Wrong Kind of Man

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` is spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` is the
channel — layered, roomless, cold; it is one voice short. `(HIM)` is Daniel's interior —
warm, roomed, his feelings arriving a beat after they are planted. `NARRATOR` is Wren,
years later, reconstructing; she cannot hear the CHORD. The listener hears everything.
Daniel hears only the room and himself. Wren hears only the room. See the README.*

---

**Silence. Not the absence of sound — the kind of silence that has a shape, that fits a
room the way water fits a glass. A house. Old timber settling. A clock somewhere,
unhurried. Underneath it, very faint, almost not there: a low, layered hum — voices, many,
breathing as one, with no words in them. And in that hum, a hole. A place where a voice
should sit and does not. Hold the hole. Let the ear lean toward it and find nothing.**

NARRATOR *(close, unhurried; a small dry warmth):* The week we buried my grandmother, the
house went quiet in a way I didn't have a word for. *(beat)* You have to understand — it
was never a loud house. My family didn't argue. They didn't need to. They'd settle a thing
between them before anyone got as far as a sentence, the way you don't argue with your own
hand about which way to turn a key.

**The hum, under. The hole in it.**

NARRATOR: But that week it went *thin.* Like a chord with a note lifted out of it. I kept
thinking the radiators had been turned down. I kept checking. They hadn't. *(beat)* I was
the only one in the house who couldn't feel what was actually missing. I just felt cold,
and assumed, the way I always assumed, that the cold was something the matter with me.

**A door. Footsteps — not hers; older, certain. A long pause in a doorway. Then the
footsteps move off. A breath let out that has too many breaths in it, layered, just under
hearing — and then only one woman breathing, in a kitchen, somewhere.**

NARRATOR: My mother stood in doorways that week. She'd never done that in her life. She'd
go to a room and then stop at the edge of it like she'd forgotten what rooms were *for.*
*(softer)* I brought her tea. I didn't know what else a person does. She looked at me —

**A pause. The clock.**

NARRATOR: — she looked at me like she was counting, and had come up one short, and was
trying to work out whether I was the one.

**The hum swells, very slightly. Hungry is the wrong word. But the room wants something.
Hold — then:**

NARRATOR: By the Thursday she sent me out. She didn't say *go and find someone.* She never
said it like that. She said, *you're no use to me moping, love, go and be among people.*
*(a small breath)* And I went, grateful, the way a dog is grateful to be given a job. I
didn't know I'd been given one. *(beat)* I never did, until it was far too late to give it
back.

---

**A cut. New acoustic — a bus, maybe, or the inside of a coat in the rain. Then a door, a
warm wash of café noise, a coffee machine. This memory comes fast and flat, almost bored.**

NARRATOR: Here. I'll show you the shape of it first, with one that doesn't matter, so you
don't have to take my word later. *(beat)* I don't remember his name. I want that on the
record — that I can stand here and tell you, in detail, the way a man I sat with for two
hours folded and unfolded a sugar packet, and I cannot give you his name, because I never
once needed it.

**Café. A man's voice, mid-thirties, lonely-friendly, the kind that's grateful to be
talked to.**

MAN *(SURFACE):* — no, you go ahead, I'm not — honestly I'm just glad of the company, it's
been a — it's been a week.

NARRATOR *(over it, flat):* It is always a week. Everyone you will ever lure has just had a
week. The trick — and it isn't a trick, it's just the only thing I've ever been good at —
is that you *listen.* Not the way people pretend to. All the way down. Until the man feels,
maybe for the first time since he was small, that there is a person across the table who
has gone quiet inside *for him.*

MAN *(SURFACE, lower, opening):* Can I tell you something I haven't — I don't know why I'm —

NARRATOR: And then you bring him home. And then —

**The café noise drops away abruptly, replaced by that cold, layered, roomless CHORD —
and the hole in it.**

CHORD *(cold, layered, close, no room around it; one voice short):* Adequate. Not for the
budding. For the table. Bring him to the daughter. The daughter will take it from here.

NARRATOR *(plain):* — and then it was never mine again. He turned, the way they all turned,
toward my sister, like a plant turning to a window. And I sat in my own family like a coat
somebody had taken off. *(beat)* That one went — wherever they go. I didn't ask. For thirty
years the not-asking felt like manners.

**The café fades entirely. Rain rises in its place — steady, against glass.**

NARRATOR: That's the shape. Now the one that mattered. The last man I ever brought home to
them. *(beat)* And remember, when I get there — I'm asking you a favour — remember that I
walked into it grateful. That's the part I can't make soft for you. Nobody made me. I went
because being sent was the closest thing to being wanted I had.

---

**RAIN, steady, against glass. A near-empty café-bar, late. A coffee machine sighs and
goes quiet. One chair scrapes. Room tone settles. Hold it — let the place be real before
anyone is.**

NARRATOR *(close):* I have always met the wrong kind of man. My mother said it like a
weather report — *there she goes again* — and I believed her, because she was never wrong
about anything. *(a small breath)* This is the last one. I didn't know that yet. What I
remember isn't his face. It's that he folded his receipt into a tiny square while he
talked, smaller and smaller, like he was trying to make it disappear before he had to ask
for anything. *(beat)* I wasn't supposed to notice things like that. Noticing things like
that was exactly what was wrong with me.

**A glass set down, gentle. The man's voice — DANIEL — close, a little tired, warm.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* You don't have to keep me company, you know. I'm a big believer in
letting people off the hook.

NARRATOR *(over it):* I brought him in with nothing but talk. That part was always mine. I
sat down across from a stranger in a dead bar in the rain and inside twenty minutes he was
telling me the true things. *(beat)* The rest — the part that came after the talk — was
never mine to do.

**The acoustic shifts subtly — we are at a table now. Two more presences arrive, settle. A
warmth comes into the air that is almost too much, too soon. A chair. A coat.**

LISS *(SURFACE — warm, easy, bright):* So you're the one who's been keeping her all to
yourself.

DANIEL *(SURFACE — a laugh, a little nervous):* I — sorry, I didn't realize I was keeping
anyone.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless — the gap where the third voice should be):* He apologizes
for taking up space. Good. Easily moved. Bring the daughter into his eyeline. Let him find
her.

DANIEL *(HIM — interior, warm, slightly wondering):* ...God. The sister. How did I not — she
was right there when we came in, how did I not *see* her?

NARRATOR *(over it, dry):* I used to think it was me. That I'd warm them up, and then, every
time, they'd turn — like that — toward whoever I'd brought them to. I told myself I just
wasn't the kind men stay pointed at. *(beat)* I had the cause backwards. But that took me
thirty years and one funeral to work out.

LISS *(SURFACE — softer now, leaning in):* What brings you here, then? Really.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* Honestly? Killing time. A friend bailed. *(self-deprecating)* Story of
my — you don't need my whole sad —

VALA *(SURFACE — first time she speaks; mild, grandmotherly, almost nothing there):* We
don't mind a sad story. We've heard worse.

CHORD: The mother is correct. We have heard worse. *(a flat ripple — the nearest this voice
comes to amusement)* He responds to being needed. So. Need him.

LISS *(SURFACE):* Then stay. Sit with us a while. It's filthy out there, and you'd be doing
us a kindness — it's been a hard week.

**RAIN, harder against the glass.**

DANIEL *(HIM — and this is the seam: let the line above arrive in his own warm voice, a
beat too soon to be his):* ...They've had a hard week. I could — yeah. I could be useful.
*(quieter, to himself)* When's the last time anyone actually needed me to just *sit* with
them.

NARRATOR: He said yes to the rain, really. To not being alone in it. *(beat)* I want to be
clear that I knew exactly what was happening. I'd done it a hundred times. I just couldn't,
for the life of me, work out why this time it felt like watching someone walk into traffic.

CHORD: The daughter has him. *(beat)* Step back. *(and now — the one instruction the
listener understands has teeth)* Step back.

**A pause. A chair, very slightly, moves back from the table. LISS and DANIEL's voices
continue underneath now — warm, indistinct, a private weather forming without our narrator
in it.**

NARRATOR *(quieter, the dryness gone):* So I stepped back. The way you're meant to. And I
sat there in my own family like a coat someone had taken off and forgotten, and I watched
him fall for my sister with a look on his face like something good was finally happening to
him. *(beat)* And I was jealous. *(she catches it — almost laughs at herself)* Jealous. Of
course I was. After everything, *that* was the rot they could never quite cut out of me.

---

**The two warm voices, close and happy, under. Rain. Then a small private sound: paper,
folded, refolded. The receipt. DANIEL, mid-laugh at something Liss has said, fingers
working at it without knowing.**

NARRATOR: While my sister did the thing she was made to do, I watched his hands. He'd
finished the receipt — folded down past the point where paper wants to fold, this hard
little square — and now he didn't know what to do with it, so he was turning it over and
over, this man who was, by every account including his own, having the best night of his
life. *(beat)* People who are happy don't do that with their hands. *(beat)* I noticed.
Nobody had asked me to notice. Nobody ever did.

**A burst of LISS's laughter, bright. DANIEL laughing too — but listen: under his laugh,
the HIM voice, low, not unhappy, just true.**

DANIEL *(HIM):* ...this is going to end. Things like this always — *(he stops himself)* No.
Be here. For once just *be* here.

CHORD: He guards himself. Naturally. *(flat)* The daughter will not rush. We have time.

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I didn't hear that, of course. The cold part. I never could. *(beat)*
But I'm going to tell it to you anyway, all the way through, because somebody should have
heard it, and I'm the only one left who'd want to. Even if I'm only guessing. Even if all I
ever had to go on was the look on his face, and what came after, and thirty years to be
sorry in.

**The clock from the opening, faint, returning under. The thin hum, the hole in it.**

NARRATOR: They didn't say it that night, but I know how my family talks when it's decided
something. There's a — settling. The way the house had gone thin all week, that Thursday it
went *still,* the way a held breath is still. *(beat)* I thought it was about my
grandmother. I thought we were grieving. *(beat)* We were. I just didn't understand that in
my family, grief and hunger are the same weather.

CHORD *(under, cold, certain):* This one. Not the table. *(beat)* This one is for the line.

NARRATOR: I'm guessing at that. But I'd bet my life on it. *(a breath)* I nearly did.

---

**Outside now. The café door, a bell. Rain on the street. DANIEL and WREN, walking, the
acoustic open and wet. Liss and Vala gone ahead or behind — for a moment it is just the two
of them, and this is the only stream the listener can fully trust.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE, warmer, looser, the wine in it):* Can I say a weird thing. *(beat)* Your
sister's — your whole family's lovely, they really are, they've been so —

WREN *(SURFACE — and this is the first we hear her *in scene,* younger, lighter, careful):*
But.

DANIEL *(SURFACE):* No, not but. *(beat)* It's just — you're the one who talked to me. Like,
*talked.* And then you sort of — went away. And I keep — *(small laugh at himself)* I don't
know. I keep wanting to check you're still there.

**A beat. Rain. And here — crucially — no CHORD line comes before this. Nothing is planted.
It is only him.**

DANIEL *(HIM — quiet, unforced, and the listener must be able to tell this one is real):*
She's got the saddest, kindest face. Why would they keep someone like that at the edge of
everything.

WREN *(SURFACE — and she misreads it, the way she always will):* I'm always still here.
*(beat, dry, deflecting)* I'm the furniture. You'll learn.

NARRATOR *(over, present-day, and it costs her):* He said a true thing to me, in the rain,
and I made a joke, because a true thing said to me had to be a mistake. Someone had got the
wrong girl. *(beat)* I have gone back to that sentence — *I keep wanting to check you're
still there* — more nights than I'll admit. I've turned it over till it went soft, like his
receipt. *(very quiet)* I'll never know if it was real. I couldn't hear the part of him that
would've told me. *(beat)* I only ever got the room.

---

**RAIN. Then the warm voices fold back in — Liss returning, laughing, slipping her arm
through Daniel's. The private weather closing over again. WREN, half a step back, where she
belongs.**

LISS *(SURFACE, bright, drawing him on):* Come *on,* you two, you'll catch your deaths —
Daniel, you're staying for tea, I won't hear otherwise —

DANIEL *(SURFACE, swept, happy):* I — okay, yeah, okay —

CHORD: Good. He comes willingly. *(beat)* The daughter is ready. The mother is ready. *(and
then, into the gap where the third voice is not)* ...We are short. *(flat, not grief — a
hand reaching for a tool that is not on the bench)* We are short, and we will not stay short.

**The hole in the chord, loud now in its silence. Then the rain swallows it.**

NARRATOR: That's where I'll stop tonight. Him saying yes a second time — yes to the tea,
yes to the warmth, yes to all of it — and me a step behind, jealous, useless, *noticing.*
*(beat)* I'm going to tell you what we were. I'm going to tell you what I am, and what he
was for, and what I did. *(beat)* But I want you to remember the receipt. The little folded
square he made of it.

**The two warm voices, going away up the wet street. The thin hum under. The clock.**

NARRATOR: Because by the end of it, I was the only person left alive who'd ever noticed it
at all.

**SNAP to silence. Rain only. Out.**
# Episode 2: Reps

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` is spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` is the
channel — layered, roomless, cold, one voice short. `(HIM)` is Daniel's interior; his
feeling for Wren is the one thread no CHORD line ever precedes. `NARRATOR` is Wren, years
later, reconstructing; she cannot hear the CHORD. See the README.*

---

**A house at evening. The same old timber, the same unhurried clock. A kettle coming up to
the boil. Three women moving through rooms — you can hear it in the floor, the way they
never collide, never call out, never need to ask where another one is. Under it: the thin
layered hum, the hole in it. A door; a coat hung; the kettle clicks off.**

NARRATOR *(close):* He came on the Sunday. I want to tell you my family lived somewhere
grand, somewhere with a name, but it was just a house — too big for us, the way every house
we ever had was a little too big and a little too far from the next one. *(beat)* We moved
a lot. More than anyone I've ever met since. I used to think we were unlucky with
landlords. *(a small dry beat)* There was a fireplace in that house and we never once lit
it. My mother kept the matches in a drawer in the kitchen, at the back, behind things, the
way other people keep a thing they're frightened of where they won't come across it by
accident.

**A knock. A real knock, on real wood. Then warmth pouring out — LISS's voice, bright at
the door.**

LISS *(SURFACE — delighted):* You came! You actually came, Mum said you'd lose your nerve —

DANIEL *(SURFACE — laughing, off the back foot, charmed):* I — no, I'm — these are for you.
Both of you. All — however many of you there —

VALA *(SURFACE — from deeper in the house, mild):* Three. There are three of us. *(the
smallest pause — a thing she has had to start saying differently and is getting used to)*
There are three of us, dear, come in out of the cold.

CHORD *(cold, layered, roomless; and in the gap where the third voice should be, nothing —
the count is off and the channel notes it without feeling it):* Three. *(flat)* The number
is wrong. Proceed as if it is not. He must find a whole house. A house missing a voice asks
to be looked at. We do not get looked at.

NARRATOR *(over it):* I carried the flowers to the kitchen. That was a job I could do.

---

**The table. Warm clatter — cups, a tin opened, biscuits. LISS and DANIEL close; their
voices easy, the rhythm of two people getting on. WREN further off — the scrape of her
chair is a half-second behind everyone else's, always.**

LISS *(SURFACE — warm, leaning in, that bright attention):* — no, but tell me the actual
thing, not the version you tell at parties. The friend who bailed. You do that, don't you,
you make everything into the funny short version so nobody has to —

DANIEL *(SURFACE — caught out, pleased to be caught):* God. Yeah. *(beat)* Okay. The actual
thing.

CHORD: She has him opening. Warm the words; keep nothing under them. *(precise, like a hand
on a dial)* Attend to him completely. Mean none of it. This is the skill. Run it warm. Do
not let it catch.

NARRATOR *(present-day, quiet):* My sister had a gift I used to envy so badly it kept me up
at night. She could give you her *whole* face. Every bit of her, turned on you like a lamp.
*(beat)* It took me half my life to work out what was wrong with the way she did it, and
when I finally did, I couldn't have told you in words. It was just — *(searching)* — you
know how a hotel room is warm? The heating's on, it's a perfectly warm room, and you still
wouldn't call it cosy, because nobody lives there. *(beat)* She was a warm room nobody lived
in. And men walked into her like coming in from the rain.

LISS *(SURFACE — soft, perfectly timed):* That's so sad. *(beat)* No — I mean it. That's
really sad, Daniel.

DANIEL *(HIM — and the bloom comes a beat late, and he owns it completely):* ...she *gets*
it. She actually — nobody's ever just said *that's sad,* nobody just lets it be sad, they
all try to fix it or top it. *(wondering)* I could fall for someone who just lets a thing be
sad.

NARRATOR: He didn't know he'd been handed the feeling. Neither did I, then. I just thought:
lucky her. Lucky him. *(beat)* I brought the tea over and nobody looked up, and I went back
for the milk nobody had asked for, so I'd have a reason to stand.

---

**A small thing now — and the listener must catch that no CHORD line precedes it. DANIEL,
mid-sentence to Liss, glances off — toward where Wren is standing with the milk.**

DANIEL *(SURFACE — to the room, to Wren really):* Sorry — Wren, is it? You've barely — come
and sit, you're hovering like you're on shift.

CHORD *(cold; a small recalculation):* He turns to the sister. Unhelpful. *(beat)* Draw him
back. Have the daughter laugh.

LISS *(SURFACE — laughing, reclaiming):* Oh, ignore her, she's happiest hovering, aren't
you — Daniel, tell me about the brother, you said there was a brother —

**And here — the resistance, and the patience. DANIEL lets himself be drawn back. But.**

DANIEL *(HIM — under it, true, un-planted):* ...she does look like she's on shift, though.
In her own kitchen. Who taught her to stand like that. *(a beat)* Like she's waiting to be
told she can sit down.

CHORD: He returns. *(flat — no alarm; the chord does not panic; the chord waits)* He
returns slowly. Note it. The sister holds his eye a half-beat past the others. We will not
seat them together again tonight.

NARRATOR *(present-day, and it lands quietly on her):* You hear that? *(beat)* He noticed how
I stood. *(a small broken sound that isn't quite a laugh)* I'd have given a finger, then, to
know a man had spent one second on how I stood in a kitchen. *(beat)* I'd have told you it
couldn't have happened. That nobody clocks the furniture. *(very quiet)* I have spent years
deciding whether I made that up. I've decided both ways. Tonight I've decided it was real.
Ask me tomorrow.

---

**The acoustic opens — a different memory pushing in. Daylight. A street, traffic, a
supermarket's automatic doors. WREN, present in the past, mid-errand. And then a voice she
half-knows — a man's, but wrong. Flattened. The colour turned down out of it, like a
photograph left in a window.**

MAN *(SURFACE — and his voice should be audibly *diminished:* the words intact, the life
under them drained; warm content, no warmth):* — sorry, do I — you look so — have we —

WREN *(SURFACE — careful, kind, not placing him yet):* I think maybe? Sorry. I'm terrible
with —

MAN *(SURFACE, that flat patient drift):* No, it's — you've got one of those faces. *(beat)*
I don't really — I've not been sleeping great. Since. *(beat)* Since I don't know when,
actually. Funny. You lose track.

NARRATOR *(over it, and you can hear her not-letting-herself):* I knew him. I'd brought him
home. Not that spring — the spring before, maybe. He'd been — I remembered him *loud.* Full
of opinions about football and his ex-wife and a band he'd seen in '99. *(beat)* And here he
was, in the cereal aisle, and somebody had turned him *down.* Like a radio in another room.
All the words still there. No song in them.

MAN *(SURFACE):* Anyway. *(beat)* You take care. *(beat)* Whoever you are.

**His footsteps go. The automatic doors. WREN, not moving.**

NARRATOR: I stood there a long time. *(beat)* And I want you to watch what I did next,
because it's the whole of me, it's the thing I did my entire life until it killed the people
I loved. *(beat)* I thought: *poor man. Life does that to people.* And I picked up my
shopping. *(beat)* There was a question, right there, with its hand up. I could feel it. The
question was: *every man you have ever brought to that house — where are they now? What do
they sound like now?* *(beat)* And something in me reached out, very gently, the way you'd
settle a child, and put the question back to sleep. *(beat)* I called that being good. I'd
been trained to call it being good. The not-asking. I was so, so good at it.

CHORD *(cold, from memory, certain):* The drained do not return to us. They have nothing
left to give and no reason to come. *(flat)* This is tidy. We prefer tidy. *(beat)* The
sister sees one in the street and grieves him a little and forgets. She is built to forget
the thing she is built to do. It is why she is bearable to keep.

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I didn't hear that. *(beat)* But I lived it. Word for word.

---

**Back to the house. Night now. Daniel gone home — a car, a door, the gravel. The three
women alone. The thin hum, the hole in it. Vala in the kitchen; Liss somewhere above; the
floor telling us where everyone is. And here the CHORD is at its coldest and clearest,
because there is no man to perform for — this is the house talking to itself.**

CHORD *(layered, roomless, decided):* The man is good. *(beat)* Not for the table. *(a
weight comes into it — the nearest the channel gets to gravity)* This one is for the line.

NARRATOR *(over it; she is climbing the stairs in the memory, half-hearing the house go
still beneath her):* I came in from putting the cups away and the house had done the thing
again. Gone still. That held-breath stillness. *(beat)* My mother was standing in the
kitchen doorway — she'd started doing that since the funeral, standing in doorways — and she
looked at me, and for once she smiled, and said —

VALA *(SURFACE — warm, and it is the warmest she has been; the curdled-mother register, real
love bent into ownership):* You did well, love. You brought us a good one. *(beat)* You see?
You're not no use. When you're useful you're so —

**She doesn't finish it. She doesn't have to.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, and this is the wound the whole show turns on):* *You're not no use.*
*(beat)* You'd have to have grown up the way I grew up to know what that sentence does to a
body. I felt it go all the way down. I'd have brought her a hundred men to hear the back
half of it. *(beat)* The thing is — and I need you to hold both of these at once, because I
can't seem to put them down — the thing is, she *meant* it. That's not a monster talking.
That's my mother, who loved me, telling me I'd been good. *(beat)* It was the kindest she'd
been to me in a year. *(beat)* And I floated up those stairs on it like a girl who'd been
told she was pretty.

CHORD: The sister is useful and therefore content. *(flat)* Contentment in the sister is a
maintenance state. Maintain it. *(beat)* For the line, the budding will be soon. The mother
grieves; the grief makes her quick; quick is acceptable. The daughter is ripe. *(beat)* It
is time.

**The hum. The hole in it. The clock.**

NARRATOR: I lay in bed that night happier than I'd been since before the funeral. *(beat)*
A man had noticed how I stood. My mother had said I wasn't no use. *(beat)* And somewhere
under the floor of my own house, in the one room I was never allowed into, in the one
language I was born without — it had been decided what Daniel was for. *(beat)* He was for
the line. *(beat)* I didn't know the words. I want that understood. I didn't have the words.
*(very quiet)* But I'd felt the house go still like that before, every time, and every time,
a few weeks later, there'd be one fewer place set at the table, and a man somewhere in a
supermarket with the colour turned down out of him, saying *whoever you are* to a girl he
used to know loud.

**Silence opening under the words.**

NARRATOR: I knew. *(beat)* The way you know a thing you've decided your whole life not to
know. *(beat)* I'd have told you, that night, that I didn't have the first idea. *(beat)*
And I'd have been lying, the way I lied to myself — gently, lovingly, the way you settle a
child — every single day of my life.

**SNAP to silence. The clock, alone, three beats. Out.**
# Episode 3: The Hard Week

*Three streams. **Bold** is sound. `(SURFACE)` spoken in the room. `(CHORD)` the channel —
layered, roomless, cold, one voice short. `(HIM)` Daniel's interior; his feeling for Wren is
never preceded by a CHORD line. `NARRATOR` is Wren, reconstructing; she cannot hear the
CHORD. See the README.*

---

**The house, a week on. It has filled out a little — there is a man in it now, on and off,
and you can hear how a house changes when someone is being made welcome: more chairs moved,
a second pot of coffee, music low. But under the welcome, the thin layered hum, and the hole
in it, unchanged. The amputation does not heal. It only gets dressed for company.**

NARRATOR *(close):* He started coming most days. *(beat)* That's fast, I know. I thought so
too, at the time — I had a whole speech ready about how he shouldn't, how it was too quick,
how he barely knew us. I never gave it. *(a small dry beat)* You don't slow a thing down when
the slowing-down would mean admitting you can see where it's going.

**Daniel and Liss, somewhere in the house, laughing — that easy, established rhythm now.**

NARRATOR: And he was *happy.* That's the part I can't get round, even now. I'd watch him
come up the path with his shoulders down out of his ears for once, this man who folded his
receipts to nothing, and he'd be — light. Whatever we were doing to him, it didn't feel to
him like being done to. It felt like being chosen. *(beat)* I knew that feeling. I lived for
that feeling. *(beat)* I should have known sooner what it cost. I, of all people, should have
known what it costs to feel chosen by us.

---

**A room with the two of them — Liss and Daniel, close, intimate, the courtship at its
warmest. Wine. Rain returning at the window. And underneath, for the first time, we hear the
suppression as a live thing: the CHORD working Liss like a hand on a dial, and a flicker
where it almost slips.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, and this is the hinge of the whole work):* That's the moment. *(beat)*
If you take one thing out of all of this, take that — because it took me thirty years and it
nearly killed everyone I loved, and I'd like it not to have been for nothing. *(beat)* My sister
had been *included* her whole life. Folded in, dissolved, carried, never for one second alone.
*(beat)* And not once, in all of it, had anyone ever *seen* her. *(beat)* Because you cannot see
what you will not let be separate from you. *(beat)* You can only ever see yourself in it.
*(beat)* The most loving family that ever lived, and it had never once perceived a single one of
its own. *(beat)* And the thing they threw out — the gap, the separateness, the lonely defective
*me* they tried to melt out of me with love — *(beat)* — turned out to be the one and only
instrument in that whole long bloodline that could do it. *(very quiet)* Love isn't the holding.
*(beat)* I thought my whole life that love was the holding, because I'd been starved of it.
*(beat)* It isn't. *(beat)* Love is the *seeing.* And you can only see across a gap. *(beat)* The
ones who never feel alone never see anyone at all. *(beat)* They just feel themselves, in a
larger and larger room, and call the feeling family.

---

**And as Wren reaches Liss — as Liss accepts being a self because there is, at last, a self
across the gap to be seen by — the chord cannot hold her. The sound: the two-voice hum,
fraying, fraying — and then a voice in it simply *stops.* Not screams. Not fades. *Stops,* the
way a held note stops when the breath behind it is gone. And VALA hears it. The third loss. The
last but one.**

VALA *(SURFACE — and this is the death of the chord, heard from inside the only one left in
it):* ...oh. *(beat)* Oh. *(beat)* She's — *(a sound of something vast and ancient going
quiet)* — I can't hear her. *(beat)* I can't — there's no one — *(beat)* — it's just me. *(beat)*
It's just *me* in here. *(beat)* After all this time it's just —

**Silence where the chord was. The hum — gone. For the first time in seven episodes, the
roomless cold layered voice is not there at all. There is only room tone. Only the world. Only
separate people breathing in a lamplit bedroom.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, and she grieves it — truly, this is not triumph):* And here is the part
I can't let you cheer. *(beat)* Because I felt it go. Not the way they felt it — I never could —
but I felt the house change, I felt the thing my whole family *was* simply stop being, like a
sound you didn't know was there until the fridge clicks off in the night and the silence rushes
in. *(beat)* And it was *beautiful,* what died. *(beat)* I have to tell you that, or I've told you
nothing true. *(beat)* They were never alone. They were never afraid. Not one of them, in all
those centuries, ever once lay awake at three in the morning in the cold of a single skull,
staring at the ceiling, knowing they would die in there by themselves. *(beat)* They had
something I have wanted every single day of my life and will want on the day I die. *(beat)* They
were *whole.* *(beat)* And it was a wholeness with no room in it for anyone else to be real — and
that's the horror of it, yes — but God, do not let anyone tell you it wasn't also the most
peace a person could ever — *(beat)* — they had the thing. *(beat)* They had the thing, and they
could not love, and I cannot have the thing, and I can. *(beat)* And if you made me choose, knowing
everything I now know — *(a long beat, and the truth of her whole life)* — I'm not sure, even now,
even after all of it, that I wouldn't still choose theirs.

NARRATOR *(quieter):* And here's the mercy in it — the only mercy, and it took me longest of all to
believe — *(beat)* — it was always going to die. *(beat)* Not because of me. Not really, not in the
end. *(beat)* Every man we ever took left a little of himself in us, the thing we couldn't help
needing him for; a drop a generation, the very thing coming in on the tide we lived on. *(beat)* The
duds had been coming more often. The houses we fled, more often. We were an old, narrow, failing
thing, older than the word for old, and getting thinner every life. *(beat)* I wasn't the flaw that
broke us. *(beat)* I was the tide, finally, all the way in. *(beat)* My one act of mercy didn't doom
a thing that would have lasted forever. *(beat)* It only chose the night. *(very quiet)* I've had to
hold both of those, all this time, and never let go of either: that I did it — and that it was
always, always coming. The grief is mine. The fault was the world's.

---

**VALA — alone now, the last of the chord, a self that has no concept of being a self, holds out
her arms. The Ep3 gesture. *Come here to me.* The thing Wren would cross any room for. And this
is "she would still go back," dramatised — the offer, the pull, the renunciation, and then the
worse thing: the reach that cannot be received.**

VALA *(SURFACE — lost, a child in a grandmother's voice, holding her arms out to the only
daughter she has left):* Wren. *(beat)* Wren, love, come here. *(beat)* Come here to me. *(beat)*
Don't leave me in here on my own, I've never — I don't know how to — *come here.*

**A silence. Hold it. This is the hardest second in the play.**

NARRATOR *(present-day, barely):* I want to lie to you about what I felt. *(beat)* I want to tell
you I felt nothing, or pity, or the clean cold of a person who's finally free. *(beat)* I felt my
whole body turn toward those arms like a plant to a window. *(beat)* Thirty years. *(beat)* Thirty
years I'd wanted exactly that, exactly those words, that voice, *come here to me,* and never quite
got them, and built my whole self around not-quite-getting-them. *(beat)* And now I had them. Now
my mother was holding out her arms and asking me — *needing* me — to come in. *(beat)* And the
thing in me that has wanted that since before I could speak stood up and started across the room.
*(very quiet)* I would have gone. *(beat)* I need you to know that I would have gone. There is no
version of me that doesn't want to have gone.

**A footstep. A pause. The footstep does not continue.**

NARRATOR: But I'd just learned what was on the other side of those arms. *(beat)* I'd just spent
my whole life delivering men into them. *(beat)* I knew, now, that to walk into that warmth was to
stop being able to do the thing I'd done four feet away, three minutes ago, on the floor, for my
sister. *(beat)* You cannot hold someone and see them. Not the way she holds. *(beat)* To go in is
to never love again. *(beat)* And I had just, for the first time in my life, loved. *(a breath)*
So I didn't go in. *(beat)* I did the other thing. The new thing. The thing none of us had ever
done.

**WREN crosses to her mother — but not into the arms. She takes her mother's hands. Holds them.
Across a gap. As one separate person reaching for another.**

WREN *(SURFACE — gentle, wrecked, loving):* I'm not coming in, Mum. *(beat)* I can't. *(beat)*
But I'm not leaving you either. *(beat)* I'm right here. On the outside. Where I've always been.
*(beat)* I can see you. *(beat)* I see you, Mum. *(beat)* You're so frightened. *(beat)* I see how
frightened you are.

**A silence. And then — the cruelest, truest beat in the whole work — nothing. VALA does not
soften, does not receive it, cannot. She has no gap. There is no way in for love that comes from
outside, because there has never been an outside. Her hands stay in Wren's hands, and feel
nothing, because there is no one there to be reached by another. She is, at last, the unreachable
one.**

VALA *(SURFACE — uncomprehending, lost, looking at the daughter who is finally, fully there, and
unable to find her):* ...I can't feel you. *(beat)* You're right there and I can't — there's
nothing coming through. *(beat, plaintive, a being with no organ for what is being offered)* Why
can't I feel you, love. *(beat)* Everyone's gone so quiet.

NARRATOR *(present-day):* I held my mother's hands and loved her with everything the disease in
me could do — *(beat)* — and she couldn't feel a thing. *(beat)* Do you see it? *(beat)* My whole
life, I stood outside the room and couldn't get in. *(beat)* And at the very end, I got as close
as a person can get, I reached all the way across with the one good thing I had — *(beat)* — and
it was *her* turn to be on the wrong side of the glass. *(beat)* The merged can't be loved. That's
the thing nobody tells you about the people who never feel alone. *(beat)* You can love them till
it kills you. *(beat)* It can't reach them. There's no door in. *(very quiet)* I forgave her, in
the end, for never seeing me. *(beat)* How do you stay angry at someone who turns out to have been
standing, her whole life, on the far side of the only thing that was ever wrong with me — *(beat)*
— and was just as alone in it as I was, and never even got to know it.

---

**The room settling into its new, terrible ordinariness. Liss, on the floor, a self. Vala,
standing, the last of a thing that no longer exists. And NARRATOR turning, now, to the line —
the line from the very first minute of the very first episode — and finally understanding all of
it.**

NARRATOR *(close):* My mother said it like a weather report, when I was a girl. *There she goes
again. She always meets the wrong kind of man.* *(beat)* And I believed her, because she was never
wrong about anything. *(beat)* Let me tell you, now, at the end, everything that sentence means.
*(beat)* It meant, first, the thing I thought it meant: that I was the sort of woman things don't
work out for. The one men don't stay pointed at. *(beat)* It meant, second — and I worked this out
too late to stop a single thing — that they *never went wrong at all.* Every man I "lost," every
one who "went nowhere," went exactly where I'd been sent to take him. *(beat)* My whole romantic
life, every failure my mother clucked over — that was my *job,* succeeding. I never once lost a
man. I *delivered* every one. *(beat)* And third. *(a long beat)* The last thing it means. The
thing I'd give my life not to have learned. *(beat)* The one time I met the *right* one — the one
my father's heart, the heart they could never melt out of me, actually reached out and
*loved* — *(beat)* — that was the most catastrophically wrong man I could possibly have met.
*(beat)* Because the man good enough for *me* to love is, by the same measure, exactly
the man good enough for *them* to use. *(beat)* Same rightness. Same aliveness. The very thing that
made him worth loving made him worth taking. *(beat)* My one true romance and my one ruinous
professional failure were the same man, the same night, the same act. *(beat)* I always met the
wrong kind of man. *(beat)* And the one time, the only time, I met the right one — *(beat)* — that
was the wrongest of all. Because loving him is what brought the whole house down.

---

**And now — the frame closes. The narration has been, all along, Wren reconstructing Daniel: the
act of finally perceiving him, too late, the only way she has left to be beside him. She does it
now, completely. His interior — which only ever reached us because she pieced it together — one
last time. And then she lets him go, unhealed.**

NARRATOR *(very quiet):* I never saw Daniel again. *(beat)* I want to be honest about him, because
he's the one I'm not allowed to make better. *(beat)* He's alive. *(beat)* That's the truth, and
it's worse than the other truth. He's out there, with his colour turned down, living a smaller,
greyer life than the one he should have had, and he does not know why, and he never will. *(beat)*
He doesn't remember us. Not really. There's a road he couldn't find, a house he can't picture, a
night he knows was good the way you know a word you've forgotten. *(beat)* He'll fold a receipt
into a tiny square, sometimes, in a café, without knowing he's doing it, and some woman across the
table will not notice, because almost nobody notices, because the only person who ever noticed is
me, and I'm not there. *(beat)* I can't reach him. *(beat)* I can't tell him it was real — the one
thing in him that was never put there, the one feeling no one engineered, the way he kept ending
up in the kitchen with me. *(beat)* I'll never know for certain it was real. I couldn't hear the
part of him that would have told me. *(beat)* So I've done the only thing left. *(beat)* For thirty
years I've sat in the dark and built him back — every true, hopeful, lonely thing he was — and I've
listened, finally, all the way down, to a man I delivered to be hollowed out, the way I once
listened to men in cafés to deliver them. *(beat)* Same skill. *(beat)* Opposite direction. *(beat)*
It does him no good. *(beat)* He'll never know he was heard. *(beat)* But somebody should have heard
him. *(beat)* And I'm the only one left who'd want to. *(beat)* So.

DANIEL *(HIM — reconstructed, the room around it gone, but the warmth in it real, the way she has
finally let herself hear it):* ...she's got the saddest, kindest face. *(beat)* Why would they keep
someone like that at the edge of everything. *(beat)* I keep ending up in the kitchen with her.
*(very quiet, the last of him she will give us)* I think I could have loved her. *(beat)* I think,
if someone had told her she was allowed —

**It cuts off. Not finished. It is never finished. That is the cost, kept honest.**

NARRATOR *(barely):* I'll never know if that's what he'd have said. *(beat)* I made it up. *(beat)*
I made it up the way you make up the voice of someone you should have loved out loud while there was
still time. *(beat)* My father gave me a heart and got thrown away for it, a generation before I was
born, and I never knew his voice either. *(beat)* Daniel was what my father was. *(beat)* They're the
same grief, in the end. The man who gives the one real thing, and is discarded for the giving, and
leaves a daughter behind with the working heart he gave her and no one to spend it on. *(beat)* I grieve them
together. I grieve them, and the next one, whoever he'll be — *(beat)* — because there's a child
coming, now. *(beat)* Did I say? Liss is carrying. *(beat)* The first child in the history of our
line who'll be born to people who'll have to learn it as a *stranger.* Who'll have to cross a gap to
reach it. Who can get it wrong. *(beat)* The most dangerous, most ordinary, most human way there is
to raise a child. *(beat)* The only kind of love that's actually love. *(very quiet)* God help us.
We'll be terrible at it. *(beat)* We'll do it badly, the two of us, with the bent and broken tools
our mother left us, reaching for that child across a gap we never asked to have. *(beat)* And we'll
do it *truly.* *(beat)* And badly and truly, it turns out, is the whole of it. Badly and truly is all
anyone has ever managed.

---

**The very end. Years later, now — the reconstructing voice, in its present. And LISS, separate,
real, reaching back across the gap toward the sister who can, at last, receive it. The two of them.
Bereavement and consolation in one breath.**

**A quiet room. Two women. Ordinary sounds — a kettle, maybe, far off; a child, very faint,
somewhere in the house. No hum. No hole. Just the world.**

LISS *(SURFACE — older now, separate, and loving Wren the way only a self can love another self —
across the distance, on purpose, getting it slightly wrong):* You've gone all quiet again. *(beat)*
You do that. *(beat, gentle)* Come back. *(a small breath — she hears the old word, fixes it)* No —
not *come back.* I always say it wrong. *(beat)* I mean — *(searching, reaching)* — stay where you
are. I can see you from here. *(beat)* I just wanted you to know I can see you.

**A silence. And then WREN — who has spent her whole life filing every warmth pointed at her under
*mistake* or *they want something* — does the thing she has never once been able to do. She lets it
land. She receives it.**

NARRATOR *(close, and for the first time, unguarded, the verdict-scanner finally switched off):*
And I let her. *(beat)* That's it. That's the whole of what I learned, the only thing I have to
give you, after all of it. *(beat)* Someone said *I can see you* — and for the first time in my
life I didn't check it for a trap, didn't translate it into pity or a mistake or a thing somebody
wanted. *(beat)* I just — let it be true. *(beat)* I let myself be seen. *(beat)* It's the hardest
thing I have ever done. *(beat)* It's harder than the loving. *(beat)* Anyone can do the loving
once they know they're allowed. *(beat)* It's the *being seen* that you have to learn last, if you
were the one who grew up certain there was something in you that, if anyone ever really looked,
they'd have to turn away from. *(a long beat)*

NARRATOR *(softer):* So here's what I know, and then I'll let you go. *(beat)* If you were ever the
one who couldn't merge — who never quite belonged, who felt the gap where everyone else seemed to
feel the warmth, who was told, in a hundred small ways, that the ache in you, the too-much, the
needing-people, the noticing — was what was wrong with you — *(beat)* — listen to me. *(beat)* That
ache was never the defect. *(beat)* It was the organ. *(beat)* It was the gap that lets the light
in and the love out. *(beat)* The ones who never felt it — the ones who never felt alone — they
weren't whole. *(beat)* They just never had to learn the one thing you've known since you were
small: that another person is real, and far away, and worth reaching for anyway. *(beat)* You were
the one who could love all along. *(beat)* You. The cast-out one. The one at the edge of the
kitchen. *(beat)* The only one who ever really noticed the receipt.

**A pause. The faint child, somewhere. The kettle. The world, with no hum under it.**

NARRATOR *(the last thing, quiet, almost a smile):* Go on. *(beat)* You know the one I mean. The
person you've been not-quite-reaching, because the gap felt like proof you'd get it wrong. *(beat)*
The gap isn't the proof you can't. *(beat)* The gap is the *only place it's ever happened.* *(beat)*
Go and stand on the far side of it from someone, tonight, and let them be real, and be wrecked by
it, and reach. *(beat)* Badly. *(beat)* And truly. *(beat)* That's the whole of it.

**A breath. The room. And then —**

**Out.**
