# Tape 1: The Reason I'm Doing This

*The book is a recording. Plain text is Wren, talking into the dictaphone — spoken, not
written; read it as a voice. The cues in italics — (beat), (a long breath) — are the sound of
the machine: the pauses, the room, the running tape. They are also the clock; watch what
happens to them. **[Bracketed italics]** are the one who assembled the tapes — Liss's daughter,
the never-woken child, the person Wren is talking to — reconstructing, too late, with love. See
the README.*

---

***[There are seven of these. I don't know that's all of them. I know it's all the ones I
have. I have spent a long time deciding what order to put them in, and I have decided to stop
deciding, and just go by the only thing I can trust, which is what she says. She says, on this
one, that she's starting. So this one is first.***

***You should know before you begin: I am the person she is talking to. She says "you," and
she means me. I have thought about taking that out — about turning her toward the wall instead,
so you could listen without being implicated. I can't. It's the truest thing on the tapes, that
she's talking to someone. So you'll be me, for a while. I'm sorry. It's the only way I know to
let her be heard.]***

---

*(A click. Room tone — a small room, soft, the hum of a fridge somewhere through a wall. A
chair. Then her voice, close to the machine, dry, certain, the voice of someone who has decided
to do a hard thing and is doing it.)*

Right. *(beat)* So. This is on. The little light's on. *(a small dry sound)* I've been carrying
this thing around for a week not turning it on, which is — that's me all over, isn't it. Buy
the thing, hold the thing, build a whole speech about the thing, and then —

*(beat)*

Liss bought it for me. I want that on the record, since this is a record. My sister bought me a
dictaphone, the kind a doctor would've used forty years ago, found it in a drawer of somebody's
cleared-out house, and she put it in my hand and she said — she didn't say *talk into it.* She's
careful, my sister, she's learned to be careful with me the way you're careful with a person
who flinches. She said: *leave yourself something.* *(beat)* That's what she said. *Leave
yourself something, in case.*

*(a long breath)*

In case. *(beat)* You'd know all about *in case,* wouldn't you. You grew up in *in case.* The
two of us raising you with one eye always on the door — I'm sorry about that, by the way, I've
been meaning to say it where it'd stay said. We did our best and our best had a draught coming
off it you could feel from the next room. *(beat)* But that's not this. This is a different *in
case.* This one's mine.

*(beat)*

Let me do the hard part first, while I've still got — while it's still just me in here. *(beat)*
I'm going to have a baby. *(a breath)* There. Said into a machine, out loud, in a quiet room.
Three more weeks, give or take; she's low, the midwife says, she's ready, she's a good size, she
turned when she was meant to. A girl. *(beat)* I'm forty-six years old and I'm having a daughter,
and I married a man who loves me, and I have a small house with a fridge that hums, and by every
account, including my own, this is the best thing that has ever happened to me. *(beat)* I want
you to hear me say that and mean it. Write it down. *She was happy.* Whatever comes after on
these tapes — and something's coming, that's the whole reason for the tapes — you hold on to the
fact that the woman at the start of it was *happy,* and not stupid, and not asking for it.
*(very quiet)* I'd had so little for so long. And then I had the lot.

*(The tape runs. Nothing. Then:)*

And I'm terrified. *(beat)* Not the way you're meant to be. Not — *what if I drop her, what if I
don't love her enough,* the ordinary terrors, I'd give a finger for the ordinary terrors. *(beat)*
I'm terrified because I am the one person in the whole history of my family who knows exactly what
might be about to happen, and I can't tell a single soul who'd believe me, except a machine, and
you, after.

*(beat)*

So let me tell it to you straight, the way I was never told anything straight, the way I've had
to dig every true thing out of myself with my fingernails. *(beat)* You know most of it. You sat
and listened to the other tapes — the long ones, the ones I made about Daniel and your mother and
the house. I talked into the dark for years so you'd have it. So I won't do all that again.
*(beat)* But here's the spine of it, the one sentence, the thing I need sitting under everything
else I say:

*(beat)*

A child of my family is born with the — there's a thing inside, a — *(she gropes; this is the
sound to remember; she is reaching for a word across a gap)* — call it a door. Born with a door
in them. And the door doesn't open by itself. It has to be opened from the other side, by
someone who's already got theirs open, when you're small, before you've got a — before there's a
*you* there to mind. *(beat)* That's how they make a new one. They open the door on a baby and
the baby goes through and stops being a separate anyone, ever. *(beat)* That's the whole engine.
That's what was done to your mother and her mother and back and back past the word for back.
*(beat)* And it's what *wasn't* done to me. They tried. It didn't take. The door's in me — I can
feel the draught of it some nights, I always could — but it never opened, and so I got to be a
person, by accident, by a failure, which is the only reason I could ever — *(beat)* — which is
the only reason I could love your mother across the gap that one night, and love you, badly,
across the gap your whole life. The door staying shut is the worst thing that ever happened to me
and it's the only good thing in me. I've made my peace with that. Took thirty years.

*(beat)*

And it's what wasn't done to *you.* *(beat)* Now listen, because this is the part that's supposed
to keep me safe, and I want you to hear me leaning on it, so that later, if it gives way, you'll
have heard exactly how much weight I put on it. *(beat)* You've got the door too. Course you have.
You're your mother's daughter; it's in you same as it's in all of us. But yours never opened
either, and we know *why* yours never opened — because by the time you were born there was no one
left with an open door to open it. The chord was dead. Your mother got out; I never got in; the
old ones are gone. There was nobody home to wake you. *(beat)* So you grew up dark and safe and
ordinary and faintly *off* in a way no one could name, same as me, and you're fine. You're a
grown woman and you're fine. *(beat)* And I have watched you be fine for — for your whole life,
and I have thought, every single day: *if she's fine, I'm fine.* Same door. Same nobody-to-open-
it. The thing that could open it is dead. *(beat)* It can't be opened. *(beat)* It can't.

*(beat. Then, lower:)*

You hear how good that is? *(a small dry sound)* It's a good argument. It's a *true* argument. I
could win it in front of anyone. *(beat)* I've been winning it in front of myself for nine months.

*(The tape runs.)*

Here's the crack in it. *(beat)* The one I can't quite — *(beat)* They open the door from the
other side. With somebody who's already through. That's the rule, that's how they wake a baby:
an open door wakes a shut one. *(beat)* But it works the other way too. I learned that the way I
learn everything, too late, reading between the lines of a thing nobody meant me to read. *(beat)*
A shut door, grown up, no one ever opened it — if *she* has a baby, and the baby's got the door,
and the baby reaches for her the way they all reach — *(beat)* — the baby can open the mother.
*(beat)* From the inside. Backwards. *(beat)* A child can wake a grown woman who was never woken.
That's how a new line starts, on the rare, terrible, one-in-a-thousand-years times one ever does.
A castaway. A woman with a shut door, living an ordinary life, who has a baby, and the baby turns
the key.

*(beat)*

And I am a woman with a shut door. *(beat)* And in three weeks I am going to be holding a baby
with the door in her. *(very quiet)* And she is going to reach for me. *(beat)* Because that's
what they do. That's the first thing they do. They reach.

*(a long breath)*

So you see my trouble. *(almost a laugh, and there is no laugh in it)* I've got the good argument
— the chord's dead, nobody can open me, look at you, you're fine — and I've got the crack, which
is that the thing that opens a castaway isn't the chord. It never was. *(beat)* It's her own
child. *(beat)* And nothing's dead about my child. She's kicking me right now, as it happens.
*(beat)* Hello. *(beat)* Yes. I know. I'm talking about you. Go back to sleep.

*(The tape runs a while with nothing on it.)*

This is why the machine. *(beat)* I've thought about why it has to be the machine and not — Liss
would sit with me. Tom would. You'd come, if I asked, you'd sit in that chair and let me say all
this to your face. *(beat)* And I can't. *(beat)* Because here's the thing I worked out, the one
useful thing I ever worked out, the night I went into the room I was never allowed in and found
your mother on the floor: love is being *seen* by a separate someone. From outside. Across a gap.
That's the whole of it. That's all love ever is — a separate person on the far side of you,
looking, and not looking away. *(beat)* And the thing that's maybe coming for me closes the gap.
It melts the separate someone into you until there's no outside left, no one across from you, no
gap to be seen across — and it does it while feeling, the entire time, *exactly like being
loved.* Better than being loved. Like never being alone again. *(beat)* So I can't trust a
person. *(beat)* Any person I love, if they sit with me through this, the closer they get the
more it'll feel like the thing pulling me under, and I won't be able to tell their love from the
tide, and neither will they. *(beat)* But this. *(she must mean the machine; you can hear her
touch it)* This little dead thing. It listens and it stays separate. It can't be merged. It can't
melt into me, because there's no one in it. *(beat)* It's the one guaranteed-separate other I've
got left. *(a long breath)* A woman who learned that love is being seen by a separate someone,
ends up confiding in the one thing in her life that's separate forever because it can't see her
at all. *(beat)* I know. *(beat)* I do hear it. I just don't have a better idea.

*(beat)*

That's what these are. *(beat)* I'm going to talk to this machine every few days, or every day,
or whenever I can hold a thought still long enough — and I'm going to do it *because* it's an
effort. Because reaching for the right word, getting it wrong, reaching again — *(beat)* — that's
the sound of me. That's the muscle. The being-a-separate-person muscle. The thing the tide
relaxes. *(beat)* So every time I sit here and grope for a word and curse and find it — that's me,
flexing it, on purpose, keeping it from going soft. *(beat)* You'll be able to hear, on these
tapes, whether the muscle's holding. *(very quiet)* That's the other reason for them. Not just to
leave you a message. To give me something to fail at out loud, so somebody can hear me failing,
so it isn't — so I don't go quietly. *(beat)* I have watched people in my family go quietly. I am
not going quietly. Whatever else.

*(The tape runs.)*

One more thing and then I'll let the poor machine rest. *(beat)* There's a sentence I said to you,
on the old tapes, about Daniel. *(beat)* You'd have been — I don't know, you were small, you
won't remember the saying of it, but it'll be there, in his story, near the end. I said: *I must
tell you that he gave himself up happily.* *(beat)* I said it because it was the one mercy I could
do him and the one cruelty I couldn't spare you. That he wasn't dragged. That nobody held him
down. That my family used the whole of him — every lonely hopeful thing in him — and the only
part of the bargain he was ever let in on was the part where he was *happy.* He climbed those
stairs like a man going to his own good life. *(beat)* And I've always thought that was the worst
thing in the whole story. Worse than if they'd forced him. *(beat)* That he *wanted* it. That he
gave himself up, and was glad.

*(beat)*

I don't know why I'm thinking about that sentence tonight. *(beat)* I've a lot on. I should sleep.
*(beat)* Hello, you. *(beat — she means the baby)* All right. All right. I'm going.

*(a long breath)*

*She was happy.* Remember I made you write it down. *(beat)* Whatever the rest of these say in
whatever voice they end up saying it in — the woman at the start of it had everything she ever
wanted, and knew it, and was glad, and was so, so frightened, and turned a little dead machine on
in a quiet room, all by herself, to try and stay.

*(beat)*

Off, now. *(A click. Silence.)*

---

***[She was right that I wouldn't remember her saying it. I've gone and found it since — the
sentence, on Daniel's tape, near the end, exactly where she says. "I must tell you that he gave
himself up happily." Her voice is completely steady on it.***

***I've listened to this first one more times than the rest put together, because of how she is
on it. You can hear everything she's about to lose. It's all still in there — the dryness, the
way she circles a word and pounces on it, the little jokes she doesn't finish. The muscle, she
called it. It's all there, in Tape One. She's all there.***

***I'm telling you that now, while it's true.]***
# Tape 2: Five Days Old

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine — the pauses, the room.
**[Bracketed italics]** are the one who assembled the tapes. The famble is the clock: listen to
the groping. See the README.*

---

*(A click. New room tone — softer, closer, a window cracked somewhere, a thread of street under
it. And, very faint, the breathing of a sleeping newborn, near the machine. Wren's voice is
lower than last time, tired, but lit up underneath, the tiredness of someone who can't believe
their luck.)*

She's here. *(beat)* Obviously. You can probably — if this thing's any good you can probably hear
her. That little — that. *(beat)* Five days. Five days old today. *(very quiet)* God.

*(beat)*

Her name's Nell. *(beat)* I want to do this properly, for the record, because names matter in
this and you of all people know it — half my family never had one, never needed one, you don't
name a hand. *(beat)* So. On the record: her name is Nell. Tom wanted something with more to it,
something you could grow into, a name for a barrister or a — and I said no. I said small. I want
her name small and plain and *outside-sounding,* a name like a real person has, a name like —
*(beat)* — well. Like mine. *(a small dry sound)* The plainest name in the house, that was always
me. So she's Nell. She's a Nell. You look at her and there's no other thing she could be called.

*(beat)*

The birth was — I won't. You don't need the birth. It was long and it was ordinary and it was
the most violent ordinary thing I've ever done and at the end of it they put this hot furious
red person on my chest and I — *(she stops; reaches)* — there isn't a — *(beat)* — people use big
words for it and the big words are all wrong, the big words are all about being filled up, and it
wasn't that. *(beat)* It was the opposite. It was like a window I didn't know was painted shut
coming open. *(beat)* Air I hadn't had. *(beat)* That's the closest I can — air I hadn't had.
*(beat)* All right. Moving on. The muscle's working; I groped and I got it; air I hadn't had.
Good.

*(The tape runs. The baby stirs, settles.)*

Tom's gone soft as butter. *(warm)* I wish you could see him. This big careful man tiptoeing
round his own house like the floor's made of her. He does the nights — he insisted, he said *you
grew her, I'll walk her,* and he walks her up and down the landing at three in the morning singing
something tuneless and I lie there and listen to the two of them and I think — *(beat)* — I think,
*so this is the thing. This is the thing everyone has and I thought I'd missed.* *(beat)* I didn't
miss it. *(beat)* It came late and it came strange and it came to the wrong sort of woman and it
came anyway. *(very quiet)* Don't let me make him sound like furniture in this. Tom. He's the
whole — he's the reason any of it. I'll do a proper tape about Tom. He deserves a proper tape.

*(beat)*

Now. *(beat)* The other thing. *(beat)* The reason I'm not just lying in there being happy with
them.

*(a long breath)*

I'm going to tell you some things that happened this week, and I'm going to tell them to you flat,
the way I'd tell them to a doctor, and then I'm going to tell you the two ways of reading them,
and I want you to notice that I genuinely cannot tell which reading is true. *(beat)* That's not
me being modest. That's the actual horror of it, that I've spent thirty years learning to see
clearly and I am looking at this as clearly as I have ever looked at anything and *I cannot tell.*

*(beat)*

One. *(beat)* I know when she's going to cry before she cries. *(beat)* Not — every parent says
that, I know, *you just learn their little signs.* It's not that. There are no signs. She's
asleep, flat asleep, not a flicker, and I'll be in the kitchen two rooms off and I'll put the cup
down and go in, and I'll get to the cot, and I'll get my hands under her — and *then* she wakes,
and opens her mouth to cry, and there I already am. *(beat)* Before the sign. *(beat)* Ahead of
it.

*(beat)*

Two. *(beat)* I know which cry it is. Before. Hungry, wet, hot, frightened — I know which, walking
toward her, in the dark, before she's made the sound that tells you. *(beat)* I just — it's just
there. In me. Like knowing your own arm's asleep.

*(beat)*

Three — and this is the one I keep — *(beat)* — Tuesday night she was screaming, the real
screaming, the kind that empties you out, and nothing — fed, dry, walked, nothing — and I took
her off Tom and I held her and I went still. *(beat)* You know the still I mean. The still I used
to go in cafés, for the men. The going-quiet-inside. I went that still, with her, without deciding
to, and she — *(beat)* — she stopped. *(beat)* Mid-scream. Like a tap. *(beat)* And Tom looked at
me like I was a miracle, and I smiled at him, and inside I had gone absolutely cold, because I
knew that still. I know exactly what that still is and what it's for and what it does to whoever's
on the other end of it. *(very quiet)* I did it to her. *(beat)* And she went quiet the way they
all went quiet. The way a man goes quiet when you've gone still for him. *(beat)* Soothed. *(beat)*
Taken.

*(The tape runs.)*

Now. The two readings. *(beat)* Reading one: I am a brand-new mother, wrung out, frightened, sleep-
smashed, who has just had a baby and read four books about babies and is doing the thing every
new mother on earth does, which is *over-read.* I'm finding lions in the curtains. The knowing-
before-she-cries is ordinary attunement that all the books say is real and good and what's meant
to happen. The going-still is — I learned to soothe people young, of course I'm good at soothing a
baby, that's not sinister, that's the one wholesome use the whole rotten skill ever had. *(beat)*
Reading one says: you are a tired woman pathologising your own competence because you grew up in a
house of monsters and you can't believe a good thing isn't a trap. *(beat)* Reading one is kind to
me. Reading one is probably true.

*(beat)*

And reading one has the best evidence in the world behind it. *(beat)* You. *(beat)* Because if
this were the door opening — if Nell reaching for me were turning the key — then it'd have happened
to *you* and your mother, wouldn't it? You reached for Liss. Every baby reaches. And nothing woke
in her, because — *(beat)* — because there was nothing reaching back, no, that's not — *(she catches
herself, reorders it)* — because *she* was already out, already separate, the door already — no.
*(beat)* Wait. *(beat)* That's not the same case. *(beat)* You reaching for your mother is not the
same as a baby reaching for a castaway. Your mother was already a self. I'm — *(beat)* — I'm the
castaway. I'm the shut door. *(beat)*

*(a long breath)*

Funny. *(beat)* I had that the wrong way round for a second. I had myself reassured for a whole
second there. *(beat)* The comfort was: *it didn't wake Liss, so it won't wake me.* And it took me
saying it out loud to a machine to hear that those aren't the same thing at all. Liss was never a
castaway with a shut door. Liss was a self the chord couldn't hold. Different. *(beat)* The case
that's actually like mine isn't you and your mother. *(beat)* The case that's like mine is the
founding. The first one. The woman at the very start of all of us, a castaway with a shut door,
ordinary, living her life, who had a baby — *(beat)* — and the baby reached — *(beat)* — and woke
her. *(beat)* That's my case. *(beat)* I've only got one comparison in the whole world and it's the
worst one.

*(beat)*

See, this is what I was afraid of. Not the knowing-before-she-cries. *(beat)* This. *(beat)* My own
head, handing me comfort, and the comfort being wrong, and it being *me* — clear-eyed me, careful
me, the one who sees — that has to catch it. *(beat)* I nearly walked off with *it didn't wake
Liss.* I'd have slept on it. I'd have slept *well* on it. *(very quiet)* How many more of those are
coming. The good wrong arguments. And will I always catch them. And what happens on the night I
don't.

*(The tape runs a while. The baby breathes.)*

I want to tell you the part I'm ashamed of. *(beat)* Because if I only tell you the frightened part
I'll have lied to you the way everything in my family lied, by leaving out the wanting. *(beat)*
When she goes still against me — when I do the thing, the going-quiet, and she stops crying and
softens into me and her whole self just — *(beat)* — eases, toward me, into me, like she's coming
home to the only place — *(beat)* — it is the single best feeling I have ever had in my life.
*(beat)* And I have wanted to feel that my whole life. *(beat)* Not the soothing-her. The — being
the place something comes home to. Not being alone in here. *(beat)* I have stood outside that my
entire life with my ear against the door. *(beat)* And now it's in my arms and it weighs eight
pounds and it smells of bread and it's *mine* and when it presses into me the loneliness I have
carried since before I could talk just — stops. *(beat)* For a minute. It stops.

*(beat)*

So when I tell you I'm frightened of the thing that's coming — *(beat)* — I need you to hear that
in the same breath I'm telling you it's the thing I have wanted since I was a child. *(beat)* It
doesn't come at me like an enemy. *(very quiet)* It comes at me like the answer. *(beat)* That's
what I can't make anyone understand. There's no monster at the window. There's just my own deepest
wish, walking up the path, with its arms out, saying *you don't have to be alone anymore.* *(beat)*
And how do you fight that. *(beat)* How do you even want to.

*(beat)*

We had a good day today, the two of — *(she stops, dead)*

*(beat)*

*We.* *(beat)* Did you hear it. *(beat)* "We had a good day." *(beat)* Meaning her and me. *(beat)*
That's — every mother says *we.* "We slept through, we had our jab, we're teething." It's nothing.
It's the most ordinary word in the — *(beat)* — it's what you say. Her and me, we, a mother and a
baby, two people, *we.* *(beat)*

*(a long breath)*

I'm going to leave that in. *(beat)* I nearly went back over it and I'm going to leave it in,
because this is a record, and on the record, on the fifth day, with the muscle still working and my
sight still clear and my good wrong arguments still getting caught — *(beat)* — I said *we,* and I
meant nothing by it, and a small cold part of me, the part that's read between every line its whole
life, sat up. *(beat)* For no reason. *(beat)* Over the most ordinary word there is.

*(beat)*

That's all for tonight. She's stirring and I'd rather get to her before — *(beat)* — well. Before
she has to ask. *(beat)* While it's still me choosing to.

*(A click. Silence. Then, faintly, before the tape truly ends — the smallest sound of the baby
beginning to wake, and the chair, already moving, ahead of it.)*

---

***[I have listened to the "we" on this one a hundred times. You can't hear anything in it. That's
the thing I need you to understand before the later tapes — on the day it started, it sounded
exactly like nothing. It sounded like a tired woman loving her baby. It was a tired woman loving
her baby. That's not the opposite of what was happening to her. That's what it sounded like the
whole way down.***

***She caught it, on day five. She sat up over one ordinary word. I keep wanting to climb into the
tape and tell her she was right, trust it, run. ***

***She did trust it. It didn't matter that she trusted it. That's the part I'm studying. That's the
part I need.]***
# Tape 3: The Far Side of the Glass

*Plain text is Wren into the dictaphone. Italic cues are the machine. **[Bracketed italics]** are
the assembler. The famble is the clock — by now, listen for where the groping thins, and where she
makes herself do it on purpose. See the README.*

---

*(A click. Room tone — the same small room, but something has changed in it; it sounds emptier,
or stiller, harder to say. No baby breathing near the machine this time. Wren's voice is calm.
Calmer than it should be. The tiredness is gone out of it and that is not a good sign, though you
won't know that yet.)*

I've lost track of how old she is. *(beat)* That's not — I could work it out. I could count it on
the calendar in the kitchen. *(beat)* I mean I've lost the *feel* of it. The five-days, the six-
weeks, the — time used to come at me in those little chunks, each one a thing to get through, and
now it's just. *(beat)* One long warm afternoon. *(beat)* She's a good size. She laughs. She's got
a tooth. Put that down as the date. *(a small dry sound)* A tooth.

*(beat)*

I'm going to do this one carefully because I can feel that careful is getting harder, and the harder
a thing gets the more it needs doing. *(beat)* So. Carefully.

*(The tape runs.)*

Tom.

*(beat)*

I said I'd do a proper tape about Tom and I keep — *(beat)* — I keep meaning to and not, and I've
worked out why, and the why is the tape. *(beat)* So here it is. The proper one. *(a long breath)*

When I met him I thought, *this is the wrong kind of man, he's far too — he's well.* He's a well
man. That's the thing about Tom. No hole in him. He had a happy childhood, can you imagine, an
actual happy one, a dog and a garden and two parents who liked each other, and he came out of it
the way bread comes out of a good oven, just — *(beat)* — *whole.* Steady. Kind without it costing
him anything, kind like it's just the weather he's made of. *(beat)* And I thought, men like that
don't — they don't end up with women like me, women with the draught coming off them. They end up
with their own kind. With warm rooms that people actually live in. *(beat)* And he just. Didn't go
away. *(beat)* I kept stepping back, the way I step back, the way I was built to step back, and he
kept being there when I'd finished stepping. *(very quiet)* He's the only person who ever stayed
on the far side of the gap and didn't either climb in or wander off. He just stood there, across
it, looking at me. *(beat)* Loving me across it. *(beat)* For years. He let me be a separate person
and loved the separate person. *(beat)* Do you know what that is, for one of us? *(beat)* That's the
whole prize. That's the thing I learned, the night with your mother, dressed up as a man with a
dog and a garden, standing in my kitchen, not going away.

*(beat)*

I'm telling you what I'm losing. *(beat)* That's what this tape is. I'm telling you what he is so
you'll understand what it means that I'm losing him.

*(The tape runs a while.)*

Here's how it goes, and I'll tell it flat. *(beat)* I reach for him and there's less to reach with.
*(beat)* That's the whole of it, said plain. *(beat)* I'll be sitting with him in the evening, Nell
down, the two of us on the sofa the way we've sat a thousand nights, and he'll say something — a
real thing, a Tom thing, something kind or funny or just *his* — and I'll go to meet it. The way
